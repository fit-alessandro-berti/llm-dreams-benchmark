...stop arguing and instead hand your friend the words—literally. You open your mouth and the argument comes out as small paper cranes, each one folded from a sentence you were about to shout. They tumble onto the table between you, and your friend picks one up, unfolds it carefully, and reads it aloud in your mother's voice.

The kitchen has become the beach where you both learned to swim. The tide is coming in, but the water is made of clock faces, each one showing a different time. Your friend says, "We were never really fighting about the thing, were we?" And you realize the important thing—the thing the argument was about—has been in the room the whole time, in the shape of a small wooden door neither of you has opened.

You decide to open it together. Behind the door is the argument itself, much smaller than you expected, curled up and sleeping like a cat. You could wake it. You don't. You close the door gently.

Your friend hands you a glass of water that tastes like forgiveness, or maybe just like the color blue. You drink it. The beach tilts. Someone is calling your name from very far away, from a room with morning light in it—
