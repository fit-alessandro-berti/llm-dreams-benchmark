...find water.

That's the first thought that cuts through the fog. Not *where am I* or *what year is this* — just the animal need, dry and immediate.

I'm lying on packed earth. Above me, a sky so deep and unmarked it feels like drowning in reverse — no contrails, no haze, just stars punched through black velvet with a thumbtack. More stars than I've ever seen, or ever will see in the world I came from.

The air smells like wood smoke and crushed sage and something sweet I can't name.

I sit up. A low stone wall runs in an uneven line to my left, and beyond it, a valley drops away into darkness. I can hear water — not close, but not impossibly far. A river, maybe, or a stream swollen with snowmelt. Somewhere down there.

My hands are bare. My clothes feel wrong — too thin, too synthetic, too *mine*. I'm still wearing the world I left like an ill-fitting costume.

A dog barks. Then another.

There are fires in the valley — orange points scattered like fallen constellations. A settlement. Not large. Maybe a few hundred people at most. And from the construction I can see in the moonlight — low buildings, flat roofs, the glint of irrigation channels — I'm somewhere I shouldn't be able to name but somehow can.

Mesopotamia. Or what will someday be called that.

I stand and my legs are shaky, and I think: *I need to get down there before dawn, because what explanation could I possibly offer in daylight?* The stranger with no tribe, no language they'd recognize, no tools, no knowledge worth trading except the useless kind — the kind that lives in a mind shaped by electricity and abundance.

But then a different thought arrives, quieter:

*What if I don't explain? What if I just... listen?*

The water is down there. The fires are down there. And somewhere in that scatter of light, people are telling stories I will never hear again in any other era — stories that won't be written down for centuries, if ever.

I start walking.
