...speak, but the microphone is a sunflower, and it leans away from your mouth every time you get close.

You look down at your notes and find they're written in your own handwriting, but the handwriting you had in third grade, all careful loops, and it says only: TELL THEM ABOUT THE STAIRS.

You don't know anything about the stairs. But the audience is already nodding — your kindergarten teacher is there, and the neighbor's dog from the house on Willow Street, grown very old, and rows and rows of people whose faces you almost remember. They are all waiting to hear about the stairs.

So you begin. "The stairs," you say, and your voice comes out as the sound of rain on a tin roof, and somehow everyone understands. A woman in the back dabs her eyes. The dog sighs with recognition.

You notice you are wearing your father's coat, or maybe the coat is wearing you; it fits the way water fits a river. The podium has become a piano. The stage is now the beach from the summer you were nine, and the tide is coming in politely, one row of seats at a time.

You keep talking about the stairs — how they go up but also sideways, how the seventh one creaks in a way that means someone loves you, how you've been climbing them your whole life without ever leaving the ground. It is the truest thing you have ever said. You have no idea where it's coming from.

The lights dim to the color of late afternoon. Everyone rises. The applause sounds like pages turning, like a thousand books closing at once, gently.

Then the sunflower finally leans toward you and whispers: "That was good. But you forgot to mention the landing."

You want to ask what landing, but the audience is filing out through a door that is also a window, and the tide reaches your shoes, warm as bathwater, and you understand — with the perfect, dissolving clarity of dreams — that the speech is over, it went well, and the stairs will still be there tomorrow.

Somewhere, faintly, an alarm clock begins to ring.

"Don't worry," says the sunflower. "I'll take questions."
