You wake up in a completely different era, with no modern technology around. As you explore your new surroundings, you realize you need to find a way to navigate this world—one where the hum of electricity is replaced by the rustle of wind through unfamiliar trees, and the glow of screens is supplanted by the soft light of dawn filtering through thatched roofs.

The air carries a scent of woodsmoke and damp earth. A path of packed dirt winds ahead, flanked by fields of golden wheat swaying in a breeze that feels both ancient and alive. Somewhere in the distance, a bell tolls—deep, resonant, marking time by a measure you don’t recognize.

Your hands feel strange without any device to hold. Your mind, accustomed to instant information, now reaches for memories of maps, constellations, or languages—anything that might help. You see a small village below, built of stone and timber, with a waterwheel turning lazily beside a stream. Figures in simple, layered clothing move with purpose, tending to animals, carrying baskets, speaking in a dialect that is at once familiar and utterly foreign.

You realize you need to learn the rhythm of this place. You need to understand its rules, its economy, its seasons. More than that, you need to understand how to belong—even if only for a dream’s fleeting moment.

Do you approach the village, watch from a distance, or follow the stream to see where it leads?
