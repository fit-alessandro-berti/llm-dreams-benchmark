You are walking through a peaceful forest when suddenly the weather changes dramatically. You feel a storm approaching and the air grows thick with electricity, like the forest itself is holding its breath. The sky bruises purple and green, clouds twisting into impossible shapes—faces you half-remember from childhood, animals with too many eyes, equations that solve themselves in lightning flashes.

Rain falls, but it’s not rain. Each drop is a tiny glowing ember that sizzles on your skin without burning, whispering secrets as it lands: *You’ve been carrying too much. Let it go.* The wind howls your name in a voice that sounds like every version of you at once—child, wanderer, dreamer, the one who almost gave up. Trees bend low, their branches forming a living tunnel that pulls you forward against your will.

You stumble into a clearing where the storm is fiercest. A single ancient oak stands in the center, roots glowing like veins of gold. Lightning strikes it three times, and with each strike the world fractures:

First flash—you’re seven again, lost in these same woods, crying until a fox with silver fur leads you home.

Second flash—you’re older, standing at a crossroads you chose wrong, the storm exactly like the one raging inside you that day.

Third flash—the fox returns, now the size of a house, its eyes full of galaxies. It speaks without moving its mouth: *The storm isn’t here to destroy you. It’s here to wake you.*

You reach out. The fox dissolves into light. The storm stops instantly. The clouds part like curtains, revealing a sky full of stars even though it’s still daytime. The forest exhales. Every leaf, every stone, every drop of ember-rain begins to hum the same quiet melody—the one you’ve been trying to remember your whole life.

You sit beneath the oak, heart finally quiet, and close your eyes.

When you open them, you’re awake in your bed. Sunlight filters through your curtains. Your pillow smells faintly of pine and rain. And in your hand—impossible, yet there—is a single glowing leaf that slowly fades, leaving only the memory of thunder and the certain knowledge that whatever storm you’re walking through right now… you’re not walking it alone.
