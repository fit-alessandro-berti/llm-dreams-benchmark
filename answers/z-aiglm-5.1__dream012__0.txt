The old boatswain — what was his name? The name slips away, as names do in dreams — grabs a line and yells to the younger hands, "You heard the captain! Trim the mainsail! Let the storm blow *through* us, not *over* us!"

There's a terrifying moment where the ship crests the wave and hangs suspended in the air, weightless, the sea a black void below. Time stretches. You can hear your own heartbeat, the rain frozen mid-fall around you.

Then the hull slams down. Joints crack. Something splinters below deck.

But she holds.

"Weather that helm!" you call out, and there's something in your voice — not fear, not exactly. It's the strange clarity that comes in the space between living and drowning, where every choice matters and the world reduces itself to something simple: *keep them alive.*

The moonlight flickers again, and for just a moment, the horizon doesn't seem quite so endless.
