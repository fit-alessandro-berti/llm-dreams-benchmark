You wake up in a completely different era, with no modern technology around. As you explore your new surroundings, you realize you need to find a way to blend in, to understand the rules of this time, and—most urgently—to figure out *when* you are.

The air smells of woodsmoke and damp earth. Your clothes feel strange—rough-spun and simple. The sounds are wrong: no engines, no electronic hums, just distant animal calls, the murmur of voices in an unfamiliar dialect, and the rhythmic tap of a blacksmith’s hammer somewhere down a muddy lane.

You see thatched roofs, small gardens of herbs and vegetables, and people moving with a purpose dictated by daylight. A woman carries water from a well, her dress a simple, long woolen garment. Men in tunics and leggings are repairing a wooden fence.

You need to know the year, the place. Your first instinct is to find someone who might understand you, but that’s risky. The language, even if it sounds vaguely familiar, might be archaic. You decide to watch, to listen.

Then you see a boy dropping a basket of apples. You move without thinking to help him gather them. He looks up, startled, then gives a cautious nod. You point to yourself, then to the sky, trying to convey “lost.” He doesn’t understand, but his expression softens. He gestures for you to follow, leading you toward the center of the village, where a larger stone building—perhaps a church or a manor house—stands.

As you walk, you overhear snippets of conversation, catch a word that sounds like a very old form of English. You start to piece it together: this isn’t just the past. This feels like the *deep* past. Medieval, perhaps.

You need to find a calendar, a date, a landmark. But first, you need a name to give, a story to tell that won’t mark you as insane or a spy. You take a breath, touch the strange fabric of your sleeve, and prepare to step into the conversation—into a world that has no idea you don’t belong.
