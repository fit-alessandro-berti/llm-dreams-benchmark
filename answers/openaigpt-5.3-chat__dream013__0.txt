You are in a maze filled with complex puzzles. Each puzzle requires intense concentration and logical thinking to solve, and as you move deeper into the maze, you begin to notice something strange: the puzzles already feel familiar.

At first it’s subtle. A sequence of symbols that you somehow recognize before you study them. A lock that opens the moment you reach for it, as if your hands already know the solution. You tell yourself it’s intuition.

But then the maze changes.

The walls rearrange themselves behind you with a soft grinding sound, like stone remembering a different shape. A puzzle appears before you—gears, mirrors, and threads of light crossing a circular frame. Without thinking, you rotate the mirrors. The beams align perfectly. The door opens.

You realize you solved it before you even understood it.

Further in, the puzzles grow impossible: equations written in shifting ink, rooms where gravity tilts sideways, corridors that loop back into themselves unless you take the wrong turn on purpose. Yet each time, a quiet certainty guides you. Not memory exactly. Something deeper.

Then you reach the center.

There is no puzzle here—only a table and a single sheet of paper.

On the paper is a map of the maze.

Every corridor. Every puzzle. Every solution.

And in the corner, written in your own handwriting, are the words:

“Start again. You almost figured it out last time.”

Behind you, the entrance door closes.

Somewhere in the maze, the first puzzle resets.
