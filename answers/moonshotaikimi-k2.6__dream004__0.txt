...and the moment my fingers hover above its surface, the whiteness begins to hum. Not with sound, but with the memory of colors that haven't existed yet. The shifting landscape behind me—a forest of liquid triangles, a sky breathing in shades of impossible blue—pauses, as though inhaling.

I realize the canvas isn't empty; it's hungry.

When my fingertip finally brushes the center, the world inverts. The colors don't splash outward; they drain inward, spiraling from the horizon into the rough weave of the cloth. The melting mountains flow like watercolor into the bottom left corner. The clouds, heavy with silver and rust, streak across the blankness in violent, beautiful strokes that I am not making—at least, not with my hands.

I step back, or perhaps the ground beneath me steps forward, and I see that the canvas has become a window. No—a mirror. In it, my reflection is composed entirely of the colors the world has lost. I am a silhouette of stolen sunsets, my eyes two points of absolute void that somehow shine. When I raise my hand, the painted version of me raises a brush that drips with the sound of rain.

Then the canvas exhales.

Paint rises from it like steam, like spirits, reconstructing the world around me but wrong-perfect: trees made of canvas texture, rivers running with thick, glossy oil, a moon that is a perfect circle of untouched white floating in a purple sky. I understand, with the sudden clarity that only comes in dreams, that I am meant to step through. The frame has grown, or I have shrunk, or perspective has simply decided to rest.

I place my palm against the painted grass. It is warm and slightly damp. Behind me, the real-or-unreal world continues its chromatic dance, but ahead, inside the frame, the colors have chosen to stay still, waiting for me to name them.

I take a breath that tastes of linseed and starlight, and I step inside, becoming the only moving brushstroke in a painting that dreams itself awake.
