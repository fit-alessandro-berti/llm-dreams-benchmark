The noise of the city doesn't quiet down, but it stops pressing in. One of them, a woman with paint on her hands and a map folded into her back pocket, smiles and says, "We get lost here on purpose sometimes. Best way to find the good stuff."

They don't rush you. One points out a bakery with steam fogging the windows, where the baker is sliding trays of bread onto a cooling rack. Another pulls a spare pair of wired headphones from his jacket and hands them over. "No directions, just a soundtrack," he says. "Cities make more sense with a score."

You walk with them for three blocks, or maybe ten. Time gets fuzzy. They tell you which murals change every month, which alley has the cat that runs the bodega, where the stairs lead down to a river most tourists miss. You're not just following anymore — you're noticing.

When you reach the subway entrance you were originally trying to find, you realize you don't need it right now. The woman with the paint hands you the folded map. It's not a real map. It's a napkin with a circle drawn in the middle and the words "you are here" written in blue ink.

"You're not lost," she says. "You just hadn't been found yet."

You laugh, and it surprises you how easy it sounds. The group keeps walking, but two of them glance back and wave, like they're making sure you know the way back to yourself.

You stand there for a moment, napkin in hand, headphones on, the city suddenly feeling less like a maze and more like a conversation you'd just been invited into. And you start walking — not home, not yet — but toward the smell of bread and the river you didn't know existed.
