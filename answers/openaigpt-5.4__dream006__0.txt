…realize you’re holding not notes, but a dripping ice cream cone.

A murmur runs through the audience. You look down at your suit and somehow it has turned into your old school uniform, one shoe is missing, and the microphone in front of you bends toward your face like a curious flower. You clear your throat to explain, but when you open your mouth, a flock of bright paper birds flutters out instead, circling the chandeliers overhead.

Instead of panicking, the crowd leans forward in delight.

So you begin anyway.

At first, no words come—only music, soft and distant, as if an orchestra were hidden behind the curtains of the sky. The stage stretches longer beneath your feet, becoming a wooden pier over a silver lake. The audience remains in their seats, but now they are seated in little rowboats, gently drifting, all still watching you expectantly.

You raise the melting ice cream cone like it is the most important object in the world and somehow this feels exactly right. You start speaking about things you did not know you knew: about all the doors you never opened, all the names you forgot, all the mornings that slipped away before you were fully awake. Your voice echoes across the water, and each sentence becomes a ribbon of light, floating out over the crowd. People reach up and tie the ribbons around their wrists.

Then, in the front row, you notice someone familiar—you, only much younger, smiling as though you’ve finally remembered the line you were supposed to say all along.

The missing shoe returns, descending slowly from above like a blessing.

The microphone flower blooms.

And just as the entire audience rises in applause, the lake begins to lift into the air, carrying the boats, the stage, the lights, and your unfinished speech up among the stars, where you continue speaking effortlessly in a language made of rain, bells, and breathing, certain that if you can just finish this one last sentence—

—you wake up, with the feeling that it was the most important speech you’ve ever almost given.
