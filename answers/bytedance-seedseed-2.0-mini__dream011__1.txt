You are about to take the final exam for a course that determines your future career. As you sit down to start the exam, you notice the questions are not printed in crisp, black textbook ink, but scrawled in smudged, honey-brown crayon across the back of a well-loved children’s book about fireflies—your copy from elementary school, the spine held together with clear tape and a sticky note that says “I love reading!” in your 8-year-old handwriting.

The exam hall itself is odd, too: there are no walls, just floating oak desks strung with fairy lights that smell like cut clover, and the proctor is your childhood self, pigtails askew and clutching a clipboard made of birch bark, who nods when you glance over. The first question stares up at you, half-covered by a fallen firefly that’s glowing soft gold:
1. When was the last time you did something just because it felt right, not because it would get you a check or a checkmark on a to-do list?

You reach into your backpack for a pen, but pull out a dandelion fluff instead that unfurls into a watercolor brush when you lift it. As you scribble down your answer—“Last Tuesday, when I stopped to help a stranger fix their flat tire on the highway overpass, even though I was late to my job interview”—the paper shifts. The second question blooms in glowing ink around the edges of the firefly:
2. What kind of work would you build if you never had to worry about money?

This time, your throat tightens. You’d spent weeks cramming for a “serious professional career” in corporate marketing, but the words that pour onto the crayon paper aren’t about spreadsheets or client pitches. They’re about a tiny shop tucked between a laundromat and a bakery, where you fix vintage bicycles and teach free drawing classes to kids who don’t have art supplies at home.

Just as you finish writing, the fairy lights flicker, and the exam hall dissolves into the exact parking lot where you stopped to help that stranger. The stranger is smiling, holding up their fixed bike, and hands you a paper cup of cold lemonade. Your childhood self appears next to you, holding the same firefly book, and tacks a new “question” to your chest: it’s a single line, written in your grown-up handwriting, that says *You already are the person this career is for*.

When you blink back to the exam desk, the crayon-scrawled firefly book is gone, replaced by a single blank page. The proctor—now your favorite art teacher from high school, with paint-stained overalls—grins and says, “The grade’s already given. You just had to remember what matters.”
