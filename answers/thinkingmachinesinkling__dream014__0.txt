One of them, an older woman with silver hair woven with tiny lights, presses a small brass key into your palm. “You weren’t lost,” she says, smiling. “You were being called here.” Behind you, the bustling city isn’t frightening anymore; its chaos resolves into music, every honk and footstep harmonizing into a lullaby. You realize the strangers have no faces of their own—only reflections of your own kindness, multiplied back at you.

Then the ground tilts gently, and you are floating upward, the key warming in your hand, unlocking not a door but a feeling: you are home, and you always have been, even in the crowd. The city glows gold, and you wake—not with a jolt, but with the lingering warmth of belonging, the dream fading like mist leaving a clear sky.
