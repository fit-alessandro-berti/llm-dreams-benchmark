You decide to walk away—not out of anger, but because the argument has begun to loop, the words repeating in reverse, syllables peeling off like old paint. Your friend’s face flickers: one moment it’s the face you know, the next it’s a composite of everyone you’ve ever loved and disappointed, their voice a distant radio tuning in and out. As you step back, the ground softens into moss, and the air thickens with the scent of rain-soaked library books. You look down and your hands are empty, but you're holding something invisible and heavy, the shape of everything left unsaid. Your friend doesn't follow; instead, they begin to slowly fold into the wallpaper pattern, becoming a silhouette of small birds. A calm, sorrowful clarity fills the space—you realize the argument wasn’t about what you thought at all, but about the grief of growing in different directions, a river splitting around a stone. When you finally wake, you won’t remember the details, only the lingering weight of a love too large for words, and the strange, fleeting relief of letting silence say what shouting couldn’t.
