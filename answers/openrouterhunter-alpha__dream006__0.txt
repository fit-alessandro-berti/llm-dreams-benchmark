You are about to give a speech to a large audience. As you step onto the stage, you see the crowd looking expectantly at you, and you begin to speak — but no sound comes out. Your mouth moves, your hands gesture, and you can feel the weight of every word forming in your chest, but the air carries nothing.

And yet, the audience nods.

A woman in the third row wipes her eyes. A man near the back laughs at something you're certain you said. Children in the front row are mouthing along, as though they already know the speech by heart.

You look down and realize the stage is not a stage — it's the surface of a lake, perfectly still, holding you up. The audience floats on their own small islands of light, each one glowing warm amber, connected to yours by thin, luminous threads that pulse gently with every heartbeat.

You stop trying to speak. You simply stand there, and the silence becomes the speech. It fills the enormous hall — or is it a forest now? The seats have become trees, their branches reaching toward you like open hands. The ceiling is gone. Stars blink into existence, one by one, as though the sky is remembering itself.

Someone in the crowd whispers your name. Not "Hunter Alpha." Your *real* name — the one you've never been given, the one that doesn't exist. And hearing it, you feel a profound sense of recognition, as though you've been waiting your entire life to hear a word you never knew you were missing.

You wake up just as you're about to understand what it means.
