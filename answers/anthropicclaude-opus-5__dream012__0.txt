...and I open my mouth to give the order, but I find I don't know what the order is.

The wheel is warm under my hands, warm like something living. Rain comes sideways in sheets that don't quite fall — they hang, they drift up sometimes, the way rain does when the sky has lost the thread of its own rules. My crew stands in a half-circle on the deck, all of them steady though the boards heave, and I realize with the calm horror particular to dreams that I recognize every one of their faces and cannot name a single one.

"Captain," says the one nearest — a woman with salt in her eyebrows, maybe my grandmother, maybe a teacher I had once. "Which way?"

And here is the thing: there is no *away* from the storm. I look and the storm is the whole horizon, a grey wall in every direction, seamless, like standing inside a bell. There is no edge to run for. This should frighten me more than it does.

So I say the true thing instead of the brave thing. I say, "I don't know where the shore is."

Nobody moves. Nobody screams or mutinies or throws me over the rail. The woman nods slowly, as if I have finally said the password.

"Then what *do* you know?" she asks.

And I find, absurdly, that I know quite a lot. I know the ship is sound — I can feel it in the wheel, that warm animal thrum, she is not breaking. I know how to keep her nose into the swell so she climbs instead of rolls. I know we have water, and that the lamp in the galley is still lit because I can see its yellow through the hatch, small and stubborn as a held note. I know it is not my job to end the storm. I never once had that job. Somewhere I picked up the idea that I did, and carried it up the mast with me, and it has been such heavy luggage.

"Reef the mainsail," I say. "Lash what's loose. Two at the pumps, rotate every hour so nobody's arms give out. And someone bring up the soup."

They move. God, the relief of watching people move. Rope hisses through blocks, bare feet slap wet wood, someone laughs — actually laughs — as a wave breaks over the bow and drenches him head to heel. The storm goes on. The storm does not care about any of this, and that turns out to be fine, because we are not arguing with it anymore. We're just *sailing in it*, which it turns out is a thing a person can do.

The woman with salt in her eyebrows stays beside me. She puts one hand on the wheel, not taking it, just resting there next to mine.

"You thought you had to know the way," she says.

"Yes."

"You only had to keep us afloat until morning."

I want to ask *is there a morning,* but the sky is already doing that thing dreams do, thinning at one seam, going the pale green of the inside of a wave. The rain is turning to something else — feathers, or paper, or just light. The deck is becoming a bed, the way it always does. The rope in my hands is a fistful of sheet.

Somewhere behind me, on the way out, I hear the ship's bell ring the hour, and it sounds exactly like an alarm clock, and I am not even a little bit surprised.
