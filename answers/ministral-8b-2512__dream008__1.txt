You stare at the drawing. The toaster-spaceship has a dial labeled **"EMOTIONAL RESONANCE"** and a button that says **"PRESS IF YOU’RE NOT SURE."** Your hands tremble—not from fear, but because they’re *too warm*, like they’ve been dipped in honey. The keyboard beneath you is sticky, and when you press a key, it doesn’t type a letter—it plays a sound: a child’s laughter, a distant train whistle, the *click* of a camera shutter.

Your boss watches you, their face flickering between concern and amusement. *"You’re the only one who can do this,"* they murmur. *"Because you’re the only one who’s ever seen the client’s other hand."*

You look down. Your own hand is *split*—one side is yours, the fingers long and precise, the other is *wrong*, like a puppet’s hand, with too many joints and a thumb that curls backward. The wrong hand starts scribbling on the blueprint on its own, drawing lines that shouldn’t exist, adding words like **"TIME IS A RIVER"** and **"BUT THE RIVER IS MADE OF GLASS."**

A notification pops up on your screen: **"DEADLINE ADJUSTED: NOW."** The time on your computer reads **12:00 AM**, but the clock in the corner of the room ticks backward. The boss sighs. *"Just… make it *useful*,"* they say, and they’re gone, their chair rolling away as if pulled by an invisible hand.

You have no idea what to do. The toaster-spaceship hums faintly, as if it’s already alive. The wrong hand keeps writing. Somewhere in the room, a door opens and closes by itself. A voice—yours?—whispers from the ceiling: *"You don’t need to finish it. You just need to *believe* in it."*

And then, the impossible happens: the toaster-spaceship *lifts off the desk*, hovering just above the floor. It tilts toward you, and you see that the **"EMOTIONAL RESONANCE"** dial is now labeled **"YOUR FEAR."** The button reads: **"PRESS IF YOU’RE READY TO LET GO."**
