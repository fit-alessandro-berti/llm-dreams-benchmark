To my left, a cluster of people in 1920s evening wear are floating an inch off the parquet floor, sipping champagne from glasses that never empty. When I try to excuse myself past them, I don't walk—I drift forward like a slow-motion astronaut, my feet hovering just above the Persian rug. 

I spot a familiar flash of blue fabric near the far end of the ballroom—the exact shade of the jacket my friend was wearing. I push off against a pillar of white marble, but instead of moving forward, the room tilts sideways. The ceiling chandeliers become wall sconces, and the murmur of conversation transforms into the sound of rushing water. 

I drift closer to the blue fabric, heart pounding with the urgent, nonsensical panic unique to dreams. I reach out a hand to tap the person on the shoulder. 

They turn around. 

It isn't my friend. It’s an old elementary school teacher I haven’t thought of in twenty years, holding a glowing hourglass instead of a drink. She smiles warmly and says, *"You're late for the exam, and you didn't bring your teeth."*

Panic sets in. I instinctively check my mouth with my tongue—and sure enough, they feel loose, rattling like loose dice. 

Behind me, the music swells—a grand, echoing waltz played entirely on glass harmonicas. I turn around to retrace my path, but the door I came through has vanished, replaced by a wall of endless, shimmering mirrors that reflect not me, but...
