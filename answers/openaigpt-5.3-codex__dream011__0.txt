…written in your own handwriting.

Not just the notes in the margins—the entire exam, every question, every typo, every doodle of a tiny ladder in the corner of page three. You flip to the back, and there’s a final prompt:

**“If you could choose who you become, why haven’t you yet?”**

The clock on the wall has no numbers, only words: *Later. Later. Later.*  
Other students are already writing furiously, but their pens make no sound. You try to read Question 1 and the sentence keeps changing:

- “Define success.”
- “Defend success.”
- “Defy success.”

Your mouth goes dry. You raise your hand, and the proctor glides over without footsteps. It’s your old self—ten years younger, eyes bright, carrying all the plans you forgot. They lean down and whisper, “It’s open-book.”

“Where’s the book?” you ask.

They tap your chest twice. “You brought it.”

Suddenly the room stretches into a long hallway of desks, each one holding a version of your life: one where you played it safe, one where you moved away, one where you quit, one where you tried again. At the far end is a blank desk with your name on it, untouched, waiting.

The timer buzzes. Everyone stands. You haven’t written a word.

Then you look down and realize your answer sheet is already filled in—line after line in steady ink:

**“I choose now.”**

The proctor smiles, stamps PASS in red across the page, and the stamp bleeds outward until it becomes sunrise through your bedroom curtains.

You wake up with your heart racing, as if you’re late for something important—  
and for once, you know exactly what it is.
