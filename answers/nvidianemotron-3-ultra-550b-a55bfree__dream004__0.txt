a brush that wasn't there a moment ago rests in your hand. Its bristles shift—feathers, then fibers, then something like light given form.

You don't remember lifting it. But the first stroke arrives anyway: a line of impossible blue that hums when you look at it sideways. The sound tastes like copper and honey.

The canvas drinks it. Hungry.

Another stroke. A circle that opens into a hallway. A hallway that folds into a bird. The bird sings in geometries—triangles dissolving into spirals, spirals blooming into petals that fall upward into a sky that hasn't decided what color it wants to be today.

You paint a door. It knocks from the other side.

You paint a window. Your reflection blinks three seconds too late.

You paint your own name. The letters untangle themselves, crawl off the canvas, and burrow into the ground like roots. Flowers erupt—clock faces for petals, each ticking a different rhythm. One runs backward. One runs in prime numbers. One ticks only when you're not looking.

The brush grows heavier. Lighter. Warm as a fever. Cold as a question you can't quite ask.

Somewhere, a bell rings. Or maybe that's the sound of the canvas breathing.

You stand before the unfinished painting. The brush waits.

What do you paint next?
