You are the captain of a ship in the middle of a turbulent sea. Your crew looks to you for guidance as the storm worsens, and you realize you cannot remember where you were sailing—or why.

Lightning tears open the sky. For an instant, the waves become mountains of black glass, and beneath them you glimpse the lights of a city sleeping on the ocean floor.

“Captain,” whispers your first mate, pointing ahead.

A lighthouse stands in the distance, impossibly balanced on the crest of a wave. Its beam sweeps across the ship, and wherever the light touches, the storm freezes: rain hangs like silver beads, gulls remain motionless in the air, and the sea hardens beneath the hull.

You order the crew toward it.

The ship climbs the frozen wave. At the top, you find a small red door set into the lighthouse. Behind it is not a staircase, but your childhood bedroom. The blankets are rumpled. Moonlight spills across the floor. On the pillow rests a brass compass, its needle spinning wildly.

When you pick it up, the needle stops and points at your chest.

Outside, the crew begins calling your name, but their voices sound farther away with every breath. You look through the bedroom window and see the ship below, breaking apart as the storm awakens. Your sailors are no longer sailors, but people you have known and forgotten, each clinging to a different piece of the wreck.

The compass grows warm in your hand.

At last, you understand: it was never meant to show you where to go. It was meant to show you what you refused to leave behind.

You open the window and step onto the sea.

The water holds your weight.

One by one, you call the crew by their true names. Each time you do, the waves grow smaller. The thunder softens. The broken ship gathers itself plank by plank, sail by sail, until it floats whole beneath a clearing sky.

At dawn, you stand again at the helm. No map waits before you, and no star marks the way.

Still, the compass points steadily forward.

This time, that is enough.
