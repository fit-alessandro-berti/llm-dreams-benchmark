…each group feels like it has its own gravity.

At first, you drift toward familiar laughter, but when you get close you realize you’re not recognizing faces—you’re recognizing *roles*. Someone is “the host,” someone is “the joker,” someone is “the couple who knows everyone.” Their conversations wash over you like music from another room: pleasant, rhythmic, and completely unintelligible in the places where meaning should be.

You keep checking the crowd for your one person, but the more you look, the harder it becomes to remember what their outline even is. It’s like trying to point at a candle flame while the wind keeps turning your wrist. You think, *Okay. Just find their voice.* So you listen.

A laugh rises above the rest—short, distinct, the exact texture of the person you’re searching for. Relief surges through you… and then you realize the laugh keeps repeating, as if it’s been copied and pasted. A dozen mouths make the same sound at different corners of the hall. Every time you move toward one, another one appears farther away, perfectly timed to lure you in.

You start walking faster, cutting through conversations with awkward apologies that feel too practiced, too smooth, like you’ve rehearsed this panic in a previous dream. People part politely. No one is rude. No one stops you. That somehow makes it worse, because it means there’s nothing to fight—no friction, no resistance, just an endless openness that keeps redirecting you.

Finally, you notice the floor.

Instead of carpet, it’s patterned like a map: streets of dark and light in looping squares. You swear you’ve seen this pattern before, in a place you can’t reach when you wake. When you step onto it, the map shifts under your feet, re-routing itself the way a river changes after rain. You follow a line that you hope leads to your person, only to watch the line split and braid into others—paths that look certain from far away but vanish into confusion when you get near.

You reach a doorway you don’t remember seeing. Beyond it is a smaller room, crowded but quieter, like the echo chamber before a storm. In the center stands a table with a single chair pulled out, waiting. There’s nothing on the table except a tiny stack of name tags—blank, except for one label that’s already printed.

Your name.

You don’t understand why it’s your name. You look around for the person you came for, but everyone in the room is facing you as if they’re watching the moment you decide what to do. Their expressions are neutral, patient—like librarians or doctors. Like people who have seen this dream before.

Your hands move on their own. You pick up a name tag, turn it over, and the back is covered in handwriting that isn’t yours but feels like it belongs in your mouth. The words make sense in your head and almost not at all in your language. Still, you can read enough to feel the intention:

*You only ever know one person because you only ever let yourself be known as one.*

The crowd’s patience tightens into expectation. You suddenly understand that you’ve been searching outward because you think the missing person is “out there.” But the dream has been asking you to check whether the “one person” is something you’re carrying—something you’ve been avoiding looking at directly.

You glance at the chair by the table.

It’s warm, as if someone just stood up. When you lean closer, you see a slight imprint on the cushion, shaped exactly like the posture of your searching hope. Not a body—an absence shaped like a body.

The laugh returns. Not from mouths this time, but from the walls themselves, coming from behind the paint like someone breathing through a shared secret. It sounds like your person again—only now it’s threaded with frustration, the way a song sounds when you’re trying to remember the next line.

Your chest tightens. You have a choice, and the dream tries to make it simple: turn around and go back into the crowd, pretend you never saw the table, keep running along the shifting map until you find what you’re trained to look for.

Or you can sit in the chair.

You sit.

The moment you do, the room tilts—not physically, but in your perception, like the dream rotates toward its hidden meaning. The name tag in your hand becomes heavy. Your fingers tingle as if holding something hot. The blank face of your search turns translucent, and suddenly you see the truth without getting the details: the “person” you’re looking for isn’t a stranger in the crowd.

It’s you—your attention, your certainty, your ability to recognize yourself as someone worth reaching.

Across the room, someone steps forward. You expect it to be familiar. Instead, their face changes every second, like a slideshow of all the ways you’ve ever tried to be someone else. Yet their eyes lock onto yours with a kind of recognition that doesn’t require a fixed identity.

They speak, but the words arrive as a feeling first: *Stop asking the crowd to do what you won’t do alone.*

A door opens somewhere you can’t see. Not an exit—an opening. Warm light pours into the room like morning through curtains. The name tag in your hand turns unreadable, dissolving into plain air, and you realize you don’t need it anymore.

Your original gathering returns, fading at the edges, as if waking is happening from the inside out. You feel your one person—your true one—settle into place somewhere behind your ribs.

And in the last second before the dream dissolves, you understand what your next step is going to be.

You stand up, not to search again, but to turn toward the crowd you’ve been avoiding. You walk out carrying nothing but the certainty that recognition doesn’t have to be found—it can be chosen.

Then you wake up with the strange calm of having finally met someone who was always waiting in you.
