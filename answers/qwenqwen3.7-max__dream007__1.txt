In the logic of the dream, the words you’ve both been shouting have materialized. They feel like tangled, vibrating wire and jagged pieces of dark glass cutting into your palms. Your friend stops mid-sentence, their mouth still open, but no sound comes out. They look at your hands, their expression shifting instantly from fierce anger to a profound, childlike curiosity.

You pull harder, and the entire argument comes loose with a sound like tearing canvas. It forms a heavy, pulsing knot in your hands, glowing with a dull, angry red light. 

Because the argument was the only thing holding the room together, the walls of the apartment begin to dissolve into fine, gray ash. The floor beneath your feet softens and turns into shallow, lukewarm water. You look down and see that you are both standing in the flooded ruins of a grand, Victorian train station. The glass ceiling high above is cracked, revealing a twilight sky filled with too many moons.

Your friend sighs, the sound echoing endlessly across the water. They sit down right there in the shallow flood, and as they do, their clothes ripple and change into the exact outfit they wore on the day you first met, a decade ago. 

"What are we actually fighting about?" they ask. Their voice doesn't travel through the air; it blooms directly inside your mind, sounding like a cello playing in an empty hall.

You look down at the heavy, red knot in your hands. You suddenly understand the "important thing" you were arguing over. It wasn't about money, or living arrangements, or a broken promise. You were arguing over who gets to keep the memory of a specific Tuesday afternoon from your childhood—a day where it rained, and youboth felt completely safe. In the dream, you realize that there is only enough room in the universe for one of you to remember it. If you keep it, they will forget it forever. If they keep it, it vanishes from your mind the moment you wake up.

You look at your friend. They are looking up at you, their eyes entirely black, reflecting the multiple moons above. They look so tired. 

You make your choice. 

You kneel in the water and gently place the glowing red knot onto the surface. It doesn't sink. Instead, the moment it touches the water, the knot unravels. The jagged glass and wire transform into a flock of hundreds of dark, wet moths. They flutter frantically for a moment, then take flight, swirling upward toward the cracked glass ceiling in a massive, chaotic cyclone.

As the moths hit the glass, they shatter it. 

The sound of the breaking glass is incredibly loud, rhythmic, and repetitive. *Beep. Beep. Beep.* 

The twilight sky begins to bleach into a blinding, sterile white. Your friend smiles, a sad, knowing smile, and begins to fade into the rising water. 

"It's okay," their voice echoes in your mind, growing fainter as the white light consumes the train station. "I'll remember it for both of us. Just wake up now. You're holding your breath."

The breaking glass turns into the shrill ringing of your alarm clock. You gasp, your lungs pulling in the cool, dry air of your bedroom. You open your eyes to the morning light filtering through the blinds, your heart hammering against your ribs, and a lingering, inexplicable sense of gratitude that you chose to let the moths fly away.
