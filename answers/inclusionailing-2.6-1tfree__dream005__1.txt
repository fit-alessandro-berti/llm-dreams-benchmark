The chandeliers hum like drowsing bees, casting honeyed light over a room that smells of crushed citrus and old perfume.  
Your only anchor is someone named Mara, who slipped away ten minutes ago to “grab a drink” and hasn’t returned.  
Every face you pass seems to have borrowed its expression from a painting—too fixed, too bright.

You drift through the first circle, where three men in tailored jackets are debating the exact color of silence.  
One insists it’s pearl, another says bruised plum, the third simply smiles and swirls the ice in his glass until it cracks.  
They don’t notice you hovering, so you slip away before they offer you a definition that might stick to your ribs.

Beyond a fern that looks suspiciously like a frozen fountain, you meet a woman balancing three stories on her tongue.  
She tells you about a childhood bedroom that kept expanding whenever she blinked, about doors that led to Tuesdays she hadn’t lived yet.  
Her laugh is a small, polite explosion.  
You ask if she’s seen a woman who answers to Mara.  
The woman’s eyes go briefly out of focus, then snap back.  
“Mara,” she repeats, as if tasting a word that no longer fits in her mouth.  
“She borrows doorways,” the woman says.  
“Try the room where the clocks are eating each other.”

You follow a hallway that wasn’t there a moment ago.  
The clocks aren’t really clocks—more like brass flowers that unfurl to reveal teeth instead of petals, gnawing at the hours and spitting out soft, gray crumbs.  
A couple stands nearby, feeding each other stories that taste like cinnamon and regret.  
When they notice you, they offer you a bite of memory: a winter in which you learned to speak in half-whispers.  
You swallow it, and for a second your ribs feel lighter, as though memory were a kind of helium.

You ask about Mara again, and the couple exchange a look that ripples like wind over wheat.  
They point you toward the conservatory, where the air is thick with the perfume of conversations nobody wants to finish.

Inside, people are holding their shadows like extra coats.  
A man with a halo of dry leaves explains that names are heavy, and you should consider leaving yours by the coat rack.  
You almost do.  
Almost let go of “you” and become someone who only knows how to arrive, never to search.  
Then a laugh cuts through the perfume—sharp, familiar, a little reckless.  
Mara, near the far wall, sipping from a glass that refills itself with questions.

You push through the crowd, step into a hush you don’t recognize, and finally reach her.  
She smiles, offers you the glass.  
The question inside tastes like home, but it’s too large to drink all at once.  
You take a sip anyway, and the room dissolves into a garden of unfinished sentences.  

In the morning you’ll wake and wonder whether Mara was ever truly lost or whether, for a little while, you simply agreed to get pleasantly tangled in the architecture of longing, where every group of strangers is a doorway wearing a different face, and the point of the gathering was never to find the person you knew but to realize how many versions of yourself were waiting, already introduced, in every room you didn’t yet know you’d entered.
