At first I think it's just a trick of the flickering torchlight, but no—the stone is inhaling and exhaling in slow, patient rhythms, as though the maze itself is alive and waiting for me to figure something out.

The first puzzle appears as a door with nine tiles, each bearing a symbol that shifts when I'm not looking directly at it. I learn quickly that the trick isn't to solve it with my eyes, but with my peripheral vision. I let my gaze go soft, and the pattern reveals itself: the symbols want to be arranged in the order I first saw them, before I knew I was supposed to remember.

The door opens. The walls exhale.

Deeper in, the puzzles grow stranger. A chessboard where the pieces argue with me about their moves. A river of numbers I must cross by stepping only on primes, but the primes keep changing their minds. A mirror that asks me to name something I've never thought about—and somehow, impossibly, I do.

With each solved puzzle, I notice something unsettling: I'm becoming better at them. Not just more practiced, but fundamentally *different*. My thoughts sharpen into unfamiliar geometries. I catch myself solving problems before I've finished reading them. The walls breathe faster, matching my pulse, or maybe I'm matching theirs.

At the maze's center, I find the final puzzle: a simple wooden box with a single question carved into its lid.

*Who is solving these puzzles?*

I reach for the answer and realize I can't remember my own name. I can remember the shape of every puzzle I've passed, every logical leap, every symbol. But the person who entered the maze—

The box opens on its own. Inside is a small brass key and a mirror. In the mirror, I see the maze looking back at me, breathing, waiting, and I understand at last that the puzzles were never obstacles.

They were *introductions*.

I pick up the key. Somewhere, very far away, I feel myself beginning to wake up—

—but not before I turn, and go deeper still.
