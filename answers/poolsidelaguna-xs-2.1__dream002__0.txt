You feel a storm approaching and the canopy above you darkens like spilled ink. The air grows thick with the scent of ozone and something sweet, like crushed violets. A single tree ahead begins to shed its leaves—not falling to the ground, but spiraling upward in slow, deliberate spirals as if pulled by invisible hands. The wind doesn’t howl; it *sings*, a wordless lullaby in a language just on the edge of your memory.  

Lightning cracks, but instead of illuminating the forest, it reveals faces in the bark of the trees—your grandmother’s, your childhood dog’s, strangers whose eyes follow you as you step into the clearing at the storm’s heart. The rain begins as mist, then hardens into a cascade of liquid light. You realize you’re not running from the storm but *into* it, and with each step, the world around you transforms: ferns bloom into butterflies, their wings etched with constellations, and the path ahead splits into two—one lined with silver birch, the other with gnarled oaks.  

A voice, neither yours nor entirely foreign, whispers from the thunder: *“Choose, dreamer. The storm is not your end, but your unveiling.”*  

As you hesitate, the ground beneath your feet gives way—not to fall, but to rise, carrying you upward into the clouds, where the storm reveals itself to be something alive, breathing, waiting to be known.
