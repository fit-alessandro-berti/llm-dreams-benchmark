…written in a language you somehow both recognize and can’t quite read.

The letters shift when you look at them directly—mathematical symbols becoming tiny insects, then musical notes, then your own handwriting from years ago. Around you, no one else seems alarmed. Pens move steadily. Pages turn. A clock at the front of the room ticks far too loudly, each second sounding like a door locking.

You flip to the second page, hoping it will make more sense, but the questions aren’t really questions. One asks you to “justify the shape of Thursday.” Another gives you a diagram of your childhood bedroom and asks which wall is “most responsible.” Another simply says:

**Define what you meant when you said “later.”**

You know, with absolute dream-certainty, that this exam determines everything—your job, your home, who you become, maybe whether morning arrives at all. You try to raise your hand, but your arm feels heavy, as though it’s submerged in water. The instructor glides past without footsteps. When they lean down, they have the face of someone you almost remember.

“You’ve had this material your whole life,” they whisper.

Then you notice there are notes written in the margins of the exam paper. Not printed—scribbled there in pencil. In your own handwriting.

**Start with the third question. Don’t trust the obvious answers. When the room changes, stay calm.**

And right as you read that last line, the classroom does change.

The fluorescent lights blink out and are replaced by sunlight pouring through tall windows that definitely weren’t there before. Rows of desks stretch farther than they should, disappearing into a haze. Some students are now children. Some are much older than any student should be. One desk holds a goldfish bowl instead of a person, and the fish is staring at you with open disappointment.

You turn to the third question.

It is a map.

Not of any country, but of places you’ve been afraid to go: a phone call you never returned, a house you keep seeing in different dreams, a version of yourself standing at a crossroads with no face. There’s a dotted line leading through all of them toward a small star in the corner of the page labeled simply:

**BEGIN HERE**

Your pencil finally touches the paper.

And the moment you start writing, you realize this is not an exam in the ordinary sense. It is measuring whether you can continue—not whether you know enough, but whether you can choose. Each answer you write becomes a hallway. Each hallway opens briefly in the air beside your desk. In one, you see yourself accepting a life because it was offered. In another, you see yourself refusing one because it frightened you. In a third, you see nothing at all, just a windy field under a darkening sky.

The clock is ticking faster now.

You write anyway.

For the question about Thursday, you answer: **It bends around regret but never breaks.**

For the wall most responsible, you answer: **The one I faced when I was trying not to be seen.**

For “later,” after a long pause, you write: **A room I kept inventing so I wouldn’t have to enter the one I was already in.**

The instructor appears at your shoulder again. This time, when you look up, they have your face—older, tireder, kinder.

“Better,” they say.

“But is it right?” you ask.

They glance at the exam, then at the windows, where the sunlight has dimmed into the color of late afternoon.

“That’s never been the question.”

A low murmur passes through the room. Students are vanishing one by one—not dramatically, just fading as if erased from a chalkboard. Desks empty. Papers flutter to the floor. The goldfish bowl is gone. The clock has no hands now, only a bright red second hand spinning in place.

You turn to the final page.

There is only one prompt left:

**When they call your name, will you answer as the person you were, or the person you are becoming?**

Below it are two blank lines.

From somewhere beyond the classroom, a voice begins calling your name.

At first it sounds like a teacher taking attendance. Then like a parent waking you for school. Then like a stranger calling to you from the far end of a station as a train begins to move. The room trembles softly. Dust falls from the ceiling—or maybe stars.

You look down at the two lines.

On the first, your hand begins to write the name you have always used, the one attached to records and expectations and all the old stories about who you should be.

On the second line, your pencil stops.

You don’t know what belongs there.

The voice calls again, louder now, and the windows are dark mirrors. In them, you can see dozens of versions of yourself standing behind your chair, waiting. Child-you. Failed-you. Imagined-you. The one who stayed. The one who left. The one who forgave. The one who never learned how. None of them speak.

Then, from the margin again, words appear in your own handwriting, as if written by an invisible hand:

**It doesn’t have to be a name. It only has to be an answer.**

So you write:

**Present.**

The moment the pencil lifts, the exam paper folds itself closed.

The classroom inhales.

Every mirror-window turns transparent at once, revealing not the campus outside but a vast dawn-colored landscape—roads, oceans, rooftops, hospital corridors, forests after rain, office buildings lit before sunrise, kitchens, stages, departures, arrivals, all of it waiting and unfinished. The instructor steps back and nods as though something small but irreversible has happened.

A bell rings.

Not the sharp bell of a school, but something deeper, older, like a signal sent across water.

“Time,” says the instructor.

You hand in the exam, though now it feels strangely light, as if the pages are blank again.

As you stand to leave, you notice one final line stamped at the bottom of the cover sheet, in tiny print:

**Results will be reflected gradually in waking life.**

The door out of the classroom opens onto a place that is not quite a hallway and not quite morning. You step through it anyway, with the uneasy feeling that you have forgotten something important and the even stranger feeling that maybe forgetting it was part of passing.

Then, just before waking, you hear someone behind you call out:

“Next time, bring a pen that writes underwater.”
