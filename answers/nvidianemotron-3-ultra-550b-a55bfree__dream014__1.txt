...follow them through a doorway that wasn't there a moment ago—a narrow blue door set into the brick wall of a closed bookshop, its sign swinging gently though there's no wind.

Inside, the city noise vanishes. The space opens into something impossible: a library with ceilings that dissolve into clouds, bookshelves growing like trees from a floor of moss and fallen pages. The strangers—an elderly woman with ink-stained fingers, a child carrying a lantern that burns with blue fire, a man in a coat made of stitched-together maps—don't speak. They don't need to.

The woman presses a book into your hands. Its cover bears your name in handwriting you recognize but can't place. When you open it, the pages show not text but *moments*: your grandmother's kitchen, the smell of rain on hot asphalt, the weight of your father's hand on your shoulder, the first time you swam in the ocean at night.

"You didn't lose your way," the child says, voice echoing from everywhere. "You lost the thread."

The map-coated man unfurls a section of his sleeve, revealing a path glowing faintly gold. It doesn't lead out of the city. It leads *deeper*—through the library's root-system, down into catacombs where the city's dreams are stored, where every forgotten thing waits in labeled jars.

The woman smiles, and her face shifts—becomes your mother's face, then your own face aged thirty years, then a stranger's face you've never seen but somehow miss.

"Now," she says. "Which memory do you need to remember first?"

The lantern's blue flame flickers. The books on the nearest shelf begin to whisper. Somewhere far above, the city continues its endless motion, but down here, time moves differently. Down here, you have all the time you need.

You turn the page.
