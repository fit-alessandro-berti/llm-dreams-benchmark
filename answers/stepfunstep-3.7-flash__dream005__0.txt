The ballroom is stitched together from two half-remembered places: the oak-paneled library from your elementary school, and the sun-baked community center where Lila’s mom used to host potlucks. The floor is made of the glossy pages of your beat-up childhood copy of *Charlotte’s Web*, crinkling under your sneakers with every step, the words shifting as you walk: some lines are from the book, others are things Lila said to you when you were 10, like “I’ll save you the last s’more” or “Let’s run away to the woods and live in a treehouse.”
The crowd is a blur of soft edges, everyone holding half-drunk glasses of something that fizzes like soda but smells like old books and cut grass. No one’s talking, just murmuring in a hum that sounds like cicadas singing in late July, the same sound that used to drift through Lila’s open window when you slept over as kids. You know you’re here for Lila, the only person you recognize: she moved three states away the summer after fourth grade and never wrote back, even after you sent her 17 letters and a jar of fireflies you caught in your backyard. You haven’t thought about her in years, but in the dream, it feels like you’ve been looking for her forever.
The first group you pass is a circle of people in 1920s fringe dresses and pinstripe suits, dancing a charleston so fast their hems blur. Their flutes of sparkling grape juice never slosh, no matter how wildly they spin. When you edge closer to ask if they’ve seen the girl with the scar on her left thumb and the chipped front tooth from falling off a bike chasing fireflies, the woman in the lead—boa made of live, chirping sparrows—tilts her head, her bobbed hair flipping up to reveal a tiny ladybug sticker on her temple. “She’s where the clocks run backwards, sweetheart,” she says, and the sparrows in her boa all take off at once, leaving the boa empty and trailing pink feathers. You glance at the wall clock behind her: the numbers are all 7s, the hands tick counterclockwise, and the second hand is a tiny glowing firefly.
You keep walking, past a cluster of seven-year-olds all wearing the neon yellow windbreaker you outgrew in seventh grade, the one with the Hello Kitty patch that fell off in the rain. They’re passing around a jar of purple-glowing fireflies, and when you lean in to ask about Lila, the tallest kid—gap-toothed, hair dyed the same faded blue Lila had when she was 12—holds up a polaroid. It’s Lila, standing next to a sunflower so tall its top brushes the ballroom’s glittery ceiling, holding up a jar of lemonade that fizzes purple. “She went to get the lemonade that tastes like memory,” the kid says, “but the man selling it only takes buttons as payment.” The fireflies fly out of the jar, turn to confetti, and the kids vanish, leaving the polaroid in your hand. When you squint, Lila’s face is smudged out, like someone rubbed it with a thumb, but you can still make out the scar on her thumb, the one you can picture clear as day even after 15 years.
Next you find a group of elderly people sitting at a long farmhouse table piled high with mismatched china, playing mahjong with tiles printed with snapshots of your shared childhood: the fort you built in her backyard out of old blankets, the time you both cried because you lost her pet goldfish Bubbles, the moment you stood on her porch waving as her family’s car pulled away. The old lady across from you has the same crinkly crow’s feet as Lila’s grandma, the one who used to give you lemon drops and tell you stories about the fireflies that lived in her garden. She doesn’t say anything, just nods toward a doorway at the far end of the room you hadn’t noticed before, draped in a curtain of chipping sea glass that chimes softly when a breeze you can’t feel blows through it.
Every step toward the doorway makes the ballroom fade a little: the dancers, the kids, the mahjong players all dissolve into soft, glowing gold dust that sticks to your skin like pollen. The sea glass parts as you reach it, and there she is: Lila, sitting on the creaky wooden porch swing from her front yard, propped against a side of the house painted the same faded cornflower blue it was when you were kids.
