You are in a maze filled with complex puzzles. Each puzzle requires intense concentration and logical thinking to solve, and as you move deeper into the maze, you feel the air grow heavier, like thought itself has weight. 

The walls are shifting — not randomly, but in response. Each time you solve a riddle of interlocking gears or a pattern of glowing symbols, the corridor ahead rearranges with a low, stone-grinding sigh. The maze is learning you.

Halfway in, the puzzles stop asking for logic. One is a mirror that only clears when you admit something you’ve been avoiding. Another is a locked door with no keyhole, just a small brass plate that reads: *“What problem are you carrying that isn’t yours?”* When you answer honestly, the hinges exhale and swing open.

Deeper still, the maze gets quiet. The final chamber isn’t a puzzle at all. It’s a simple wooden table with a half-finished maze drawn on parchment, and a quill beside it. The ink is still wet. You realize: you were never just solving it — you were writing it as you went. 

On the wall, chalk letters appear: *“The only way out is to decide what the maze was for.”*  

You pick up the quill. What do you write?
