…speak, but the microphone turns into a glass of water in your hand.

The audience doesn’t react. They wait.

You look down and see that the stage is no longer a stage but a wooden dock stretching out over a dark, still lake. The rows of people have become lanterns, hundreds of them, glowing softly in the mist. Somewhere far away, someone coughs, and the sound echoes like thunder.

You raise the glass to your lips, but before you can drink, a tiny fish swims in circles inside it.

“You forgot the first line,” it says.

You know it is right.

Panic rises in your chest, but then, from the back of the lake, a child’s voice calls out, “Just tell us what happened.”

So you do.

You tell them about the door in your grandmother’s kitchen that was never there during the day. You tell them about the hallway behind it, lined with portraits of people who blinked whenever you looked away. You tell them about the blue horse waiting at the end, wearing your old school backpack and asking whether you were finally ready to go home.

The lanterns lean closer.

The fish in your glass stops swimming.

And as you speak, you realize the audience has not come to judge you. They have come because they are all versions of you, waiting to hear the story you were afraid to remember.

At last, the lake begins to applaud.

Not with hands, but with rain.
