**You find yourself in a post-apocalyptic world, with resources scarce and dangers everywhere. Despite the hardships, you decide to** build a garden.

The ground is cracked and gray, and the wind smells like rust. Most people pass the empty lot with their eyes down, conserving energy for the next threat. But you stop. There’s a patch of dirt near a broken fountain that still gets six hours of sun.

**What happens next**

- **Day 3**: You trade half your water ration for a cracked plastic bucket and three packets of seeds. The trader laughs, says nothing grows anymore. You smile anyway.
- **Day 14**: The first green shoots break through. Thin, stubborn, improbable. A kid with a makeshift spear watches from behind a car door. You point to the sprouts and mouth "tomatoes." He doesn’t come closer, but he doesn’t leave either.
- **Day 30**: The garden is the size of a door. Marigolds at the edges to keep pests away, beans climbing rebar, potatoes hidden under mounded soil. Two more people show up with their own seeds. One brings ash for fertilizer. The kid returns with a dented watering can.
- **Day 60**: There are arguments about water, about who guards at night, about whether sharing will get you killed. You lose a whole row to a raid. You replant the next morning. The people who stayed start taking shifts.

It’s not safe. It’s not enough. The world is still broken glass and long silences. But every time someone kneels in the dirt and puts something in the ground, the math of scarcity changes a little. You’re not saving the world. You’re refusing to let it be only endings.

When you wake up, your hands feel like they’ve been in soil.
