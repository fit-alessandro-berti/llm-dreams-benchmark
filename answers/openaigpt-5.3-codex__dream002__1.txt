…the light tilts.

One moment the trees are gold and still, and the next the sky folds inward like dark silk. Wind threads through the branches in a rising whisper, then a roar. Leaves spin around you in green and silver spirals, and the path you were following dissolves beneath your feet.

Thunder doesn’t crack—it hums, low and enormous, as if the earth itself is singing. Rain begins in single, cold drops, then turns to shining sheets. You run toward a clearing, but every direction looks the same: tall trunks, flickering shadows, sudden flashes of white-blue lightning.

Then, in the heart of the storm, you see it.

A small cottage stands where there was nothing before, its windows glowing amber through the rain. You push open the door and step inside. Instantly, the noise fades. A fire crackles in a stone hearth. On a wooden table sits a teacup, steaming, as if someone expected you.

You lift it with trembling hands. The tea tastes like cinnamon and something you can’t name—something familiar. On the wall hangs a mirror. When you look, you don’t see your reflection. You see the forest again, but calm now, sunlit, waiting.

A voice behind you—gentle, ancient—says, “When the storm arrives, it is only trying to return you to yourself.”

You turn, but no one is there. The cottage is gone. The rain is gone. You are standing once more on the forest path, sunlight warming your face, a single wet leaf resting in your palm like a message you almost understand.

And just before you wake, thunder rolls one last time—far away, like laughter.
