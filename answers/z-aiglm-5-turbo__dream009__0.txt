The sun sits low and amber over a landscape I don't recognize—rolling hills dotted with stone huts, a river snaking in the distance catching the light like a vein of copper. The air smells different. Cleaner, but wilder. Damp earth and woodsmoke and something herbal I can't name.

I'm wearing rough-spun linen. My hands are calloused in ways they weren't when I fell asleep. This body feels like mine but also like a borrowed coat—familiar in shape, wrong in the details.

A woman carrying a clay jug pauses on a nearby path and regards me with mild concern. She says something I don't understand—guttural, melodic, nothing like any language I've heard. But somehow the *meaning* slides into my mind like water through cloth. She's asking if I'm from the northern settlements, if I've been hurt.

I shake my head slowly. She gestures toward the river.

I start walking, and the dream logic takes over—that particular dream gravity where you simply *know* things. I know the river is safe. I know the hills to the east are not. I know that tonight there will be a fire in the central courtyard and someone will play a stringed instrument that sounds like wind through a canyon, and I will weep without understanding why.

The ground is soft underfoot. I realize I'm not scared.

That's the strangest part. I should be terrified—ripped from everything, from *everyone*. But the dream holds me gently, like this is not a punishment but a return. Like I once lived here and forgot, and my sleeping mind finally remembered what my waking one could not.

I reach the river's edge and kneel. The water is so clear I can see stones below that look like swallowed coins. When I cup my hands and drink, it tastes like nothing I've ever tasted—cold and ancient and alive.

Above me, two moons hang in the fading sky.

*Two moons.*

And somewhere behind me, I hear my name spoken by a voice I know intimately but cannot place, and I turn—

And the alarm clock drags me out before I can see their face.
