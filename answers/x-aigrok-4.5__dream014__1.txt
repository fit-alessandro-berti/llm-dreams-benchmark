One gently takes your arm, not to pull but to steady, and points toward a side street where the noise softens into music and lantern light.  

As you walk, the city itself begins to shift—buildings lean closer like old friends, pavement stones glow faintly underfoot as if remembering your steps, and the strangers’ voices braid into stories of places they’ve lost and found. They never ask your name; they already seem to know the shape of your loneliness. At a quiet courtyard fountain they pause, turn to you, and say, “You’re not late. You’re exactly where the dream needs you.”  

Suddenly the crowd parts like a curtain and you see a door that looks almost like home—soft wood, a light behind frosted glass. When you step through, the strangers dissolve into starlight that settles on your shoulders like a familiar coat. You wake (or think you do) with the taste of that courtyard water still on your tongue and the certain knowledge that, even lost, you were never as alone as the city pretended.  

The dream ends gently, leaving a small, glowing map pressed into your palm—proof that kindness can rearrange even the busiest night.
