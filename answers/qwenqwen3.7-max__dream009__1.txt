…return a stolen shadow before the grand sundial in the town square strikes thirteen. 

I look down at my hands. I am holding a heavy, glass apothecary jar, and inside it, a silhouette of a man is thrashing against the glass like a trapped moth. It makes no sound, but the glass vibrates against my palms. 

The air smells of woodsmoke, crushed lavender, and something metallic, like impending rain. I am standing in a narrow, winding alleyway paved with uneven cobblestones. The buildings leaning over me are impossibly tall, their timber frames warping and twisting like the roots of ancient trees. There are no power lines, no hum of traffic, no glowing screens—only the flickering, warm light of oil lanterns strung between wrought-iron balconies.

I step out of the alley into a sprawling plaza. The sky above is a bruised, twilight purple, though it feels like midday. In the center of the square sits the grand sundial, a massive monument of pale marble and tarnished bronze. The gnomon casts a long, sharp shadow, creeping agonizingly close to a Roman numeral *XIII* that shouldn't logically exist on the dial. 

*Tick. Tick. Tick.* 

The sundial is ticking like a pocket watch. The sound echoes off the stone facades, loud and rhythmic. 

I need to find the owner of the shadow. I know this with the absolute, unquestioned certainty that only exists in dreams. I approach a baker’s stall, where a woman with eyes like polished obsidian is kneading dough that glows faintly like embers. 

"Whose shadow is this?" I ask, holding up the jar.

She doesn't speak. Instead, she points a flour-dusted finger toward a towering cathedral at the edge of the square. Its spires don't reach into the sky; they reach *down* into the earth, as if the building is a reflection hanging from an invisible ceiling. 

I run. As I move, the physics of the world begin to loosen. My footsteps become lighter, and with every third step, I float a few inches off the ground. The people in the square part for me, their faces obscured by porcelain masks painted with expressions of profound sorrow. They don't speak; they communicate through the soft, melodic chiming of silver bells tied to their wrists. 

*Tick. Tick. Tick.* The sundial is speeding up. 

I reach the inverted cathedral and push open the heavy oak doors. Inside, there are no pews, no altar. Instead, the vast nave is filled with a shallow, perfectly still body of water. Walking across the surface, my shoes remain completely dry. The water acts like a mirror, but it doesn't reflect the vaulted ceiling above; it reflects a starry night sky.

At the center of the water stands the Weaver. He is a towering figure draped in cloaks of spun gossamer, his face hidden beneath a wide-brimmed hat. He is holding a pair of massive, silver shears, snipping at the empty air. With every cut, a piece of the purple sky outside flakes away, revealing a blinding, white void beneath.

"You're late," the Weaver says. His voice sounds like grinding stones and rustling autumn leaves. 

"I brought it back," I say, my voice echoing across the water. I hold out the jar. 

The shadow inside stops thrashing. It presses its dark, featureless hands against the glass, reaching toward the Weaver. 

"Open it," he commands. "Before the thirteenth hour."

I unscrew the brass lid. The shadow pours out like dark ink dropped in water, flowing across the surface of the mirrored floor. It rushes toward the Weaver and wraps around his boots, anchoring him. The moment it connects, the silver shears dissolve into a flurry of white butterflies that scatter into the rafters.

The Weaver tips his hat to me. "The fabric holds. For now."

From outside, a deep, resonant gong shakes the air. The sundial has struck thirteen. 

The sound doesn't stop. It sustains, growing louder, vibrating in my teeth, shifting in pitch. The purple sky shatters like glass, raining down in harmless, glittering shards. The white void rushes in to consume the cathedral, the water, the Weaver. The gong's deep resonance morphs, sharpening, becoming more mechanical, more insistent.

It becomes a sharp, rhythmic buzzing. 

The white void fades into the blinding glare of morning sunlight hitting my bedroom wall. The buzzing is my phone alarm on the nightstand. I blink, my hand still half-curled in the shape of a glass jar, the smell of woodsmoke and lavender lingering in the air for just a second before the modern world rushes back in.
