…and you let yourself follow them, even though every street looks familiar in the way a half-remembered song does—close, but not quite right.

They don’t ask for your name. They don’t crowd you or hurry you. One of them points at the sky between buildings, where the daylight has turned the color of old paper. “There,” they say, as if it’s the simplest thing in the world to navigate by light.

You walk with them through a market that shouldn’t be there—stall after stall of things you’ve never seen but somehow recognize: jars of wind you can smell, scarves that hum when you hold them, keys that fit doors you haven’t found yet. The noise of the city fades the moment you step inside the circle of their kindness, like someone has turned the volume down on your loneliness.

When you try to thank them, the strangers all smile and glance at one another, sharing the same small secret. Their eyes keep landing on you the way hands land on a railing when you’re about to fall.

At the edge of a bridge, the city changes. The buildings stretch taller, their windows becoming frames for moments you didn’t know you’d stored away—your childhood laugh, a goodbye you never said, a promise you made in a dream and forgot when morning came. You stop, dizzy with recognition. “Which way?” you whisper.

“Not the way you were going,” one of them says, and reaches into their pocket. They pull out something that looks like a folded map, except it’s made of thin light—written in lines that rearrange themselves as you look.

The map opens, and you realize it isn’t trying to lead you somewhere else. It’s trying to lead you back to yourself.

The center of it is blank until you take a breath and speak—not to them, but to the feeling you’ve been running from. You tell the truth in the quietest voice you have. You admit you’re tired. You admit you miss someone, even if you can’t name who. You admit you thought being lost meant you were unlovable.

The blank space on the map brightens, forming an address made of soft, warm shapes—like a memory that forgives you. The strangers nod, as if they’ve been waiting for that exact sentence.

“Now,” they say, and their hands open in a gentle way, offering you the next step without demanding you take it immediately. “You can go. Or you can stay a little longer. Either way, you won’t be alone.”

You look down at the map, and it turns into a small compass. The needle doesn’t point north. It points toward the place in your chest where the loneliness has been sitting like a stone. When you follow that direction, the city around you rearranges itself—streets no longer trap you in loops, and signs begin to read like encouragement.

Finally, you reach a door that was always there but you never noticed. It’s plain, painted the color of sunrise. On it is a simple symbol: a circle, and inside the circle, a space shaped like room.

You touch the door handle. The strangers stand behind you like steady stars, and as you open the door, you realize the dream has been doing something kind: it’s giving you a way to come home, even if home was only ever meant to be found inside you.

You step through—and the moment your foot crosses the threshold, the buzzing city sound returns, but softer now, like a lullaby. The air smells like rain on warm pavement.

You wake up with the feeling that the strangers aren’t gone.

They’re just waiting somewhere in the waking world—wherever you choose to look for help, wherever you decide you deserve directions.
