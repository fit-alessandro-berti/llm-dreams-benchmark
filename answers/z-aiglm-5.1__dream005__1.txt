The first group gathers around a piano that plays itself. They're laughing, but when you approach, they turn to you with identical puzzled expressions, as if you've spoken a language they once knew but forgot. You excuse yourself and move on.

Through a hallway that stretches longer than the building should allow, you hear your friend's laugh — that unmistakable sound — and follow it into a kitchen where a dozen people are silently baking hundreds of identical cakes. None of them are your friend. The laugh echoes from somewhere else now, two rooms over, or maybe two floors up.

You climb a staircase that spirals both ways. On the landing, an elderly woman takes your hand and says with great urgency, "You just missed them." She won't say who.

The next room is outdoors — a garden somehow folded inside. A child tugs your sleeve and says, "They're looking for you too." You ask where they went. The child points at the moon, which hangs impossibly close and full.

You realize you can't remember your friend's face anymore, only the feeling of knowing them. The party murmurs around you, warm and indifferent. You keep searching — not because you expect to find them, but because stopping feels like waking up, and you're not ready for that yet.
