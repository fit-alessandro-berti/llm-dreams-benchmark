You notice the questions are **not written in ink, but in shifting constellations of light that pulse in rhythm with my own heartbeat.**

At first, I panic. My stomach drops as I scan the sheet; there are no multiple-choice bubbles, no lines to fill in. The light constellations rearrange themselves every time I look away. One moment, the paper shows a map of a city I’ve never been to; the next, a graph that plots a lifetime of laughter and sorrow against a wall of solid gray concrete.

The proctor, a figure wearing a mask made of polished mirrors, stands at the front of the room. He doesn’t speak, but his reflection shows me a thousand different faces: a child, an old man, a bird, a tree. The ticking clock on the wall grows louder with every beat, until it sounds like a hammer against a door. I realize the clock is not measuring time; it is measuring *presence*.

I look down at the pen in my hand. It isn’t plastic or metal. It is a living branch, green and humming with sap.

The first question forms clearly in the light: *What do you fear most?*

If I write an answer, the constellations fade, the room collapses, and I become a statistic. If I do nothing, the paper burns away. I realize then that the exam isn't testing what I know. It’s testing if I’m willing to be seen.

I close my eyes. I stop looking at the light. I stop listening to the hammering clock. I take the living pen and press the tip against the paper, not to write, but to bloom.

I squeeze my eyes tighter and whisper the truth that has been hiding in the dark corners of my chest since I was a teenager: *I am not afraid of the outcome. I am afraid of the silence between the choices.*

The light on the page flares bright white, blinding and warm. It doesn't burn; it feels like sunlight breaking through a heavy winter cloud. The mirrors of the proctor crack and shatter into rain. The room dissolves into a field of tall grass under a blue sky that stretches to infinity.

There is no grade here. There is only the grass, the wind, and the pen resting in my hand, now just a twig once more.

I wake up sitting at my desk, sweat on my brow, sunlight hitting the corner of a wooden table. There is no exam paper. Just the quiet of the morning and the feeling that whatever comes next, I am ready to write my own questions.
