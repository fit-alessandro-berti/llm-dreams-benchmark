You find yourself in a post-apocalyptic world, with resources scarce and dangers everywhere. Despite the hardships, you decide to tuck your half-empty pack of trail mix into the pocket of your frayed canvas jacket and follow the distant trill of a child’s laugh. The ash-gray sky streaks with the orange glow of a burning grain silo ten miles out, and every shadow between the rusted husks of semi-trucks hums with potential threat—feral packs of coyotes that have learned to hunt humans, roving gangs that mark their turf with cracked spray-painted swastikas, even the occasional raider with a modified hunting rifle slung over their shoulder.

But the laugh cuts through the hum, sharp and bright, and you break into a jog, ducking past a collapsed convenience store where a window still glows with the flicker of a broken neon “OPEN” sign. Around the corner, in an overgrown flower bed choked with kudzu, you spot a girl no older than six, balanced on the remains of a rusted swing set, twirling a dandelion fluff into the air. Her dress is a patchwork of mismatched fabric, and her bare feet are caked in mud, but she’s grinning like she hasn’t seen a single day of the hunger or fear that defines this world.

When she spots you, she freezes mid-twirl, but doesn’t run. You hold out your empty palm, the trail mix crinkling softly in your jacket pocket, and tell her you’re not going to hurt her. She nods, then hops down from the swing, her bare feet hitting the cracked asphalt without flinching. She leads you through the overgrown yard to a cramped, boarded-up cottage, and pushes open the front door to reveal a woman with a scar cutting across her cheek, stirring a pot over a rusted camp stove. The woman’s hand flies to the hunting rifle propped against the wall, but when she sees the dandelion behind your ear you’d plucked on the way over, she relaxes.

They’re the last remnants of a small farming commune that was raided three months prior, they tell you—everyone else either fled or was taken, and they’ve been holed up here, scavenging what they can from the overgrown fields around the town. You spend the night with them, sharing your trail mix and helping them patch up the hole in their roof where a stray bullet tore through earlier that week. At dawn, you help them pack up their few belongings, and they beg you to stay with them, to help them rebuild their farm.

For a second, you hesitate—you’ve spent the last two years running, hoarding supplies, surviving one day at a time. But then you look over at the little girl, who’s already teaching your boots how to toss dandelion fluff, and you remember the way the world used to feel, before the bombs, before the gangs, before you lost everyone you loved.

You decide to stay.

Over the next month, you help them clear out the kudzu from their old crop rows, repair their broken farm tools, and set up tripwires around the perimeter to warn them of approaching danger. The little girl starts calling you “Uncle Seed,” after the story you told her about growing dandelions in the cracks of concrete. One afternoon, as you’re kneeling in the dirt planting tomato seeds, the sky clears for the first time in months, and warm sunlight spills over the fields. The little girl runs over, holding a bouquet of wild daisies, and tucks them behind your ear. For a second, you forget all about the dangers, the scarcity, the grief that’s followed you for years.

In the dream, you stay there. You build a life out of the ashes of the old world, not by hoarding what’s left, but by sharing what little you have. When you wake up, your pillow is damp, and there’s a single dandelion fluff stuck to your cheek—proof, for a second, that the dream wasn’t just a dream.
