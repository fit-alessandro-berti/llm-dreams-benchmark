The crowd doesn’t blink. They lean forward, not breathing, as your first ribbon spirals into the shape of a small bird and flutters toward the rafters. You realize, with dream-certainty, that this is the speech: not a list of facts, but a slow unfolding of living images. A second ribbon becomes a silver fish that swims through the air over the front row; a third unfurls into a doorway hanging suspended in the dark, and through it the audience can glimpse a rainstorm on a summer afternoon, the smell of cut grass and thunder.

You step closer to the microphone, and your shoes make no sound. The stage boards feel soft beneath your feet, like the floor of a forest. Somewhere in the back of the theater, someone begins to hum a melody you almost remember from childhood. You smile—not nervous anymore, but curious—and reach out to touch one of the ribbons. It trembles at your fingertips and dissolves into a thousand tiny stars that drift down over the seats.

The audience catches them in cupped hands. They are smiling now, tears on some faces, laughter on others. You understand, in the way you understand things in dreams, that you have said exactly what needed to be said, even though no one will be able to repeat it afterward. The lights warm like sunrise, and you take one last bow as the room fills with light and the soft, bright sound of wings.
