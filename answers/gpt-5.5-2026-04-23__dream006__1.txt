…speak, but the first word comes out as a flock of tiny paper birds.

They flutter upward into the lights, casting moving shadows across the ceiling. The audience doesn’t laugh or gasp—they simply watch, perfectly still, as if this is exactly what they came to see. You glance down at your notes, but the pages are blank except for a single sentence:

**“Tell them what you forgot.”**

Your heart starts beating loudly, so loudly it echoes through the microphone like distant thunder. Then, one by one, people in the audience begin to stand. Each face is familiar, though you can’t quite remember from where: an old teacher, a childhood friend, someone you once passed on a train, yourself at seven years old holding a red balloon.

You take a breath.

This time, when you speak, your voice is clear.

“I was afraid,” you say.

The stage beneath your feet softens into grass. The walls of the auditorium dissolve into a twilight field filled with fireflies. The audience smiles—not with approval, but recognition—and the paper birds return, settling gently on your shoulders like snow.

Then the child version of you lets go of the balloon.

It rises into the darkening sky, and as you watch it disappear among the stars, you realize the speech is over, though you never finished it.

Everyone applauds in silence.

And you wake with the feeling that, somehow, they understood.
