:::writing
…each challenge seems designed to test something different about you.

The first is obvious: puzzles, logic problems, timed tasks. You solve some quickly and stumble through others, aware of cameras floating silently overhead. Other competitors type furiously, whisper strategies, or stare blankly at glowing screens that display shifting instructions.

The second challenge is stranger.

You’re led into a room that looks exactly like a workplace—your dream workplace. The lighting is perfect, the tools are exactly what you always wished you had, even the chair feels right. A voice over a speaker simply says, “Work.”

No further instructions.

Hours pass, or maybe minutes. Time behaves oddly. You start projects, abandon them, restart them. Some contestants panic and leave the room. Others try to guess what the judges want.

Eventually you forget about the judges.

You just start doing the work the way you always imagined you would if someone finally trusted you with it.

Later, in another hallway, the remaining competitors walk together in silence. There are far fewer of you now. At the end of the corridor is a final door labeled:

“Interview.”

Inside the room is… no one.

Just a mirror on the wall and a note on the table:

“Convince the person in front of you.”

As you begin speaking, explaining why you deserve your dream job, the reflection in the mirror slowly starts nodding before you even finish your sentences.

And that’s when you realize something unsettling about the competition:

No one has been eliminated.

People have only been eliminating themselves.
