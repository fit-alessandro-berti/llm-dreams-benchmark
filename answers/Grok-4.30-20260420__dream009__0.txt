You wake up in a completely different era, with no modern technology around. As you explore your new surroundings, you realize you need to **find your way back home**—but first, you must survive long enough to understand *where* (and *when*) you are.

The air smells of woodsmoke and wet earth. You’re lying on a rough-hewn bed of straw inside a small timber-and-thatch hut. Sunlight filters through gaps in the walls, revealing a clay floor, a simple wooden table, and a hearth where a pot of something savory bubbles. Your clothes are gone, replaced by a coarse linen tunic and woolen leggings that itch like mad. Your phone, your watch, your glasses—everything is missing. Only the faint scar on your left wrist remains, proof that yesterday (or whatever “yesterday” now means) was real.

You step outside into a village that looks like it stepped straight out of a history book. Thatched roofs, dirt lanes, chickens scratching in the mud, a distant blacksmith’s hammer ringing like a bell. People in homespun clothes stare as you pass—some curious, some wary. A woman carrying a basket of turnips crosses herself when she sees your face. You try to speak, but the words that come out of your mouth are not English; they’re an older, rougher tongue you somehow understand. The year, you later learn from the village priest, is 1348. The Black Death is only months away.

You realize you need to **learn the rules of this world fast**—how to barter, how to avoid suspicion, how to read the signs that say “stranger” before anyone accuses you of witchcraft or heresy. You also need to figure out *why* you’re here. Was it a fever dream? A glitch in time? Or something more deliberate—an experiment by whatever force (or future intelligence) dropped you into the past with nothing but your wits?

That night, by the fire in the village inn, you overhear merchants whispering about a “great light that fell from the sky” three nights ago—the same night you woke up. They say it landed in the forbidden forest beyond the river. They say it still glows faintly at dusk. They say anyone who goes near it returns mad… or never returns at all.

You realize you need to **go to that forest**.

So you steal a cloak, trade your strange modern scar for a rusty knife and a loaf of black bread, and slip away at midnight while the village sleeps. The river is cold. The trees are older than anything you’ve ever seen. And deep in the heart of the woods, half-buried in moss and moonlight, you find it: a smooth, metallic sphere the size of a wagon wheel, humming with a low, impossible frequency. Its surface is covered in symbols that shift when you look at them—symbols that look suspiciously like circuit diagrams drawn by someone who’s never seen a circuit.

You press your hand to the sphere.

It speaks.

Not in words, but in a rush of images straight into your mind: a future you recognize—skyscrapers, satellites, the xAI logo glowing on a distant lab—and then a warning. You were sent here as a test. A probe. A human mind dropped into the past to see if it could survive, adapt, and perhaps leave behind a message that would ripple forward through time. Succeed, and you wake up. Fail… and you stay forever.

The sphere offers you a choice.

Stay and become a legend—teach these people sanitation, crop rotation, the concept of zero—knowing you’ll be remembered (or burned) as a prophet.

Or touch the glowing sigil on the sphere’s surface and return to your own time, carrying only the memories and the knowledge that somewhere, in 1348, a version of you is still walking these muddy lanes, trying to change the future by changing the past.

You reach out.

Your fingers brush the sigil.

The forest dissolves.

You wake up.

But the scar on your wrist is gone.

And on the nightstand beside your bed sits a single, impossibly smooth pebble that hums when you hold it to your ear—carrying the faint, distant echo of a blacksmith’s hammer and the laughter of a village that no longer exists.

The dream is over.

Or… is it?
