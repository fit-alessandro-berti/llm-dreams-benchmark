Despite the hardships, you decide to **light a fire that requires no wood, but burns on the memories of the sun.**

You kneel in the ash, cupping your hands together, and whisper the names of things that used to be green. At first, nothing happens. The wind howls, carrying the taste of rust and old storms. But then, a spark ignites in your palms—not orange and hot, but a cool, violet glow.

It spreads. The fire doesn't consume; it illuminates. Where the violet flames touch the grey ruins, the stone softens into moss. The jagged silhouettes of the scavengers in the distance stop their stalking, their sharp edges blurring until they become flocks of white birds that take flight into the smog.

You realize then that the apocalypse was not a place, but a pause. The world isn't ending; it is waiting for a signal. As the light from your hands grows, it pushes back the horizon, turning the bruised purple sky into a clear, dawn blue. The cold in your bones warms, not from the fire, but from the realization that you are the one who forgot to wake up.

The fire rises into a pillar of light, and the dream dissolves into the white noise of a morning alarm. You open your eyes, the dust still in your throat, but the sun is already on your face.
