While the sky above ripples like oil on water, shifting from violent violet to deep ocean blue, and the ground beneath your feet morphs from jagged crystal to soft moss, the canvas remains a perfect, silent rectangle of white. It hums with a low vibration that you feel in your teeth rather than hear.

You reach out to touch it, but your hand doesn't meet resistance. Instead, your fingertips sink into the surface like fingers into deep water. A shock of cold electricity races up your arm, and suddenly, the colors of the world around you stop swirling. They freeze, hanging in the air like suspended dust.

From the center of the canvas, a single drop of paint rises upward, defying gravity. It is the color of a memory you can't quite recall—a specific shade of yellow from a childhood afternoon. As the drop expands, it forms a door. Not a painted door, but a real, wooden one, complete with a brass handle that gleams in the stagnant light.

You realize with a jolt of dream-logic certainty that you are not the painter. You are the paint.

You step toward the canvas, and the white surface ripples outward in concentric circles. As you cross the threshold, the world behind you dissolves into a thousand tiny squares of light, and the world before you is silent, white, and waiting for you to decide what you want to become. You reach for the handle, and as your grip tightens, the dream begins to fade, leaving you with the lingering sensation of being both the artist and the masterpiece.
