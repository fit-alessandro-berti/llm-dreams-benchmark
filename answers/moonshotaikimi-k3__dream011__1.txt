...written in your own handwriting, though you don't remember writing them.

*Question 1: What was the name of the dog you had before the one you remember?*

You blink, and the words rearrange themselves. *Question 1: Describe the sound of the house you grew up in at 3 a.m. Be specific.*

Around you, everyone is writing calmly, pens moving in neat, unbothered rows. The proctor is your third-grade teacher, or your father's boss, or someone whose face keeps being both. She announces twenty minutes remaining, though the clock on the wall has three hands and none of them agree.

Your pencil is soft as a candle. The answer sheet is slightly damp. You write something—anything—and the words sink into the paper like stones into water. The page absorbs them and offers a new question in return, as though the exam is interviewing you back.

*Question 7: When did you decide what you were going to be? Were you in the room when it happened?*

And suddenly you understand, with the calm certainty that only dreams allow, that this exam has been grading you for years. Every answer you've ever given out loud—to strangers, to yourself, to the ceiling at night—was being transcribed here. The whole page has been clearing space for the final question, the only one that was ever really on the test:

*Question 20: Are you ready?*

You look up to ask the proctor what "ready" means, but the room is already dissolving at the edges. The desks unbecome themselves. The fluorescent hum thins into birdsong. Your hand is still holding the pencil, poised over a blank morning, in a bed that is yours, in a day that has not asked you anything yet.
