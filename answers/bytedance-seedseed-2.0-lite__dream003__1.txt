I drag my thumb along one swirling line, and it unfurls into a tiny, moving scene I’d forgotten entirely: my 7th birthday, the blue bicycle with streamers I cried over losing when my family moved, the way the sun hit its chrome handlebars so bright they stung my eyes. Another loop blooms into the stray tabby that snuck onto my first apartment fire escape, that left dead ladybugs on my windowsill as gifts and vanished one cold winter without a trace. The patterns are all the small, unmarked moments I’d tucked away and lost, strung together like constellations across the box’s curved lid.

I realize then there’s no keyhole, no slot for anything external to unlock it. This lock only needs me to recognize what’s inside it. The second that thought lands in my foggy, dream-drunk brain, the box’s latch pops open with a soft, warm thud, like a book closing after a favorite story. A breeze spills out, smelling like my grandma’s cinnamon apple pie and summer rain on hot asphalt, and hundreds of tiny glowing motes float up, swirling around my head. Each one hums with a memory I’d dropped along the way: the laugh I shared with a stranger on a delayed cross-country train in 2019, the half-written poem I crumpled up and threw in a campus trash can, the split second I stood on an Oregon cliff and felt so small and so alive I thought my heart might burst.

At the bottom of the box, there’s a scrap of notebook paper scrawled in my own messy handwriting, though I’ve never seen it before: You don’t lock the soft things away to hide them. You lock them so you can come back and carry them with you, when the world feels too heavy to hold anything good. Before I can read it again, the box softens, melting like warm beeswax, and wraps itself around my left wrist as a thin, glowing bracelet strung with the same tiny pattern scenes.

When I lift my head, I’m no longer sitting cross-legged on the dusty attic floor where I first found the box. The cedar walls fall away to reveal the driveway of my childhood home: the blue bicycle leaning against the old oak, the tabby curled on the fence rail, grandma standing on the porch waving an oven mitt, like she’s been waiting for me to catch up to all the parts of myself I left behind. I take a step forward, and the bracelet on my wrist hums, warm as a hug, and I know I don’t have to leave any of it behind again.
