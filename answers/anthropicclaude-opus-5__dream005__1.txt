The house is bigger on the inside than it was from the driveway, though I don't remember a driveway. I remember arriving. That's different from arriving.

The first room is all coats. People are still wearing them, standing in clusters with drinks, and the coats keep going up past where the ceiling should be, so that when I look at anyone's face I have to look up through layers of wool and rain-smell. Someone says my name — my actual name, the one I use — and I turn, relieved, but it's a woman I've never seen who is telling a story about a boat. "You were there," she insists, kindly, and I nod, because in some way I might have been. The others laugh at the part about the anchor. I laugh a beat later, and no one minds.

I am looking for Dana. I know it's Dana the way you know the plot of a film you've never seen. If I could just find her, the party would resolve into something with edges.

The kitchen is doing something wrong with its light. Everyone in there is a chef, or claims to be, and they're all working on the same enormous pale cake, which they keep frosting from the inside. I ask if they've seen a woman about my height, dark hair, a green coat. They confer. One of them says, "She went through," and points with a spatula at the wall, and I feel a flush of embarrassment, because obviously she went through, everyone goes through, and I've asked the sort of question that reveals you weren't invited.

I go through.

Now it's a garden, at night, with strings of bulbs, and a hundred people are playing a game whose rules are being explained to me by three different people simultaneously and in complete agreement. Something about naming things that aren't in the room. Someone hands me a card. It's blank, but I understand that this is a good card, an enviable card, and I put it in my pocket and feel a warmth of belonging so sharp it almost wakes me.

I find Dana at the bottom of the garden, except her back is turned and getting further away without walking, the lawn unspooling between us like the aisle of a train. I call out. She turns and it's my old chemistry teacher, who says, "You're going to have to be more specific," and I understand this is the most useful advice anyone has given me all night.

So I try to be specific. I try to picture Dana's face and get only the feeling of her face, the impression a face leaves in a pillow. And the terrible thought arrives, gentle as a waiter: what if the one person I know isn't here, and never was, and I invented her so I'd have a reason to keep moving through the rooms.

Then the party takes it back. Everyone in the garden turns to me at once — not menacing, that's the thing, they're *warm* — and they say, in a loose overlapping chorus like applause, "We know you." And I realize that the arithmetic has inverted: I don't know any of them, and all of them know me, and that's fine, that's actually the arrangement, that's what the blank card meant.

A man in a magnificent coat brings me a drink I've been thirsty for my whole life. Somebody's grandmother asks if I'd like to sit. The strings of bulbs lower themselves like a slow rain of small suns and hang at shoulder height so we all have to move carefully, tenderly, between them.

And I do sit. And I stop looking. And in the exact moment I stop looking, Dana sits down beside me and says, "God, I've been trying to find you all night," and I say, "I know," and I mean *I know* in the fullest sense, the way you know it in dreams, everything at once, no evidence required —

and the coats begin ringing, softly, like an alarm.
