You decide to plant a single seed.

Not because you have any guarantee it will grow. Not because the cracked earth promises anything but dust and radiation. But because in this world of ash and silence, doing *nothing* feels like the real death.

You kneel in the rubble of what used to be a park, your fingers bleeding as you scrape away twisted rebar and broken glass. The seed is small—something you found in an overturned vending machine weeks ago, a forgotten packet of sunflower seeds, their shells cracked but the kernels inside still faintly green. You press it into the poisoned soil with trembling hands, then cover it with your last bottle of filtered rainwater. The drops sink in like tears.

That night you sleep in the hollow of a collapsed subway car, listening to the wind howl through the ruins like a dying animal. You dream of impossible things: sunlight that doesn’t burn, laughter that doesn’t echo empty, children running through fields that don’t yet exist. When you wake, the seed is still there. Nothing has changed. And yet… everything has.

Over the next days you return to that patch of ground. You scavenge broken pipes to channel rainwater. You kill a three-headed rat with your last bullet and use its bones as a crude trellis. You sing to the dirt—old songs your mother used to hum—because the silence is worse than any monster.

One morning you wake to a single green shoot pushing through the gray crust, impossibly bright against the wasteland. A sunflower. Tiny. Defiant. Alive.

You stare at it for a long time, tears cutting clean tracks down your dust-caked face. In a world that took everything, you chose to give something back. And the world, against all odds, answered.

The dream doesn’t end there. It never really does. But for the first time in years, you fall asleep smiling, one hand resting on the warm earth beside that impossible green spear, already reaching for the sky.
