and the generic corporate conference center where the competition started bleeds into something unrecognizable, room by room, each trial stitched from the quiet, unspoken things I’ve always cared about—like the judges wove the challenges straight from my own heart.

The first trial drops me and 20 other contestants into a warehouse stacked to the ceiling with unmarked cassette tapes, floating like dandelion puffs. The prompt flashes in neon across the wall: Sort the stories that matter. Most contestants dive for the loud, bright tapes: glittery pop star memoir snippets, viral influencer rants, the shiny stuff that hums with algorithm-familiar energy. I linger by a frayed gray tape no one touches, which stutters when I pick it up: it’s a grandma’s voice, taped over her own cancer diagnosis to record only her 3-year-old grandson’s first sentence, so when her family played it at her funeral, no one would have to sit with her fear, only the good thing. I tuck it into my small pile of “must-share” stories, and half the contestants vanish mid-sort, the warehouse walls narrowing to leave only 5 of us.

The next challenge shifts us to a rain-soaked main street of a small town I’ve never seen but recognize from old home videos my mom used to play. The prompt scrawled in mist on a boarded-up bookstore window: Unlock the story inside. The other finalists slide resumes and media kits under the door, yelling about engagement rates and brand deals to coax the owner out. I spot a stack of waterlogged postcards by the step, all addressed to a pen pal in Paris who stopped writing in 2003; the last one the bookseller sent said he’d planted a lilac tree outside his shop just to press a bloom for her. I sit on the damp curb and read that line out loud, soft enough that only the door could hear. Ten minutes later, the lock clicks. I don’t push inside, just stack the postcards neatly by the threshold and yell that I’d help him send new ones, if he ever wanted to write again. Two more contestants vanish, leaving only me and one other girl for the final round.

The last trial lands us on a stage, the glowing judges finally resolving into faces I’d actually met: the teen I’d helped write a coming-out post two years prior, the grandma who’d emailed me to share her grandson’s first steps video, the small-town bookstore owner, even the trans kid whose anonymous diary I’d found in a thrift store and shared on a tiny, forgotten blog years ago. The final choice is spread on a table between us: a celebrity memoir pitch guaranteed to hit 100 million views, or that same tattered thrift store diary, its pages soft from being read. The other girl grabs the memoir immediately, and the judges nod as she vanishes too—she’d won the high-flying, high-profit job she’d actually come to compete for.

I reach for the diary. I tell the judges my dream job was never to chase viral numbers; it was to find all the small, unclaimed stories that got tucked into thrift store bins and taped over on old cassettes, the ones that only one or two people in the whole world needed to hear to know they weren’t alone. The cheers that go up are soft, not loud, and the grand prize isn’t a contract or a paycheck. It’s the diary opening in my hands, and the unsent coming-out letter I’d always wondered about has a new note scrawled at the bottom: We found you. We’re proud of you.

I wake up a minute later, the scent of lilacs still stuck in my nose, and grab my phone to check that long-abandoned blog. There’s a new comment, posted five minutes prior, from someone who grew up in that same small town, saying they knew the kid from the diary, that they’d pass the message along. The dream wasn’t just a dream, after all.
