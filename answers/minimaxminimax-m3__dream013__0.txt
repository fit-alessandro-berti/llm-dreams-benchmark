...as you move deeper into the maze, the puzzles begin to change. No longer are they simply logical—they start to feel personal, as if the maze is reading you.

The first few rooms were manageable: a sliding tile puzzle, a sequence of colored lanterns that needed to be lit in a specific order, a riddle carved into stone whose answer you somehow already knew. But now, the walls have grown taller, slick with moisture that reflects light from nowhere. The corridors branch and rebranch in ways that shouldn't be possible, and you've stopped hearing your own footsteps.

You turn a corner and find a room with seven doors, each a different material—bone, glass, woven hair, rusted iron, something that pulses like flesh, wood that hums, and one that appears to be made of frozen sound. A voice, neither kind nor cruel, says simply: **"Choose the one you fear most."**

But you realize, slowly, that the puzzle isn't choosing a door. It's recognizing that you've been carrying the weight of choices you've already made—paths not taken, words not spoken. The maze hasn't been testing your logic. It's been listening to the questions you ask yourself at 3 AM.

A door opens on its own. It's the one made of frozen sound, and the air around it shimmers.

On the other side, you find not another room, but a vast open field under a sky that has too many moons. In the center stands a table with a single chair, and seated at it is an older version of yourself, patiently waiting. They look up.

*"Took you long enough,"* they say. *"The final puzzle isn't a puzzle at all. Sit down. We need to talk about the thing you've been avoiding."*

What do you do?
