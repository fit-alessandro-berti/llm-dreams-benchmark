You start to run, your feet slapping against pavement that feels suspiciously like warm bread. The city shifts around you like it's breathing—skyscrapers lean in to gossip, neon signs flicker in a language that looks like math equations having an existential crisis.

An hour. You have an hour.

You spot what might be a taxi, except it's a giant floating teacup with wheels. The driver is a raccoon in a three-piece suit.

"Important meeting?" he asks, adjusting a monocle. "Hop in. But first, tell me: what does your mother’s childhood goldfish smell like? Wrong answer and we go sideways through Tuesday."

You blurt out "regret and bubblegum" on pure instinct. The raccoon nods solemnly, like you just passed an ancient test, and the teacup lurches forward at impossible speed. The city folds like origami—alleys become rivers, rivers become escalators, escalators lead to a massive clock tower that’s also a very tired-looking owl.

The owl opens one enormous eye.

"You’re late," it hoots.

"I know, I’m—"

"Emotionally late. The meeting started twenty years ago. They’ve been waiting for you to show up as yourself instead of the version who’s scared of being late."

The clock tower cracks open like an egg. Inside is a small, perfectly ordinary conference room. Around the table sit every version of you that you abandoned: the one who wanted to be an astronaut, the one who was kind without calculating the cost, the thirteen-year-old who genuinely believed they could build a spaceship in the backyard.

They all look up at once.

The raccoon leans in from behind you and whispers, "Told you it was never about the address."

The oldest version of you—the one with laugh lines and calm eyes—slides a single piece of paper across the table. It just says:

**You were always already here.**

The city exhales.

You wake up smiling, heart still racing, with the distinct feeling that somewhere, a very important meeting just went remarkably well.
