The fluorescent lights above hum like a swarm of bees that have forgotten how to land.  
My boss is there, already, beside my monitor, tapping a finger that sounds like a woodpecker against drywall.  
“Start,” he says, though the word splits into two syllables—*st-arts*—and one of them floats away like a lost balloon.

I open the project file.  
It is not a document.  
It is a hallway that appears to be made of elevator doors, all slightly ajar, each showing a different year: 1998, last Tuesday, the year the oceans learned to fold themselves into envelopes.  
The deadline glows above the screen like a moon that has decided to work overtime.  
It is labeled *TODAY*, but the font is melting, dripping into a puddle that spells *YESTERDAY* in block capitals.

My chair is no longer a chair.  
It is a bicycle built for three, but the other two seats are occupied by mannequins wearing my clothes from when I was twelve and from when I will be seventy.  
They nod in unison, which is disconcerting because their heads click like Geiger counters.

I reach for the keyboard.  
The keys are teeth.  
When I press one, it does not type a letter; it sings a question I am not supposed to ask aloud.  
The first one I press asks, *What if the deadline is a mirror?*  
The monitor swallows the sound and replies with a spreadsheet that lists my childhood fears alphabetically.

My boss coughs, and the cough turns into a flock of origami cranes that flutter toward the sprinklers, which begin to rain confetti made of canceled vacation days.  
He points at the hallway of elevator doors.  
“Pick one,” he says, “but remember: whichever one you choose will open onto the one you avoided last week.”

I step away from the desk, but the floor is an escalator that is moving while I am not.  
It carries me toward a door labeled *Break Room* that I have never seen before.  
The handle is warm.  
I turn it, expecting coffee, but instead I step into a forest where the trees are made of interlocking to-do lists, each branch a bullet point that grows thorns the size of missed milestones.

In the distance, a printer is screaming.  
It is printing a banner that reads *CONGRATULATIONS*, but the letters keep rearranging themselves into *YOU WILL NEVER LEAVE*.  
The paper piles up and begins to knit itself into a sweater I recognize as the one I wore to my first interview here, except the sleeves are too long and the cuffs have turned into stop signs.

My boss appears again, or perhaps he never left—he is now standing on a filing cabinet that is floating in midair, wearing a hat made of deadlines, each one stitched with a tiny clock that ticks backward.  
He hands me a stapler.  
It is heavy, dense as a black hole wrapped in plastic.  
“When you staple the air,” he says, “it holds the shape of what you wish you had said in the meeting that never happened.”

I try to staple the hallway.  
The staple bends into a question mark and rolls across the floor, growing larger until it becomes a merry-go-round of doors, each one opening to reveal a version of me who quit, who screamed, who laughed and walked out into a parking lot that leads to an ocean.

The escalator floor jerks.  
I am moving upward now, even though I am standing still.  
The ceiling splits open like a banana peel, and I see above it a sky that is nothing but a giant sticky note labeled *OVERDUE*.  
The wind smells like toner and forgotten birthdays.

I decide to sit down, but the chair has become a rowboat made of rubber bands and apology emails.  
I climb in, and the moment I do, the hallway of elevator doors behind me slams shut with a sound like a book closing on its own moral.  
The rowboat begins to float through the office, slipping between desks that have turned into icebergs, each one carrying a plaque with a single word: *Soon*, *Maybe*, *Not Your Fault*, *Try Again*.

My boss’s voice comes from beneath the water, which is now rising but also staying perfectly level with my chin.  
He says, “Progress,” and the word inflates like a life raft made of thumbtacks.  
I sit on it, even though it pricks me, and I begin to row with a pen that leaks blue light instead of ink.

As I row, the office dissolves into a single sheet of paper that folds itself into a paper airplane.  
I climb aboard.  
The airplane tips its wings, and I realize I am no longer dreaming the deadline—I am the ink that is spelling it into existence, one letter at a time, each one a door, each door a choice to keep going even when the going is grammar, even when the grammar is gravity.

The airplane banks toward a window that is not glass but a photograph of tomorrow, and I understand, as the photograph crumples into a paper pellet and rolls off the sill, that the impossible deadline is just a dare dressed as a schedule, and the only way to finish it is to let it dream me back.
