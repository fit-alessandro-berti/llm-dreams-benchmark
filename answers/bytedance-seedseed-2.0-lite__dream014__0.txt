The first to step forward is a teen in paint-splattered overalls, twirling a lollipop that glows soft gold, bright enough to cut through the city’s smoggy gray, and she presses a crumpled tissue into your palm that smells exactly like your grandma’s lavender laundry soap—something you haven’t breathed in since she died, five years prior. An older man behind her hefts a wicker basket full of dented house keys that jingle like wind chimes, and he says, “We heard you muttering you were lost. We know the place you’re really looking for, not just that hotel address you kept repeating to yourself.”

They lead you through the crowd, and the honking of taxis and shouts of street vendors soften, warp like taffy, until the city that felt overwhelming and cold only minutes earlier feels like a familiar hallway you’re walking down to get home. A dumpling vendor slips you a paper bag of your favorite pork and chive dumplings—the exact ones your dad used to bring home every Friday when you were a kid—and he doesn’t ask for payment, just winks like he’s in on the secret. You turn a corner you swear wasn’t there five minutes earlier, and at the end of a tree-lined lane is the tiny blue porch of your childhood home, the one you sold when you moved across the country three years prior. Your old beagle, Max, who passed away two years back, lifts his head from the wooden step, thumping his tail so hard his whole body wiggles, and grandma is leaning in the screen door, waving, holding a plate of her chocolate chip cookies still warm from the oven.

The older man with the key basket tosses you a tiny brass key, engraved with your full name, and calls out before the group turns to melt back into the city’s glow, “That works anytime you need it. Even when you’re not lost in a dream anymore.” You clutch the key in your fist, and by the time you turn to thank them, they’re gone, just a trail of golden glows bobbing like fireflies far down the lane. You sit on the porch step, Max curling into your lap, and for the first time in years, that sharp, constant ache of being alone and adrift fades completely. When the dream softens at the edges, pulling you toward waking, you swear you can feel the cool weight of that brass key under your pillow, even when you open your eyes back in your small city apartment.
