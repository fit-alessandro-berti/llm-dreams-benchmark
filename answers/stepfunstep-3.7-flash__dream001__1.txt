You start to jamb your hand into the deep pockets of the wool coat you don’t remember putting on, scratchy fabric catching on your wrist, and fumble for your phone. It’s not the cracked iPhone you fell asleep scrolling TikTok on—it’s a dented silver flip phone, the screen oozing slow, silvery mercury that pools in the creases of your palm. When you blink it clear, the only app open is a map, but it only shows the narrow cobblestone street you’re standing on, labeled in looping, half-legible script: *Maybe 5th, Maybe Not*. A tiny red pin blinks at your feet, marked “URGENT MEETING: 14:00”, and when you glance up at the sky, the sun is a smudged watercolor dot hovering halfway up the lavender clouds, no way to tell if it’s morning or afternoon.

Panic prickles hot under your skin, the kind that feels thick, like you’re moving through syrup. You start to run, but your legs drag, each step sinking half an inch into the cobblestones. The city around you bleeds into focus as you move: skyscrapers carved from translucent blue glass that hum low, like a choir holding a single note, street signs scrawled in half-English, half-musical notation, every passerby wearing floor-length coats printed with faded, crumpled maps, their faces turned resolutely away from you. You lunge for a woman in a coat marked with the grid of the neighborhood you grew up in, but when your fingers brush her shoulder, the fabric unfolds, unfurls into a full, crinkly map of the entire city, and she just keeps walking, no hint she noticed you.

You can’t remember what the meeting is for. The thought slips every time you reach for it, slippery as a bar of soap: all you know is if you’re late, the sound of your mom’s laugh will vanish from your head forever. The mercury screen flickers, and now it says 27 minutes left. The firefly streetlights buzzing in glass jars overhead rattle louder as you pass.

A kid no older than eight, hair made of dandelion fluff that drifts off in tiny tufts when the wind picks up, steps directly in your path. They’re holding a folded paper crane that glows soft gold, and they tilt their head like they’ve been waiting for you. “You’re looking for the Tower of Unwritten Minutes,” they say, their voice like wind through tall grass. “Third floor, the room where the clocks run backwards. You’re going the wrong way.” They nod down a narrow alley that definitely wasn’t there ten seconds ago, its brick walls smudged with charcoal sketches of half-erased things: a golden retriever, a beach, a jar of fireflies. “Don’t open the doors marked with years you miss,” they call as you start to run, “you’ll lose more time than you have.”

The alley is lined with dozens of tiny wooden doors, each carved with a year: 2021. 2008. 2012. You sprint past, but your foot catches on the step of the one marked 2017, and you stumble. Before you can right yourself, the door creaks open, and you’re hit with the smell of salt and coconut sunscreen, the bark of your childhood dog, your best friend from college yelling your name from the waves. You stand there for what feels like a second, petting the dog’s soft ears, and when you jerk back to the alley, the mercury phone in your hand says 8 minutes left. Your sneakers are dissolving, turning to tufts of dandelion fluff, and you can feel the wind on your toes.

At the end of the alley, the tower looms, its spire disappearing into the lavender clouds, each step of its winding stone staircase carved with a single faded memory: your first day of school, the time you cried because you lost a sparkly hair clip, the first time you drove a car alone. You climb, your breath ragged, and you can hear the murmur of the meeting room upstairs, voices too faint to make out, like they’re speaking through a wall of water. The stairs get steeper, and the cuckoo clock bolted to the stairwell wall runs backwards, its hands ticking faster and faster.

You burst through the third-floor door so hard it slams into the wall, and freeze. The room is empty, save for a single folding metal chair in the center, and on it, your normal cracked iPhone, buzzing. You grab it, and the only notification is a text from your roommate, sent 47 minutes ago: “Bro you overslept, the all-hands meeting was last Tuesday lmao. We grabbed tacos after, there’s leftover guac and those pickled onions you like in the fridge. Don’t kill me for waking you up, you were snoring so loud the walls shook.”

As you stare at the text, the room starts to peel away at the edges, like wet paper pulling off a wall. The glass tower dissolves, the hum of the city fades, and you’re back in your bed, your alarm blaring 7AM, your blankets tangled around your legs. You’re groggy, still half-convinced you can smell salt and dandelion fluff, and when you brush your hand against your pillow, you find a single, perfect tuft of dandelion down, glowing faint gold in the morning light.

When you pick it up, it hums, the same low note as the glass skyscrapers, and for half a second you swear you can hear the ocean, and your old dog’s bark, and the kid’s laugh, before you blink and it’s just a piece of plant fluff, and you’re late for work
