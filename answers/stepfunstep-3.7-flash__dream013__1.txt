as you move deeper into the maze, you notice the puzzles stop drawing from generic logic grids and start pulling scraps of your own memories to build their rules. The first lock you encounter beyond the entry hall isn’t a cipher or a sequence—it’s a brass plate etched with the exact voicemail your grandma left you the week she died, asking you to pick up milk on the way over. To open it, you have to fill in the blank of what you *should* have said back, but every answer you land on feels wrong, sharp as broken glass in your throat. When you finally write the half-honest, half-forgotten line you’d muttered to yourself when you listened to it last, the plate dissolves into glittering dust, and the hallway beyond smells like the lavender detergent she used to fold your sheets in.

The further you go, the more the maze bends to the intensity of your focus. When you stare too hard at a puzzle requiring you to map the twist of the corridors, the walls rearrange themselves to match the map you’re drawing in your head, stretching the path out for another hundred feet. A puzzle that asks you to count the number of times you’ve lied this month makes the numbers swim on the page, each digit shifting to match the lies you’re trying not to think about in that moment. You start to notice that every solution you calculate doesn’t lead you out—it weaves more of the maze into being, threads of your own thoughts and regrets stitched into the cinder block and paper-mâché walls.

Halfway through, you stumble into a circular room where the floor is a sprawling, tile-based puzzle you have to cross to reach the door on the far side. Each tile is a choice you still ruminate on: taking the job in another city, answering that text, staying home that night instead of going to the party. You try to pick the “right” path, the one that aligns with the life you think you wanted, but every tile you step on sinks a little, and the room fills with the hum of all the other versions of that choice you didn’t make. You realize the puzzle isn’t asking you to pick a path—it’s asking you to admit that none of them were ever the “right” one, that every choice was just a guess. When you stop trying to solve it, just stand in the middle of the room and let the hum wash over you, the tiles stop shifting, and the door on the other side creaks open.

You step through, and you’re back at the entrance of the maze. The brass plate with your grandma’s voicemail is right in front of you again, unmarked, waiting for you to solve it. You look down at your hands, and they’re dusted with the same glittering residue you earned hours (or years? you can’t tell time here) ago. The small glowing orb that’s been trailing you this whole time buzzes, and its cracked screen flashes a single line: you’re still concentrating. You haven’t stopped solving.

A cold, dream-logic understanding clicks into place then: the maze never wanted you to escape. It feeds on the intense, unspooling focus you pour into every puzzle, the way you twist your own memories and regrets into problems to be solved with logic. Every time you think you’re moving deeper, you’re just weaving more of yourself into the walls, until the maze isn’t a separate place you’re trapped in anymore. It’s you. And you’re still solving, still moving deeper, still listening for the faint chime that sounds like your front door opening, even though you haven’t had a front door in years. You open your mouth to call out, to stop thinking, to let the logic go—and the next puzzle appears on the wall in front of you, glowing, waiting, and your brain is already working on the answer before you can stop it.

You wake up with the taste of burnt sugar on your tongue, your wrists sore from where you’ve been clenching them like you’re holding a pen, and for half a second you can still see the glowing edge of the puzzle hovering in the dark of your ceiling.
