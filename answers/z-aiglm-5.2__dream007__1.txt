You are in the middle of a heated argument with a close friend. The disagreement is about something important to both of you, and you decide to walk away before you say something you can't take back.

You turn and push open a door that wasn't there a moment ago. It leads not to the room you expected but to a long hallway lined with photographs — pictures of you and this friend at various ages, in places you recognize and places you don't. In one photo, you're both children building something in the sand. In another, you're old, sitting on a porch, not speaking, but clearly at peace.

The hallway opens into a vast, empty field. The grass is the color of wheat, and the sky can't decide if it's sunset or sunrise. Your friend is standing in the distance, but when you walk toward them, the distance doesn't change. You try calling out, but the wind swallows your voice.

Then you notice something in your hand. It's small — a key, or maybe a stone, warm from your grip. You don't remember picking it up. You look down at it, and when you look back up, your friend is right in front of you, close enough to touch. They're not angry anymore. They're just waiting.

You open your mouth to speak, and instead of words, a small bird flies out. Then another. They circle once and disappear into the undecided sky.

Your friend laughs — not at you, but with surprise and delight, the way they laughed when you first met. And you realize the argument was never the point. The point was always this: the willingness to come back.

You wake up with the echo of that laughter still in your chest, and an ache that feels like gratitude.
