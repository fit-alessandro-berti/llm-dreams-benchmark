The fluorescent lights above flicker, not with electricity, but with the dull, bruised purple of twilight. My desk dissolves into a floor of shifting sand.

My boss is there, but he is made of polished chrome, his face a blank mirror that reflects my own widening eyes. He doesn't speak, but the air hums with the vibration of impossible deadlines—a sound like a glacier cracking. In his hand, he holds not a folder, but a ticking clock, its glass face spiderwebbed with fractures. He presses it into my chest. It adheres like a second skin, the cold metal seeping through my shirt, freezing my ribs.

*"Start,"* the clock chimes, and the sound echoes like a gunshot in a cathedral.

I look down at my hands. They are no longer hands; they are spindles of raw, unspun thread, dripping with wool the color of storm clouds. To work, I must spin this thread into a tapestry of finished code, finalized reports, and perfect presentations. But my fingers snag. The thread resists. With every pull, the clock on my chest beats faster—a frantic, arrhythmic thrumming that rattles my teeth.

The walls of the office melt away, revealing that I am standing on the edge of a cliff. Below me is not ground, but a churning ocean of liquid ledgers and rising red ink. If the clock stops, the cliff crumbles.

I try to spin, but the thread catches on something sharp. I look up to see the knot in the yarn: it is a tiny, perfect replica of my own childhood bedroom, stuck in the spool. The harder I pull to meet the deadline, the tighter the knot grips the memory of my mother’s lullabies. Time is running out. The clock is glowing white-hot, burning a hole through my sternum.

I realize, with the lucid clarity of the dream, that the only way to finish the project is to let the clock shatter. But if I do, the thread will unravel, and I will fall into the red ink.

The sand beneath my feet gives way. I take a breath, reach for the knot, and pull—not forward, but deep into the center of the spool, back toward the lullaby, back toward the beginning.

The clock cracks.

The thunder of silence that follows is the sweetest sound I have ever heard.
