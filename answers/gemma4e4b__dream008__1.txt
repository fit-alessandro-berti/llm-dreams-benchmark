...I suddenly realize I cannot move.

It isn't a physical paralysis; it’s a neurological, existential one. My limbs feel coated in fine, crystalline sugar—cloying and immobilizing. The hum of the fluorescent lights above me, which usually just *is*, now pitches into a deafening, oscillating *whine*, like a million trapped cicadas singing off-key.

I look at the project scope—a thick binder filled with flashing, neon-green deadlines printed on impossible flowcharts. The due date isn't just "tomorrow"; it's marked with a jagged, screaming glyph that resembles a burning stopwatch.

My boss, Mr. Harrison, is leaning across the gleaming mahogany desk. He isn't angry, which is worse. He is profoundly, unsettlingly *calm*. His smile is too wide, too fixed, like a porcelain mask glued onto his face.

"So," he purrs, tipping forward, "just dive in, Karen. We need this whole platform operational by the… end of the week."

But suddenly, the air smells wrong. It smells like burnt caramel and ozone, like the moment before a great storm, amplified by the stale scent of lukewarm coffee.

I try to speak, to ask a question—*What resources? What constraints?*—but my throat feels like it’s stuffed with wet gauze.

And then the impossible detail catches my eye.

I look past the binder, past Mr. Harrison's expectant face, and I see the actual *surface* of the desk. It’s not polished wood. It’s not metal. It’s a sheet of perfectly still, obsidian-black water.

The whole office—the beige walls, the potted plastic ferns, the framed degrees on the cubicles—it's all sitting *on* the water.

A profound, chilling wave goes through me. The impossibility isn't the deadline; the impossibility is *everything*. The solid world of deadlines, quarterly earnings, and KPIs is nothing more than a shimmering film laid over a vast, echoing void.

I blink, hard.

The obsidian surface ripples slightly, and the high-pitched whining of the fluorescent lights drops in pitch, humming down scale until it settles into the deep, throbbing resonance of a giant cello string.

Mr. Harrison’s unnervingly calm smile begins to stutter. His eyes, which had been fixed on me with corporate expectation, widen incrementally, as if for the first time, they see the water, or the void beneath it.

I realize I am not at *my* job. I am at the edge of some enormous, submerged mechanism.

And I simply stop being frightened. The terror doesn't dissipate; it becomes an icy, exhilarating awareness. I look straight into my boss's eyes, past the professional veneer, and I see nothing but a reflection of the vast, dark emptiness beneath the work table.

Then, I laugh.

It’s a strange, breathless, hysterical sound that echoes too loud in the sudden, oppressive quiet. I push back my chair—the one that was solid hardwood—and it doesn't scratch the floor. It skitters across the water, making a clean, liquid *glug* that rings out like a dropped crystal glass.

The fluorescent lights don't go out; they simply fade into the black mirror, and the true deadline, I understand, is not measured in calendar days, but in the sheer, terrifying moment of realizing you are surrounded by a perfect, fragile lie.

And I take a step forward, placing my shoes *into* the water, preparing to walk out of the office and into the waiting, unknowable darkness.
