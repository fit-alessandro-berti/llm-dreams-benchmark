step onto the slick deck, boots sliding against the rain-slicked wood, and raise your voice above the howling wind.

"Secure the mainsail!" you bellow, and the words tear from your throat like something alive. Salt spray lashes your face as you grip the helm, knuckles white, arms aching against the rudder that fights you like a living thing.

The ship crests a wave that shouldn't exist—that rises black and impossible against the lightning-split sky—and for a moment you hang there, suspended between the crest and the void below, and you see it.

A light. Beneath the water.

It pulses. Draws you.

"Captain!" your first mate screams, but her voice comes from very far away now, stretched thin as the space between heartbeats. The crew scrambles across the deck in slow motion, their movements thick, dreamlike—and you realize with sudden, calm certainty that they have always been moving this way. That you have always been standing here. That the storm is not a storm.

The light beneath the water brightens. The sea parts.

And there, cradled in the trench between waves like something long asleep, is a city. Spires of pale stone wreathed in kelp, windows glowing with that same pulse, that same rhythm as your own blood singing in your ears.

The wheel goes slack beneath your hands.

The ship begins to descend—not sinking, not falling—*called*.

And you understand, as the water closes over the deck like a blessing, that you were never the captain of a ship.

You were always the invitation.
