as you move deeper into the maze, the stone walls shift subtly beneath your touch, their carvings twisting into patterns that don’t make sense at first glance—arcs and symbols that seem to hum faintly when you lean in. The air grows thick with the smell of damp moss and something sharp, like burnt rosin, and the sound of your own breathing echoes louder than it should, as if the maze itself is listening.

The first puzzle you encounter isn’t a lock or a riddle, but a series of indentations in the floor: six shallow holes, each sized to fit a hand or a foot, arranged in a spiral that leads down a narrow side passage. You kneel to run your fingers over them, and suddenly a memory flickers: the first puzzle the maze showed you, a mirrored wall that only revealed its hidden door when you stood in the exact center of the room. You count the holes again—one for each time you’d paused to solve a riddle, one for each turn you’d taken that felt wrong, then right. You place your left hand on the topmost indentation, your right foot on the one below it, and wait. For a long heartbeat, nothing happens. Then the spiral glows, pale silver, and the passage swings open just wide enough to slip through.

Deeper still, the maze’s challenges blend together. There’s a room where the walls close in slowly, and you have to stack the fallen stone blocks from earlier to reach a narrow vent high above—your palms scraped raw, your knees shaking, but you make it, and the vent leads you to a corridor lined with framed notes: scraps of paper left by people who came before you, some scribbled with half-solved equations, others with crude drawings of the maze’s more brutal traps. One note, scrawled in smudged pencil, reads: “The exit isn’t at the end. It’s where you stopped running.”

You tuck that note into your pocket, even though it feels like a dream within a dream. The next puzzle is a massive stone door, carved with the face of a wolf whose eyes are two empty sockets. When you press your palm to one socket, it hums, and the other socket lights up with a beam of soft blue light that points to a small niche in the wall beside it. Inside the niche is a tiny, rusted key—familiar, like something you lost as a kid, the kind you’d hide under your mattress. You fit it into the door’s lock, and it turns with a sound like a sigh.

The door slides open, and beyond it, the maze opens up into a vast, glowing chamber. The floor is covered in constellations that shift and move, matching the stars you saw through the maze’s only skylight earlier that day. At the center of the chamber stands a single pedestal, and on it is a weathered journal, its pages filled with someone else’s journey through the same maze—turns that looked like dead ends, puzzles that seemed impossible, a moment where they almost gave up. You flip to the last page, and your breath catches: it’s your own handwriting, detailing the exact moments you’ve just lived.

Just then, a soft voice hums through the chamber, not with ears, but like a feeling in your bones: “You’ve been solving someone else’s maze.” A door materializes behind you, carved with the same wolf’s face, but this time its eyes are open, warm and golden. Before you can move, the constellations on the floor shift again, forming a path back to the start of the maze—and then you wake up, still holding that crumpled note from the corridor, the smell of burnt rosin still clinging to your clothes.
