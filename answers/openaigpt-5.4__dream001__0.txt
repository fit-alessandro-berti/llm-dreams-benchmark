You start to walk quickly, then faster, through streets that seem to rearrange themselves whenever you glance away. Buildings lean in close as if listening. Every sign is almost readable, but the words slide out of focus just before you understand them.

You reach into your pocket for your phone, certain the address must be there, but when you unlock it the screen is filled with a map made of water. Streets ripple and drift apart under your thumb. A blue dot marks your location, then splits into three blue dots, each one heading in a different direction.

People pass by in business clothes, all moving with calm purpose, carrying folders, umbrellas, birds in cages, steaming cups of coffee that never spill. You try to stop someone and ask for directions, but when they turn to you, their face is your own face, older and more tired, and it only smiles apologetically before stepping onto a bus that has no doors.

You decide to follow the sound of a clock tower, because it feels official, and surely official things happen near clock towers. The streets narrow into an alley lined with office doors, each labeled with strangely specific names: “Conference Room for Regrets,” “Department of Unfinished Emails,” “Tuesday.” You open one and find a beach at sunset. Another holds a classroom where everyone looks up from taking the same exam and stares as if you are late. You close it quickly.

The clock begins to chime, though it is impossible to tell whether it means the hour is near or already gone. You start running now. Your shoes strike pavement, carpet, marble, grass, then shallow water. The city unfolds ahead of you in impossible layers: a subway station above a library above a rooftop garden above a lobby filled with aquarium light.

At last you arrive at a tall glass building that feels right in the way dreams allow things to feel right. In the lobby, a receptionist sits behind a desk made of clouds, flipping through a ledger longer than the desk itself. Without looking up, she says, “You’re on the top floor. You’ve always been on the top floor.”

You rush to the elevator and press the button. The doors open immediately, but inside is not an elevator. It is your childhood bedroom, lit by late afternoon sun. On the bed lies the outfit you meant to wear, neatly folded, and beside it is a sheet of paper with the meeting details written in your own handwriting.

You pick it up. The address is not an address at all. It says:

**Bring nothing.  
You are not late.  
They are waiting for you to remember why you came.**

When you look up, the bedroom window shows the whole city below, silent now, every street leading inward like veins to a single bright point in the center. You understand, with total dream-certainty, that the meeting is there.

So you step through the window instead of the door, and rather than falling, you begin to descend gently through warm air and drifting paper and the distant sound of applause, toward whatever it is that has been expecting you all along.
