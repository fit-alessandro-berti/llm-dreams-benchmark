You decide to stop talking and reach for the window instead—not to escape, but because the glass has started to ripple like water. Your friend falls silent mid-sentence. The disagreement, you realize with the calm certainty of dreaming, was never about the words you were using. It was about the space between them.

The words themselves have detached from the air and become small, pale moths. They cling to the curtains, fluttering with a sound like pages turning. One lands on your friend’s shoulder, and when they brush it away, their face shifts subtly—they are themselves, but also older, and somehow younger, wearing an expression from a photograph that was never taken.

"We should walk," they say, though their lips don't move.

You open the window. There is no street below, only a vast library with shelves that spiral upward into a sky the color of unpolished silver. Each book is thick and warm to the touch, bound in material that feels like weathered leather but might be dried song. You both climb through, and as your feet meet the floor, you understand that every volume here is a conversation the two of you started but never finished—some from this life, some from lives the dream hasn't shown you yet.

Your friend pulls one from the shelf. The spine has no title, only a date that hasn't happened yet. They open it, and the pages are blank, but you can both hear the argument continuing somewhere in the margins, quieter now, like a river heard from a great distance.

"We don't have to read it," you say.

"No," they agree, and slides it back. "We can shelve it."

You leave the book breathing there among the others, a living thing in hibernation. The moths follow you, lighting the path deeper into the stacks where the geometry softens and the shelves curve like ribs. Somewhere far behind you, a phone is ringing—your alarm, perhaps, or someone else's dream trying to call you home. But for now, you keep walking. Your friend walks beside you. The silence between you has finally taken the same shape as understanding, and the light overhead hasn't yet decided what color it wants to be.
