...not written in words, but in liquid, shifting ink that moves across the page every time I try to focus on them.

I squint, leaning closer. Question One slowly stabilizes into focus, reading: *Calculate the weight of the door you closed five years ago.*

Panic spikes in my chest—a cold, heavy lump. I turn the page, but the paper feels like wet velvet. Question Two: *Demonstrate, using diagrams, how to breathe underwater when you forget your own name.*

I look up, desperate to catch the proctor's eye, but the figure standing at the front of the hall has no face—just a smooth expanse of polished brass reflecting the harsh fluorescent lights. Around me, dozens of other students are writing furiously. Their pens move in perfect, terrifying unison, scratching rhythmic patterns into the wood of their desks.

*Scratch. Scratch. Scratch.* The sound morphs into the heavy ticking of a clock that isn't there.

I reach down to grab my pencil, but my arm feels as though it’s submerged in cold, thick glass. Moving it takes an eternity. When my fingers finally touch the pencil, it melts, dripping through my hands like warm, black wax.

The test paper in front of me begins to ripple like the surface of a pond. The text dissolves completely, replaced by a deep, dark opening in the middle of the desk. Down in the depths of the paper, I can see a hallway I haven't walked through since childhood, glowing under a soft, golden light.

A voice echoes over the auditorium speakers, loud, echoing, and strangely calm: 

*"Time is up. Please turn in who you used to be."*

The floor beneath my chair tilts forward, dissolving into cool air, and as I start to fall into the drafty dark, the exam room snaps away like a turned-off television—and I suddenly catch my breath, gasping awake in the quiet dark of my bedroom.
