The material of the box refuses to settle; it is iron-cold one moment, velvet-warm the next, and occasionally it feels like holding a handful of static electricity.

You press your palm against the center of the lid, and the patterns glow—not with light, but with *sound*. A low, thrumming chord that resonates in your sternum. You realize, with the absolute certainty that only arrives in dreams, that the box is not locked to keep you out. It is locked to keep something *in*, and the key has been with you all along, though it is not metal. It is a specific memory: the smell of rain on hot asphalt from a Tuesday afternoon when you were seven, or perhaps the exact shade of blue your bedroom wall was before you learned to name colors.

You close your eyes and offer the memory forward. The lock inhales. The lid unlatches with a sound like a violin string being plucked underwater.

Inside, there is no darkness. There is a staircase spiraling downward, each step carved from the pages of books that haven’t been written yet. You descend. The air tastes of cinnamon and ozone. With every step, your clothes loosen, then fall away, then transform—your shirt becomes a flock of moths, your shoes turn to river stones. You are neither afraid nor embarrassed. You are becoming *essential*.

At the bottom, you find a room the size of a thimble and the size of a cathedral simultaneously. In the center sits a mirror, but your reflection is holding the box. You watch yourself, reflected, open it. Inside *that* box, you see your own sleeping face, eyelids fluttering, the real world a pale blue sheet tangled around your knees.

Your reflected self looks up at you and smiles. They place a finger to their lips, then reach through the glass—not to pull you out, but to hand you something small and glowing. A key, finally. But it is melting, fast, like ice.

You wake just as your fingers close around it, the sensation of cold metal lingering in your palm for three full seconds after your eyes have opened.
