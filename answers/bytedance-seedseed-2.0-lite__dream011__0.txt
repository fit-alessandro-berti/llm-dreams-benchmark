The ink bleeds and shifts across the yellowed paper like it’s being written right in front of your eyes, the first question blurring into sharp focus first:
1. On October 12th of this semester, you snuck to the back row after lecture to cry because you thought no one saw you. Name the classmate who left an extra-sweet matcha latte (just how you like it) on your desk before you looked up, and explain why they didn’t stay to ask if you were okay. (25 points)
Your throat tightens. That was a day you’d never told anyone about— a brutal work rejection that spilled over into the career-pivot course you’d gambled your savings on, a moment you’d sworn you’d kept private. You flip the page, and the next question stings worse:
2. You turned down the team lead position last March because you were convinced you’d never be “ready enough.” List three small, uncelebrated times you led something well—no big wins you brag about to your parents over holiday dinner, just the quiet ones you forgot to count. (35 points)
You glance up, and the exam hall warps into classic dream nonsense: the kid hunched in the seat in front of you is 16-year-old you, scribbling furiously about the time you chickened out of running for student council, and the gray-haired woman napping in the back row is 72-year-old you, who winks when you catch her eye. The professor looms at the front, a weird merge of your strict high school principal and your current supportive manager, and when he catches you staring, he just taps his watch not with anger, but soft patience.
You flip to the last page of the exam, and there’s no third question—just a handwritten note scrawled in the same messy script you use to jot grocery lists to yourself: This course wasn’t ever to test if you’re good enough for your future. It’s to remind you your future’s already been built by all the small, messy, kind things you’ve done that you never think count. You don’t need a perfect score to pass. Turn it in whenever you’re ready.
Before you can reach for a pen, the fire alarm blares so loud your teeth rattle, and you jolt awake in your bed, your laptop still open to the job application you’d been too scared to submit the night before. The dream lingers like a nudge, and you first shoot a text to Lila—your old coworker, the one who’d brought you that latte, who’d been navigating her own breakup that same month and didn’t want to draw attention to your shared vulnerability—before you click “submit” on the application.
