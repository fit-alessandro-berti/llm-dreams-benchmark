• You are at a large social gathering where you only know one person. As you try to find them, you encounter various
  groups of people and...

  the room keeps changing.

  At first, everyone is laughing too loudly, each group orbiting a different center of gravity—a couple of colleagues
  arguing about old photographs, a circle of strangers trading life-changing business cards, a ring of aunties comparing
  travel plans and children’s schools, a row of people in identical navy suits smiling with the practiced calm of people
  who have already decided who gets which wine.

  You keep scanning for your friend’s face.
  “Sorry, have you seen—?” you ask, to no one in particular.
  No one answers, not with cruelty, but with that polite blankness that means they don’t recognize the question, or they
  do, and remember a different person with that name.

  Then you notice the first odd thing: no one is still.

  A couple in one corner lifts their glasses and in that instant becomes a trio in old school uniforms. A woman you just
  shook hands with now has a little yellow notepad in her palm and is counting people. Someone nearby asks if you’re “in
  the right place for the fourth toast” and looks around for a ring of light.

  You check your phone. The name you typed a week ago is still there—Mara—with a tiny smiley beside it from when she
  first sent you directions, “left of the red wall, by the ficus, third row, looking for the girl with the green scarf.”
  No green scarf now. No red wall. No ficus. Just a glass staircase and a chandelier that seems to flicker in rhythm
  with the bass.

  You move on.

  ———

  At the buffet table, a man in suspenders is explaining kombucha to a teenager in a tux.
  “They’re all here, right?” he says, tapping his fork against a bowl.
  “Who?” you ask.
  “Everyone,” he says, and grins as if that should satisfy you.

  You ask three people in different clusters if they’ve seen Mara. Each gives a different answer.

  “A tall laugh. Wears silver earrings?”
  “Maybe. I saw a woman like that near the coat rack.”
  “Name?”
  “They called her her,” the second says.
  “Not helpful,” the third says, but she is already nodding at someone behind you.

  You head for the coat rack. There are coats hanging that can’t exist in this room: a wool winter coat with pockets
  full of marbles, a yellow raincoat, an aviator jacket with a boarding pass stitched inside the collar, a tiny baby
  blanket folded like a military uniform. None belong here. One of them has your name on it in neat black ink.

  No one else seems to notice.

  ———

  In the hallway, the music drops to a low, distant hum and you realize the crowd thins into smaller circles, each lit
  by a different color. One hallway is sunlit, though the doors are closed. Another glows blue like an aquarium and
  smells faintly of cedar. Third is amber and warm, full of laughter. You pick amber, because that’s where people
  usually gather when they’re trying to belong.

  Inside, you find a circle of teenagers from your school, or maybe your past self, singing a melody you can’t identify
  but know by heart. They’re waiting for someone to arrive and clap rhythmically with their hands in a way you almost
  remember from childhood. One looks up, meets your eyes, and says your childhood nickname.
  You freeze.

  “Come in,” she says. “She’s expecting you.”
  “Who?”
  She points to a mirror.

  ———

  There’s a woman at the mirror, not looking at you yet. She has your friend’s haircut, maybe, or maybe she had that
  haircut once and you filled in the rest. She is standing with her back to the mirror, one hand on the edge of the
  frame as if trying not to lose balance on a ledge.

  “Too loud,” you say, trying to sound normal. “You look tired.”

  She turns, and her face is both familiar and impossible, because it includes lines of someone who has been waiting a
  long time.

  “Mara?” you ask.

  She smiles, but it’s careful, like she is trying on your name. “You found me.”

  You follow as she leads you out through a doorway. The music disappears behind you. A quieter room appears—small, with
  a round table, two chairs, and a single lamp with a warm shade shaped like a small moon. She sits, gestures for you to
  sit. In the pocket of her coat hangs a key: brass, old, with a notch you recognize from a locker you never had.

  She opens the folder on her lap, the one you had once promised to keep safe. It contains photographs of the two of you
  from places that never happened. Beaches, stations, a bridge under snow. In one, you’re both at the edge of a lake in
  late afternoon; in another, you’re older, older than you are now, both of you laughing under a paper lantern. A line
  of text under the last:
  If you can see me, you’re still in it. If you can’t, remember me anyway.

  “Why this?” you ask.

  “Because this is the only place things are where you left them,” she says.

  “Did you disappear,” you start, “or did I just miss you?”
  She tilts her head. “In dreams, disappearance is just the other person finishing the story they were in.”

  Outside the door, muffled voices rise as another announcement begins: Thank you everyone for coming to the ninety-
  third— and then a laugh rolls into the room like a wave.

  “What happens when I wake up?” you ask.

  She shuts the folder gently. “You keep asking after. You keep finding the groups, trying to decode who’s missing from
  which circle. But you won’t ask as hard as you think.”

  You look around the table. On it, a spoon, a folded napkin, a single blue scarf.

  Her hand reaches out and places the key in yours.
  “Take it. Open the door behind the ficus. Not in here.”

  When you step outside again, the ballroom is gone.

  You are in a narrow hallway of your old apartment building. The elevator door stands open, and beyond the glass is
  dawn, faint and gray. The ficus in your living room is still there. You check your coat rack. The coat with your name
  is gone.
  On the hallway floor, the blue scarf is folded once, with a small label tucked inside: You were never lost. You were
  early.
