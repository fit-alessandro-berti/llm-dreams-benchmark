You are in the middle of a heated argument with a close friend. The disagreement is about something important to both of you, and you decide to raise your voice—one last attempt to make them understand.

But the sound that comes out isn't your voice at all. It's a low, resonant hum, like a cello string vibrating in your chest. Your friend's mouth is moving, but their words have become subtitles scrolling across the air between you, translucent and pale blue. You reach out to touch one and it dissolves into a flock of tiny silver birds that scatter toward the ceiling.

The ceiling is no longer there.

You're both standing in a field of tall grass that wasn't there a moment ago. The argument isn't gone, but it has changed shape—it sits between you like a physical object now, a small dark stone on the ground. Your friend looks down at it. You look down at it. Neither of you wants to pick it up.

Your friend kneels and places their palm flat against the earth. The grass around them begins to glow faintly amber. They look up at you, and their eyes are wet, and you realize with a strange dream-clarity that the thing you were fighting about was never the thing you were actually fighting about. The real thing—some older hurt, some quiet fear—has been living underneath both of you this whole time, like groundwater.

You kneel across from them. You place your hand on the ground too. The stone between you warms. The field holds its breath.

And then your friend says your name—in their real voice, quiet and cracked—and you wake up with your heart pounding, not sure if you've lost something or just found it.
