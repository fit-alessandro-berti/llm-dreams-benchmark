You start to walk, hoping the right street will somehow recognize you.

The city feels half-awake. Tall glass buildings lean over narrow stone alleys, and the street signs keep changing languages when you look away. People pass you in slow motion, carrying briefcases, birds, umbrellas that drip sunlight.

You check your phone. The map opens, but instead of streets it shows a shifting maze. A small blinking dot—maybe you—moves on its own, sliding down corridors that don’t seem to exist.

You turn a corner and suddenly you’re inside a train station. You don’t remember entering. Trains arrive without sound. Each door opens to a different place: a forest, a classroom, a beach at night.

A conductor in a silver coat looks at you like he’s been expecting you.

“You’re late,” he says gently.

“For what?” you ask.

“For the meeting.”

He hands you a ticket with no destination printed on it. The train behind him breathes slowly, like a sleeping animal.

You step aboard, and as the doors close you notice something strange: everyone inside is quietly preparing notes, adjusting ties, rehearsing presentations.

And then you realize they all have your face.
