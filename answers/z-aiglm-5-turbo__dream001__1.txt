Panic flares in your chest—a heavy, buzzing sensation. You look up to ask a passerby for directions, but the people on the sidewalk are moving backward, their shoes clicking in reverse rhythm against the pavement. You spot a doorman standing perfectly still under a crimson awning. He is dressed in a Victorian tailcoat, but his face is completely smooth, like an unshaped lump of pale clay.

"Where is the Meridian Conference?" you ask, gasping. 

The clay-faced man doesn't speak. Instead, he raises a gloved hand and points down an alleyway that seems to stretch downward at a gravity-defying angle. 

You take the alley. The walls are lined with thousands of brass birdcages, all empty, clanking softly in a wind you can't feel. You know you are losing time. You check your wristwatch, but the numbers have been replaced by letters, spelling words that change every second: *SOON*, *LOST*, *FLY*, *LATE*.

The alley suddenly opens up into a massive, cavernous space that shouldn't fit inside a city block. It looks like a grandiose, decaying train station, but instead of trains on the tracks, there are enormous, sleeping whales suspended in shallow tanks of green water. 

A loud, distorted chime echoes through the space—the hour. You are late. 

You sprint across the polished marble floor, your shoes making no sound, toward a set of ornate double doors at the far end. You burst through them, expecting a boardroom full of angry executives. 

Instead, you step out onto a tightrope stretched across an endless, purple twilight sky. Sitting on a small velvet chair on the tightrope is a giant rabbit wearing thick-rimmed glasses. It holds a clipboard.

"You're late," the rabbit says, its voice sounding like a cello being tuned. 

"I couldn't find the building," you pant, balancing precariously as the wind picks up. "Where are we even meeting?"

The rabbit pushes its glasses up its snout and looks at you with deep, weary pity. "The meeting is inside you," it says. "You always forget that."

It stamps a heavy red "ABSENT" onto the clipboard. Suddenly, the tightrope snaps. You plummet through the purple sky, the wind rushing past your ears, growing louder and louder until it becomes the insistent, shrill cry of—

*Beep beep beep beep beep.*

Your eyes snap open. You are in your bed. Morning light is cutting through the blinds, and your alarm clock is screaming on the nightstand. The anxiety of the city is already fading, leaving only the strange, lingering residue of the rabbit’s disappointment.
