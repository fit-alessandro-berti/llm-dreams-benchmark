You find yourself in a post-apocalyptic world, with resources scarce and dangers everywhere. Despite the hardships, you decide to plant a garden.

It makes no sense, and somehow that's why it matters. You've found seeds in the pocket of an old coat—tomato, squash, something unlabeled—and in the cracked courtyard of a collapsed library, you begin clearing rubble with your bare hands.

Others notice. At first they watch from a distance, suspicious, the way everyone watches everyone now. But a girl with ash-streaked cheeks brings you a rusted watering can. An old man who hasn't spoken since the collapse kneels beside you and starts pulling weeds, as if his hands remember something his voice has forgotten.

The dream does that strange thing dreams do: time folds. Seasons pass in moments. The garden grows impossibly fast—vines climbing the library's broken pillars, green spilling over scorched concrete. People come from miles away, not for the food exactly, but to *see* it. To stand in front of something growing.

One night, raiders come. You've dreaded this. You stand at the garden's edge, heart pounding, holding nothing but a trowel. Their leader steps forward, and in the dim light you see his face change—he's staring past you at the tomatoes, red and absurd against all that gray. He whispers, "I'd forgotten what that color looked like."

He sits down in the dirt. The others follow.

In the final moments of the dream, the library behind you isn't ruined anymore—or maybe it is, but it doesn't matter, because someone is reading aloud from a salvaged book, and there are lanterns, and laughter, and the smell of soil and rain. You realize the apocalypse was never the end of the world in the dream's logic. It was a question: *what do you do when everything is taken away?*

And your answer was: *begin again, with seeds.*

You wake with dirt under your fingernails—or you dream you do.
