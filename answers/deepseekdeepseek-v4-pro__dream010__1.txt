...and the challenges become increasingly surreal and personal.

In the first round, I’m handed a pair of scissors and told to cut the perfect silhouette from a sheet of living shadow — it squirms and whispers forgotten memories as I trim, and when I hold it up to the light, it’s the exact outline of my childhood self, raising a hand to ask a question I never got to voice.

In the next, I’m dropped into a vast library where every book is a life I could have lived. The challenge: find the one volume that contains my truest calling, but the pages are all blank until I speak my own name aloud — not the one I was given, but the one I’ve secretly called myself in the mirror at 3 a.m. It takes me what feels like years, but when I finally whisper it, the ink rushes in like a flock of startled birds, forming a single sentence: *You already know the way.*

The final challenge is the strangest: I stand before a panel of judges who are all earlier versions of myself — at seven, seventeen, twenty-five, and last Tuesday. They don’t ask questions. They just watch, waiting. In their eyes, I see all my old doubts and discarded passions. I realize the competition isn’t about winning a job; it’s about winning back the parts of myself I left behind. So I do the only thing that makes sense in a dream — I step into the judges’ table and merge with them, becoming whole for the first time in years.

The grand prize isn’t announced with confetti or a contract. Instead, I wake up slowly, the morning light warm on my face, and I know exactly what I’m supposed to do with my day. The dream job was never out there — it was the one where I finally show up as all of myself.
