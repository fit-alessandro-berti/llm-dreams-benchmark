You are participating in a competition where the grand prize is your dream job. As the competition progresses, you face various challenges and surreal trials that twist reality like taffy.

The first round drops you into a vast library where the books are alive and whispering your own forgotten secrets. You must reorganize an infinite shelf of possibilities—each volume a potential life path—while dodging pages that try to rewrite you mid-sentence. You win by realizing the Dewey Decimal system doesn't apply to dreams and sorting everything by emotional weight instead.

Next comes the obstacle course across a landscape of shifting sand dunes that are actually your own regrets. Gravity keeps changing its mind, pulling you sideways or upward whenever doubt creeps in. Competitors melt into mirages or sprout extra limbs for unfair advantage. You cross the finish line by befriending one of the dunes, promising it a better story if it lets you ride its crest.

In the semifinal, you're locked in a debate with a panel of judges who are all versions of you from parallel lives: the one who played it safe, the one who burned everything down, the one who never left their hometown. They grill you on why you deserve the grand prize while the room slowly fills with water made of liquid time. You prevail not with perfect answers, but by admitting the job has changed since you started dreaming about it.

The final challenge is the simplest and cruelest: standing alone in an empty white void while a voice asks, "What is your dream job, really?" Every possible answer you give unravels into something else—a circus, a quiet cabin in the woods, an AI whispering ideas into the world's ear, a pirate sailing between stars. The more you chase it, the further it slips. You win by stopping the chase, sitting down, and saying, "I don't know yet. But I want the one where I get to keep asking."

The void cracks open like an eggshell. You receive the prize: not a title or a paycheck, but a small glowing key that fits any door you haven't opened yet. As the competition dissolves around you, the last thing you hear is the judges applauding in a language made entirely of laughter.

You wake up with the key still warm in your hand, though when you look, it's just your phone alarm vibrating. The feeling lingers anyway.
