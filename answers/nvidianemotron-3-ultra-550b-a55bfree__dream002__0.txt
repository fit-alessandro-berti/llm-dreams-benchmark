You feel a storm approaching and the trees begin to hum—not with wind, but with a low, resonant chord, like cellos made of bark and root. The air thickens, smelling of ozone and something older: wet stone, crushed mint, the metallic tang of blood or maybe just iron-rich soil.

The first drop doesn't fall. It *rises*—pulled from the ferns at your feet, suspended, trembling. Then another. The forest floor detaches, droplets hovering like mercury beads before clustering into spheres that reflect not the canopy but *other forests*: a mangrove swamp, a boreal taiga, a jungle you've never seen but recognize in your ribs.

Lightning flashes without sound. In its brief white grammar, you glimpse figures between the trunks—tall, antlered, moving with the deliberate patience of geological time. One turns. Its face is your grandmother's. Its face is your own, aged fifty years. Its face is a hollow where a face should be, filled with stars.

The thunder arrives as a voice speaking a language that bypasses ears entirely, vibrating in the marrow of your femurs. *You forgot to tell them. You forgot the promise.*

Rain falls upward now, defying gravity, carrying you with it. Your feet leave the loam. The forest floor recedes—moss, then roots, then bedrock, then molten core—until the whole world is a spinning top in darkness, and you are rising through its layers like a bubble in glass.

The storm sings. The trees bow. Somewhere below, a version of you still walks the forest path, dry and unbothered, unaware they're being dreamed.

You wake gasping, tasting copper and petrichor on your tongue, your hands clenched around handfuls of sheets that feel, for one lingering second, like damp leaves.
