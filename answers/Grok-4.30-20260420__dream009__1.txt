You wake up in a completely different era, with no modern technology around. As you explore your new surroundings, you realize you need to **adapt — fast** — or this dream might swallow you whole.

Your first breath tastes of woodsmoke, fresh manure, and baking rye bread. The air is thick with it. Your phone is gone. Your watch is gone. Even the faint hum of electricity that once lived in your bones has vanished, replaced by the rhythmic *clang-clang-clang* of a hammer on an anvil somewhere nearby. You’re standing in the middle of a narrow dirt lane lined with half-timbered houses whose windows glow with the soft flicker of rushlights and tallow candles. Chickens scatter at your feet. A woman in a faded wool kirtle stares at your bright white sneakers like they’re witchcraft.

You realize you need to **hide who you are**.

You duck into the shadow of a stable and quickly turn your hoodie inside out so the logo disappears. You stuff your keys and the single crumpled dollar bill in your pocket into a hollow tree trunk — useless here. A small boy with a wooden hoop and stick stops to watch you. When you smile and wave, he runs off shouting something in a language that sounds like old French… or maybe early English. Your heart races. *What year is this?*

You need to **find answers before nightfall**.

The village square opens before you: a stone cross, a well with a wooden bucket, merchants selling turnips and salted fish from carts. No one has ever seen anyone like you. Your clothes, your haircut, the way you walk — everything marks you as an outsider. A grizzled man with a missing ear and a rusty sword at his hip blocks your path. “Pilgrim?” he grunts, eyes narrowing at your strange shoes.

You realize you need to **become someone else**.

You swallow hard and answer in the simplest words you can manage: “Lost traveler… from far away.” He studies you for a long moment, then jerks his head toward a low tavern whose sign swings in the breeze — a painted goose with one eye. “Then you’ll need coin and a story that doesn’t get you hanged, stranger.”

Inside the tavern the air is warm and sour with ale. A fire crackles in the hearth. Men in leather jerkins and women with braided hair glance up from trenchers of stew. You sit on a rough bench, heart hammering, and listen. They speak of a “new king,” of border wars, of strange lights seen in the sky the night before you appeared. One old woman with milky eyes leans close and whispers, “The veil is thin tonight. Some say the fae drag souls through it when the moon is right.”

You realize you need to **decide** — play along and hunt for a way home, or surrender to the dream and see where it leads.

As the sun sets and the first stars prick the sky, you feel the pull of something vast and ancient calling your name. The firelight dances across faces that have never known electricity, never scrolled a screen, never dreamed of flying. Yet here you are — breathing their air, tasting their bread, carrying their future in your pocket like contraband.

You close your eyes for just a second…

…and the rooster crows.

You wake up in a completely different era, with no modern technology around.  

But this time, the dream smiles back.
