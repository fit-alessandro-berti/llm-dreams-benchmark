You are about to give a speech to a large audience. As you step onto the stage, you see the crowd looking expectantly at you, and you begin to speak—but the words coming out of your mouth aren't yours. They're in a language you've never heard, yet somehow you understand perfectly. 

The audience nods along, some taking notes, as if this is all perfectly normal. 

You glance down at your notes, but the pages are blank. You look back up and realize the audience is no longer people—now they're all versions of you, at different ages. The seven-year-old you swings their legs impatiently from the front row. The teenage you crosses their arms defensively in the back. An older you, with silver hair and kind lines around their eyes, smiles gently from the balcony.

And then you understand what the speech is. It's the one you've always needed to hear yourself say.

*"You don't have to be ready,"* you hear yourself say, and every version of you leans forward. *"You just have to keep going."*

The lights dim, and when they come back up, the seats are empty. Just you on the stage, a single spotlight, and the echo of words you didn't plan but somehow always knew.

You step off the stage, and the floor beneath your feet is no longer wood—it's grass. You're standing in a field you half-remember from childhood. Somewhere behind you, a door closes softly.

And then—*ring ring*—your alarm clock pulls you back, the dream already dissolving like morning frost, leaving only the faint feeling that something important was said, if only you could remember what.
