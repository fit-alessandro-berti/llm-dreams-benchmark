...written in a language you almost recognize.

At first, it looks like ordinary text: neat black ink, numbered paragraphs, boxes waiting to be filled. But when you lean closer, the letters rearrange themselves into memories.

**Question 1:** *Explain why you stopped drawing after age twelve.*

The classroom is suddenly too quiet. Around you, everyone else is writing furiously. Their pencils scratch like insects. You look down and realize you do not have a pencil. In your hand is a key.

The proctor walks past your desk. It is your third-grade teacher, except she is wearing your father’s glasses and your own winter coat.

“Begin,” she says, though everyone already has.

You turn the page.

**Question 2:** *Solve for the year you became someone else.*

The room tilts slightly. The desks slide a few inches to the left, but nobody reacts. Outside the window, instead of the campus lawn, there is a train station at night. People you once knew stand on the platform holding suitcases, but their faces blur whenever you try to remember their names.

You raise your hand.

“Yes?” asks the proctor.

“I think I studied the wrong material.”

She smiles sadly. “Everyone does.”

The clock above the chalkboard has no hands. Beneath it, someone has written in chalk:

**You may use everything you have forgotten.**

You look back at the exam. The key in your hand has begun to warm. On the next page, there is no question, only a small drawing of a door.

You press the key against the paper.

A lock appears.

The other students stop writing. One by one, they turn to watch you. Their faces are your face at different ages: six, thirteen, twenty-one, tomorrow. The youngest one gives you an encouraging nod.

You turn the key.

The paper-door opens inward, and behind it is not another room, but a wide field at dawn. The air smells like rain and pencil shavings. Far away, someone is calling your name—not the name people use now, but the one you had before you learned to answer quickly.

The proctor places a hand on your shoulder.

“This is the final question,” she says.

In the field, there is a path splitting in a hundred directions. At the start of each path stands a version of your future: doctor, artist, stranger, teacher, failure, parent, wanderer, nobody, legend. They all look impatient. They all look afraid.

On the exam paper, the final question writes itself:

**Choose the life that will forgive you.**

You look at the paths.

Then you look at the key.

For the first time, you understand that it was never meant to open a door.

It was meant to lock one.

So you close the exam. You stand up. The classroom gasps, but the sound becomes wind through grass. The walls fall away. The desks become stones. The clock becomes the sun.

You walk into the field without choosing a path.

Behind you, the exam folds itself into a white bird and lifts into the morning. It circles once above your head, then flies toward a horizon you cannot see.

When you wake, your heart is racing.

On your palm, where the key had been, there is a small dark mark shaped like a question mark.

And for the rest of the day, every door you pass seems to be waiting.
