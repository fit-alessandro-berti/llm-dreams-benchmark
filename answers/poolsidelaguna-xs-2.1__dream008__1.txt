You turn to face your boss, who is tapping a pen against a clipboard with unnerving impatience. The deadline pinned to the wall—a date circled in red—seems to pulse like a heartbeat. You open your mouth to ask for clarification, but the words dissolve into static.  

Suddenly, the office walls melt into a shifting kaleidoscope of spreadsheets and blueprints. Your colleagues freeze mid-sentence, their faces blank, as the air fills with the sound of a clock ticking backward. You grab your laptop, only to find it displaying a single message: *"Time is a construct. Begin."*  

Panic surges. You sprint toward the door, but it’s now a revolving vortex of office chairs. The floor gives way to an endless corridor lined with doors that each reveal a different version of your project—one complete, one crumbling, one on fire. You reach for a light switch, but the bulb in your hand explodes into a flock of paperclips.  

Then, a voice cuts through the chaos—yourself, from a future you, whispering, *"Breathe. The deadline isn’t real. Start where you are."*  

You snap awake. The office is silent. Your boss is still waiting, clipboard in hand, but the circled date has vanished from the wall. You pick up your pen, smile faintly, and begin writing.

*Dreams often mirror our fears of inadequacy or time pressure, but they also remind us that control can be reclaimed in small, intentional acts—like starting where you are.*
