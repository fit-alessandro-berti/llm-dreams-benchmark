As you trace your finger along one of the swirling lines, the wood doesn't feel like wood at all. It yields to your touch, warm and soft, pulsing gently against your fingerprint. 

You suddenly realize you don't need a key. The concept of a key feels absurd, like trying to smell a color. Instead, you simply *will* the box to open. 

The patterns on the lid begin to unspool. The intricate gold lines stretch and detach from the box, floating into the air like loose threads, pulling the dark wood apart with them. The box doesn't open; it unravels. 

But inside isn't a hollow space. Inside the box is a tiny, perfectly spherical storm. You hear the ocean crashing against rocks, though the sound is muffled, as if you are underwater. There is a flash of bright violet lightning, no bigger than a firefly, crackling silently in the center of the sphere. 

You lean closer, your reflection catching in the curved, impossible surface of the storm. But the reflection isn't yours. It’s a version of you with different eyes—maybe older, maybe from a life you haven't lived yet. The reflection smiles, not with a mouth, but with its entire face, and mouths a single word you can't hear over the phantom sound of the sea. 

You reach your hand into the storm. It doesn't feel wet or electric; it feels like slipping your hand into a pile of dry, warm autumn leaves. Your fingers brush against something hard and cold at the very bottom. You pull it out.

It is a single, perfect teardrop made of polished obsidian. But as it rests in your palm, it grows warm, then hot, then incredibly heavy—so heavy your arm begins to sink through the floor. The gold threads floating in the air suddenly snap back into place, the box reweaving itself around your sinking wrist, locking you to it. 

You look up. The room you are standing in is no longer a room. It is the inside of a giant clock, the pendulum swinging back and forth, slicing through the air with a deep, resonant *tick*. You try to call out, but when you open your mouth, a flock of white moths flies out instead. 

As the moths scatter into the gears, you wake up with the taste of salt on your lips, the image of the gold patterns still flashing behind your eyelids, slowly fading into the dark.
