You start to run.

The pavement beneath your feet feels wrong—too soft, like it's breathing. Your shoes sink slightly with each step, and the street signs overhead rearrange themselves when you're not looking, their letters sliding into new languages you almost recognize. "One hour," you mutter, checking your wrist. The watch face is now a tiny aquarium; tiny goldfish swim where the numbers should be, and the second hand is a miniature diver waving goodbye.

You flag down a taxi that isn't a car at all—it's a translucent bubble with wings made of old subway maps. The driver is a version of you from high school, wearing a nametag that says "ASK ME ANYTHING." You climb in.

"Where to?" the younger-you asks, voice echoing like it's coming from underwater.

"The important meeting," you say. "I don't know where it is."

The bubble lifts. Below, the city unfolds like a pop-up book written by someone who never finished the story. Entire neighborhoods fold away and reappear as forests of glass trees. People on the sidewalks have clocks for heads, all ticking at different speeds. One of them looks up and mouths, *You're already late for the part where you remember why you're here.*

The bubble pops without warning. You tumble onto a rooftop garden that smells like your grandmother's perfume and burnt toast. In the center stands a single door—no building attached, just a door floating three stories up. A sign above it reads, in your own handwriting: **THE MEETING IS IN HERE. BRING YOUR QUESTIONS.**

You hesitate. The hour is almost gone; you can feel it in your teeth. You push the door open anyway.

Inside is a room no larger than an elevator, and you're already in it. The other you—the one who knows—sits across a small table set for two. Between you: a single steaming cup of coffee and a clock that has stopped at the exact moment you fell asleep.

"You're early," the other you says, smiling with your mouth but someone else's eyes. "The meeting was never about finding the place. It was about noticing you were already walking toward it."

You sit. The coffee tastes like every good decision you almost made. Outside the floating door, the vast city folds itself into a single point of light and vanishes.

You wake up with the faint smell of coffee on your pillow and the sudden, ridiculous certainty that whatever today brings, you'll find the room.
