...walk away. But the ground beneath your feet is wrong—soft, like memory foam, and with each step it gives way just enough to make you feel like you're losing your balance without actually falling.

Your friend keeps talking, their mouth moving, but the words are arriving wrong. You hear them as if played backward, then sped up, then in a voice that belongs to someone else entirely—maybe your mother, maybe a teacher you had in third grade whose name you can't remember.

You turn to say something, to explain why you're leaving, and realize you're not in the room anymore. You're on a bridge. Below is not water but a dark field of something that might be wheat or might be hair. The wind is warm and smells like a summer you're not sure you actually lived.

Your friend is there too, still arguing, but now they're holding something out to you. A jar. Inside the jar is a small light—not fire, not electric—something that pulses like a heartbeat. They're saying you dropped it, that they've been carrying it for you, that this whole argument has actually been about that.

You reach for the jar.

Your fingers pass through it.

You wake up with your hand closed around nothing, and a feeling sitting in your chest like a stone dropped into still water—the ripples still moving, the reason for it already sinking out of reach.
