You wake up in a completely different era, with no modern technology around. As you explore your new surroundings, you realize you need to **figure out how to survive**.

At first it feels like a movie set—stone walls damp with mist, smoke curling from chimneys, the sharp smell of woodsmoke and animals layered over the dirt streets. But the reality settles quickly. No one looks at you like they’re curious; they look like you’re *unfinished*, like a person who might catch trouble just by existing.

You try the obvious things—your phone is gone, your watch is gone, even the idea of instant help is gone. You search your pockets anyway, half-hoping some leftover miracle survived the jump. Nothing. Just old cloth and a scrap of paper you don’t remember taking.

It’s a map, hand-drawn and oddly specific: a sketch of streets you can’t yet name, arrows pointing toward a building with a carved sign—**THE HALL OF LEDGERS**—and a note written beneath it in your handwriting:

*If you can’t buy food, learn to keep accounts. If you can’t keep accounts, learn to listen. If you can’t listen… find a way to speak.*

You don’t know why you have it, but you follow it.

---

The first challenge is food. You walk too confidently at first and a merchant notices your accent, your shoes, your lack of grime. People here know what strangers look like. You learn that quickly—one glance turns into a conversation you don’t understand, punctuated by pointing and gestures that have the sharpness of a warning.

So you stop trying to “pass” and start trying to “fit.”

You watch. You imitate. You learn the rhythm of bargaining: not the words, but the timing—how long someone hesitates before they say a number, how they soften their tone, how they use their hands to show what they mean. You don’t win right away, but you do manage to trade something small for something basic: a meal that’s mostly bread and stew.

It tastes like survival. It tastes like you’ve been given time.

---

The Hall of Ledgers turns out not to be a grand temple of knowledge, but a sturdy building with a worn wooden door and a bell that sounds too harsh for the peaceful street around it. Inside, there’s ink everywhere—on ledgers, on fingers, on the air itself. Men and women sit at long tables with quills, counting, checking, arguing quietly, and pretending not to stare.

You hover in the doorway until you’re noticed.

A clerk—an older woman with sharp eyes—looks you over and asks something you can’t fully understand. But she points to the scrap of paper in your hand.

You show it.

Her eyebrows lift. She doesn’t smile exactly—she studies the paper as if it might bite. Then, with a gesture, she ushers you closer.

“You,” she says slowly, using a few words you recognize from whatever language your brain has remembered from dreams. “Can you write?”

You answer too quickly and your accent betrays you again, but you hold out your fingers like proof. You sit at a table. A pen is placed in your hand.

The ink takes. The paper accepts you. For a moment you feel like you’ve found the lever that makes the world move.

You begin copying numbers you don’t fully understand but can still arrange. The clerk watches your accuracy. You’re careful. You double-check. You think about modern spreadsheets and how, even without devices, patterns still exist—columns, rows, totals, errors that always repeat in the same ways.

It’s astonishing how quickly your mind returns to the familiar logic of tasks you’ve done a thousand times: *verify, correct, don’t guess.*

The clerk nods once, hard enough to be an order.

“Not clean work,” she says. “But work.”

---

That’s when the second challenge reveals itself: you aren’t just surviving. You’re becoming useful, which means you’re now at risk.

The ledger hall isn’t only about bookkeeping. It’s about trust. People come here to settle debts, to dispute theft, to record taxes, to secure contracts. If your handwriting is better than the average scratch, you become valuable. If your knowledge is suspicious, you become dangerous.

That night, as you copy figures by candlelight until your eyes sting, the clerk pulls you aside.

She speaks in clipped sentences, mixing formal phrases with something sharper beneath. You catch enough to understand the gist: there’s a problem in the records—a mismatch that someone wants hidden. Someone has been altering entries. Someone is using paperwork like a weapon.

And because you arrived out of nowhere—because you can write quickly, because you ask fewer questions than other novices—they assume you’re either part of the solution…

…or part of the cover-up.

The Hall of Ledgers is suddenly the center of a storm.

---

You need to decide what kind of person you’ll be in this era: a survivor, a tool, or a lever.

You could play along with whatever story they believe about you and hope it keeps you fed. You could try to hide your “future” instincts and become a background figure.

Or you can do what you always do when you recognize a pattern: you look for the flaw.

You start to compare dates. You note which pages have been re-inked. You test whether the ink stains differently with age. You notice something you shouldn’t be able to notice—because your brain, even without technology, is trained to see inconsistencies.

This is where you realize the key isn’t just learning to live.

It’s learning to **protect yourself by understanding the system you’re trapped in**.

---

So you choose the third path: you gather proof.

You do it quietly, in pieces, using only what’s available—scraps of paper, memory, careful copying. You memorize totals before you copy them. If someone changes a number later, you’ll know.

The next day, a man arrives wearing fine fabric and a confident smile. He speaks to the clerk like someone used to getting answers without waiting.

He wants you to “fix” a discrepancy.

He slides a page across the table and points to the line where the math doesn’t add up. “It’s wrong,” he says, and his tone implies that *wrongness* is something you can erase.

Your fingers hover over the quill.

Your heart pounds.

This is the moment the era tests you. Not with monsters or magic, but with temptation and fear—the simplest kinds of control.

You remember your scrap of paper’s last line:

*If you can’t listen… find a way to speak.*

So you do.

You ask a question—not an accusation, not a challenge. Something small, careful, and reasonable. You frame it like procedure. Like you’re simply ensuring correctness.

The man’s smile tightens.

The clerk watches you closely, realizing you aren’t blindly obedient.

And in that tension you feel it—the thin wire between safety and disaster.

---

He tries again, more politely this time. Offers. Flattery. A promise that you’ll never go hungry again.

You accept nothing.

Instead, you keep working, slowly, meticulously, rewriting only what you can verify. You don’t make the mistake he wants. You don’t “help” too quickly. You force the process to be honest in a place where honesty is inconvenient.

Then, just as the candlelight begins to sink, the clerk—sharp-eyed, now impressed instead of suspicious—orders a check of related entries.

The discrepancy isn’t fixed. It’s exposed.

The man’s confidence cracks.

And now the question becomes: exposed for whom?

Not for him.

For you.

Because once people realize you can’t be bought, they start to wonder where you learned to see through lies.

---

By the time the building empties, you’re exhausted, but you aren’t helpless anymore. You have proof. You have patterns. You have a single ally—the clerk, who finally believes you’re not just another confused stranger.

She tells you, in a low voice, what happens next:

“Tonight, someone will try to remove you from the story,” she says.

“Remove… me?”

She nods. “A person disappears here the way ink smears on cloth. Quietly. Like it was never there.”

You think of how modern technology would have solved this instantly—recordings, backups, witnesses. But none of that exists here. Only people. Only timing. Only what you can carry in your head and protect with your hands.

So you decide.

You don’t run randomly. You don’t beg. You don’t wait.

You turn your survival instinct into strategy.

You gather the proof page into a hidden place, then you do the only thing you can do in an era where words are currency:

You make yourself a *necessary problem*.

---

When you step outside into the night, the sky is darker than you expect and the streets are quieter, like the town is holding its breath. Somewhere, far off, a bell rings once—one of those signals you can’t yet interpret, but your body understands as urgency.

You move toward the map’s next arrow, back behind the market stalls where the poorest people trade by lamplight. If you can’t trust the powerful, you trust the ones who have less to lose.

There, you find a group of apprentices and scribes who know each other’s work, who notice when someone’s book changes. You show them the proof—careful, in fragments—until their anger turns into purpose.

You tell them the truth as far as you can without revealing impossible details:

“Someone’s been altering records. They’ll come for anyone who can see it.”

They believe you quickly—not because you’re convincing, but because they’ve always known someone gets away with it.

What they don’t know is *who will guide them*.

So you guide them.

---

By dawn, the storm has a shape.

The man who tried to buy your ink is arrested—not dramatically, not with handcuffs and shouting, but through the slow leverage of community and paperwork and witnesses finally aligned. In this era, justice is a chain of signatures.

And your name is suddenly connected to the chain.

You are not free yet. You are not safe yet. You are simply no longer alone.

The clerk meets you near the Hall’s doorway and hands you a key.

It’s small, heavy, and real.

“You can keep things here,” she says. “Important things.”

You stare at it, then at her, and you realize something about the dream you didn’t expect to learn so fast:

You didn’t wake up here by accident.

You woke up because you *have a job to do*.

You can survive the era by learning its rules—but you can change it by understanding where those rules are breaking.

---

You take the key.

You take the proof.

And you realize you need to **choose your next move carefully**—because now that you’ve interfered with power, the future you came from has less to offer, and the present you’re living has more stakes than you could have guessed.

So the dream doesn’t end with safety.

It ends with a plan:

**Find out who altered the records before the man—because the real thief is never the one they blame first.**
