You find yourself in a post-apocalyptic world, with resources scarce and dangers everywhere. Despite the hardships, you decide to plant a garden.

It doesn't make sense, not at first. The soil is gray and tired. Water is something you ration by the capful. But you'd found a tin of seeds in the glovebox of a rusted-out car—tomatoes, maybe, or beans, the label long faded—and something about holding them made your hands shake.

So you clear a patch behind the collapsed gas station. You haul water from the cistern you don't tell anyone about. You kneel in the dirt and press the seeds down with your thumb, one by one, like you're tucking them in.

The others think you've lost it. *You can't eat hope*, the tall one says, the one who carries the rifle and counts every bullet twice. But she keeps watch while you dig, anyway, and she doesn't have to.

In the dream-logic way of things, the garden grows overnight and over years all at once. Green shoots fist their way up through the ash. You weep over the first true leaf. The sky, which had been the color of an old bruise, lightens at its edges—

And then there's a moment, just before waking, where you understand something you can't quite hold onto afterward: that the apocalypse wasn't the end of the world. It was just the part where you finally found out what you'd do anyway, when everything else was stripped away.

You'd plant something.

You'd wait for it to grow.
