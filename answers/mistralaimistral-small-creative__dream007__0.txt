The air between you and your friend hums with static, like a radio tuned between stations. Your voices rise, but suddenly, the words *stop making sense*—not because you’re shouting over each other, but because the syllables stretch and warp, dissolving into colors. Your friend’s mouth moves, but what emerges is a swarm of fireflies, their golden light pulsing in time with their frustration. You reach out to grab their arm, but your hand passes *through* them like mist. **"Wait—!"** you cry, but your voice is a chorus of overlapping echoes, as if you’re speaking from every angle of the room at once.

The argument isn’t about the original topic anymore. It’s about *the fireflies*. Your friend insists they’re *their* fireflies, a gift from a childhood they never told you about—a summer spent in a house by a lake where the trees glowed at dusk. You counter that the fireflies are *yours*, too, because you once saved one from a spiderweb in a dream *just like this*. The friend’s eyes widen. **"That wasn’t a dream,"** they say, but their face is now half-shadow, half-moonlight, and their voice layers into something deeper, like two people speaking at once.

The room tilts. The walls breathe. The argument spirals into a physical tug-of-war with the fireflies, but every time you catch one, it dissolves into a memory: a laughter-filled car ride, a shared secret, a time you let them down. The friend’s hands are suddenly *your* hands, and you’re both pulling against yourselves. **"We’re the same person,"** you realize aloud, but the friend shakes their head—now they’re wearing *your* face, but their eyes are ancient, like a stranger’s. **"No,"** they say, **"we’re the parts of you that don’t speak to each other."**

The fireflies surge upward, forming a constellation. The room dissolves into the night sky, and you’re floating, weightless, the argument now a silent pull between two stars. Your friend is a voice in the dark: **"You always do this—turn fights into metaphors."** You laugh, but it’s a sob. **"Only in dreams,"** you say. Then the stars *blink*, and you’re back in the room, but it’s morning. Your friend is asleep on the couch, snoring lightly. The air smells like rain. On the coffee table, a single firefly rests in a glass jar, its light flickering weakly.

You pick it up. It’s warm. When you unscrew the lid, it doesn’t fly away—it *melts* into your palm, leaving behind a single word written in light on your skin: **"Remember."**
