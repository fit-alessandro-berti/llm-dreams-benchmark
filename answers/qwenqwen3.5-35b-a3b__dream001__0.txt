You start to run, but your feet touch down only for a moment before the gravity beneath you feels like it’s pulling you sideways, like you're drifting across the surface of a pond. The streetlamps don't hum; they whisper coordinates in a language you almost recognize, and the buildings above tower so high their tops disappear into a sky that is less blue and more the color of static noise.

You ask a man standing by a newsstand, but when he turns, he has no face—only a smooth, shifting texture of polished marble that reflects the street behind him. He points toward a subway entrance, but the stairs keep getting longer, stretching into an infinite staircase that spirals into a dark abyss.

You check your watch again. It’s not ticking; it’s melting, dripping onto your wrist like golden wax. The hour you were given is evaporating into the humidity of the air. Panic rises in your throat, but as you do, the city around you begins to dissolve into watercolors, the brick and steel bleeding into soft strokes of gray and indigo.

You realize the subway doors are opening in your hand. You step through, expecting a train, but you step directly into a quiet, white room. There is no conference table. There is no boss. There is only a mirror.

In the reflection, you are sitting in your own bed, eyes closed. The alarm is ringing, sharp and jarring, the sound of a physical world crashing back into your mind. You wake up gasping, the dream still clinging to the edge of your vision like mist. The clock on the nightstand reads 7:00 AM. You have exactly one hour to get to the office, and for a terrifying moment, you aren't sure which side of the mirror you are on.
