The microphone in my hand isn’t metal—it’s a smooth, warm obsidian stone pulsing faintly with light.

My voice doesn’t come from my throat. It echoes from everywhere at once—behind me in the darkness, beneath the stage, and from the very air. I speak not words, but memories: the smell of rain on pavement, the weight of a father’s hand on my shoulder, the moment I first understood silence. The audience doesn’t clap. They *remember*. Tears fall from their eyes—not out of sadness, but recognition. One woman stands, not to cheer, but to whisper to the stars, “I forgot I missed that.”

And then, the stage begins to rise—not up, but *into* the sky. The crowd isn’t below anymore; they’re floating with me, tethered not by gravity but by shared breath. We are no longer speaking to each other—we are speaking *through* each other, each syllable a bridge between souls. My speech has become a lullaby for the earth, a reminder of how fragile we are, how beautiful.

I don’t remember the end. I only remember the silence that followed—and the sudden, soft *click* of a door opening in the distance.

And then I wake, heart pounding, the taste of starlight still on my tongue.
