You stop a passerby to ask for help, and it's your third-grade teacher, wearing a postal uniform. She tells you the meeting was moved to yesterday. "You should have been there," she says, not unkindly, and hands you a small paper bag. Inside is a single key and a sandwich you remember from childhood.

You check your phone, but the screen shows only the weather in cities you've never visited. It's going to rain in all of them.

The buildings begin to lean in slightly, the way interested listeners do. You have the distinct feeling they know where the meeting is but have agreed among themselves not to say.

Then—relief—you remember: it's in the tall building. Of course. The tall one. You look up, and every building is tall. You look down, and you're wearing only one shoe, and somehow this is now the most urgent problem of all.

A taxi pulls over before you even raise your hand. The driver is your father, or a dog, or both, in a way that doesn't seem strange. "I know where it is," the driver says, and you believe it completely. The meter runs backwards. The city unfolds past the window like a map being uncrumpled, and for a moment you feel perfectly calm.

You arrive. The building is your childhood home, except with a revolving door. Through the glass you can see the conference room. Everyone is already seated around the table, and they are all you—at different ages—and the youngest one looks up and says—
