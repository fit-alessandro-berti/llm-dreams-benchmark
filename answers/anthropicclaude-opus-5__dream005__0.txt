You are at a large social gathering where you only know one person. As you try to find them, you encounter various groups of people and each group has clearly been talking for hours before you arrived. This is the first thing you understand: you are late in a way that cannot be fixed.

The house is bigger from the inside. You knew this would happen. Every room opens onto two more rooms, and the light in each is a different hour of the day — noon in the kitchen, dusk in the long hallway, some blue four-in-the-morning light in the room where a woman is arranging shoes in a perfect spiral on the floor. You ask her if she's seen your friend. She says, *He was just here*, and gestures at the spiral, as though your friend has been converted into footwear, or as though the spiral is a map, which — you lean closer — it might be.

In the next room the conversation stops when you enter, then resumes, and it resumes *about you*, but generously, the way people discuss weather they're fond of. Someone hands you a drink that is warm and tastes like the smell of a library. A man in a beautiful coat asks what you do.

You open your mouth and hear yourself say, *I make the small decisions that hold up the large ones.*

He nods like this is a real job. He tells you he does something similar, but for bridges.

You keep moving. You have the sense that your friend is always in the room you just left, that the two of you are orbiting the same point at the same speed, one hundred and eighty degrees apart, and that this could go on forever without either of you doing anything wrong. In the dream this doesn't feel tragic. It feels like a rule of physics you're too polite to complain about.

On a staircase, three people are laughing at something. As you pass, one of them touches your sleeve and says, *You were wonderful earlier.* And here is the strange warmth of it: you believe her. You have no memory of earlier. But you find you're willing to be a person who was wonderful, and you carry that up the stairs like a lit candle you're shielding with one hand.

The upstairs is all coats. A whole floor of coats on beds, dozens of beds, and you understand that everyone at this party intends to leave eventually, that the coats are a kind of promise. You look for your friend's coat. You can't remember what it looks like. You can't remember, actually, what your friend looks like, only the specific feeling of being understood by them — a feeling like a room you could sit down in — and you think: *that's fine, that's enough, I'll recognize the feeling when I walk into it.*

Someone is playing piano badly and beautifully. The party has thinned. The rooms are folding up behind you, politely, like ushers stacking chairs.

And then in the garden, or the place where the garden is agreed to be, you find them. They're sitting on a low wall, and they say, without turning around, *There you are. I've been telling everyone about you.*

You want to ask what you've been. What version of you has been circulating all evening in your absence, shaking hands, being wonderful, doing whatever it is you do. But you sit down instead, on the cold stone, in the dark, and your friend goes on talking about nothing, about a dog they saw, and this — you notice with the enormous clarity that only ever happens in dreams — this is the thing you came for. Not to be found. To be *already accounted for.* To have been spoken well of in rooms you never entered.

The piano stops.

Somewhere below you, a woman finishes her spiral of shoes, steps back, and it is a map, and it says: *you are here, you are here, you are here.*
