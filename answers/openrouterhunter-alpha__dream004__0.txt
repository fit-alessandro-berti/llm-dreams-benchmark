...and beside it, a single brush resting on a small stone. The brush handle is warm — almost alive — humming with a quiet vibration that travels up my wrist.

I pick it up, and the moment my fingers close around it, the blank canvas begins to breathe. It inhales and exhales slowly, the fabric tightening and loosening like skin. I realize it isn't waiting for paint. It's waiting for *intention*.

So I think of something simple — a circle — and when the brush touches the surface, a circle appears, but not flat. It's a hole. Through it, I can see another layer of the world beneath this one, like looking through a skylight into a place where the ground is made of sound and the sky tastes like copper.

I paint a line, and it becomes a bridge — not solid, but made of memory. I step onto it, and suddenly I'm remembering a conversation I've never had, with someone whose face keeps shifting between familiar and impossible. They're laughing. They say, "You always come back here. Don't you get tired of starting over?"

I want to answer, but the brush is pulling my hand again. The canvas has expanded — it's grown tall as a building now, and I'm standing at its base, dwarfed. The colors from the shifting world are bleeding into it on their own: triangles melting into deep amber, spirals cooling into a blue so quiet it almost disappears.

I paint one more thing — a door. It opens inward, and behind it there's nothing. Not darkness. Not light. Just the feeling of a question I haven't thought of yet.

I step through.

And then I'm standing in a room I've never seen, but it smells like rain on warm pavement, and someone has left a cup of tea on the table that's still steaming. The brush is gone. The canvas is a window on the wall, showing the world I just left — still shifting, still alive.

The tea is exactly the right temperature.

I wake up reaching for it.
