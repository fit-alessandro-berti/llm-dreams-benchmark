They don't ask you where you need to go. They just fall into step beside you — an older woman with laughing eyes and a shopping bag full of oranges, a teenager with headphones around his neck, a man in a paint-splattered apron.

The city is still bustling, still loud. Neon signs buzzing in the rain, bicycles rattling over cobblestones, a thousand conversations happening at once. But it doesn't feel like it's pushing you around anymore. It feels like it's moving *with* you.

"You're not lost, honey," the woman says, and presses a warm orange into your hands. "Everyone just takes the long way sometimes."

The teenager shows you a shortcut through an alley you would have sworn was a dead end yesterday. Inside, the walls are covered in glow-in-the-dark stars and notes people have left for each other. *You got this. Keep going. We were here too.*

The painter man opens a door at the end of the alley — just a small, ordinary blue door — and beyond it you can see exactly where you need to be. It isn't an address on a map. It's a small kitchen table with light spilling over it. It's a feeling of home. It's familiar, even though you've never seen it before.

You look back to thank them, but they're already waving, already turning back into the crowd to help someone else.

And you step forward, holding the warm orange, knowing exactly where to go.
