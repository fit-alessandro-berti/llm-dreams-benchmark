...and with every round, the boundaries of the arena begin to warp. 

At first, the tasks make sense. I am standing in a gleaming, endless skyscraper made of polished obsidian and brass, asked to design a city that breathes. My fingers move across air, leaving trails of glowing blueprint lines. But as I look around at my competitors, I realize their faces are blurred, like watercolors caught in the rain. Every time I try to focus on one of them, they speak with my own voice, echoing my deepest insecurities: *“Are you sure you belong here?”*

The second round drops us without warning into a silent, velvet-black ocean. The water is warm, smelling faintly of old books and rain. The challenge isn't to swim, but to listen. Deep below, a hum vibrations through my chest, and I realize the "dream job" isn't a title or a salary—it is the role of the Architect of Quiet Thoughts, responsible for weaving the dreams of others while they sleep. To win, I must catch a falling star before it hits the water and extinguishes.

I dive. The ocean turns into a dense, twilight forest of giant clock towers. Ticking echoes everywhere. I sprint past grandfather clocks whose hands spin backward, vaulting over pendulum branches. My lungs don't burn; in fact, I don't need to breathe at all. Up ahead, resting on a pedestal of woven sunlight, is the final objective: an envelope sealed with wax that constantly changes color.

I am the first to reach it. My fingers touch the cool wax. 

A panel of judges appears—figures draped in robes woven from starlight and morning mist. The head judge steps forward. They have no face, only a mirror where their features should be. As I look into it, I see myself, not as I am, but as I want to be: entirely at peace, unfettered, and fulfilled.

"You have passed every test," the Judge says, their voice soundless yet reverberating through my bone marrow. "You have proven you can build from nothing, navigate the dark, and outrun time itself. The position is yours. But tell us... what is the salary you require?"

I open my mouth to answer, but instead of words, a soft, golden light spills out. 

The forest begins to dissolve into pale pinks and yellows. The ticking of the clock towers slows down, morphing into a rhythmic, distant sound—a bird chirping outside my bedroom window, or perhaps the faint, rising beep of an alarm clock on my nightstand.

The envelope in my hand turns to warm sand, filtering through my fingers. I try to hold onto it, to claim the title, but the realization settles over me with a gentle, floating weight: *I was never competing against anyone else. I was just remembering who I am.*

The dream recedes like a tide, leaving me suspended in that peaceful, weightless second right before I open my eyes to the morning.
