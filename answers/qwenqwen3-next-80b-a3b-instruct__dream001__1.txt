You start to run—though “run” isn’t quite right. Your feet barely touch the ground; each step launches you forward like you’re gliding over liquid glass. The city unfolds around you in impossible architecture: towers twist like doubloons spun by giants, bridges arc between buildings that don’t align with gravity, and streets spiral upward into clouds that hum with forgotten lullabies.

A street vendor calls out, “Time’s pastry! Freshly baked in yesterday’s minutes!” He holds out a croissant shaped like an hourglass, golden and steaming. You grab it without thinking. The moment your teeth pierce the crust, a voice echoes—not from the vendor, but from inside your skull:

*“The meeting is where the silence remembers your name.”*

You stop. The city freezes. Birds hang mid-flight. Raindrops hover like suspended beads. Then—a whisper from a nearby alley, where a mirror hangs on a wall that shouldn’t exist, its frame woven from old letters and broken watches.

You step toward it.

The mirror doesn’t reflect your face. Instead, it shows you as you were at seven years old, sitting cross-legged on the floor of your childhood bedroom, holding a paper airplane you’d folded a hundred times but never launched.

“I’m late,” you whisper to the reflection.

The child looks up, smiles faintly, and says: “You were never late. You just forgot to show up for yourself.”

The mirror shatters.

And the city breathes.

All at once, the clock towers chime—not with bells, but with the sound of your own heartbeat, synchronized across every spire. The streets unfold into a single path, lined with doors labeled only with smells: burnt toast, rain on concrete, the sharp tang of a cracked pencil, your mother’s perfume.

You follow the scent of lavender and old paper.

At the end, a plain wooden door. No handle. Just a keyhole shaped like a question mark.

You reach into your pocket. Inside, you find not a key, but the paper airplane from your childhood. You slide it gently into the keyhole.

The door opens.

Inside, the room is empty except for a chair. On the chair: a single envelope.

You open it.

It’s blank.

But the moment you touch the page, words bloom like ink in water:

> “Welcome. You’re exactly on time.  
> The meeting was always with you.”

You sit.

The dream exhales.

And you wake up… but not to your alarm.

To the sound of a distant, familiar laugh.

And for the first time in years—you remember how to smile without wondering why.
