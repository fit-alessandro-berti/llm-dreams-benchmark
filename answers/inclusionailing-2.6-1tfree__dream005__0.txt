The chandeliers hum, not with electricity, but with the vibration of too many voices compressed into velvet air. I am adrift in a tide of silk and unfamiliar colognes, armed only with the half-remembered instruction: *Find Elias. He’s wearing the blue scarf.*

I drift past the first orbit—a cluster of astronomers arguing over the color of dark matter. They are gesturing wildly with flutes of champagne, tracing invisible graphs in the frosty air. One woman leans in close, her eyes reflecting a nebula I cannot name, and whispers, “Gravity is just the universe remembering how to be lonely.” I nod, though I don’t understand, and move on before the loneliness becomes contagious.

Next, I round a corner into a courtyard strung with paper lanterns that pulse like jellyfish. A group of musicians is playing instruments made of glass and hollowed bone. The music doesn’t travel through the air; it travels through the floor, up through the soles of my shoes, vibrating in my molars. They are laughing, and their laughter has the same crystalline pitch as their instruments. For a moment, I am afraid that if I stay too long, my own bones will begin to hum, and I will forget how to speak in words, only in tones.

I slip away, following the scent of crushed mint and old books.

In the library—impossibly located in the middle of the ballroom—I find a circle of historians debating the invention of silence. They wear waistcoats embroidered with dates and look at me with the pity of people who know exactly how the night will end. “You’re looking for someone,” one says, not looking up from his scroll. “Don’t. Every time you find a person, you lose a version of yourself you didn’t know you were carrying.” I touch my chest, wondering which version is already gone.

Through the French doors, I see a crowd gathered around a magician who is pulling colors out of the air and bottling them. The bottles glow with contained sunsets, trapped moonrises. People are bidding for them with stories instead of money. I watch a man trade his first heartbreak for a vial of dawn, and his face smooths into something unbearably young. I wonder what I would have to give up to buy a way out.

Then, above the hubbub, a clock chimes—not the hour, but a name. *Elias.*

I push through a hedge of whispering ferns and find him at the edge of the terrace, leaning on the marble balustrade. He is not wearing the blue scarf. Instead, his coat is unbuttoned, his hair ruffled by a wind that doesn’t touch the rest of us. He turns, and his smile is the relief of a door opening after a long flight of stairs.

“You’re late,” he says, though I am not sure to what.

“I got distracted,” I say. “By the dark matter, the glass instruments, the silence…”

Elias laughs, and the sound unknots the tension in my shoulders. “Come here,” he says, taking my hand. “The party is only a dream anyway. But while we’re in it—” He pulls me toward the railing, where the city below is made of honey and starlight, flowing like a slow river. “—we might as well fall.”

I look down, and the drop doesn’t look like an ending. It looks like a conversation.

“Hold on,” he says, and we step off the edge.
