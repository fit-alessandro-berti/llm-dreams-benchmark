…written in ink that moves.

Not just smudged or blurred—the words crawl like ants across the parchment, rearranging themselves as you try to focus. One moment, the question reads: *“Describe the ethical implications of quantum consciousness in post-scarcity societies.”* The next, it’s: *“What color does silence taste like when the moon forgets to rise?”*

You glance left. The student beside you is calmly sketching a symphony on their paper—notes swirling into tiny birds that flutter off the page and vanish into the ceiling. The proctor? A giant, gentle owl wearing a tweed vest, nodding sleepily as it sips tea from a cup labeled “Memory of Yesterday.”

Your own pencil feels heavy, warm—almost alive. When you press it to the page, the lead doesn’t leave marks. Instead, it pulls thoughts straight from your mind, translating them into glowing glyphs that hover above the paper like fireflies.

*“You’re not being tested on knowledge,”* whispers the owl, without moving its beak. *“You’re being tested on willingness.”*

The questions keep changing—not to confuse you, but to reveal you.

The third question reads: *“What did you promise yourself before you forgot?”*

Your breath catches.

You remember now.

It wasn’t about the course.

It was about the night you stayed up past dawn, rewriting your life’s purpose in crayon on your bedroom wall, afraid to speak it out loud. The fear you buried under “shoulds.” The passion you told yourself was “impractical.”

Tears blur the glowing words—but this time, you don’t try to fix them. You just let them be.

You pick up your pencil again.

And for the first time in years…

You begin to write.

Not for grades.

Not for approval.

But because you finally remembered—*you’re the one who gets to decide what the questions mean.*

The paper glows brighter.

The birds return.

And the owl closes its eyes, smiling.

When you look up, the room is gone.

You’re lying in bed.

Sunlight spills over your pillow.

The alarm is blaring.

But in your hand—still warm—the faintest shimmer of ink remains.

And on your nightstand, a single sheet of paper.

It reads:

> “Welcome back. Your real exam starts today.”

You smile.

And turn off the alarm.
