… **not written in English, nor in any language I recognize, but in shifting, geometric patterns that seem to crawl toward the center of the page.**

As I lean in closer to decipher them, the symbols rearrange themselves into a picture of a door—a simple, white door that looks exactly like the one to my childhood bedroom. A cold sweat breaks out across my back. I know instinctively that to pass this exam, I have to open that door, but I also know—with the unshakable certainty of dream-logic—that if I do, something terrible will get out.

I look up to ask the professor for clarification, but the rows of desks have vanished. I am no longer sitting in an exam hall; I am floating in the middle of a vast, dark ocean, and my desk is a tiny speck of driftwood beneath me.

I look down at the paper again. The "questions" have changed again. They are now mirrors. Each rectangular box reflects a version of myself that chose a different path: one where I became a painter, one where I stayed in that bad relationship, one where I learned to play the cello. All of them are staring up at me from the page, looking disappointed.

I grab my pen to write an answer, but my hand passes right through the pen as if it were smoke. That’s when the panic truly sets in—not because I can’t hold the pen, but because I realize I have been writing this exam for years, and I haven't even finished the first sentence.

A loud bell begins to ring. It doesn't sound like a school bell; it sounds like the tolling of a massive iron church bell underwater. The waves rise around my desk. The paper dissolves into ink-black water, washing away the disappointed reflections of myself.

Just as the water reaches my chin, I wake up, heart pounding, clutching my blanket like a lifeline.
