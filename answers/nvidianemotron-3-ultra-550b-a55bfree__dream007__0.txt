...walk away. Not storm off — just turn and walk, your feet knowing a path your head hasn't caught up to yet.

The street you find yourself on doesn't match the neighborhood you were standing in. The houses lean toward each other across the street, roof peaks nearly kissing, and the windows are wrong — too many, too tall, glowing with light that pulses in time with something you can't hear. Your friend doesn't follow. You don't look back to check.

Ahead, the street ends at a body of water. Lake, ocean, something vast and dark. There's a dock, and tied to it, a small wooden boat. No oars. No motor. Just a single white sail folded neat as a letter.

You get in. The boat accepts your weight without rocking.

The sail unfurls itself.

Wind comes from nowhere — or from the argument, maybe, all that hot air finally useful — and you move across water so smooth it reflects not the sky but *memories*: the birthday party where they knew exactly what you needed before you said it. The night you sat in the ER waiting room together. The time they showed up at your door with soup and a terrible movie and didn't ask why you'd been crying.

The water shows you these things in reverse order, moving backward through years.

You're crying. The dream lets you notice this without shame.

On the shore behind you, a figure stands at the end of the dock. Too far to see their face. They raise a hand — waving, or maybe reaching, or maybe just shielding their eyes against the light.

You realize you still have your phone in your pocket. It buzzes once. Twice. The screen, when you pull it out, shows a text from them that hasn't been sent yet.

*I'm sorry. Can we —*

The boat drifts into fog. The shore vanishes. The water goes perfectly still.

You wake up with your hand reaching for the nightstand, fingers closing on nothing but cool air and the weight of words you didn't get to say.
