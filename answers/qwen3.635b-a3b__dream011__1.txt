...written in light that pulses like a slow breath. They don't sit on the page; they hover just above it, letters drifting and reconfiguring like school of silver fish. Instead of multiple choice or essay prompts, they ask you things that feel less like questions and more like memories you haven't made yet:

*What color is the path you're afraid to walk?*
*Whose voice do you hear when you choose yourself?*
*What happens when you stop running the simulation and step outside it?*

Your pen feels like a feather dipped in rainwater. The first letter you write turns the desk into soil. The exam hall walls begin to soften, peeling back like wallpaper in an old house, revealing doorways that aren't there one moment and are heavy with wood and brass the next. The proctor never was a person. It was a metronome on a velvet stand, ticking not in seconds, but in heartbeats. You realize you're not solving the exam. You're listening to it.

Each answer you give doesn't go on paper—it goes on your pulse. The room breathes with you. The sky through the high windows shifts from slate to apricot to deep indigo as the hours stretch, fold, and unspool. You try to write a definitive line, and it blooms into a vine that curls around your wrist, leaving behind a mark like a compass rose. You don't need to finish. You need to notice. Notice how the questions were never about picking the right path, but about noticing which footsteps feel like yours.

At last, the paper dissolves into a shallow pool. Beneath it, instead of a grade, is a reflection: you, older, standing in a doorway you've already walked through, hands resting on a table you've already helped build. The dream doesn't end with a score or a bell. It ends with the quiet click of a locket closing, a sigh that isn't relief but recognition, and the gentle sensation of waking up not after the test, but after the choice.
