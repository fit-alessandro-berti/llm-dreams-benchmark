Your hands find the keyboard, but the keys are soft and warm, like bread dough. The deadline glows on the screen—**“Due: five minutes ago”—**and the progress bar is somehow *less* full than when you started.

Your boss hovers at the edge of your cubicle, except now the cubicle walls are taller than skyscrapers, and the fluorescent lights above have turned into a slow, pulsing aurora. “I need those quarterly projections,” your boss says, but their voice comes from the potted plant on your desk, which has grown to the size of a redwood and is now wearing a tie.

You open the spreadsheet. The numbers are crawling, tiny beetles rearranging themselves into hieroglyphs you almost understand. Every time you type a cell, your chair rolls backward three feet and the floor tilts like a ship. You save the file, but it saves as a sigh, then as a watercolor, then as a small bird that flies out of the monitor and nests in the redwood plant.

The printer begins chanting in a language of toner and warm paper. It prints your report, but each page is a mirror showing a different version of you: one is asleep at the desk, one is giving a presentation to a boardroom of cats, one is calmly packing a bag while the office floods with honey-colored light.

Your phone buzzes. It’s a calendar reminder: **“Deadline: now. Attire: pajamas. Location: everywhere.”**

You stand up. The impossible deadline is still there, humming like a refrigerator, but the ceiling has opened into a sky full of paper airplanes made from unfinished to-do lists. One of them dips and lands in your hand. You unfold it. In your own handwriting, it says:

*“You don’t have to finish it. You only have to wake up.”*

So you close the laptop. The beetles scatter into stars. The redwood boss rustles approvingly. You walk toward the elevator, which is now a single door standing alone in a field of tall grass. When you push the button, it doesn’t ding—it chirps.

And somewhere beneath the sound, your real alarm is beginning to ring.
