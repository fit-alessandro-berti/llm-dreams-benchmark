Despite the hardships, I decide to follow the child with the paper wings.

She appears at the edge of the crater every dawn, her silhouette backlit by a sun that hangs too low in the sky, bleeding amber across the dust. The others in the settlement warn me—*she is not real, she is a trick of the radiation, a fever dream dressed in skin*—but I know that in this place, the unreal may be the only truth worth pursuing.

I pack my canteen, half-full of rainwater that tastes of rust and old pennies, and a satchel of dried mushrooms that glow faintly blue in the dark. The road is not a road but the spine of some great fossilized beast, its vertebrae jutting through the asphalt. As I walk, the landscape breathes. Buildings tilt their heads to watch me pass. The wind carries whispers of advertisements from before the fall, selling vacations to seas that have turned to salt flats where crystal deer now graze.

On the third day, or perhaps the third year—time here is sticky, stretching like honey—the child stops at the lip of a valley I do not remember from any map. Below, the earth is not scorched but blooming. Not with green, but with sound. Flowers made of tuning forks shiver in a breeze I cannot feel, ringing out chords that make my teeth ache. She turns to me. Her eyes are the color of a television tuned to a dead channel, static and snow, and she holds out her hand.

“You brought the seeds,” she says. It is not a question.

I open my satchel. The mushrooms have transformed. They are no longer fungi but small glass birds, their hearts pulsing with the same blue light. I understand, with the sudden clarity that only comes in dreams, that I was never meant to survive in the old way—hoarding, hiding, biting my nails to the quick. I was meant to become a vessel. A lantern.

I release the birds. They do not fly. They shatter against the valley floor, and from each fragment rises a tree of stained glass, its branches singing. The child smiles, her paper wings unfolding into something vaster, a canopy of sheet music and starlight. The dangers do not vanish, but they step back, shielding their eyes. The scarcity reverses itself; the valley pours forth light, and the light is edible, wearable, weaveable into blankets for the cold nights ahead.

I kneel and plant my hands in the soil. My fingers root. My hair becomes grass, silver and swaying. I am no longer just a traveler. I am the first chapter of the world choosing, against all logic, to begin again.

And then the alarm clock is a bird that refuses to sing, and I am waking, the taste of rust still on my tongue, the chord still vibrating in my sternum, wondering if somewhere, in some sleeping dimension, my hands are still blooming.
