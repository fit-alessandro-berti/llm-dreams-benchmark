You are given a mysterious locked box with no key in sight. As you examine it closely, you notice intricate patterns and what look like frozen, glowing fireflies trapped in the carvings—tiny flecks of opal that catch the half-light of wherever you stand, which is not the sunlit meadow you remember stepping into a moment ago. The air smells like cut clover and warm vanilla candle wax, and when you lean in to brush a finger over one of the swirling vine patterns, the fireflies stir, flickering to life in a slow, winding chain that traces the box’s entire lid, turning its dark oak surface into a faint, shifting galaxy you can trace with your gaze.

A soft, resonant thrum hums under your touch, vibrating up your forearm until your wrist tingles, and you realize there’s no keyhole after all—instead, a tiny, sun-shaped indentation nestled at the base of the largest vine curl. You pat at your pockets, fumbling for a trinket that might fit, and your fingers close around something smooth and round: not a key, but the polished river stone you found on your first hike with your little cousin last summer, which you’d totally forgotten about until now. When you ease the stone into the indent, it clicks into place with a satisfying snick, and the fireflies flare bright enough to cast tiny, lacy shadows on the grass around your ankles.

The lid lifts itself, slow as a falling leaf, and inside the box isn’t gold or jewelry or any of the treasures you half-expected—just a nest of downy chickweed feathers, each streaked with the same opal fire as the carvings, and curled in the very center is a crumpled sticky note, its edges frayed from being folded and unfolded a hundred times. You pick it up, and the handwriting is your own, scrawled in shaky blue ink three months ago, when you were panicking about a job interview: *You’ve practiced this. You’re enough.*

Just as you finish reading, the meadow around you blurs at the edges, the fireflies dimming to pinpricks, and the box slips from your grasp, floating upward like a dandelion seed caught in a soft breeze. You reach for it, and suddenly you’re sitting on your childhood bedroom’s window seat, the box perched on the sill next to the chipped ceramic mug your grandma gave you for your 12th birthday, and outside, the first snow of the winter is falling, soft and silent. A voice, warm and familiar like honeyed tea, whispers in your ear—"Don’t leave it out too long, the frost will ruin it"—and you turn to see your grandma standing in the doorway, wearing her favorite floral apron, her hands dusted with flour from the sugar cookies she’s always baking. Before you can say hello, the dream fades into a soft, warm haze, leaving only the faint smell of vanilla and the ghost of her smile.
