The first puzzles are familiar—number sequences, hidden patterns, mechanical locks—but the deeper you go, the more unusual they become. One asks you to remember a conversation you've never had. Another requires you to fit together pieces of a memory that doesn't belong to you. Somehow, in the logic of the dream, the answers feel obvious.

With each puzzle solved, a new passage opens. The maze twists and expands, becoming less like a structure and more like a living mind. Corridors branch into impossible angles. Staircases loop back onto themselves. Doors lead to rooms that contain entire landscapes.

Eventually, you reach the center.

There is no final puzzle waiting there.

Instead, you find a table with a single sheet of paper. On it is a question:

*"Why are you trying so hard to find the exit?"*

You search for clues, expecting a riddle, a trick, a hidden code. But the page remains blank except for the question.

For the first time since entering the maze, you stop thinking.

The walls around you become transparent. Through them, you see every path you took, every wrong turn, every challenge that seemed impossible. From this perspective, the maze is beautiful—a vast pattern connecting thousands of choices into a single design.

Then you realize something unsettling: there was never an exit marked anywhere.

Only a center.

As the realization settles in, the paper folds itself into a small origami key. The maze begins to dissolve into drifting fragments of light. You take the key and place it in your pocket.

A distant sound echoes through the fading corridors—a morning alarm.

Just before you wake, you reach into your pocket to check whether the key is still there.

And for a brief moment, it feels solid.
