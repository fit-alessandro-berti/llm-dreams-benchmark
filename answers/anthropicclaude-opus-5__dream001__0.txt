You start to ask directions, but the words come out in the wrong order, and the woman I've stopped nods anyway as though I've said something perfectly sensible. *Third floor,* she says, *but the third floor moved.* She points up with her chin. The building behind her has no floors at all, only scaffolding and a ladder made of piano keys.

I have the invitation somewhere. I do have it — I can feel its edge in my coat pocket, the crispness of good cardstock. But when I pull it out it's a train timetable for a country I've never visited, and the times are all written as fractions. 7/9. 11/4. I understand, in the dream's flat way, that this is *worse* than useless, and also that I should have studied it earlier.

The city keeps opening. Every street I take widens into a plaza I've already crossed. There's a fountain with no water in it, just a dry basin full of umbrellas, hundreds of them, folded like sleeping birds. Someone has left a briefcase on the rim. I know it isn't mine, but I check anyway, and inside there are pages and pages of my own handwriting, which I've never had, saying things I don't remember thinking.

The hour is going. I can feel it going the way you feel water leaving a bath — that slow tilt in the body.

Then I'm in the meeting. That's how it happens; there's no door, no arriving. A long table, warm lamplight, twelve people who all look up with the specific patience of people who have been waiting a long time and don't mind. I'm out of breath. I start to apologize, to explain about the timetable, the fractions, the floor that moved —

And the man at the head of the table says, *You're right on time. You've always been right on time. We only started when you got here.*

And I want to argue. I want to say I was lost, I was genuinely lost, I want someone to acknowledge the vast wrong city and the umbrellas and the ladder of piano keys. But everyone is turning to me now with such unhurried attention, and I realize I have no idea what the meeting is about, and that it doesn't matter, because the meeting is about me being here, and I am here, and someone slides a glass of water toward me, and the light through it makes a small trembling coin on the table, and I think: *oh — this is the part I was hurrying toward.*

And then the ceiling is a sky. And the table is a field. And the twelve of them are hedgerows in the early dark, and I'm still holding the water, and I don't wake up for a while yet.
