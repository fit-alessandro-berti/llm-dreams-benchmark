• You are the captain of a ship in the middle of a turbulent sea. Your crew looks to you for guidance as the storm
  worsens, and you keep one hand on the wheel, the other over the map, feeling the hull shudder like a living thing
  beneath your feet. The waves rise like dark walls, spray turning to glass in the moonlight, and every rope, every
  lantern, every shouted order feels as fragile as breath.

  You do not shout. You do not panic. You trim the sails with calm precision, keep the bow angled just so, and tell your
  helmsman to trust the old rhythm of the wind instead of the fear in his voice.

  Then, through the roar, you hear it: a long, low groan from deep below, as if the sea itself is shifting. A hidden
  reef, they say. A grave. But there—behind the spray, where lightning flickers and disappears—one of the lantern boys
  swears he sees a narrow channel of pale foam cutting through the chaos.

  You take command of the impossible.

  “Starboard two degrees. Hold fast the portside lines. We ride the line, not the wave,” you say.

  The ship answers.

  For one breathless minute the storm and you are both wild and balanced, and then, just as the old saying goes, the sea
  gives and the line holds. A break appears. You steer through black water and broken wind into a quieter basin the
  charts never marked.

  By dawn, the storm has moved on. The crew is soaked, exhausted, alive. The captain’s hand is cut and numb from the
  rope burn, but they gather behind you at the bow and breathe easier. No one asked for thanks; no one needed words.

  You glance at the horizon. Still heavy, still dangerous—but farther away now—and point to it, quietly.

  “Next knot. Same hand. Same eyes.”

  And the ship, steadier now, cuts forward into the long, patient day.
