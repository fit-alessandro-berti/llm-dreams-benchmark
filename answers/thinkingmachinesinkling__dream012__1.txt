...you do not reach for the wheel. Instead, you notice the crew’s faces are not frightened, but waiting—each one a mirror of your own unspoken doubt. The ship beneath your feet is no longer wood and iron, but bone-white coral, groaning like a sleeping whale. You realize the storm is not weather, but a vast, swirling question calling your name from the dark.

You tell them not to bail the water, but to open every sail wider—to drink the wind. The ropes turn to silver vines in their hands. As they move in perfect, silent harmony, you understand that guidance was never about escaping the sea, but about becoming the current itself. 

You steer not away from the tempest, but directly into its dreaming eye. The waves do not crash; they lift the vessel gently upward, through a ceiling of black glass and moonlight, into a sky that smells of salt and forgotten songs. There, in the calm at the center of the chaos, you finally speak the only command that matters:

*Let go. We are already home.* 

And the crew smiles, dissolving into mist, as the ship becomes a constellation, and you—still dreaming—wake just enough to feel the rain on your real face, knowing you have never truly left the sea.
