...and the room keeps changing shape. At first, it seems like a gallery opening—white walls, soft lighting, people holding wine glasses they never actually drink from. Everyone is in conversation, but I can only catch fragments: "...after the threshold..." and "...the third Tuesday of never..."

I spot my friend across the room, or someone who looks like them, but when I move toward them, the floor stretches like taffy. The group nearest me has been discussing a movie I feel certain I watched, except no one can agree on the ending. One woman turns and says, "You were there too, weren't you?" I nod, though I wasn't.

I weave past a cluster of academics debating a color no one can name. A man in a velvet jacket tells me my friend moved to "the other wing," gesturing vaguely. I follow a hallway that opens into a garden, then a kitchen, then a library where the books have no titles. I try to remember why finding my friend felt so urgent. Something about a message. Something I was supposed to tell them.

Now the gathering has become a wedding, or maybe it always was. I don't recognize the bride. Someone hands me a small silver key and whispers that my friend is "behind the third door on the left," but every door I try leads to the same room—a childhood bedroom I haven't seen in years.

When I finally find them, they're standing alone by a window, older somehow, though not in years. They look at me with relief and say, "I thought you'd never arrive. We've been waiting since you left."

I want to ask what they mean, but I realize I don't know which version of me they're talking to. The dream is beginning to fold in on itself. I feel the familiar tug of waking, soft but insistent, like a hand pulling at my sleeve.

And I know that if I can just say the thing I came to say, I'll finally be able to—
