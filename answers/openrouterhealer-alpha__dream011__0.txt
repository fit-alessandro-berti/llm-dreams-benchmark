*"Describe the moment you first understood what it means to heal."*

You look around. The exam hall isn't quite right. The ceiling is too tall, fading into a grey fog that doesn't quite end. The other students are there, but their faces are soft and indistinct, like photographs left in the rain. Nobody seems alarmed.

You pick up your pen. It's warm in your hand — almost alive — and when the nib touches paper, the words come out before you think them. Not your usual handwriting. Something slower, more deliberate, as if your hand is being patient for you.

*"Healing is not the absence of the wound. It is the decision to let light reach it."*

The sentence glows faintly gold, then sinks into the page like water into soil.

The next question appears:

*"You have been preparing for a career you never chose. Show us what you actually became."*

Suddenly the desk beneath your hands is changing — grain and texture shifting — and you realize it's not a desk anymore. It's a drafting table. A kitchen counter. A garden planter. A stage. The room keeps becoming different rooms, and you keep becoming someone slightly different in each one: focused, then uncertain, then laughing, then still.

You aren't studying anymore. You're *remembering* — except the memories haven't happened yet.

A voice, gentle and close, though no one is beside you:

*"The exam was never to test what you know. It was to show you what you were brave enough to become."*

The paper fills with light. The fog above lifts, just enough to show a sky that isn't quite a sky — more like the first clear thought you've had in a long time.

You wake up.

But you can still feel the pen in your hand.
