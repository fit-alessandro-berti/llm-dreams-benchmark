...and you begin to speak, but no sound comes out—only a slow, silver mist that rolls across the stage and into the crowd. You reach for your notes, but the pages have turned to water, running through your fingers like a cool stream. The audience doesn’t murmur or grow restless; instead, they lean in closer, their faces softening into expressions of deep, familiar recognition, as if they’ve been waiting not for words, but for this exact silence.

Then the ceiling of the auditorium opens like a flower, revealing not sky, but an ocean suspended overhead—dark, calm, and full of drifting lanterns. You step forward, and your feet no longer touch the floor; you are floating just above the front row, and suddenly you understand that you aren’t there to be heard. You are there to remember something together.

The crowd begins to hum, a single low chord that vibrates through your chest, and you join them. The speech was never about information. It was a door, and now, in the dream, it is opening. The lights dim not to black, but to the color of old gold, and you wake with the hum still in your ears—unsure if you were the speaker, the audience, or the song itself.
