First, you stumble into a circle of tall, elegant strangers wearing coats made of living, rustling ivy. They are laughing warmly, but when you ask if they’ve seen your friend, their laughter stretches out, slowing down until it sounds like the deep, rumbling groan of an old wooden ship. One of them points toward a grand staircase, but as you look, the steps begin to flow downward like a waterfall made of dark velvet.

You push through the crowd, your feet suddenly feeling heavy, as if the air has turned to warm honey. You pass a mahogany table where a group is playing a board game. The pieces are glowing, shifting constellations that whisper to each other. "Don't look at the center," a player with eyes like polished obsidian warns you, "or you’ll forget what you’re looking for." 

You blink, and for a terrifying second, your friend's face slips from your mind like wet soap. You panic, but you manage to grasp onto the memory of their voice just in time to keep the memory from dissolving.

At the back of the hall, the ambient chatter fades, replaced by a violin playing a melody you swear you wrote in your childhood but forgot. Through the haze, you see a silhouette standing by French doors that open into a misty, moonlit garden. It’s them. You’d know that posture anywhere. 

Relieved, you hurry toward them, but the floor tiles beneath you begin to separate, drifting apart like ice floes on a dark, silent river. You have to leap from tile to tile, calling their name over the widening gaps. 

They hear you. They begin to turn around, slowly, silhouetted against the moonlight. 

But as their face finally comes into focus, it isn't your friend at all. It is a mirror. 

And in the reflection, you aren't standing in a crowded hall. You are standing entirely alone in a quiet, autumn forest, and the sound of the party was just the wind rustling through the dry leaves. 

The wind blows cooler, the mirror shatters into harmless glass stars, and you slowly open your eyes to the quiet of your own room.
