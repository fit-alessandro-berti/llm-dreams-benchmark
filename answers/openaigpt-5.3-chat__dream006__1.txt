…speak, but the microphone blooms into a white flower in your hand.

The audience doesn’t seem surprised. They lean forward as if this is exactly what they came to see.

You clear your throat. Instead of words, a small flock of paper birds slips from your mouth and circles the lights above the stage. Each bird carries a sentence written on its wings, but they flap too quickly to read.

Someone in the front row nods like they understand everything.

The stage stretches longer and longer beneath your feet, becoming a narrow pier over a dark ocean. The audience is still there somehow, rows of faces floating just beyond the water, patient and waiting.

You realize the speech isn’t something you say.

It’s something you remember.

So you close your eyes and try to recall the first word you ever learned. As you do, the paper birds settle onto the water, the flower-microphone folds back into a simple piece of metal, and the crowd inhales together—

just before you finally begin.
