I grip the wheel until my knuckles match the whitecaps. The crew’s eyes are lanterns in the dark, bobbing, waiting. I open my mouth to give an order—*hard to port, batten the hatches*—but the wind steals the words and turns them into gulls that circle the mast, crying out coordinates in a language I almost understand.

The waves aren’t water anymore. They’re glass. They’re pages from books I haven’t read yet. They’re the heavy velvet curtains of my childhood bedroom, billowing up to swallow the bow. The helmsman beside me is my father, or perhaps he’s the version of me that woke up this morning; his face keeps slipping. He isn’t afraid. In dreams, fear is just another kind of weather.

I realize, with the sudden certainty that belongs only to sleep, that I don’t need to steer *through* the storm. I need to steer *into* its heart.

I let go of the wheel.

It spins wildly, but instead of chaos, the ship *lifts*—not sinking, but flying, or perhaps the sea is falling upward to meet a sky made of polished obsidian. The crew doesn’t panic. They understood before I did. They’re already untying the ropes, which unravel into silver thread, into smoke, into the thin strings of a music box I lost years ago. The lightning freezes in the air, becoming new rigging, becoming a bridge of violet light that stretches from our prow to the horizon.

We walk across it, leaving the ship behind. It was never wood and nail, anyway. It was a promise I made to myself before I knew how to keep it. The storm quiets. The water turns to silk beneath the bridge, and I can see, far below, not fish, but stars—thousands of them, sleeping in the deep, waiting to be born.

Somewhere, a clock is ticking that isn’t on any wall. The bridge dissolves at my feet into warm sand. I am still the captain. The crew are only shadows now, waving from a distance, smiling. The sea is still listening. And I am beginning to wake.
