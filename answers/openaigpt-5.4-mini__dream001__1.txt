You start to walk faster, as if purpose could be manufactured by momentum alone.

The streets keep rearranging themselves. One moment you’re on a narrow lane of wet cobblestones, the next you’re in a boulevard lined with trees made of glass, their branches chiming softly in the wind. Every sign points somewhere different, and every passerby seems to recognize you without knowing your name.

You ask a woman in a red coat where the meeting is. She smiles with pity, then hands you a paper map that is blank except for a single dot moving slowly across it. “You’re already late,” she says, and keeps walking.

You look down and realize the dot is you.

A bell begins to ring somewhere overhead. Then another. Then all at once the city answers with sound: tram bells, bicycle bells, church bells, the distant clatter of dishes in hidden cafés. The noise swells into a rhythm, and as you follow it, the streets become familiar in the way dreams make things familiar—never by memory, but by certainty.

You turn a corner and find a building you know you have never seen: tall, narrow, and leaning slightly as though listening. Above the door is a brass plaque with no letters on it, only a small keyhole. The door opens before you touch it.

Inside is a room lit by morning, though no windows are visible.

At the far end sits a long table, and around it are people you almost recognize: your old teacher, your childhood neighbor, someone you once loved, someone you may become. They are all waiting for you with the patient expressions of people who have been waiting a long time.

At the head of the table, an empty chair is pulled out for you.

You check your watch. It has no hands.

Still, somehow, you know: you are not late. The meeting is not about work, or schedules, or anything that can be written down. It is about the thing you’ve been searching for all day without naming it.

You sit.

And as you do, the city outside goes quiet, as if it has finally found what it was trying to tell you.
