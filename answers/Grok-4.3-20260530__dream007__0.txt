You decide to walk away mid-sentence, slamming the door behind you with a force that shouldn’t exist in real life. The sound echoes like thunder. But instead of the hallway outside, you step into an endless corridor of floating photographs—every argument you’ve ever had with this friend, frozen in mid-yell, every shared laugh and late-night secret suspended like stars.

The air smells like old coffee and rain. Your friend’s voice chases you anyway, growing louder, then warping into birdsong. You turn around and they’re there, but now they’re ten feet tall, skin made of shifting constellations, eyes glowing with the same hurt and love that started the fight. “You always do this,” they say, voice cracking like lightning. “You run when it gets real.”

You open your mouth to argue back, but instead of words, a flock of paper airplanes bursts out—each one carrying a half-finished apology you’ve never sent. They swirl around you both, cutting through the tension like soft knives. The corridor dissolves. Suddenly you’re standing on the roof of your old childhood treehouse, the one you built together when you were twelve. Below, the entire argument replays in miniature on a tiny stage made of clouds: you see yourselves shouting about the thing that mattered so much—whatever it was—then pausing, realizing how ridiculous the volume had become.

Your friend (still glowing, still impossibly tall) sits down cross-legged beside you. The anger drains out of the dream like water through sand. “We’re both idiots sometimes,” they say, and you laugh so hard the treehouse sways. You decide, right then, to reach out—not to win, not to explain, just to touch their constellation hand. It feels warm. Familiar.

The fight doesn’t vanish. It just… transforms. The important thing you were arguing about floats up between you like a paper lantern, catches fire in the gentlest way, and drifts into the sky as harmless sparks. You both watch it go.

You wake up with your heart still racing, but lighter. The dream lingers on your tongue like the aftertaste of something sweet you can’t quite name. And for the first time in weeks, you pick up your phone and actually text them: “Hey. Coffee later? No yelling, I promise.”
