Despite the hardships, you decide to **keep walking west**, toward the faint amber line where the sun used to set cleanly against the sky. Your canteen is almost empty, your boots are held together with electrical tape, and the last radio signal you heard was three weeks ago—a woman’s voice reading numbers in a language you didn’t recognize. Still, you walk.

The road becomes a river of cracked asphalt and rusted cars. Somewhere in the hollow of a collapsed overpass, you find a single intact thing: a glass jar, sealed with wax, containing three dried seeds. They rattle like tiny bones when you shake them. You don’t know what they’ll grow into, but in a world where even hope feels rationed, you pocket them like treasure.

By dusk, you reach the skeleton of a greenhouse. Its metal ribs are bent but still standing, and inside, moonlight falls through shattered panes onto soil that has somehow stayed dark and soft. You plant the seeds in three small mounds, pouring the last of your water over them. Then you sit against the frame, exhausted, and watch the stars try to come out through the haze.

That night, you dream within the dream: roots splitting the earth, green vines climbing the rust, flowers opening like lanterns, and the sound of bees returning after a long silence. When you wake, your cheek is damp with dew, and in the center of each mound a pale shoot has broken through—impossibly quick, impossibly alive.

You don’t know if they’ll survive the week. You don’t know if *you* will. But for the first time in months, you smile, because you have chosen to grow something in a world that only knew how to take.
