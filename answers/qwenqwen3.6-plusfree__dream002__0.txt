You are walking through a peaceful forest when suddenly the weather changes dramatically. You feel a storm approaching and, instead of turning back, your footsteps slow as if the earth itself is asking you to listen. The sky fractures into ribbons of bruised violet and molten gold, and the wind arrives not as a gale, but as a slow, deep breath that makes every leaf turn silver on one side. Thunder rolls in low and rhythmic, like a distant drum circle tuning itself to your pulse.

The first raindrop that reaches you doesn't fall. It hovers just above your shoulder, catching the dim light like a bead of liquid glass. Then another. And another. Soon the air fills with them, drifting downward in a weightless cascade. Where they meet the forest floor, moss ripples and tiny bioluminescent flowers open, sigh out a faint scent of petrichor and old paper, and dissolve into mist. 

You notice the trees are no longer just bark and branch. Their trunks pulse with a slow, amber light, and the wind carries half-remembered sounds: a porch swing creaking, a page turning, laughter you haven't heard in years. The path ahead curves into a shallow basin where the rain gathers into a perfectly still pool. You kneel and look down. The water doesn't show your face. It shows a doorway, slightly ajar, spilling warm light and the quiet hum of a room waiting for you to step inside.

You don't move toward it. You just rest your fingertips on the surface, and the water rings like a struck bell. The storm doesn't break. It simply exhales, folding the sky inward like a closing book. You blink, and the forest is still again, the air clean and heavy with possibility. You wake with the impression of damp leaves on your palms and the quiet certainty that you've just been exactly where you needed to be.
