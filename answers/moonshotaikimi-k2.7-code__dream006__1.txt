You begin to speak, but the words that leave your mouth are not the ones you rehearsed. They are softer, older, like a language you once knew in childhood but forgot. The audience does not laugh or frown. Instead, they lean forward, and you notice their faces are familiar—your second-grade teacher, a neighbor who moved away, someone you swore you only met in another dream.

The podium beneath your hands turns to warm sand. The stage lights become a late afternoon sun. You are no longer in an auditorium; you are standing at the edge of a garden you have never seen, yet every path feels correct. The crowd is still there, seated now on folding chairs among the flowers, waiting.

You keep talking. You tell them about the time you tried to catch a moonbeam in a jar, about the letter you never sent, about the way regret tastes like copper and rain. With each sentence, a bird takes flight from somewhere behind you. The audience nods as if you are finally saying what they came to hear.

Then someone in the back raises a hand. It is you—another you, younger, wearing the sweater your mother knitted. The younger you stands and says, “You don’t have to finish. We already understand.”

The crowd begins to applaud, but the sound is not clapping. It is the rustle of leaves, the hush of water over stones, the soft click of a door closing somewhere far away. You bow, not because the speech is over, but because you have remembered, at last, that you are asleep.

And as the dream starts to dissolve, you carry one sentence with you into waking: *They were never waiting for perfection. They were only waiting for you.*
