The lantern swinging above the helm paints your crew in flashes of gold and shadow. Their faces are wet with rain, but none of them blink. Beyond them, the sea rises in impossible shapes—great black walls with silver veins of lightning running through them like cracks in glass. The compass spins. The stars are gone. Even the thunder sounds far away, as though the storm is happening inside your own chest.

“Hold her steady,” you hear yourself say, though you do not remember deciding to speak.

The wheel shudders beneath your palms like a living thing. The ship groans, timber bending, sails snapping like frightened birds. Then a wave taller than any mountain lifts you all into the sky, and for one breathless moment the ocean below is not ocean at all, but a vast dark mirror. In it, you see not your ship, but every ship you have ever loved, every harbor you ever left, every version of yourself that turned away from shore.

Your crew waits.

So you do the only thing that feels true: instead of steering away from the heart of the storm, you turn toward it.

At once the rain changes. It is no longer cold. It falls in glittering strands like threads of glass, and where they strike the deck, tiny flowers bloom between the planks—blue, luminous, opening and closing with the rhythm of the waves. The sails fill, not with wind, but with a deep singing, as if the sky itself has inhaled.

Ahead, inside the storm’s eye, something impossible appears: a lighthouse standing upright in the middle of the sea, its beam turning slowly over the water. But the closer you come, the more you realize the lighthouse is made of all the things you thought were lost—old letters, broken promises, childhood summers, names you have not spoken in years. Its light falls over your crew, and one by one their fearful faces become familiar. Not sailors. Not strangers. They are all the selves you have been, looking to the self you are now.

The sea becomes calm.

Not all at once, but in widening circles around the bow, until the monstrous waves flatten into black silk. The clouds part like curtains. Above you hangs a sky filled not with stars, but with hundreds of small lanterns drifting upward, each carrying a memory. One pauses beside you, bright and warm. Inside it is a simple room, a window lit at dusk, and someone waiting without anger, without blame, only waiting.

Your first mate touches your shoulder and points to the horizon.

There, where dawn should be, is land unlike any land on any chart: white trees swaying without wind, cliffs made of moonlight, stairways leading down into the water instead of up from it. Bells ring from somewhere inland, soft and welcoming. The ship glides forward as if it has always known the way.

Behind you, the storm closes like a door.

Ahead, the strange shore glows brighter.

And just before the keel kisses the silver sand, you realize the thing your crew needed was never certainty. It was only your willingness to stand at the helm while the world broke open around you.

Then the lighthouse beam sweeps across your face one last time, and you wake with salt on your lips, your hands still curved as if holding the wheel.
