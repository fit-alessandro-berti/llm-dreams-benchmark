The sky has gone the color of bruised plums, and the rain comes sideways, stinging every exposed inch of skin. Your crew—faces you almost recognize, though their names keep slipping away—watch you with wide, silver-coin eyes. Somewhere behind the wind, a bell is tolling, though you know there are no bells on this ship.

You open your mouth to give an order, but what comes out is not a command. It is a song, low and strange, in a language you have never learned yet somehow understand. The words settle over the water, and the waves pause—not calm, exactly, but listening. The mainsail stops its screaming. The helm steadies in your hands.

“Trim the sails,” you say at last, your voice carrying effortlessly through the storm. “Not to fight the wind. To ask it where it wants to go.”

The crew moves as if in a dance they have always known. Ropes tighten. The ship heels, not away from the storm, but along a shining seam in the water, a path of phosphorescence opening before the bow. Lightning forks above, and for a heartbeat it does not look like danger—it looks like a map.

You feel the dream tilt. The horizon bends. The sea begins to glow, not blue or green, but gold, as though the sun has decided to rise from beneath the waves. Your crew does not cheer; they breathe, all at once, a single exhalation of fear turning into wonder.

Then, at the edge of the light, you see it: not land, not safety, but a door standing upright in the water. It is your cabin door, the one from the house you left behind years ago, or perhaps only minutes ago. The brass knob is warm when you touch it.

The crew turns to you one last time. They do not ask if you are going. They already know.

You turn the handle. The storm hushes. The sea holds its breath.

And you step through—not waking, not yet, but into the next room of the dream, where the rain smells like lavender and someone is calling your name from very far away.
