...step back, take a deep breath, and turn toward the heavy oak door of the room to just walk away and clear my head. 

But when I turn the brass knob, the door doesn’t open to the familiar hallway. Instead, it opens to a dense, silent pine forest, and the air smells sharply of ozone and old paper. 

I step through anyway, because in this moment, the logic of the argument feels heavier than the impossibility of the doorway. I expect my friend to stay behind, but when I look over my shoulder, they are right there, standing on the mossy ground. Their anger has vanished, replaced by a blank, hollow expression, their features slightly blurred at the edges, like a photograph left out in the rain.

We are still holding the thing we were fighting over. I look down at my hands. It’s not a document, or a phone, or a piece of paper. It’s a glowing, translucent hourglass. But the sand inside isn’t falling; it’s flowing relentlessly *upward*, defying gravity, defying the time we just wasted yelling.

"We're running out of it," my friend says, but their voice doesn't come from their mouth. It echoes from the trees around us, sounding exactly like my own voice from five minutes ago. 

I reach out to touch the glass of the hourglass to stop the sand, to make it make sense. The moment my fingertip makes contact, the room—and the forest—dissolves. The angry words we just shouted at each other materialize in the air between us as heavy, dark stones. They hover for a second, vibrating with tension, and then shatter into a swarm of bright blue moths.

The moths flutter upward into a sky that has no sun, only a massive, unblinking eye made of stained glass. My friend reaches out to catch one of the moths. As it lands on their palm, they look at me, and a profound, weightless sorrow washes over the dream. 

"We are on the wrong side of the mirror," they whisper. 

I try to speak, to apologize, to ask what they mean, but my mouth fills with warm, silver sand. The ground beneath us gives way, not falling, but floating upward into the stained-glass sky. I try to grab my friend's hand, but my fingers pass right through their wrist like smoke. 

And just as the blue moths completely envelop my vision, I feel the sharp, sudden jolt of waking up, the echo of the argument still vibrating in my chest, and the lingering, impossible scent of pine trees in my bedroom.
