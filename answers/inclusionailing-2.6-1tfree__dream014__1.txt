Their voices move like lanterns—soft, deliberate, unhurried—cutting through the neon rain without demanding you hurry. One woman hands you a cup of something warm that tastes like cinnamon and forgotten birthdays; a man with kind eyes points not down at your shoes but toward a bridge you hadn’t noticed, its arches strung with old streetlamps like prayer beads.  

As you walk, the city loosens its grip. The crowd no longer pushes; it parts gently, as if learning your name. The strangers don’t tell you where you’re going, only that you don’t have to carry the weight alone. Somewhere above, a clock chimes, and for a moment the buildings lean in to listen.  

You realize you’ve started humming—then laughing—because the direction you’re walking doesn’t feel like lost anymore. It feels like being found. And as the bridge comes closer, you see your reflection in the river below, not as someone missing, but as someone arriving, lanterns blooming in your chest like slow fireflies saying, *Here. Safe. Still here.*
