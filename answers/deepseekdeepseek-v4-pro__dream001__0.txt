You start to walk, but the streets rearrange themselves like shuffled cards. Each step makes the buildings grow taller, their windows blinking at you in silent Morse code. The map on your phone shows only a looping labyrinth and the words “you are here” pulsing in every direction at once. A parade of clock-faced people marches past, their hands pointing urgently but never to the same hour. You try to ask for help, but your voice comes out as a cloud of moths that flutter toward a neon sign flickering the name of a street you almost remember from a childhood you didn’t have. The meeting, you now sense, is about something you’ve forgotten how to do—like breathing underwater or remembering the color of your own front door. And yet, somehow, you know exactly which way to go: down into the subway where the trains run on silence and the destination is always “Soon.”
