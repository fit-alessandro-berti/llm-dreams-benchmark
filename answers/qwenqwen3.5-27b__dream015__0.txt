Despite the hardships, you decide to **walk toward the sound of the wind.**

It is not the howling gale that strips the rusted skeletons of skyscrapers, but a softer, melodic hum coming from the north. In this dream, logic is fluid; you know the wind shouldn't sing, yet it does. You leave your scavenged pack behind, feeling the weight of the water canteen lift from your shoulders as if gravity has lost its interest in you.

The ground beneath your boots shifts from cracked asphalt to soft, grey moss. As you walk, the ruins begin to change. The broken windows of the dead city fill with liquid light, glowing like amber honey. The dangers—the shadowy figures that lurked in the alleys—dissolve into mist as you pass them, their threats turning into whispers of encouragement.

You reach the source of the sound: a solitary tree growing out of the center of a collapsed highway overpass. Its leaves are not green, but made of stained glass, chiming softly in the breeze. Underneath it, there is no shelter, no food, and no weapon. There is only a small, wooden chair.

You sit. The moment your back touches the wood, the sky above tears open, not with rain, but with a shower of memories. You see faces of people you haven't thought of in years, laughing in sunlight that feels real. The apocalyptic grey fades, replaced by the vibrant, impossible colors of a sunrise that belongs to a world that no longer exists.

The tree's glass leaves chime a final, resonant note. You realize you are not looking for survival anymore; you are looking for the memory of peace.

The ground beneath the chair begins to dissolve into white light. You feel the sensation of falling, not down, but up, and the last thing you hear before the dream fades into the morning is the wind saying, *"It is enough."*
