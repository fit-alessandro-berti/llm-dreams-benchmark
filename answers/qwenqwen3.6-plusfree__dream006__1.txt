...speak, but the words don't leave your mouth as sound. They spill out like handfuls of warm sand, drifting into the spotlight and catching the air as they fall. The first syllable turns into a slow-floating dandelion clock. The second becomes the distant ring of a bicycle bell you haven't heard since childhood. You don't stop them; they just keep unspooling, weaving into the space between you and the crowd.

The stage floor softens beneath your soles, losing its rigid edges until it feels more like packed earth, then like the deck of a quiet boat on still water. The expectant faces in the bleachers begin to shift: strangers resolve into people you loved, then into versions of yourself from different years, all watching with the same quiet recognition. You realize there are no notes to read, no clock ticking. The speech was never about telling them something new. It was about remembering how to listen.

You close your eyes and let your voice drop to a whisper, but the room doesn't go silent. The air itself begins to hum, syncing with your pulse. A woman in the third row nods, and it feels like a door unlocking somewhere deep in your chest. The lights don't fade so much as dissolve into a pale, moon-washed sky, and the upward slope of the stage gently tilts, lifting you into it.

You're floating now, weightless, still murmuring, still being held. The dream doesn't wake you up. It just settles into your ribs like a secret you were always meant to keep.
