

You are dreaming. The air hums with too many conversations, a low thrum like a hive disturbed. You scan the sea of unfamiliar faces—smiles fixed too wide, eyes darting—searching for the one person you know: Sam, your quiet friend from college who promised to save you a spot near the snack table. But the room shifts as you move, hallways stretching into atrium-like spaces you didn’t notice before, each turn revealing a new cluster of people engaged in rituals that make no sense yet feel oddly significant.

First, you stumble upon a circle near a faux-marble statue, where six people in identical charcoal suits pass a single teacup around in absolute silence. Every time it reaches the person directly opposite the pourer, they take a sip, then instantly replace their face with a smooth, featureless mask of white porcelain. The cup continues its circuit; the masks blink in unison when not worn. You hesitate, but the suit-clad figures don’t acknowledge you—only the cup’s journey matters.

Further on, a group lounges on velvet cushions piled high against a wall draped in deep blue fabric. They’re all wearing mismatched shoes—one boot, one ballet slipper; a clown shoe and a hiking boot—and they’re meticulously folding intricate origami cranes from sheets of paper covered in your own handwriting, though you don’t remember writing the equations, shopping lists, and half-remembered apologies scrawled across them. When you reach out to touch a crane, it dissolves into fine, glittering sand that smells like rain on hot pavement.

Your pulse quickens. Where is Sam? You push through a doorway draped in frayed red velvet and find yourself in a quieter library nook. Shelves groan under books with titles that change when you glance away (*“The History of Sighs,” “How to Speak to Shadows”*). And there, seated in a wingback chair by a window showing a starless twilight, is Sam—but their hair is silver where it should be brown, and they’re knitting something long and serpentine from strands of liquid shadow. They look up, and their eyes are yours.

“You’re late,” Sam says, voice soft but layered, like multiple voices speaking at once. “We’ve been waiting for you to notice the pattern.” They gesture to the knitting: a scarf, but where the yarn loops, tiny scenes flicker—you laughing at a childhood birthday, arguing with a stranger on a train, standing alone in this very room years ago. “You only come here when you’re lonely in a crowd. But you’re never alone. You’re just forgetting how to see the threads.”

As Sam speaks, the library dissolves. You’re back in the main hall, but now the crowd parts silently. People turn—not to stare, but to bow their heads slightly, as in respect or farewell. The unfamiliar faces soften, revealing fleeting glimpses of people you’ve loved and lost, strangers who once helped you, versions of yourself from different paths. Sam is gone from the chair, but beside you, a warm presence settles—solid, familiar, reassuring. You don’t need to search anymore. You understand, with dream-certainty, that you were never lost; you were remembering.

You wake with the echo of liquid shadow on your fingertips and the quiet certainty that some gatherings aren’t about finding someone else—they’re about recognizing who’s been walking beside you all along.
