You open the project file. The document is blank except for a single line of text that wasn’t there a second ago:
**"To complete this, you must remember what you’ve forgotten."**

A cold dread settles in your stomach. You *do* remember something—fragments, really. A childhood game where you had to "fix" broken toys by whispering to them. It’s your old college roommate, the one who moved to Mars (or was it a cult?). **"You’re close,"** they say, their voice crackling like a bad radio signal. **"But the answer isn’t in the file. You look up. The office is empty. Your boss is gone. The project brief has dissolved into a puddle of ink that’s slowly forming into the shape of a door. Behind you, the printer whirs to life, spitting out page after page of the same image: a mirror reflection of *you*, but the reflection is working at a desk in a version of the office that’s slightly *off*—the walls are too tall, the chairs are made of bones, and your reflection is smiling.

It mouths: **"You’re almost out of time."**

The clock on the wall (which wasn’t there before) strikes 13. The hands spin backward. Your skin prickles. You realize, with a sickening lurch, that the "project" was never about the work. It was about *you* remembering how to **un-build** things. How to take apart the rules of this place. How to wake up.

You reach for the door made of ink—

—and the dream *shatters*.

You gasp awake, your heart hammering. Your alarm clock reads **3:33 AM**. On your nightstand, a single sheet of paper you don’t remember writing on. It says, in your handwriting:
**"The deadline was never real. The real work was learning to stop."**

Outside your window, the streetlamp flickers. For just a second, you see a figure standing under it—your boss, but younger, their face half-hidden in shadow. They raise a hand in a wave. Then the light steadies, and they’re gone.
