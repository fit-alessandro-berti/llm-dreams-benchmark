**You wake up in a completely different era, with no modern technology around. As you explore your new surroundings, you realize you need to** find water before the sun climbs any higher. 

The air smells like woodsmoke and damp earth. No power lines, no car hum, no buzz of notifications. Just the sound of a bell tolling somewhere past the thatched roofs. 

**Where you are**  
Looks like a village, maybe 1300s Europe. Dirt road under your bare feet, market stalls with handwoven cloth, iron tools, baskets of root vegetables. Everyone’s in linen tunics and wool cloaks. Your jeans and sneakers would get you burned as a witch, so first problem: you stick out. 

**What hits you first**  
1. **Clothes**: A woman selling eggs eyes you like you fell from the sky. You trade your zipper hoodie for a rough brown tunic and leather belt. The deal feels like daylight robbery, but now you don’t look like a demon. 
2. **Language**: They’re speaking something close to Middle English. You catch one word in four, but “bread” and “water” translate pretty well through pointing. 
3. **Money**: Your wallet is useless. Those coins are silver pennies, and you have exactly zero. So your wealth is now your knowledge. 

**What you need to survive**  
- **Clean water**: No taps. There’s a well in the square, but dysentery is real here. You remember: boil it. Finding fire is easier than finding antibiotics. 
- **Food + shelter**: You can’t Google “how to make a plow,” but you do know about crop rotation and germ theory. That’s weirdly valuable. The blacksmith’s son has a cough. You suggest he cover his mouth and wash his hands. His father offers you a spot in the hayloft for the night. 
- **A role**: No ID, no skills they recognize. But you can do math, read, write, and you know the stars. The priest decides you’re either a scholar or possessed. You lean hard into “traveling scribe.” 

**The twist**  
Night falls and there are no streetlights. The sky is insane: Milky Way like spilled salt, no light pollution. You realize you can navigate by it. You also realize how quiet the world is without engines. Just wind, animals, and a distant lute someone’s tuning badly. 

You’re lying in the hayloft when it clicks: the thing you need most isn’t water or food. It’s **a story that keeps you alive**. If they think you’re useful, or holy, or at least interesting, you get another sunrise. 

So tomorrow, you’re going to “invent” soap, explain why the miller’s wheel could be more efficient, and maybe casually predict the next lunar eclipse. Small moves. 

And you’re going to miss Wi-Fi like a lost limb.
