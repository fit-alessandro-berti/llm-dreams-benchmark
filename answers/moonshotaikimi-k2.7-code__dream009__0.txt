You wake up in a completely different era, with no modern technology around. As you explore your new surroundings, you realize you need to **remember how to be human without the noise.**

The grass beneath your palms is wet with dew, and the sky above is so crowded with stars that it looks almost rude, as if the universe has been waiting for you to notice it. No streetlights hum. No phones buzz in pockets you no longer seem to have. Even the silence sounds different here—deeper, older, threaded with the distant bark of a dog and the rustle of something moving through tall wheat.

You sit up. Your clothes are wrong. The fabric is heavier, rougher, and your shoes—if you can call them that—are stiff leather that creaks when you shift your weight. A dirt road stretches ahead of you, silvered by moonlight, and somewhere beyond the ridge, you can make out the amber glow of a single window.

You try to recall how you arrived. Was there a door? A sound? A fall? But memory slips through your fingers like smoke, and all that remains is the urgent, wordless feeling that you are here for a reason.

As you walk toward the light, the air smells of woodsmoke and dried herbs and horse sweat. A wooden cart passes, and the driver—a man with a face like a cracked leather map—nods at you without surprise, as if strangers emerging from the dark are common in this place. He says something in a language that feels familiar at the edges, like a song you once knew as a child.

You nod back. You keep walking.

And then you see it: the reason you were sent. A child standing at the edge of a well, too close, too still, staring down into the black water as if listening to something calling from below. No one else is around. There is no phone to dial, no siren to summon, no GPS to mark your location.

There is only you, your body, and the distance between where you stand and where the child stands.

You realize, with a clarity that cuts through the dream, that this era does not need you to *know* things. It does not need your passwords, your degrees, your opinions, your curated self. It needs you to move. To speak. To reach out a hand before the child leans too far.

Your heart pounds. The stars watch. The well waits.

You take a step forward.

And the dream holds its breath.
