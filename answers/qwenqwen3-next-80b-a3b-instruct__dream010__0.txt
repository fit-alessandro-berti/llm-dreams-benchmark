You are participating in a competition where the grand prize is your dream job. As the competition progresses, you face various challenges and surreal trials that blur the line between skill and soul.

The first test? *Walking backward through a library made of clouds.* Each book you touch whispers a memory you’ve forgotten — your grandmother’s lullaby, the smell of rain on your first day of school, the sound of your laughter when you were seven and didn’t know yet what “failure” meant. You must recite one memory correctly to unlock the next aisle. Fail, and the shelves sink beneath you. You stumble, cry, and whisper the wrong one — “I wanted to be an astronaut” — and the book dissolves into fireflies. But then, you try again… “I wanted to make people feel less alone.” The aisle opens. A feather pen floats into your palm.

The second trial: *You must compose a symphony using silence.* The judges are faceless figures sitting atop floating pianos, their eyes glowing like old TV screens. No instruments. No notes. Only the spaces between breaths. You close your eyes and breathe — in, out, in — and let your grief, your joy, your quiet hope take shape as soundless melody. The air trembles. One judge weeps silver tears. The others nod.

The final challenge? *You stand before a mirror shaped like a door. Behind it: every version of you who quit — the one who took the safe job, the one who moved away, the one who forgot their passion entirely.* The mirror doesn’t show your reflection. It shows *them*. And they all speak at once: “You still don’t deserve this.”

You don’t answer. You don’t plead.

You reach forward — not to touch the glass — but to press your palm flat against nothing. And you say, simply:

“I’m still here.”

The mirror shatters.

Not into glass — into a thousand paper cranes, each folded from pages of your old journals, scribbled with dreams you thought you’d lost.

They lift into the sky, forming a bridge.

At the end of it stands your dream job: not a title, not a desk, not a title on a business card — but a *space*. A sunlit studio filled with books, instruments, and children’s drawings taped to the walls. A sign above the door reads: “You didn’t win. You remembered.”

You wake with tears on your cheeks… and the sound of cranes still echoing in your chest.

And on your nightstand — a single, folded crane… made from yesterday’s to-do list.
