The audience isn't made of people; the seats are occupied by rows of heavy, velvet-lined clocks, all ticking in rhythmic unison. The sound of the ticking grows louder, filling the cavernous hall like a rising tide.

I realize that I haven't prepared a speech—I have prepared a secret. I look into the front row and recognize my own face looking back at me from one of the clocks. My double stands up, the wood of its frame creaking, and whispers, "You never needed the words. You only needed to let the sand run out."

I close my eyes, and the stage beneath my feet turns into a vast, dark field of tall grass. The silence is suddenly broken, not by my voice, but by the sound of a thousand birds taking flight at once. I am no longer standing on a stage; I am weightless, drifting upward into a sky the color of a bruised plum, watching the audience—my audience—dissolve into a swirl of starlight and paper. 

I take a deep breath, and for the first time, I am not afraid of the silence. I am the silence.
