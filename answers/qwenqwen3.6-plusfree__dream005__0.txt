...and each cluster seems to orbit a different kind of gravity. A circle of people in velvet coats debates the weight of Tuesday as if it were a physical object. Another group laughs in reverse, their joy echoing backward into a crystal pitcher that pours upward. You ask if they’ve seen your friend, and they all point in directions that refuse to line up—toward the ceiling, through a fogged mirror, into the narrow space between two potted ferns.

The floor shifts beneath you without warning: first thick carpet, then cool tile, then glass suspended over a slow-moving stream of brass keys. You step carefully, but your footsteps chime like distant wind bells. Every time you think you spot their familiar jacket or catch the cadence of their voice, the crowd parts to reveal a stranger wearing the same silhouette, or someone who simply presses a folded paper crane into your hand. “They’re just ahead,” they say, “or just behind. It depends on which corridor of time you’re walking through.”

You begin to notice the rooms aren’t connected by doors, but by thresholds of atmosphere. Step through an arch of hanging ivy and the air turns autumn-crisp; pass a wall of bookshelves and suddenly you’re wading through a murmuring crowd at a train station that doesn’t exist on any map. Your friend is never far, just always one conversation away, one turned head, one half-heard syllable.

Eventually, the searching tires you. You stop scanning faces. You let the hum of overlapping voices wrap around you like a coat. And in that stillness, the gathering rearranges itself. The noise thins. The lights soften. 

They’re already sitting at a small table near a window that shouldn’t fit in the room, watching a slow parade of paper boats drift across a floor that’s become shallow water. They don’t look surprised to see you. They just slide a single key across the table. It’s warm. It smells faintly of ozone and old paper. You pick it up, and suddenly you understand: you weren’t meant to find them. You were meant to let yourself be found.

The ceiling dissolves into pale morning light. The music fades into the sound of sheets rustling. And just as the key melts into your palm, you wake up—fingers curled around nothing, heart still humming with a roomful of almost-strangers, and the quiet certainty that somewhere, in another layer of waking, that key still fits.
