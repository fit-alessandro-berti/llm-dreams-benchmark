You open your mouth to shout the order to batten down the hatches, but instead of words, silver ribbons of light pour from your lips, unraveling into the gale. The ribbons catch the wind, and suddenly, the wind stops making sound. The roaring thunder becomes a mute, slow-motion ripple across the sky, like a stone dropped in dark water.

Your crew doesn't panic. They don't even blink. Their eyes are completely black, like polished obsidian, and they are smiling. They stare at you, waiting, completely unbothered by the towering wave that is frozen mid-crash above the ship, hanging suspended in the air like a cathedral of green glass.

You look down at your hands. You are no longer wearing a captain’s coat, but robes made of woven starlight. You understand then that you aren't trying to navigate the storm; you are the storm. 

With a thought, you turn the breathing wheel. The ship doesn't cut through the water—it phases through it, sinking gently into the depths of the wave without getting wet. The sea around you isn't water anymore, but the night sky itself, filled with swirling galaxies. You are sailing through space, the stars churning like whirlpools around the hull.

Your first mate steps up beside you. He doesn't speak, but he hands you a compass where the needle isn't pointing north. It’s pointing inward, toward the center of the glass, toward you. 

"Where are we going?" you ask, though the sound comes out as a chord played on a cello.

The first mate points to the horizon. There is no land there, only a massive, golden eye opening in the fabric of the cosmos, blinking slowly. 

You realize you aren't the captain. You are the dreamer. And as you turn the wheel toward the golden eye, the ship dissolves into a flock of a thousand white doves, and you wake up with the taste of salt on your lips.
