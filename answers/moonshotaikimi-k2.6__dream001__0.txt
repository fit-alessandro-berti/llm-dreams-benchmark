You start to pat your pockets for your phone, but your fingers close around something soft and warm—a pigeon feather, a handful of sand, a key with no teeth. When you finally find the device, the screen is liquid mercury, rippling with faces you almost recognize. The calendar app opens on its own. The meeting isn’t listed under a name or place, but under a sensation: the color of burnt cinnamon, the sound of a door closing three rooms away.

You start walking. The sidewalks are tessellating, shifting from cobblestone to glass to something that looks like the inside of an eggshell. You recognize a bakery—no, a bank—no, your childhood bedroom window—reflected in a skyscraper’s facade. You turn left at the corner that smells of ozone and regret. A man with your father’s hands but a conductor’s uniform points you toward the river, but when you reach the bridge, it’s become a corridor of doors, each one numbered with your pulse.

You choose the door that hums. Inside, the elevator has no buttons, only tastes—copper, lavender, rain. You select rain. The elevator doesn’t rise; it unfolds, like origami, depositing you in a lobby where the receptionist is a fox asleep on a typewriter. The clock on the wall has thirteen hours, and the minute hand is a centipede crawling backward.

You’re early. You’re late. The meeting was never about you. You open the conference room door and find a mirror, but your reflection is already seated at the mahogany table, wearing a suit made of moth wings. Your reflection gestures to the empty chair. The agenda floats in the air, written in smoke: *Discuss the color blue. Apologize to the ocean. Vote on whether gravity should continue.*

You sit. The chair exhales. Outside the window, the city begins to unbuild itself, floor by floor, revealing not sky but a vast, dark calm. Your reflection pours you a cup of ink. You drink. It tastes like waking up.

But you don’t wake. The meeting has just begun.
