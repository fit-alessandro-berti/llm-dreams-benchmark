…symbols etched into its surface—not carved, but *breathing*. They pulse faintly, like veins beneath glass, shimmering between gold and deep indigo. Each symbol shifts as you lean closer, rearranging into words you almost understand: *“What you seek is not inside, but what you leave behind.”*

Your fingers tremble as you trace one. It tingles—not cold, not warm, but *remembering*. Suddenly, the box clicks open, though no lock turned, no key moved.

Inside is not treasure, not a message, not even emptiness.

It’s a mirror.

But the reflection isn’t yours.

It’s *you*—but older, weathered, eyes full of quiet grief and quiet grace. You’re wearing clothes you haven’t owned in years. Behind you, in the mirror’s background, is your childhood home—only it’s glowing, whole, surrounded by trees you planted as a child, now towering and alive.

The version of you in the mirror smiles, barely.

“You forgot to say goodbye,” they say. Not out loud. The words bloom inside your skull like incense smoke.

You try to speak, but your throat won’t work. Instead, you reach into the box—and pull out a single feather. Soft as a sigh. Light as regret.

The mirror whispers again:

*“Open the box again when you’re ready to let go.”*

And as you turn away, the box vanishes—but the feather remains. In your palm. Still warm.

You wake up.

The feather’s still there.

On your nightstand.

And for the first time in years, you pick up the phone…

...and dial your mother’s number.
