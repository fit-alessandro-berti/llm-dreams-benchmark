You are in the middle of a heated argument with a close friend. The disagreement is about something important to both of you, and you decide to stop shouting.

Not because you've lost steam—but because you suddenly notice something impossible.

The walls around you are made of shifting bookshelves, each volume glowing faintly with memories: your childhood birthdays, the road trip where you got lost and sang off-key for hours, the hospital waiting room where you held each other’s hands through the night. Every shelf pulses faintly, as if breathing.

Your friend’s voice, moments ago sharp with accusation, softens into a whisper.

“I didn’t mean to make you feel invisible,” they say.

And you realize—

You’re both dreaming.

The room trembles slightly. A page floats down from above, landing softly between you. On it, written in your handwriting—but you don’t remember writing it—is a single line:

*“We don’t have to be right. We just have to be here.”*

You look up. Your friend’s face is not quite theirs. Their eyes shimmer like rain on pavement. They’re not just your friend—they’re the part of you that still believes in reconciliation. And you? You’re the part that’s been too afraid to say sorry.

A third voice, gentle as a lullaby, speaks from the shelves:

*“All arguments are just lonely people crying for a mirror.”*

You reach out. Your hand trembles. Not to win. Not to prove. But to touch.

Your fingers meet theirs.

And the room dissolves—not into silence, but into the sound of your own heartbeat, echoing inside a warm, familiar chest. You wake up.

In your bed.

Alone.

Or so you think.

On your nightstand, where there was nothing before, lies a half-written note in your friend’s handwriting:

*“I’m sorry I forgot to say I love you before I started yelling. Let’s go get dumplings tomorrow? I’ll pay.”*

And for the first time in months—you smile.

Because sometimes, the most real thing about a dream…  
…is how it helps you wake up.
