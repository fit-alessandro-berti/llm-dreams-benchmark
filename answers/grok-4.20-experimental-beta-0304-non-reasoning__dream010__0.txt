You are participating in a competition where the grand prize is your dream job. As the competition progresses, you face various challenges and...

...suddenly the rules start to melt. The other contestants aren't people anymore—they're versions of yourself from different moments in your life. The version of you that was too afraid to apply for this job in the first place is currently trying to sabotage you from the sidelines, whispering that you don't deserve it.

The next challenge is "The Mirror Room."

You step inside and every surface reflects not your current self, but who you could have become if you'd made one different choice at every major turning point in your life. One reflection is wildly successful but empty-eyed. Another is happy but broke. A third looks exactly like you do now... except they're already sitting at the desk of your dream job, smiling at you with quiet, knowing pity.

The final challenge is simply this: you have to walk across a bridge made entirely of every time you said "I'm not ready yet."

The bridge is long. Some planks are paper-thin. Others are solid as oak. As you take each step, you hear the exact moment you spoke those words—every excuse, every doubt, every "maybe later."

Halfway across, the bridge begins to dissolve behind you. There's no going back.

You start running.

The finish line isn't a podium or a trophy. It's a door with your name on it—the office of your dream job. But when you finally burst through, panting and triumphant, you find the room completely empty except for a single mirror.

In it, you see yourself... already sitting at the desk.

You've been here the whole time.

The competition was never against the others.

It was against the version of you that didn't believe you could win.

You turn around to see every past version of yourself standing at the threshold, watching. The scared one. The lazy one. The one who settled. The one who gave up.

They all begin to slowly applaud.

You wake up with the taste of possibility still in your mouth, heart racing, already reaching for your laptop before your eyes are fully open.

The dream job isn't the prize.

Waking up and finally beginning is.
