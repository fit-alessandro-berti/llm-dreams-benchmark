You wake up in a completely different era, with no modern technology around. As you explore your new surroundings, you realize you need to **find the forgotten key**—the one object that can open the door between worlds before the dream ends and you're lost here forever.

The first thing you notice is the silence. No hum of engines, no distant traffic, no glowing rectangles in your pocket. Just birdsong, the creak of wooden beams, and the faint crackle of a hearth fire somewhere nearby. You sit up on a straw pallet in a low-ceilinged room lit only by a single beeswax candle. Your clothes are rough-spun wool and linen, unfamiliar but comfortable. Through the open shutter you see dawn breaking over a cluster of thatched cottages nestled against rolling green hills. Smoke rises from chimneys. A woman in a long skirt carries a bucket from a well. A boy leads a goat down a dirt path.

You step outside and the air smells of wet earth, fresh-cut hay, and woodsmoke. People nod politely but look at you with quiet curiosity—your modern haircut, your way of walking, the way you keep glancing upward as if expecting satellites or airplanes. A blacksmith hammers rhythmically at an anvil. A priest in a simple robe walks past carrying a staff carved with strange symbols. No one seems shocked by your presence; it’s as if the village has seen strangers before… or perhaps they simply accept that the world is full of mysteries.

You realize you need to find the key before nightfall. The dream has rules—you can feel them humming in your bones. The key is not metal. It’s something older: a small, smooth river stone etched with a spiral that matches the one that appeared in your mind the moment you woke. Legend in this village says the stone was carried here centuries ago by a traveler who “fell through the sky.” Only someone who truly belongs to neither time can wield it.

You spend the day asking gentle questions, trading a story from your own world for a bowl of barley stew and a crust of dark bread. The miller’s wife tells you the stone lies in the hollow oak at the edge of the forest, guarded by nothing but an old riddle: *“What has roots as nobody sees, is taller than trees, up, up it goes, and yet never grows?”* (The answer, you realize with a smile, is a mountain—the same one that looms on the horizon, its peak hidden in mist.)

At twilight you reach the oak. Inside the hollow you find the stone, warm to the touch, glowing faintly with the same soft light that once lit your phone screen. The moment your fingers close around it, the world tilts. The village fades like mist. You hear the blacksmith’s hammer slow, then stop. The priest raises his staff in farewell. Somewhere a rooster crows, but it sounds like your alarm clock.

You wake up in your own bed, heart pounding, the dream already slipping away—except for one thing: on your nightstand lies a small river stone etched with a perfect spiral, still faintly warm.

The dream is complete. But part of you wonders… if you ever need to return, the stone will be waiting.
