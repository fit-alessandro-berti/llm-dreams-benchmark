...not written in any language you recognize. The letters shift and swim on the page, rearranging themselves like insects crawling across white sand. You blink, and now the words are in English, but the questions make no sense:

*"If the ocean remembers its own tide, what does the wind forget?"*

*"Calculate the weight of every promise you've ever broken. Show your work."*

You look up. The exam hall stretches impossibly long now, desks receding to a vanishing point that shouldn't exist in a room this size. The other students are writing furiously, their pencils making a sound like rain on tin. But their faces—their faces are smooth and featureless as eggs.

The clock on the wall has no hands. Just a circle, going nowhere.

You look back down at your paper. There's a new question now, one that wasn't there before:

*"Describe the person you could have become. Use both sides if necessary."*

Your hand finds your pencil. It feels heavier than it should, warm, almost alive. You press it to the paper and begin to write your answer, but the graphite doesn't leave a mark. Instead, the paper *absorbs* your words, drinking them in, growing thicker and softer beneath your palm until it feels like skin.

Somewhere behind you, a door opens. Light pours through—warm, golden, the color of Sunday mornings and childhood kitchens. A voice you almost recognize calls your name.

But the exam isn't finished. There's one more question:

*"What are you afraid of finding on the other side of that door?"*

Your pencil hovers. The rain-sound of writing has stopped. Every featureless face in the hall is turned toward you now, waiting, patient as stone.

The door is still open.
