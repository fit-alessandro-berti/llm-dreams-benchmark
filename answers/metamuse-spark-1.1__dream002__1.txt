You are walking through a peaceful forest when suddenly the weather changes dramatically. You feel a storm approaching and —

for a second, everything goes completely still.

The birds stop mid-song. The leaves stop falling. Even the dust motes hanging in the sunbeams freeze in place. The air gets thick and electric, like the moment right before someone says something important, and you can taste metal and rain on the back of your tongue.

Then the forest inhales.

The wind doesn't just blow *through* the trees, it blows *from* them. All at once, every leaf turns over to show its pale underside, like a million hands turned palm-up. The light changes. The soft gold goes bruise-purple, bruise-green.

You should feel afraid. But you don't. 

You realize the low rumble you thought was thunder isn't in the sky at all — it's coming from under your feet. The ground is warm.

The storm isn't approaching. It's waking up.

A huge oak just off the path creaks and bends toward you, not threateningly, but protectively. Its trunk splits open — not like wood breaking, but like a curtain being pulled aside — and inside is hollow, dry, glowing faintly with the same firefly light that was in the sunbeams before.

You slip inside just as the sky finally breaks.

But there's no rain. Instead, the storm pours down memories. Cold, bright drops that evaporate the second they touch the forest floor, releasing little flashes: a childhood kitchen, a song you forgot, a face you haven't thought of in years, your own laugh from far away. The forest is drinking them.

You stand in the hollow of the old tree, dry, breathing slow, watching the storm wash everything clean. The wind howls your name, but gently, like it's trying to remember it.

And then you understand — you can let it howl. Or you can answer.

You lift your hands, and because this is a dream, the storm lifts with you.
