...grip the wheel until your knuckles bleach white. The wood is slick and warm beneath your hands — too warm, you think distantly, like skin.

Lightning splits the sky, but it doesn't fade. It hangs there, frozen in a jagged white scar, illuminating everything in harsh, shadowless clarity. The waves have stopped moving too, frozen mid-crash, spray suspended in the air like glass beads.

Your crew stands motionless on the deck. You turn to look at them — really look — and realize you don't recognize a single face. They are all wearing your face. Dozens of versions of you, some older, some younger, some with eyes that are entirely wrong — too dark, too far apart, like something wearing you as a mask.

One of them opens its mouth.

"You've been here before," it says. Not aloud. The words form directly behind your eyes.

The frozen wave beneath the ship begins to slowly tilt upward. The ship rises with it, impossibly steep, until you're climbing a wall of black water that reaches higher than you can see. The wheel spins freely now. You're no longer steering anything.

But you don't feel afraid. Underneath the strangeness, there's something familiar about this — the way the air tastes like copper and old rain, the specific quality of the silence. You've *been* here. You just can't remember what happens at the top.

The ship crests the wave.

Below you is no ocean. Below you is a bedroom. Your bedroom. The bed is empty, sheets pulled back, and on the nightstand a clock is spinning its hands in lazy, exhausted circles.

The version of you standing nearest — this one has gray streaks in its hair and a scar you don't have yet — leans close.

"It's time to wake up," it whispers. "But you won't remember this part."

The ship begins to fall.
