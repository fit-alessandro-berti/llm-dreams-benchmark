 grip the wheel with both hands, feeling the rain strike your face like thrown gravel.

For a moment, the whole world is only thunder and black water.

Then, through the chaos, you see it: a pale green light pulsing beneath the waves off the starboard bow. Not lightning. Not a lantern. Something deeper.

“Captain?” your first mate shouts, barely audible over the roar.

The ship lurches hard, and a wave rises like a wall, towering above the mast. Your crew freezes. You feel their fear as sharply as the salt on your tongue.

But somehow, you know what to do.

“Hold steady!” you command. “Trim the sails! Turn her toward the light!”

No one questions you. They move as one, scrambling over the slick deck, ropes snapping tight in their hands. The ship groans as you steer into the impossible glow beneath the sea.

The wave crashes down.

For a heartbeat, everything disappears.

No sound. No wind. No sky.

Then the ship breaks through—not into calmer waters, but into a vast, moonlit ocean beneath the ocean. Stars shimmer below instead of above. Strange silver fish drift through the air like birds. The storm is gone, replaced by a silence so deep it feels ancient.

Your crew gathers at the rail, speechless.

Ahead, rising from the water, is a city made of coral, glass, and gold. Its towers spiral like seashells, and at its harbor waits a figure in a cloak of seafoam, holding a lantern filled with the same green light.

You realize then that the storm was never trying to destroy you.

It was bringing you here.

The figure raises one hand in greeting, and in a voice that seems to echo inside your chest, says:

“At last, Captain. We have been waiting for you.”

You look back at your crew, then forward at the impossible city.

And though you do not know why you have been summoned, you feel the wheel beneath your hands turn warm, as if the ship itself is alive and ready.

So you lift your chin and give the order.

“Take us in.”
