Your voice catches in your throat, and suddenly the room feels too small, the walls leaning in as if they’ve been listening. Your friend’s face is flushed with anger, but beneath it you can see hurt—old hurt, the kind neither of you meant to bring into this moment.

So you take a breath and say, quietly, “I don’t want this to become bigger than us.”

The argument falters.

Outside the window, rain begins falling upward into the sky. Neither of you comments on it. In the strange logic of the dream, it feels natural. Your friend looks away, then back at you, and their expression softens just enough for you to step closer.

“I’m scared,” they admit.

And suddenly you realize you are too.

The room dissolves around you, becoming a long bridge suspended over dark water. You and your friend stand on opposite sides, the important thing between you glowing faintly on the wooden planks. For a moment, you both hesitate. Then, at the same time, you walk toward the center.

When you meet there, neither of you apologizes at first. You just stand together, listening to the rain rise into the clouds.

Then your friend says, “Can we try again?”

You nod.

And as soon as you do, the bridge turns into a familiar street from childhood, warm with afternoon light, and the anger feels far away—like something that happened to other people, in another dream.
