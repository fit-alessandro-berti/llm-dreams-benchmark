You begin to speak.

But no sound comes out.

You open your mouth, and for a second there is just a soft puff of air into the microphone, and it makes the whole auditorium hum. The crowd doesn't laugh. They lean in closer. Every single face is turned up toward you.

You look down at your hands. You're supposed to be holding notecards. Instead, you're holding a warm loaf of bread. Steam is rising off it.

Of course. The speech.

You can't remember what you were supposed to say. You can't remember what this event even is. You look out past the lights and realize the audience goes back impossibly far, rows and rows disappearing into a soft haze, like the theater has no back wall.

And then you notice - they're all wearing your clothes. Not the same outfit. *Your* clothes. The t-shirt you lost in high school. The jacket you wear to get the mail. The Halloween costume from when you were seven.

You start to laugh a little, because what else can you do? And when you laugh, finally sound comes out, but it's not your speaking voice. It's music. A single, clear note that hangs in the air.

The note makes the wooden stage under your bare feet — when did you take your shoes off? — ripple like water.

So you stop trying to remember the speech. You put the bread down on the podium, tear off a piece, and take a bite. It tastes exactly like being home.

You begin to tell them the only thing you actually know for sure:

"This is a dream."

The moment you say it, the audience exhales all at once, relieved. They were waiting for you to figure it out. A few of them start to float, just an inch above their seats. The stage lights get warm like sunlight through a window.

You don't need the microphone anymore. You don't need the stage.

You just keep talking, and with every word, the seats, the crowd, the huge expectant hall gently folds up around you like a paper crane, until it's just you, standing in that warm light, holding the rest of the loaf, with absolutely nowhere you need to be.
