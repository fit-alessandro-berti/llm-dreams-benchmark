The project lands on your desk with a heavy, wet thud, though it’s just a single sheet of paper. The words on it writhe slightly, reconfiguring themselves. The deadline isn't a date; it's just the word "Yesterday," blinking softly in a font that seems to leak a faint, gray smoke.

You look up at your boss. He’s smiling, but his teeth are too uniform, like the keys of a brand-new keyboard that's never been touched. “Well?” he asks, but his lips don't move in time with the sound. The air around him smells faintly of ozone and toner ink.

You open your mouth to explain the impossibility, but what comes out is, “I’ll just need to recalibrate the core memory sequencer.” The words aren't yours. They feel like they slid into your mouth from somewhere else, cold and perfectly shaped, like ice cubes made of jargon. Your boss’s smile widens, a single pixel at a time. “Naturally.”

You turn to your computer, but it’s no longer a screen. It’s a fishtank, filled with humming, neon-blue eels that twist into the shapes of spreadsheets. One of them stares at you with a depressingly human eye and says, in the voice of a colleague you vaguely dislike, “The syntax on the Q4 reports is having an existential crisis. It’s your ticket.”

This makes perfect sense. Your ticket. The project is a train. You don't have to build it, you just have to catch it. You push away from your desk, which is now inexplicably on a dune of fine, white sand. Your chair wheels squeak a symphony in B-flat minor.

You run, not down the office hallway, but into a tunnel of perfectly stacked, vibrating Jell-O cubes, each one containing a memory of a meeting you never attended. You can feel the deadline breathing down your neck—it’s a warm, damp wind that whispers forgotten passwords into your ear. You clutch the smoke-leaking paper, which has now folded itself into a paper airplane with a tiny, sharp-toothed mouth. It bites your thumb affectionately.

“The conference room is a metaphor for the unconscious mind,” your boss calls from a thousand miles away, his voice now the gentle hum of a server farm. “And the meeting has already started without you. The client is finally awake.”

You burst through a door that wasn't there before and find yourself not in a boardroom, but standing on the edge of your own bed. The moonlight is spilling through the window, pooling on the floor like a rejected proof of concept. The client is there, sitting cross-legged in the middle of your blanket. It is a giant, serene tortoise with an old-fashioned rotary phone fused to its shell. The phone begins to ring, a sharp, analog sound that perfectly slices the digital silence.

The project snaps into sharp focus. The impossible deadline. The thing you had to do. You know the answer now. You walk over to the tortoise, pick up the heavy, bakelite receiver, and hold it to your ear. You don’t say hello.

You take a deep breath and, with the absolute confidence of a dreamer who has solved the unsolvable riddle, you say, “No, thank you. I’m just browsing.”

The tortoise blinks one ancient, knowing eye. The receiver clicks. A dial tone of specific, profound relief washes over you. You wake up.
