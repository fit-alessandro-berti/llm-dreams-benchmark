I sit down, but the chair is made of water. It holds me anyway.

I reach for the keyboard, but the keys are soft like clay, and when I press them, they don't type letters. They bloom. Small, glowing flowers push up through the plastic, filling the screen with pollen that smells like rain. My boss leans over my shoulder, but their face is a blank, smooth stone. I can feel their breath, though; it tastes like old copper.

"The time is running out," they say, but the voice comes from the ceiling fan, spinning slowly above.

I look at the wall clock. The numbers are melting, dripping down the face like wax. The deadline isn't a time anymore; it's a color. A deep, pulsing violet that is creeping across the floor, swallowing the carpet tiles. I have to finish before the violet reaches my feet.

I stop trying to type. Instead, I close my eyes and imagine the project. In the dream, I realize the work isn't something I *do*, it's something I *remember*. I remember the solution. It was there all along, hidden in the hum of the fluorescent lights.

I open my mouth to speak the answer, but instead of words, a flock of origami birds flies out. They swirl around the office, folding themselves into the walls, repairing the cracks, painting the windows with sunlight that isn't there. The violet color stops at my ankles.

The stone-faced boss claps their hands. The sound is like a bell tolling underwater.

"Good," the ceiling fan whispers. "Now you can wake up."

The office dissolves into white fog, and I feel the sensation of falling, just before my alarm clock begins to ring.
