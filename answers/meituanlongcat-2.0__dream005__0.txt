The first group gathers under chandeliers made of frozen tears. They are all humming a childhood lullaby in reverse, and one of them hands you a key that is warm like a heartbeat. "This opens the next room," she says, "but leave your name at the door." You do, and your name shatters like glass when it hits the floor.

The second room is a ballroom where everyone dances with their own reflections. The reflections are not mirroring movements—they are leading. You join a slow waltz, and your partner is your own face, but older, with eyes that have seen the end of the world. "You're getting closer," your reflection whispers, "but you must forget the way back." You nod, and the memory of the entrance dissolves.

In the third room, people are building a staircase out of whispered secrets. Each step is a confession you never made. You climb, and with every step, you feel lighter, as if the weight of your unspoken truths is being left behind. At the top, a door stands ajar, and through it you see a garden where the flowers are all the colors of forgotten dreams.

Finally, you step into the garden, and there they are—the one person you know. But they are not alone. They are surrounded by versions of you: you as a child, you as an old man, you as a stranger you might have been. They turn to you and smile, and you realize they have been searching for you too, through all the same rooms, shedding their own names and memories. When you reach out to touch their hand, your fingers pass through like mist, and you understand: you are the gathering, and the gathering is you, dreaming of a friend who is just another face of yourself, waiting to be recognized.
