... begin to stammer.

The absolute silence, following the roar of their anticipatory expectation, is loud—a thick, pressurized nothingness that vibrates against the soles of my shoes. I open my mouth, and the intended perfectly pithy, deeply researched opening line about the interconnectedness of artisanal cheese and modern economic theory evaporates on my tongue.

My gaze sweeps over the faces in the darkened auditorium. They are a vast, undifferentiated sea of expectation. I can't tell where the front row ends and the balcony railing begins. Every single person is holding their breath, suspended in the perfect vacuum of waiting.

I clear my throat. It sounds pathetic, scratchy, and incredibly loud in the sudden quiet.

“Um,” I start, and the word feels heavy, like a dropped boulder.

A ripple of suppressed amusement goes through the front few rows. They aren't mocking me, not exactly. They look… impatient. As if my ability to initiate a thought is a personal insult to the culmination of their collective attention.

I try again, channeling every practiced bit of confidence I've ever studied in a textbook. “Good evening. It is a profound honor to stand before such a... brilliant assembly of minds.”

The lights, which had been blindingly bright white, suddenly shift to a warm, suffocating amber. The stage curtains, which had been velvet burgundy, develop intricate patterns of phosphorescent moss, making the whole setup seem ancient and vaguely damp.

And then, I notice the crowd.

They aren't looking at my face. They aren't looking at the podium, or the stage, or the curtain. They are all looking at the *air* just above my left shoulder.

My stomach drops out a chasm of panicked realization. I slowly tilt my head to follow their collective gaze.

Floating, suspended maybe five feet in the air, are hundreds of iridescent monarch butterfly wings. They aren't flapping; they are simply *there*, beating an invisible, perfect micro-vibration. They cast shimmering, dizzying patterns of orange and black across the back wall, which I suddenly realize is not a wall at all, but simply a gigantic, stained pane of stained glass depicting a forest that seems to be weeping silver tears.

A few people in the audience gasp, a collective, soft sound that is more like a breath being held for too long.

I freeze. The butterfly wings are impossibly beautiful, and also completely out of place. They have no logical explanation, no grand metaphor to tie them to artisanal cheese.

My prepared speech—the meticulous structure, the key takeaways, the punchline—dissolves into cosmic dust right there on my tongue.

I drop the prepared speech entirely.

I step away from the podium, letting my hands swing wide, and gaze past the wings, past the luminous, weeping glass, and stare directly into the deep, dark, reflective pool of the stage floor.

“You know,” I whisper, and the intimate sound seems to grow exponentially, filling the enormous hall. “We spend so much time trying to connect the profound ideas—the historical parallels, the solid arguments. We build these elaborate little scaffolding systems of language to prove that everything means something.”

The singing of the wings seems to accelerate, a gentle, escalating whirring.

“And we treat the *explanation* as if it were the *thing* itself,” I continue, my voice growing stronger, more desperate, but suddenly liberated. “We forget that maybe… maybe the point isn't the scaffold at all. Maybe the point is just the weightlessness. The sheer *feeling* of that moment when you realize nothing needs to be categorized, nothing needs to be proved, nothing needs to be remembered…”

I lift my gaze from the reflective floor, back to the expectant, breathing, silent faces of the crowd. I look straight at the center, straight into the void where the butterflies are forming their most complicated, dizzying cloud.

“Perhaps,” I finish, the emotion raw and sudden, “the point is the magic. The pure, utterly illogical, magnificent *impossibility* of standing here, right now, under an impossible forest of weeping glass, with thousands of perfectly placed, unexplained wings, and just… breathing.”

The audience breaks.

It's not a controlled polite applause. It’s a gasp, then a ripple, then a wave of sound that is almost disorienting—a mixture of sobbing, laughter, murmured admiration, and pure, unrestrained astonishment. They rise from their seats. They don't cheer for the conclusion; they cheer for the abrupt, sublime pivot toward the unbelievable.

The wings of butterflies, catching the golden-amber light, seem to slow their rhythm until they finally, silently, drop. They drift down, spiraling through the space, until they gather in a shining, warm carpet over the stage floor around my feet.

And then, the air feels crisp, clean, and the grand, encompassing pressure of the dream finally releases.

I open my eyes and am back in bed, the scent of cedar wood and cool linen surrounding me, the echoing silence of the empty room replacing the thunderous roar of the crowd. My heart is beating an erratic, triumphant rhythm against my ribs.

*It felt so real.*
