You notice the questions are written in your own handwriting from when you were seven years old—big, looping letters with the occasional backwards "S." But the content is all wrong. Question 1: "If your future self is already disappointed in you, does that mean you can skip the exam and go play in the rain instead?" Question 2 asks you to diagram the sentence "I forgot why I came here" using only the emotions you felt during your first school dance. The multiple-choice options for Question 3 are all versions of you: one in a lab coat, one holding a guitar case, one in a cubicle with dead eyes, and one simply labeled "the one who left."

The room is getting smaller. Or maybe you're getting bigger. Your desk is now the kitchen table from your childhood home, and the other students have all turned into your parents, your first boss, and that one teacher who said you'd never amount to anything. They're all grading your paper in real time, murmuring. The clock on the wall has no hands, just a single word spinning slowly in the center: "Almost."

You start writing anyway. Your pencil is a broken crayon. The words you put down aren't answers—they're just the things you never said out loud. When you look up, the exam proctor is you, but older, and they're smiling like they already know how this ends. They slide a new page across the desk. This one is blank except for one line at the top:

"What if the test was never about the career?"

The lights flicker. The room smells like rain and old paper. You feel your heartbeat in your throat, and for one long second you understand everything and nothing at the same time. Then the bell rings—not the exam bell, but the one from the ice cream truck you used to chase down the street when you were small.

You wake up with the taste of crayon in your mouth and your heart still racing.
