written in a language that shifts as you read it. The first question is in English, the second in what might be Arabic or Hebrew, the third in geometric symbols that pulse gently on the page. By question seven, the text has become sheet music. Question twelve is a recipe for your grandmother's pierogi, written in her handwriting, though she died before you started this program.

The proctor is a version of yourself from three years ago, wearing a sweater you donated to Goodwill. She walks the aisles on knees that don't bend quite right. When she passes your desk, you smell rain on hot asphalt and your first boyfriend's cologne.

You reach for your pen but your hand holds a paintbrush. Then a scalpel. Then a garden trowel. The exam booklet grows heavier. Its pages sprout moss at the edges. Question twenty-three asks you to diagram the precise angle of sunlight on your childhood bedroom floor at 4:47 PM on the Tuesday your parents told you they were separating. You don't remember the angle. You remember the dust motes. You remember how your mother's voice cracked on the word *trying*.

The clock on the wall has no numbers. Its hands are fingers. They count backward.

Someone in the back row begins to cry, but the sound comes out as violin music. The proctor-you kneels beside them and places a hand on their shoulder. Where her fingers touch, the desk blossoms—actual flowers, white roses pushing through laminate, roots cracking the floor.

You look down at question one again. It now reads: *Name the moment you stopped believing you were allowed to take up space.*

Your paintbrush-scalpel-trowel hovers. The moss creeps across your knuckles. Through the window—when did a window appear?—you can see the campus quad, but it's underwater. Koi the size of sedans glide between the library spires. A figure stands on the bell tower, waving a white flag. It might be you. It might be everyone you've ever disappointed.

The proctor-you appears beside your desk. Her face is smooth, unlined by the three years of grief and joy and mundane miracles you've lived since she existed. She whispers: *You don't have to answer all of them. You just have to stay in the room.*

The exam booklet catches fire. The flames are cold. They burn blue and smell like mint.

You wake with your fingers pressed to your sternum, clutching nothing.
