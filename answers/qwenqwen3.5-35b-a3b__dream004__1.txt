As you explore this surreal landscape, you come across a blank canvas and it hums.

It is not wood or linen, but a suspended sheet of pure, matte light, hovering at eye level in the center of a void where the ground is made of floating, liquid glass. The air around it tastes like static electricity, sharp and cool. Above the canvas, the sky ripples like oil on water, shifting from a violent, angry violet to a soft, soothing amber. But this canvas remains untouched by the chaos. It is a stillness in the storm.

You reach out, but your fingers do not touch the surface. Instead, the moment your intent sharpens, a brush materializes in your palm. It feels warm, heavy with the weight of every forgotten dream you've ever had. The bristles are not animal hair but wisps of your own memories, smelling of rain and old paperbacks.

You realize then that you are not here to paint *on* the canvas. You are here to paint *into* it.

You dip the brush. The white surface ripples like a lake disturbed by a stone. Instead of paint, a color bleeds out from the tip of the brush that doesn't exist in the waking world㻡 hue that sounds like a cello playing a low note. You drag it across the void. Where the stroke lands, the chaos of the world solidifies. A floating shape made of liquid glass coalesces into a door. A drifting cloud becomes a bird that sings a language you understand perfectly, though you haven't heard it in decades.

You paint furiously. You paint a blue key that opens nothing but the memory of a house you lived in as a child. You paint a red circle that represents the silence you felt during your last real argument. The canvas grows heavy, pulling at your wrists. The world around you stops spinning, drawn into the painting's gravity, collapsing into the image you are creating.

But then, the painting begins to shift on its own. The brush slips from your fingers. You watch as the blue key melts into the red circle. The landscape reflects itself back on the canvas, but it is upside down. You realize with a sudden, jarring lurch that you are not the artist.

The canvas is painting you.

The strokes of the liquid colors rise up to wrap around your arms. The door you painted opens, but instead of a room, it opens into a blinding white light. The canvas expands, the frame dissolving into dust, the white light engulfing the shifting shapes of the world.

You feel yourself being pulled toward the canvas, not by gravity, but by a magnetic pull of memory. You try to hold onto the sensation of your feet, the air in your lungs, the weight of the brush. But they are dissolving into the stroke of a color you can't name.

And then, the silence breaks. Not a noise, but a sensation. A cool breath against your neck. The hum of a distant fan. The rough texture of a cotton blanket against your cheek.

You open your eyes. The room is dark, illuminated only by the streetlights outside. But for a fleeting second, before sleep takes you again, you swear you see the outline of a blue door on the wall, painted in the dark.
