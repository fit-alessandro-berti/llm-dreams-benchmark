… and a paintbrush that feels warm in your hand, as if it’s been waiting for your pulse.

At first, the canvas is truly blank—no texture, no grain, no hint of a surface—just an endless, quiet white that seems to swallow sound. You lift the brush. The bristles don’t touch the canvas so much as *hover above it*, and you realize the paint isn’t “in” the brush. It’s in you. The moment you decide to make a mark, the world behind you flickers—like a film reel skipping—and the colors outside the canvas begin to thin out.

You try a simple line. A single stroke appears: a ribbon of blue, perfectly clean, impossibly vivid. It doesn’t just sit on the canvas; it *sings* in a way you can feel in your teeth. The blue line stretches farther than your hand moves, curving toward something you can’t quite name—like a path leading to a place you’ve forgotten how to reach.

You look up, and the landscape around you is changing faster now. Triangles become circles midair. Shadows detach from objects and drift like slow jellyfish. The sky briefly turns into a close-up of your own eye, then flips back to a sky made of moving watercolor.

The blank canvas responds to your attention. When you hesitate, the white becomes gray, then darker, as if it’s learning the meaning of doubt. When you try again—this time with a circle—the circle refuses to stay flat. It bulges forward, becoming a tiny doorway framed in gold. Behind it, you see a room made of shifting shapes: shelves that slide into ladders, windows that open onto other windows.

You reach toward the doorway, and the paintbrush *pulls back*, not physically—more like a gentle refusal written into the air.

A voice appears—not spoken, but formed from the edges of the colors. It feels less like a question and more like a rule:

**“You can draw a way in,”** it says, **“but you can’t draw a way out unless you remember what you came from.”**

You try to remember. The dream gives you flashes—your waking life like a quick slideshow: the last thing you said to someone, the smell of something familiar, the weight of your own hands. But each memory, the moment you grasp it, dissolves into new hues. The world won’t hold still long enough for you to keep anything.

So you change tactics.

Instead of drawing a doorway, you draw a *constellation*—not of stars, but of moments. You paint a small red dot. It matches the color of urgency, the sensation of a clock leaning forward. You paint a green smear beside it, and it becomes the feeling of relief after rain. A thin black thread appears, then you realize it’s not ink—it’s the outline of a thought you once tried to hide from yourself.

Each mark you make becomes a key. Not a key to open a door, but a key to unlock a feeling. The canvas begins to hum, and the changing world outside starts to synchronize with your rhythm. The triangles stop flipping. The circles stop unraveling. Colors settle into palettes that look almost intentional.

The voice returns, softer now:

**“Good,”** it says. **“Now make something you can carry.”**

You realize you can’t keep drawing doors forever. Eventually, you will have to live with what you create. You need something that won’t vanish the moment you stop looking at it.

You decide to draw a small object: not a trap, not a shortcut—something that can exist in any version of reality. You draw a single candle flame. The flame is simple, but when it forms, the air around you warms. The shadows settle. Even the whispering shapes in the landscape quiet like people listening.

The flame doesn’t burn. It *remembers heat*.

With the candle on the canvas, the doorway doorway-frame—gold and impossible—fades into something steadier: a window, not a portal. You can sense the difference immediately, like the way you know whether a song is a lullaby or a warning.

You step closer, and the canvas grows large enough to feel like a wall you can lean against. Your reflection appears in the paint—except your reflection is made of the colors that used to be everywhere else. It looks like you have become a map of your own shifting mind.

The window opens.

On the other side is not another world. It’s your own dreamspace, but arranged—cleaner, quieter—like it has been put back in order by someone patient. The colors are still changing, but they change more slowly, as if they’re listening now.

Before you cross, the paintbrush warms further, and the voice gives you one final instruction:

**“Carry the candle. Leave the rest.”**

So you do.

You press your thumb against the painted flame, and the waxy-gold color transfers into your skin. Not as an injury—more like a brand of light. When you pull your hand away, the candle on the canvas remains, steady and complete. The window stays open just long enough for you to choose what you’ll bring with you.

You step through.

The world pauses—just for a breath. For the first time since you arrived, shapes hold their forms without collapsing into new ideas. Colors wait their turn.

And then you wake up.

Only when you try to remember the candle, you realize something strange: it isn’t fading. The warmth is still there, tucked behind your ribs, like a tiny lantern you can feel even with your eyes closed.

You look around your room—everything solid, everything ordinary.

But in the corner of your vision, you swear you can still see the blank canvas, faint as a thought.

And somewhere in the shifting edge of the day, you can feel the brush again, waiting—patient, warm, and sure that one day you’ll return to draw your way through whatever comes next.
