You finish your coffee, turn to your monitor, and your boss is already there—a silhouette in the doorway, backlit by the fluorescent hallway. “Project’s in,” they say, their voice flat, echoing strangely. “Client needs it by end of day. Tomorrow’s too late.”

You nod, heart thudding. End of day. The words hum in your ears like a distant alarm. You open the file. The screen glows, but the text is faint, almost shimmering. You blink. The deadline at the top flickers—it now says “End of hour.”

A bead of sweat traces your temple. The air in the office feels thick, warm. You glance at the clock on the wall. The second hand is moving… backward.

Your boss hasn’t moved. Their reflection in the dark screen of your monitor is watching you, even as their physical form stands in the doorway. The reflection smiles. You look away.

You start typing. The keyboard feels soft, yielding, like pressing into warm clay. The letters you type appear, then slowly dissolve into a fine, golden dust that drifts off the screen.

The project brief rearranges itself. The requirements are now in a language you’ve never seen—geometric, pulsing. A single word you do understand floats to the center: **NOW.**

The hum of the office servers deepens into a low, resonant tone. The ceiling lights dim, one by one, leaving your screen the sole source of light. The golden dust from the keys gathers in the air before you, swirling into a shape.

Your boss’s reflection in the screen steps forward, out of the glass, and stands beside you. It places a hand on your shoulder. The touch is cold, electric.

It whispers, from both the doorway and right beside your ear at once: “Don’t worry. You have all the time in the world.”

The clock on the wall shatters, its face cracking. The hands spin wildly forward, then stop. The time is blank.

You are alone with the glowing screen, the gathering dust, and the impossible task. You take a breath, and begin to dream.
