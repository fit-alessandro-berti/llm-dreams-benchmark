I wake up with a start, lying on cool, damp earth that smells of moss and old rain. The air is crisp and carries the scent of wild herbs and woodsmoke. Above me, instead of a ceiling, there's a canopy of colossal, ancient trees with leaves of an impossible, emerald green. The light filtering through is dappled and golden, like in a forgotten painting.

I sit up, my clothes strangely foreign to me—rough-spun wool and linen in muted earth tones. As I stand, I realize I’m in a small, sunlit clearing. In the distance, a cluster of thatched-roof cottages sends ribbons of smoke into the sky. The only sounds are a trickling stream, birdsong, and the faint, rhythmic sound of a distant axe.

As I explore my new surroundings, I realize I need to find my way back. But a deeper, more pressing need quickly surfaces: **I need to find fresh water and something to eat.** My stomach is hollow, and the stream looks clean, but I have no modern tools to boil or purify it. I also need to understand the rules of this place—where I am, *when* I am, and if I can trust the people in the village. A strange, carved symbol on a nearby standing stone seems to pulse with a faint light, hinting that this might not be a simple past, but a place where the boundaries of time and dream are thinner than I think.
