You feel a storm approaching and realize the wind is blowing from every direction at once, carrying the scent of ozone and something older—like pages of a book you haven’t read yet. The trees don’t sway; they *inhale*, their roots lifting from the soil like bare feet stepping out of a bath. The sky doesn’t darken so much as turn transparent, revealing not clouds but a vast ceiling of rippling water suspended above you.

You expect thunder, but instead there is a silence so complete you can hear the sap moving in the branches. Then the first drop falls—not of rain, but of silver light. Where it strikes your skin, you don’t get wet; you *remember* things that haven’t happened. The forest floor becomes a reflection of the canopy, and you’re no longer sure if you’re standing on earth or sky.

The storm, you realize, isn’t weather. It’s a rearrangement. The trees begin to turn inside out, revealing clockwork cores and nests of sleeping stars. You reach out to touch the bark of an oak, and your hand passes through it into warm summer air—the same forest, but three years ago, or perhaps three minutes from now. 

You step fully inside the tree, and the storm follows you, not as destruction, but as a door closing behind you, sealing the forest in amber. On the other side, the rain is falling upward, and you are both the one walking and the one watching yourself walk, safe in the eye of something that was never angry, only enormous.
