and you reach for your keyboard, but your fingers have gone translucent, passing straight through the keys. The deadline glows red on the monitor—3:00 PM, but the clock on the wall is melting, its hands crawling backward. Your boss hasn’t moved; he just stands there, smiling with too many teeth, holding a coffee mug that steams with something that smells like rain and burning rubber.

You open your mouth to explain that the project—wait, what *is* the project? The email said something about *retroactive architecture for a building that hasn’t been designed yet* and *deliver yesterday*. The files on your desk are just stacks of blank paper that rustle like wings. When you pick up a pen, it turns into a fish, slippery and gasping.

The office floor tilts. The cubicle walls stretch upward like redwoods, and you realize you’re wearing pajama bottoms. Of course you are. Your boss says, “We’ll need it in five minutes,” but his voice is coming from the ceiling tiles, and also from inside your left shoe. You try to run to the conference room to find the missing data, but every step takes you further from the door, which is now across a chasm where the break room used to be, and Karen from accounting is floating there, calmly stapling clouds together.

You sit down—no, you’re already sitting. The screen blinks: **DEADLINE EXPIRED**. But your boss is still smiling, because in the logic of the dream, you haven’t started yet, you will never start, and the project is due right now, forever.
