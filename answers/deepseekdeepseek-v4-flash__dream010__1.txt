The host, a woman whose face shifts like smoke in a wind, gestures toward a vast, labyrinthine library. Shelves stretch into a hazy infinity. "Your first challenge," she says, her voice a chime of distant bells, "is to find the one book that contains the answer to a single question: *What is the one thing you must learn to truly deserve your dream job?*"

You step into the library. The books are not just books. Some are humming with low, electric light. Others weep saltwater onto the floor. One, bound in what looks like bark, grows small, anxious leaves. You remember the job you crave. For you, it’s not a CEO position or a famous artist's studio. Your dream job is to be the Curator of Forgotten Memories, a hidden role in a quiet archive where you restore the emotional echoes of moments that people have chosen to lose.

You search for hours, guided by instinct. You touch a book of woven spider-silk and feel a pang of first heartbreak. You pass a tome made of frozen breath and shiver with forgotten regret. Finally, you find a book with no title, its cover a mirror that reflects your own worried face.

You open it. The pages are blank.

Despair washes over you. But then, you look closer. The paper isn't blank. It's covered in the faintest, finest lines—not ink, but the pattern of your own fingerprints, the unique whorls that only you can see. You press your thumb to the page. The lines begin to glow, to lift from the page, and swirl into a story.

The book reads itself to you in a whisper that is your own voice. It tells you a memory you’ve forgotten: a day in your childhood when you found a dead bird in the garden. You didn't cry or run away. You sat with it for an hour. In the dirt, you drew a circle of pebbles around it. You whispered, "I'll remember you were here."

The book slams shut. The library dissolves like fog.

The host is before you, smiling. "You passed," she says. "You didn't need to find the answer. You needed to remember you already knew it. A curator of memories must first be a guardian of their own."

The next challenge begins. The floor vanishes, and you are standing on a tightrope. Below, instead of a net, there is a roiling sea of other people's incomplete thoughts, fragmented sentences that buzz and sting like gnats. "Complete this sentence," says a disembodied voice, the source of the tightrope, "without losing your balance: 'The memory I am most afraid to touch is...'"

You take a slow, steadying breath. The tightrope vibrates with the pressure of your own buried pasts. You know you could lie, give a safe answer. But the dream job is truth. You look down at the swarm of buzzing thoughts, at the audience of shifting shadows who represent all the people whose memories you hope to guard.

You whisper your answer, and the tightrope steadies. "The memory I am most afraid to touch is the moment right before I gave up on a dream the first time. Because I am afraid it will feel exactly the same as this moment, and I will freeze."

A pure, clear note rings out. Below, the angry sea of thoughts calms into a placid, mirror-like lake. You are not on a tightrope anymore, but standing on a bridge of solid, polished obsidian.

The host's shifting face settles, for just a moment, into a look of deep, genuine recognition. "The final challenge," she says, her voice now warm as a hand on your shoulder. "Is behind you."

You turn. On the bridge is a single, simple door. It’s the door to the archive. You’ve been standing in front of it the entire time. The competition wasn't a journey to the prize. The prize was the journey of proving you would be gentle with the weight you were about to hold.

You reach for the handle. It's cold iron, shaped like an open eye. As your fingers close around it, the dream begins to shimmer, to vibrate, to pull you gently toward waking.

The host’s voice is the last thing you hear, fading like a beautiful echo: "The door is always open for those who know how to see the faint lines on a blank page. See you in the stacks..."

But you haven't opened the door yet. You’re still in the dream. Do you turn the handle and step inside to see your new archive, or do you hesitate, wanting to ask one last question?
