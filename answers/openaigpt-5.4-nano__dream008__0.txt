…hand me a clipboard like it’s a lifeline, except the pages are blank except for one line written in red:

**“Deliver the impossible.”**

I look up, expecting a joke, but my boss doesn’t smile. Their eyes flick once toward the clock mounted high on the wall—an enormous, round clock with numbers that seem slightly wrong, as if they’ve been copied from a different time zone. The second hand doesn’t tick. It *slides*, smoothly, like it’s being dragged by invisible string.

“Start,” they say.

The moment they speak, my desk rearranges itself. Files slide out of drawers that weren’t there before. A phone I’ve never used lights up with messages that already have timestamps—messages I supposedly sent months ago. My keyboard feels heavier than it should, as if made of stone that learned how to type.

I try to ask for clarification, but the words in my mouth turn into keys clacking under my fingers. My hands begin typing on their own, and the document header reads:

**PROJECT: MIRROR ORBIT // PHASE ZERO**

A progress bar appears at the top of the screen. It is stuck at **0%**, but the percentage keeps changing in the background like it’s being recalculated by something impatient. Every time I blink, the cursor jumps to a different section of the document, writing sentences that don’t belong to any language I know but somehow still *make sense*.

Across the room, coworkers are moving too. Not walking—*circling*. They orbit their own desks like satellites, dragging chairs with a faint squeal, eyes fixed on screens that glow without any content. Their mouths open and close in silent loops, as if repeating answers to questions nobody asked.

I turn toward the clock again.

The deadline isn’t a time. It’s a shape.

A countdown appears on the wall in large digital numbers, but between the digits there are commas that look like doorways. The numbers keep collapsing toward each other like sand running through an hourglass that’s been inverted.

**00:59…** then **00:59…** then **00:59…**

It can’t be counting down. It’s stuck. But I can still feel pressure building, like gravity strengthening with every breath.

My boss comes closer, and now I notice something I didn’t before: their shadow is missing. Not absent—*separated*, hanging behind them like a second person made of ink. When they step, the shadow hesitates a fraction of a second, then follows late, as if learning the rules.

“You’ve done this before,” my boss says, and their voice doesn’t come from their mouth. It comes from the air above the desk, from the empty clipboard, from the blank pages that suddenly fill with handwriting I recognize as my own—scribbled formulas, diagrams, and a checklist of things I’ve never built.

At the top of the clipboard is a sticky note:

**DON’T FINISH IT. ONLY START IT.**

I swallow. “What does that mean?”

My coworker who’s closest—still circling, still silent—stops for the smallest moment, just long enough for their eyes to meet mine. Their pupils are too large, like someone stared into a camera flash. Then they resume orbiting, but their mouth forms a word.

It isn’t sound. It’s an image:

A door.

Not a metaphorical door. A real, physical door appears at the end of the hallway behind my cubicle, where there should only be more wall. The door is made of frosted glass, and inside it is a room lit by the same sickly fluorescent light as our office, but the space is impossibly deep—like looking into a tunnel that remembers other tunnels.

There’s a handle on our side, but it’s on the wrong side of the door, as if physics got confused.

My boss nods toward it. “Start there.”

“What about the project?” I ask, and my voice finally works.

My boss’s smile is thin, and it doesn’t reach their eyes. “The project will start you.”

The clock’s pressure intensifies. The seconds begin to blur, not because time is moving faster, but because it’s forgetting how to be measured. My hands start writing again without me. This time the document title changes:

**DELIVERABLE: ONE BEGINNING**

The progress bar fills instantly to **1%**—but when I look closer, the “1%” is not numeric. It’s a tiny opening in the shape of a percent sign, like a keyhole.

I stand, chair scraping across the floor. The sound is loud, too loud, like it wakes someone. Every orbiting coworker reacts at once, turning their heads toward me, their expressions blank but their bodies trembling as if waiting for a cue.

I walk to the frosted-glass door. My feet don’t quite step like they should. Each footfall lands a heartbeat late, as though the floor is buffering my movement. I reach for the handle.

It’s cold enough to sting.

When I pull, the handle doesn’t move. Instead, the door’s *frost* swirls upward, forming letters through condensation:

**YOU CANNOT ENTER WITH YOUR DEADLINE.**

My throat tightens. “Then what do I enter with?”

The air crackles. The office lights flicker. My keyboard, behind me, clacks rapidly as if someone else is typing in a different room. The clipboard in my hands warms, and the blank pages begin to fill again—this time with a single instruction written in thick, familiar ink:

**TRADE TIME FOR TRUST.**

I look back at the clock. The countdown has stopped changing. It’s frozen at a number that feels like a dare, like a trap that wants me to count it forever.

I realize something: in dreams, deadlines aren’t meant to be met. They’re meant to be *broken*.

So I do the only thing that feels like rebellion.

I stop trying to finish the project.

I close the document on my screen. The progress bar vanishes. My hands hover above the keyboard, suddenly lighter, as if they’ve been waiting for permission to be human again.

Then I place the clipboard on the floor, gently—like it’s a sleeping animal—and I take a breath that tastes like paper and rain.

I turn toward the frosted door again and, instead of pulling the handle, I press my palm flat against the glass.

The frost melts in a circle under my hand, revealing something behind it: not a room, but a *version* of my workplace where the desks are empty and the clock is gone. The air is calmer there. Softer. As if sound doesn’t have to struggle to be heard.

On that side, a single sheet of paper floats upright, held in place by invisible current. It has one line printed in crisp black ink:

**WELCOME TO THE START.**

My boss’s voice returns, quieter now, almost kind. “Now you can begin.”

I step through.

The moment my foot crosses the threshold, the impossible deadline shatters like thin ice. The fluorescent lights stop buzzing. The air stops pressing. Behind me, my coworkers begin to exhale at once, as if they’d been holding their breath for years.

On the other side of the door, my desk is there again—but this time it’s *mine*, in the way dreams sometimes make things personal. The computer boots to a blank screen. No project title. No progress bar.

Just a blinking cursor waiting like a heartbeat.

I sit down.

I lift my hands.

And this time, when I start typing, it’s not the project that begins—

It’s me.
