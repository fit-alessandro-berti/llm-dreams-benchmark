...you feel a storm approaching and the air grows heavy with an electric hum. The sky, moments ago a canvas of soft blues and drifting whites, darkens to a bruised purple. The peaceful chirping of crickets falls silent, replaced by the low, ominous growl of thunder from a horizon that shouldn't exist—because you were just walking through a forest, but now the trees seem to lean in, watching.

You quicken your pace, but the path behind you vanishes, swallowed by mist that writhes like liquid shadow. The first raindrops fall, but they don’t wet you—they *ring* against your skin like tiny bells of cold iron, each one a note of warning. Then the wind howls, not through the trees, but *inside* your thoughts, tugging at memories you’ve buried: a childhood argument, a goodbye you never properly said, the echo of a name you’ve forbidden yourself to speak.

Lightning splits the sky—not in jagged forks, but in slow, deliberate strokes, painting silhouettes on the trunks around you. In one flash, you see your own reflection in a rain-streaked trunk: older, perhaps, or younger, wearing a face you don’t recognize, mouthing a word you can’t hear. The thunder that follows isn’t sound—it’s a *feeling*, a pressure in your chest that makes your heart stutter in time with the distant, rolling menace.

You realize the storm isn’t outside you. 
It’s learning your shape.

And as the final branch snaps overhead—not from wind, but from some unseen weight—your dream-self stops running. The rain begins to fall upward, gathering in the hollow of your palms like liquid starlight. You remember now: in dreams, you don’t just walk through storms. 
You breathe them in.
