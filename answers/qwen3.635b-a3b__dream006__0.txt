Petals of amber light unfurl from your lips, drifting down to rest on the front row like fallen leaves. The audience inhales them, and for a heartbeat, every face becomes the same: calm, patient, waiting. Not for answers. For recognition.

The stage floor turns to glass. Beneath it, a slow river of names, dates, and half-remembered conversations flows. You try to keep going, but your throat fills with the smell of rain on hot pavement, and suddenly the microphone is a branch of cherry wood. You don't grip it—it rests against your palm as if it's always been there.

And then you know. You're not giving a speech. You're untangling a dream you've been having for years. The crowd isn't watching you perform. They're watching you wake up inside the story. The ceiling peels back like wallpaper. The walls breathe. Your voice becomes the only steady thing in a room learning how to forget itself.

You stop. Not because you've run out of words. Because the dream has finally handed you the pencil.
