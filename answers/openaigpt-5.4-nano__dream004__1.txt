When you touch it, it doesn’t feel like paper. It feels like warm air, like the surface of a pond just before ripples. The moment your fingers hover over it, colors begin to wake up in your wake: a thin line of blue appears, not where you pressed, but where you *thought* you would press. A triangle blooms beside it—sharp and bright—then immediately melts into a circle, as if the canvas can’t tolerate commitment for long.

You try to stay careful. You imagine a simple picture: a house, a window, a tree. But the world refuses to be simplified. The canvas keeps answering your thoughts with its own grammar of sensation.

- Your imagined house turns into a nest of geometric staircases that rearrange themselves as you blink.  
- Your imagined window becomes a square of moving weather—inside it, you can see tiny storms arguing over what shade they should be.  
- Your imagined tree grows branches made of decisions you haven’t made yet: each leaf is a question mark that keeps changing into an exclamation point, then into a pause.

You step back to understand what’s happening. The surrounding landscape responds like a choir adjusting to your tone. Distant shapes stretch and settle. Colors soften, then brighten again, as if listening for the “correct” melody.

From somewhere behind you—though there is no behind in a place where directions keep bending—a voice made of shifting gradients calls, *Not what you draw. What you choose.*

You turn, and the blank canvas begins to fill itself. Not with paint, but with memory. Images rise from the fibers like ink surfacing from water: moments you forgot you had—your first laugh, a hallway you ran down once and never returned to, a conversation you rehearsed in your head for years and never spoke aloud.

Each memory is rendered in a different style of impossible art.

One becomes a stained-glass landscape where every color has a temperature. Another becomes an origami animal that folds into a different creature each time you decide you’re done looking. Another is drawn in negative space, as though the absence of something is what holds its meaning.

You try to interrupt—your chest tightens with the urge to control the outcome. You press your palm to the canvas harder, and the entire scene warps around your touch. The colors begin to behave like animals: they scatter, regroup, and circle you. The triangles turn into teeth for a moment, then into sails, then into clocks without hands.

The voice returns, closer now, though it still comes from everywhere. *If you force it, it turns into fear. If you wait, it turns into truth.*

You realize the dream isn’t asking for an image. It’s asking for permission.

Permission to feel the things you kept folded away. Permission to let the shapes be messy. Permission to accept that change isn’t a flaw—it’s the medium.

So you stop trying to make the canvas obey. You set your hands on either side instead, like steadying a breath. You close your eyes and choose—quietly, intentionally—the first emotion that arrives when you think of yourself without editing.

Not happiness, not sadness. Something older and simpler.

Curiosity.

As soon as you choose it, the canvas does something gentle. The chaotic shifting slows. The colors stop darting and begin to flow. The house you imagined doesn’t appear as a house anymore—it appears as a *threshold*. A doorway made of light that doesn’t lead somewhere outside, but somewhere inside.

Along the edge of the canvas, new shapes assemble themselves: circles become moons, moons become punctuation marks, punctuation marks become a sentence written in ripples:

*You are dreaming because you are learning how to look.*

The world around you quiets. The surreal landscape—the constantly changing colors and shapes—holds still long enough for you to see that it was never random. It was reacting to you the way a dream reacts when you finally understand you’re the one holding the pen.

You step through the doorway drawn on the canvas.

And instead of landing somewhere else, you find yourself back at the same blank space—but now the canvas is your skin, and the changing colors are your thoughts. Every time you decide, a new shape appears. Every time you hesitate, the shapes soften and rearrange.

The voice, now unmistakably you, whispers: *Finish it when you wake up.*

You look down at the canvas-body and realize the dream is unfinished on purpose. There is room for you to return later—to keep choosing how you see.

Then the colors surge one last time, flooding the world with a final impossible hue, and you wake up—still holding the feeling of curiosity in your chest, as if the canvas followed you out of the dream and left a faint, luminous outline behind.
