...build a sanctuary.

The air is thick with the scent of rust and smoke as you gather what little remains of the world—twisted metal, broken glass, and the occasional glint of something still usable. You’ve learned to listen to the wind, to the distant howls of creatures long since mutated by the world’s decay. But here, in this hidden valley, the silence is different. It hums with possibility.

You start small: a wall of scavenged wood and concrete, reinforced with the skeletal remains of old vehicles. Then a garden—hardy plants that thrive in the poisoned soil, their leaves bitter but edible. You dig a well, tapping into deep reserves of water, and rig a solar panel from the wreckage of a fallen satellite dish, its light flickering like a stubborn star.

Others find you. A child with hollow eyes, clutching a stuffed rabbit missing an ear. A woman who speaks in riddles but knows the old ways of medicine. A man who can fix anything with a wrench and a prayer. You don’t ask where they’ve come from. You only ask if they’ll stay.

At night, you tell stories by the fire—tales of a world before the collapse, of oceans that weren’t poison, of skies that weren’t choked with ash. The children listen, their faces half in shadow, half in the firelight. You wonder if they believe you. You wonder if it even matters.

One evening, as the sun bleeds into the horizon, you stand at the edge of your sanctuary and watch the horizon. Somewhere out there, the world is still burning. But here, in this fragile circle of light and life, it feels like the beginning of something new.

Then you wake up.
