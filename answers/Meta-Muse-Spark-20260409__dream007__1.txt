The door clicks shut behind you and the noise of the party muffles to a low hum. It's suddenly just the two of you on the porch, the night air cool against your skin. Both of you are still breathing hard, words from inside still hanging between you like smoke.

Your friend paces to the railing, runs a hand through their hair, and exhales. "I hate that we’re doing this," they say, not looking at you yet. "But I can't pretend this doesn't matter to me."

You lean against the doorframe. The anger hasn’t vanished, but out here it doesn’t have an audience. It’s just two people who know each other too well to walk away. 

You decide to sit down on the top step. After a second, they do too, leaving a careful foot of space between you. 

"Okay," you say, and your voice comes out rougher than you meant. "No interrupting. You go first. All of it. I’ll actually listen this time."

They blink, surprised. The fight script you’ve both been running says to keep volleying points back and forth. Choosing to listen breaks the rhythm. 

So they talk. Really talk. Not the rehearsed arguments, but the fear underneath them. Why this issue cuts so deep for them, what they’re afraid will happen if you don’t agree. And because you’re not gearing up for your next rebuttal, you hear things you missed inside.

When they finish, it’s quiet except for the crickets. Your turn. You tell them your side, and the same thing happens. You say the part you were too defensive to admit earlier. The part that isn’t about being right.

Neither of you says "you’re right" right away. That’s not how it works. But the heat drains out of the argument and leaves something steadier. 

Your friend nudges your shoulder. "We’re terrible at this." 
"Yeah," you grin, "but we’re still here." 

You don’t solve everything on that porch. Dreams rarely wrap up that neatly. But you decide to walk back in together, not as opponents. As people who remembered why the friendship was worth arguing for in the first place.

Then you wake up, and the feeling that lingers isn’t the anger. It’s the relief of choosing the relationship over winning.
