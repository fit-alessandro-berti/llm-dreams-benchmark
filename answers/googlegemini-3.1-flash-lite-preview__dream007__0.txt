...and you decide to stop using words, because the air between you has grown too thick to breathe. 

Instead, you turn toward the window. The room you are in—a familiar living room from your childhood—suddenly begins to lose its edges. The walls are dissolving into a soft, bioluminescent indigo mist, like the underside of a wave. 

You look back at your friend. Their face is flickering, the features shifting like light dappled on water. They aren't angry anymore; they look confused, as if they are trying to remember the script of the argument they were just participating in. 

"Do you hear that?" they whisper, though their voice sounds like it’s coming from miles away, echoing through a long, copper pipe.

You listen. Beneath the silence, there is a low, rhythmic thrumming—the sound of the world’s foundation breathing. You realize then that the disagreement wasn't about the topic you were arguing; it was just a tether, a frantic, human way to stay anchored to the ground while the floorboards beneath you started to pull apart into stars.

You reach out to touch their shoulder, but as your fingers make contact, your hand passes through them as if they were made of cool smoke. You feel a strange, profound sense of relief. The argument evaporates entirely, replaced by the crushing, beautiful realization that nothing here is solid. 

"We don't have to agree," you say, your own voice sounding foreign and distant to your ears. "We only have to wake up."

The room collapses inward. The indigo mist rushes toward you, cold and smelling of ozone and crushed mint. You take a final breath, and just as the walls close in to crush the reality of the argument, you feel the sensation of falling—a long, weightless drop through a sky that has no bottom.

Your eyes snap open. The room is dark. The silence of your bedroom is so heavy it rings in your ears. You are alone, and the argument is gone, but the ghost of it lingers, leaving your heart racing and your palm still tingling from where you tried to reach out to a shadow.
