The auditorium stretches out like a hall of mirrors, each reflection showing a different version of the job: one where I’m behind a sleek desk, one where I’m presenting to a sea of nodding faces, one where I’m laughing with a team who actually feels like family.

**The first challenge** arrives as a maze of doors. Each door has a label—*Confidence, Patience, Reputation, Fear*—and I have to choose which ones to open and which ones to leave behind. Fear’s door rattles the loudest, so I leave it closed and take Patience with me, slipping Confidence into my pocket like a lucky coin.

**The next challenge** is a debate on a stage made of clouds. My voice keeps coming out as static, then music, then whispers, until I realize the judges aren’t listening to my words—they’re watching whether I keep going anyway. So I speak in colors, in questions, in half-formed ideas that slowly find their shape. The cloud stage solidifies beneath my feet.

**Then comes the waiting room**, which is the hardest challenge of all. Hours pass like drops of honey. Other contestants shimmer in and out like ghosts. Doubt walks in wearing my own face and sits down beside me, offering a chair that looks suspiciously like giving up. I offer it a cup of tea instead and wait some more.

Finally, a single envelope floats down from the ceiling. I catch it. Inside, there’s no letter—just a key, warm to the touch, and a note that says:

*“The job was never the prize. The prize is that you already became the person who could win it.”*

The key fits a door that wasn’t there a moment ago. I turn it, and on the other side is not an office, but a sunrise over a city I’ve never seen, with my name written somewhere in the light. I step through, smiling, and somewhere in the waking world, I already know what to do next.
