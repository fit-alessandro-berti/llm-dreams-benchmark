It smells like the back of my grandma’s linen closet and summer rain, the kind of scent I haven’t breathed in years, and when I lean closer I can make out what the little scenes are: the chipped plastic slide I lost my first tooth under, the scruffy orange stray cat that followed me home from 7th grade, the crumpled half-birthday card I never mustered the courage to give my best friend before her family moved across the country. All the tiny, half-forgotten bits of my life I’d tucked away and assumed I’d lost forever.

The lock, a tiny dented brass loop that had looked solid enough when I first picked up the box, doesn’t even have a keyhole—just a faint, palm-shaped indentation exactly the size of my hand. I’d been squinting for a key this whole time, turning the box over and shaking it, listening to something soft rattle inside, and I’d missed it entirely. When I press my palm flat to the indentation, the lock clicks so loud it makes the grass under my knees rustle, and the lid creaks open on its own.

It’s not full of gold or jewels, like I’d half-expected. It’s full of all those tiny lost things: my little plastic tooth fairy container, the frayed red collar the orange cat had been wearing when I first met him, the crumpled birthday card, even the lollipop I’d hidden in my jacket pocket the day my grandma died and forgot to eat. At the bottom, there’s a handwritten note in my own 10-year-old scrawl: You never lost any of it. You just put it somewhere safe for when you needed it back.

Before I can reach for the note, the box stretches, its edges blowing out like smoke, and suddenly I’m not kneeling in my childhood backyard anymore. I’m on grandma’s back porch, the sun warm on my face, the orange stray curled up on the step purring, and grandma is leaning in the kitchen doorway holding a tray of chocolate chip cookies, flour still smudged on her apron. She laughs, the same crinkly laugh I’d memorized as a kid, and says, “Took you long enough to figure out the only key you ever needed was the part of you that never forgot how to miss these things. The box was never locked, baby. You were just too busy running forward to stop and open it.”
