The air smells of wet slate, woodsmoke, and a sharp, wild mint that doesn't exist in the world you left behind. Towering ferns rise like green ribcages against a sky the color of bruised plums. There are no power lines slicing the clouds, no hum of distant traffic—only the terrifying, beautiful silence of a world yet unpaved.

I look down at my hands. They are stained with the blue juice of woad berries, and my modern clothes have dissolved into a heavy, coarse-spun tunic of undyed wool. Dream-logic whispers in my ear, cold and urgent: *The villagers here do not speak your language, but they know the shape of an outsider. If they find you, they will offer you to the river.*

I begin to run, my bare feet sinking into moss so thick it feels like treading on velvet water. The trees begin to thin, revealing a valley dotted with low, circular stone huts topped with turf. Plumes of grey smoke spiral lazily upward. In the center of the settlement, suspended between two massive, ancient oaks, hangs a giant wooden frame—the Loom.

As I draw closer, concealing myself behind a ring of standing stones, I see the threads. They aren't made of wool. They are spun from liquid light, shifting from gold to twilight blue. A figure in a cloak of crow feathers stands before it, throwing a shuttle back and forth with rhythmic, heartbeat thuds. 

With every pass of the shuttle, my memories of the modern world—the glow of my phone, the hum of the refrigerator, the faces of those I love—begin to fray, like loose threads pulled from a sweater. I realize that to wake up, I cannot run away. I have to step forward, reach into the warp and weft of the light, and weave my own thread back into the pattern before the final knot is tied.
