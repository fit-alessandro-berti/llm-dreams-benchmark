The first one reads: *Where did you put the key to the door you never opened?* Beneath it, the answer choices aren't letters at all, but tiny objects resting in neat rows: a spool of red thread, a smooth river stone, a ticket stub with no destination printed on it, and a single dandelion clock.

You reach for your pen, but your fingers pass straight through the barrel. It dissolves into a handful of moths that drift upward, circling the fluorescent lights which have quietly faded to the color of late afternoon. You look back down and the paper is breathing. The margins creep inward like tide lines, and the questions shift, rearranging themselves into shapes—constellations, branching paths, the silhouette of a skyline you've only ever seen in photographs.

Someone clears their throat. You glance up and realize the classroom has peeled away into an open-air amphitheater, tiered and stretching toward a sky filled with slow-moving clouds shaped like question marks. The other students aren't writing either. They're just listening. To a sound like pages turning, or maybe distant rain on a tin roof.

You press your palm flat against the desk. The wood is warm. Pulsing, almost. You realize you don't know a single answer, and the panic you expected never comes. Instead, a quiet certainty settles over you, heavy and sweet as honey. The proctor walks by—not a person, but a slow-turning compass made of brass and shadow—and nods.

You close your eyes. When you open them, the sheet is blank except for a single line at the bottom, written in your own handwriting: *You already know. The rest is just practice.*

Somewhere, far beneath the dream, a real alarm begins to ring. But here, in the quiet after the test, you're already packing your bag. You're already walking out. You're already exactly where you need to be.
