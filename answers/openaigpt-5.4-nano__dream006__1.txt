…and you realize—too late—that you can’t remember a single word you practiced.

The microphone squeals as you reach for it, and your voice comes out half a second late, like your mouth is waiting for permission from the rest of you. You glance down at the notes in your hands. They aren’t notes at all. They’re blank sheets, smooth as fresh skin, reflecting the stage lights as if they’re holding their own secrets.

The audience stays perfectly still, not bored, not confused—just expectant in a way that feels personal. A few faces tilt forward. Someone in the front row smiles as though they know what you’re going to say, and the smile doesn’t fade when you search for the thought you’re supposed to be delivering.

You try again. “Good—” you begin, and the word dissolves midair. The syllable turns into steam between your teeth, and when you swallow, it comes back as a different sound—something you recognize from childhood, a lullaby you haven’t thought of in years. But the lullaby isn’t yours. It feels borrowed, like a costume you’re being dressed in while you stand there, helplessly aware.

Your hands start moving without your consent. You lift them to gesture, and your fingers unfold into the shape of a sentence. For a moment, the air looks written: faint black lines, invisible ink, curving and forming letters only when your eyes follow them. You can almost read what’s there, but every time you try, the letters slide away like fish in dark water.

Then the stage light shifts—subtly, as if the room is breathing. The audience doesn’t change, but the space between you and them stretches. The distance becomes a hallway you’ve walked before. You can feel doors along it, each labeled with a date you can’t quite place. Every door is slightly ajar, and you can hear the soft rustle of past versions of yourself, waiting behind thin walls.

You step forward to keep control, but your feet don’t land on the floor. They land on a memory—one that creaks underfoot—so when you shift your weight, the floor gives a quiet sigh, like approval or impatience. The audience’s faces blur and then sharpen again, as if focusing costs them effort. When you look back at them, the front row is full of people you’ve never met… and also people you lost.

Your throat tightens. You open your mouth to ask for help, but what comes out isn’t a question.

It’s the truth.

Not the kind that sounds like a polished statement—more like a confession that has been waiting under your tongue for years. As you speak, the blank sheets in your hands begin to fill with words that don’t feel like yours until you hear them. The handwriting is yours anyway, the loops and slants familiar, like the same pen that kept you alive is finally allowed to sign its name.

You say:

“I thought I would have to become someone else to be heard.”

The crowd exhales all at once, a soft wave through the room. The microphone stops squealing and begins to capture your voice perfectly, crisp and intimate, as if you’re speaking to each person at the exact moment they needed you to speak at all.

You continue, but now the speech is no longer a speech. It’s an opening.

The stage behind you unzips, not with sound but with light—like a seam in fabric parting to reveal another space. Beyond it is not a room, but a vast, quiet place filled with shelves of objects: small, ordinary things—keys, buttons, photographs, paper cranes. Each item glows faintly as you pass, reacting to your presence as though you’re reading them by touch.

You realize what the audience is: not judges, not strangers, but witnesses.

You could stop. You could try to return to memorized lines, to the safe shape of a talk. But safe doesn’t feel like the point.

So you keep going, walking through the opening while still speaking, still hearing your own words arrive as if they were always meant to be heard aloud.

And in the moment your feet leave the stage completely, you understand the last, strange rule of the dream:

The longer you try to perform, the emptier the page becomes.

But the moment you tell the truth, the world begins writing back.

You look at the audience one final time. Their eyes don’t demand. They welcome.

Then you step forward into the light—and the speech becomes something you can finally hold.
