The air is thick with ash and the metallic tang of old rust, but you kneel anyway, your knees pressing into the gritty dust. Around you, the skeletal remains of skyscrapers loom like silent giants, their windows shattered eyes watching your every move. A distant howl echoes—perhaps wind through broken glass, perhaps something else—but you ignore it. You have spent months scavenging for this moment: not for food, not for fuel, but for this tiny, wrinkled kernel of life you found sealed in a forgotten botany textbook.

You dig a small hole with your bare hands, ignoring the sting of debris under your fingernails. As you place the seed into the dark soil, you feel a strange warmth pulse from the ground, faint but undeniable. The gray sky above seems to lighten just a fraction, as if the world itself is holding its breath.

Suddenly, the rubble around you begins to shift. Not with violence, but with a gentle, rhythmic trembling. Vines, green and vibrant, burst forth from the cracks in the pavement, spiraling upward toward the sunless sky. They weave together, forming a canopy that blocks out the desolation, replacing it with dappled light and the scent of damp earth and blooming jasmine.

The dangers don’t disappear, but they recede into the background, muted by the sudden, overwhelming presence of life. You realize then that survival wasn't about enduring the end; it was about insisting on the beginning. You stand up, brushing the dirt from your clothes, and watch as the first leaf unfurls, bright and defiant against the apocalypse.
