*You find yourself in a post-apocalyptic world, with resources scarce and dangers everywhere. Despite the hardships, you decide to*  

…make your way toward the skeletal remains of the old city library.  

Every step is careful. The air tastes like rust and old smoke. You move through the hollow shells of buildings, their windows like empty eye sockets, watching. Your map—a scrap of faded paper—leads you to a collapsed section of the library’s east wing.  

You’re not looking for food or weapons this time. You’re looking for *books*.  

They say knowledge is a kind of power, and in a world where electricity is a memory and machines have long since fallen silent, the printed word feels like a sacred relic. You pry open a warped plywood board covering a shattered window and slip inside.  

Dust motes dance in the thin shafts of afternoon light. Shelves have toppled like dominoes, but here and there, volumes still cling to their perches. You brush grime from a leather-bound atlas, its pages swollen with damp but still legible. A children’s science encyclopedia with bright, faded illustrations. A book of poems, its spine cracked but holding.  

You gather them in your pack, each one a silent promise. *We were here. We wondered. We created.*  

As you turn to leave, a sound—a soft, terrified whimper—from the shadowed corner of the room. You freeze. It’s not the scrape of a mutant or the click of a bandit’s trap. It’s the sound of a child, maybe no older than ten, clutching a torn teddy bear, eyes wide with fear in the gloom.  

You could ignore it. You could slip away, unseen. That would be the safe thing.  

Instead, you slowly hold out your hand, palm up, and place a clean, wrapped protein bar from your pocket on a nearby shelf within their sight. You step back.  

“We can share the way out,” you say, your voice rough from disuse. “If you want.”  

The child hesitates, then takes a shaky step forward. In their other hand, you see they’re holding a tattered picture book of constellations.  

The dangers outside haven’t changed. The scarcity is still a gnawing ache in your belly. But as you guide this small, brave soul through the broken window and into the uncertain light, you feel a different kind of resource ignite within you—one that the ruins could never take.  

You walk toward the hidden, fortified community you’ve heard whispers of in the wastes—a place where they say people are planting seeds in rooftop soil and telling stories by lantern light. Your pack is heavy with books. Your path is a little less alone.  

And in the vast, silent emptiness of the world, you carry with you the stubborn, illogical, absolutely necessary belief that what you save is worth saving. Not just for yourself. But for the pages yet unwritten.
