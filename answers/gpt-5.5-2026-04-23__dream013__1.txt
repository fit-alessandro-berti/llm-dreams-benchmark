…begin to realize the puzzles are not guarding the way forward—they are describing you.

The first room asks you to arrange mirrors so a beam of light reaches a locked door. When you finally angle the last mirror, the light doesn’t strike the lock. It strikes your own reflection. The door opens anyway.

In the next chamber, numbers crawl across the walls like insects. You try to calculate their pattern, but they scatter whenever you focus too hard. Only when you stop forcing an answer do they settle into a single sentence:

**Not every maze is escaped by solving it.**

You keep walking.

The corridors narrow. The air grows warm and heavy, as if the maze itself is breathing. Ahead, you find a final puzzle: a table with three objects on it—a key, a map, and an hourglass. Above them is carved:

**Choose what you need most.**

You reach for the key, but it crumbles into sand. You reach for the map, but it folds itself into a paper bird and flies into the dark. At last, you pick up the hourglass. The sand inside is flowing upward.

The walls tremble.

Suddenly, every path in the maze appears at once—not as hallways, but as memories: choices you made, words you didn’t say, doors you were afraid to open. You understand then that the maze has never been trying to trap you. It has been waiting for you to notice the center.

And at the center is a simple wooden door.

No lock. No riddle. No puzzle.

You open it.

On the other side is your bedroom, washed in pale morning light. The maze is gone, except for a faint line of sand across your palm and the strange certainty that somewhere, deep inside you, a door has remained open.
