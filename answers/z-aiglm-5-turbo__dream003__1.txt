You run your fingers along the patterns and realize they aren't carved. They're moving. Tiny silver creatures, no bigger than ants, shifting and rearranging themselves into new symbols you almost understand. A language that lives just on the edge of comprehension, like a word on the tip of your tongue.

The box grows warm.

You didn't notice when the room changed, but the walls are further away now. The ceiling is higher. Or maybe you're smaller. The floor beneath you has become soft, like moss, and you can smell rain even though everything is dry.

You press your ear to the box. Inside, you hear something impossible — your own voice, but younger, laughing. Then it stops, and there's a long silence, and then a whisper that says:

*"You already opened it."*

You look down. The box has no lid. It never did. It's not a box at all — it's a frame, and inside the frame is a doorway that shows a room identical to this one, except in that room, someone is standing where you are now, holding a box.

They look up at you.

You recognize their face, but it takes a moment to understand why.

It's the face you'll have in thirty years.

They set the box down carefully, almost reverently, and mouth something you can't hear. Then they step through the frame, and the room folds like origami, and suddenly you're falling — not downward, but *inward* — through layers of the dream that peel away like pages of a book being read by someone else entirely.

And just before you wake, you understand the patterns on the box.

You understand everything.

But you can't take it with you.
