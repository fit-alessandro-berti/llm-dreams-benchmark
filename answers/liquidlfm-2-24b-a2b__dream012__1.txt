"Steady, men! We’ve weathered worse storms than this! The *Sea Serpent* was built for these waters—she’ll hold!"

I glance at the horizon, where the storm seems to stretch endlessly. The compass spins wildly, useless in this magnetic fury. My first mate, Elias, shouts over the gale, "Captain, the mast—!"

I turn, squinting through the rain. The mast groans, one of its ropes snapping like a whip. Without hesitation, I grab a coil of rope and sprint toward the rigging, my boots slipping on the wet planks. The crew follows, ropes in hand, eyes fixed on me.

"Elias, cut the damaged line—now!" I shout.

He nods, and the crew works in frantic unison. The mast creaks, threatening to snap. I brace myself against the mast, muscles straining, and with a final heave, the rope snaps free. The mast tilts, then begins to fall—slowly, agonizingly—toward the sea.

I brace for impact, but the storm swallows the sound. When I look up, the mast is gone, swept away by the waves. The crew erupts in a mix of cheers and groans. I let out a breath I didn’t realize I was holding.

"Captain!" someone calls. "The storm—what now?"

I stare out at the endless dark, the sea now eerily calm behind us. The sky is clearing, the wind dying to a whisper. I feel the weight of command settle on my shoulders, heavier than ever—but also, strangely, right.

"We sail on," I say, voice low but clear. "But we watch the horizon. The sea gives, and the sea takes. We are not its masters—only its guests."

And with that, I turn to the helm, ready for whatever comes next.
