...written in a language I almost know.

Not a foreign language — my own, but sideways. The letters keep their shapes only while I'm looking directly at them. Question 1 is four lines long and every time I reach the end I've forgotten the beginning, so I read it again, and again, and the reading is smooth and pleasant and gets me nowhere, like walking down an escalator that's going up at exactly my speed.

I look around. Everyone else is writing. The sound of pencils is enormous, like rain on a tin roof, and I understand — the way you understand things in dreams, without being told — that they were all given a different exam, the real one, and that mine was printed for someone who no longer exists.

The proctor is someone I loved once. She won't look at me. She's holding a stopwatch with no hands on its face, only the words SOON and LATER, and the needle trembles between them.

I decide to skip to Question 2. It says: *Describe the room you are in.* This seems fair. This seems doable. But when I lift my pen, the room begins waiting to be described, and the walls go soft and provisional, and I realize that whatever I write is what it will become, and I've already used up the desk by mentioning it.

Question 3 is a mirror.

Question 4 asks about a book I was supposed to have read in a year I did not attend.

Somewhere past the middle the questions stop pretending. *What did you actually want. Whose voice is this. If you finish, what will you do with the rest of it.* I know these answers. I have known them for years. But my pen has become a small bird, patient and warm, and it will not make marks, only turn its head to regard me with one black eye.

The bell rings. It's a doorbell. Someone is at the door of the examination hall with a package for me, and I stand up to sign for it — relieved, actually relieved, to be interrupted — and as I stand, everyone else's papers rise off their desks and hang in the air, blank. All of them. Blank the whole time.

The proctor finally looks up. She says, "You can take it again."

And I say, "When?"

And she turns the stopwatch around so I can read the back, where someone has scratched, with a key or a fingernail: *you already are.*
