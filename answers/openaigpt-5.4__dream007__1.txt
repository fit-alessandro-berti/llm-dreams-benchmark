The room around you doesn’t look like any room you’ve ever been in, though in the dream you know it is both your kitchen and your old school library and the bus stop where you once waited in the rain together. The tiles beneath your feet ripple like pond water. Your friend is crying, but the tears are tiny glass beads, bouncing silently off the table and rolling into the cracks in the floor.

“I just needed you to understand,” they say, though their voice comes from the light fixture overhead, soft and buzzing.

You open your mouth to argue again, to list the old hurts, the missed calls, the promise that was broken, but instead you hear yourself say, “Show me.”

At once the wall behind them unzips.

Inside is a night sky folded up like a coat. Your friend reaches into it and pulls out scenes you had forgotten: the day they sat beside your hospital bed and said nothing because they knew silence was kinder; the message they typed and deleted a dozen times because every version sounded wrong; the way they stood outside your door once, too afraid to knock.

The argument changes shape. It is no longer a weapon between you but a large animal pacing in circles, tired and misunderstood. You both look at it. It looks back with your eyes and theirs.

“I was angry,” you say.

“I was scared,” they answer.

The floor becomes a shoreline. Foam curls around your shoes. All the sharp words you nearly said wash up as shells, already empty. You kneel to pick one up, but it dissolves into a small white moth and flies into the dark.

Your friend sits beside you. Their shoulder touches yours, and suddenly the moon is very low, close enough to bruise. It hangs over the water like a listening face.

Neither of you apologizes first; in dreams, that sort of order doesn’t matter. Instead, you begin building a bridge from whatever is near at hand: library cards, wet matches, spoons, receipts, the tiny glass tears, all of it fitting together perfectly. The bridge stretches over the black water to a door standing alone in the distance.

When you reach the door, your friend hesitates.

“What if it’s too late?” they ask.

You turn the knob. On the other side is morning: two mugs on a table, steam rising, a chair waiting for each of you, and a conversation beginning again—slower this time, gentler, as if both of you have learned that being heard is not the same as winning.

Then, just before you wake, your friend smiles in that old familiar way and says, “There you are.”

And you realize the dream was never about the argument.

It was about finding each other before the room disappeared.
