You begin to
feel the words you spent three nights cramming for dissolve like sugar in warm tea the second you open your mouth. The crowd blurs at the edges, watercolor smudged by a thumb, and the cold metal of the microphone in front of you tastes like the cherry lollipops you used to get at the doctor’s office when you were small.

Your hand dives into your pocket for the index cards you tucked there, but they’ve turned to dandelion fluff, drifting through your fingers when you pull them out. You’re about to apologize, about to turn and run off the stage before anyone notices you have no idea what you’re doing, when you spot the front row: your best friend from 7th grade, who moved across the country the day you graduated middle school, is waving, and next to her is the scruffy orange tabby you used to feed salmon pate on your porch for three months, the one that vanished the week your family moved out of that house. He’s wearing a tiny crocheted bowtie, and he’s holding a neon green sign scrawled in crayon that says WE LIKE YOU ANYWAY.

You laugh, and the sound is wobbly and louder than you expect, and suddenly you don’t care about the speech you prepared. The mic squeaks a little when you lean in, and instead of your planned opening line about “the importance of perseverance” or whatever it was, you say, “I forgot what I was supposed to talk about.”

The crowd chuckles, soft, like they knew that already. You talk instead about the way wet pavement smells after a summer rain, about the time you tried to make s’mores in the microwave and set off the smoke alarm so bad the fire department showed up, about the lullaby your mom used to sing that you still hum when you’re nervous. As you talk, the auditorium ceiling peels back like a piece of construction paper, revealing a twilight sky strung with floating paper lanterns that glow like fireflies. The crowd starts passing things forward: crumpled concert tickets, a pressed four-leaf clover, a polaroid of a golden retriever you had when you were 8 that you thought you’d forgotten, a single neon pink hair tie you lost in high school. They pile up on the podium next to the jar of sea glass you keep on your nightstand at home, the one you didn’t even realize you brought with you.

Halfway through, you look out and see all the younger versions of you sitting in the seats: 5-year-old you with a gap-toothed smile and a sparkly tutu, 10-year-old you with skinned knees and a backpack covered in dinosaur stickers, 17-year-old you with blue hair and a heartbroken expression, all nodding along like they get exactly what you’re saying. The stage under your feet has gone soft, like the clover patch you used to nap in as a kid, and the spotlight no longer burns—it smells like cinnamon, like your grandma’s kitchen when she baked apple pie.

When you run out of things to say, you don’t feel nervous. You just shrug, and the crowd erupts in cheers that sound like ocean waves, like the beach where you collected the sea glass in that jar. You step off the stage, and the orange tabby rubs against your leg, and your 7th grade best friend hands you a slice of peach pie still warm from the oven.

You’re just lifting it to your mouth when you feel your alarm go off, and the taste of peaches fades, leaving only the faint smell of cinnamon on your tongue.
