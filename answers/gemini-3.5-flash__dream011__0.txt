...not written in ink, but etched in shifting, iridescent light that refuses to stay still on the page. 

Instead of questions about logic, history, or science, the first prompt reads: 

*“Translate the precise feeling of a heavy heart into a mathematical formula, then solve for peace.”*

I look around the room. The other examinees are writing furiously, their pens scratching against the paper with a sound like dry leaves scraping across pavement. Nobody else seems confused. 

I look back down. The second question has already changed since I last looked at it. Now it asks: 

*“If you are the sum of your memories, whose memories are these?”* Attached to the page is a black-and-white photograph of a wooden porch I have never stood on, under a twilight sky I have never seen, yet a sudden, crushing wave of nostalgia aches in my chest. 

Panic, cold and sharp, pools in my throat. I reach for my pencil, but when I look at my hand, my fingers are translucent. I can see the outline of the desk right through my skin, my edges dissolving into tiny, floating pixels of soft gray light. 

The clock on the wall has no numbers. Its hands spin wildly backward, emitting a low, rhythmic hum that sounds like a distant dial-up modem. 

The proctor—a towering silhouette whose face is a blur of static—walks slowly down my aisle. They stop beside my desk. 

“You have negative five minutes remaining,” they whisper, their voice sounding like wind rustling through dry grass. “The future has already happened. Please write your soul on the line provided.”

I desperately press my pencil to the paper, determined to write *something, anything*, to prove I belong here. But the lead tears right through the page, and instead of tearing paper, it rips a hole in the fabric of the room. A dark, star-filled void opens up beneath my desk. 

I begin to fall forward, slipping through the tear in the paper, tumbling into the stars as the classroom dissolves into a quiet, electric hum. And just before I hit the bottom of the sky, I open my eyes.
