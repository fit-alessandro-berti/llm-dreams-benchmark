...and the air itself begins to shift textures, turning from invisible gas into a thick, violet mist that clings to your skin like a wet dress. 

To your left, a group of waiters in tuxedos are serving soup from cups that have no bottoms. As the soup falls through, it turns into gold dust that swirls around the table before settling on the guests' heads. They laugh, but their mouths don't move.

You push through the mist, your feet sinking slightly into the floor as if it were damp sand. You pass a group of children playing a game of catch with a ball that glows like a miniature sun. One throws it at you, but the ball passes harmlessly through your chest, leaving a warm spot on your shirt that smells like rain on pavement.

"Have you seen them?" you ask a man in a blue suit. He is holding a phone, but the screen is showing you a view of the room from the outside, looking in. He doesn't answer. Instead, the room tilts. The chandelier drops, but it hangs suspended in the air, dripping liquid light.

You keep moving, drawn by a magnetic pull toward the grand staircase. The music changes from a jazz trio to a low, rhythmic thrumming that you feel in your ribs.

At the top of the stairs, the crowd disperses, opening a path that wasn't there before. In the center of the room, by a window that shows a night sky filled with too many stars, you see them.

The person you know is sitting on a sofa that floats a few inches off the ground. They are reading a book, but the pages turn themselves too quickly to read. They look up as you approach, their eyes reflecting the infinite sky outside.

"I've been waiting for you to get lost," they say, their voice sounding like it's coming from a distant room.

"Everyone else... they're gone," you say, looking at the empty space around you. The golden dust has settled, the children are silent statues, the waiters are frozen.

"They are dreams within the dream," they reply, tapping the book. "But I'm the anchor. If you touch the book, the gathering ends."

You reach out, your hand trembling. The mist thickens, turning gray. You touch the book. The stars outside the window go dark, one by one.

You sit up in your own bed, heart pounding, the morning light already slicing through your curtains. Your hand is empty, but you can still feel the faint, golden warmth of the dust on your chest. You look at your nightstand. There is a playing card there, face down. You turn it over. It's blank.

You sit there for a long time, listening to the silence of the house, wondering why you haven't opened the window yet.
