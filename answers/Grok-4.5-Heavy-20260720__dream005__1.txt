You are at a large social gathering where you only know one person. As you try to find them, you encounter various groups of people and each one seems to know a different impossible version of your story.

One cluster floats a few inches above the polished floor, arguing the proper way to fold fitted sheets in zero gravity and whether time tastes more like cinnamon or regret. Another circle all wear your face and debate whether you are fashionably late or already forgotten. A third group offers you glasses that change in mid-air—a flute of champagne becomes a brass key, then a small paper bird that bursts into flight and vanishes into the rafters.

Whenever you ask after the person you know, their answers dissolve into more of those paper birds, scattering in every direction. Hallways stretch without warning. Doorways open onto the same crowded room you just left. The music is only the soft, continuous sound of distant laughter, changing tempo with your footsteps.

You catch glimpses of the familiar face at the edge of every group, always turning away just as you call out. The crowd thickens; the distance expands. At last you push through and find them standing calmly at the quiet center of the gathering, as if the entire party has been orbiting them the whole time.

They look at you with easy recognition and say, “There you are. I’ve been waiting.”

For a moment everything stills. Then the walls begin to breathe, the lights soften, and the rooms grow wider than they should be able to.
