You are about to take the final exam for a course that determines your future career. As you sit down to start the exam, you notice the questions are written in the handwriting of every person who ever doubted you—your parents, old teachers, that ex who said you'd never amount to anything. They aren't exam questions at all. The first one reads: "Why are you still pretending this version of your life fits?" The second: "If failure is a door, how many times will you knock before you notice it's already open?" 

The ink begins to bleed and rearrange itself into tiny doors, each one slamming shut as you stare. Your pen has turned into a spoon. The clock on the wall is your own face, years older, looking exhausted and mouthing "run." When you glance at the other students, they are all earlier versions of you—age 8, age 17, age 25—some crying, some laughing maniacally, all of them finishing their tests instantly while you sit frozen. 

The room tilts like a sinking ship. Your chair becomes the bed you had as a child. Somewhere far away, a goldfish in a tiny professor's robe bubbles, "Time's up," and you wake up gasping, heart hammering, already reaching for your real notebook to write down the answer you finally understood in the dream.
