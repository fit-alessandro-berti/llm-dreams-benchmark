…and you let them.

They don’t crowd you or ask a lot of questions. Instead, they speak softly, as if your thoughts are fragile glass. One of them holds out a paper map—creased and worn, like it’s been carried through other nights—and another points to the sky where a thin ribbon of moonlight cuts between buildings. A third keeps pace beside you, matching your steps to a rhythm you didn’t know you were missing.

You tell them you don’t know how you got there. You don’t even remember what “home” looks like in the day, only the feeling of it—warm, familiar, safe. They nod like they understand without needing details.

Together, you move through the city’s bright, too-loud streets. The sidewalks ripple under your feet, turning puddles into mirrors and street signs into short poems. Every time you start to drift back into panic, the strangers gently steer you—by a touch on your sleeve, a shared laugh, a whispered “Look,”—until the fear thins out and something steadier fills the space.

At an intersection, the lights stop changing. They hang suspended, glowing like held breath. The group gathers closer, and for a moment you’re surrounded not by people, but by options—different directions, different chances, all equally real. You realize you don’t have to solve the whole map at once. You only have to choose the next step.

The one holding the map closes it carefully, and the paper opens into a doorway instead—an archway made of folded light. Inside it, you see a hallway that looks like your own memories: a kitchen table with a chipped mug, a school corridor, a hallway you once ran down during a storm. The details shift and rearrange themselves, becoming gentler as you watch.

You step through, and your chest loosens as if something heavy has been carried away. The strangers follow you, but they don’t stay separate. Their outlines soften until you can feel their kindness living inside you—like a borrowed coat that still warms your shoulders when you take it off.

Then you wake up standing at the edge of your bed, not fully sure whether you walked through the city or the dream walked through you. But you know what to do next.

Outside, the morning air is ordinary and bright. Still, you find yourself searching for the nearest familiar landmark—not with fear, but with curiosity. And you promise, silently, that if you ever see someone lost the way you were, you’ll offer them a hand without trying to take their story away.
