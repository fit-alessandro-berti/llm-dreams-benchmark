You decide to **sing**.

Not some half-hearted hum, either. You belt out the stupidest, most off-key version of "Sweet Caroline" the wasteland has ever heard, voice cracking like dry leather as you walk straight toward the pack of mutated coyotes eyeing you from the ridge.

They freeze. One of them actually tilts its head like a confused golden retriever.

Because here's the thing about this dream: the moment you stop acting like prey, the world forgets how to be apocalyptic.

The coyotes don't attack. They start... swaying. One of them throws its head back and howls in what might be the worst harmony in history. You keep singing, louder now, laughing between verses as you march past them like you own the place.

The sky cracks open with color it has no right to have anymore—violent pinks and impossible teals bleeding across the ash clouds. Ruined buildings begin sprouting impossible flowers from their broken windows. A rusted-out school bus has become a greenhouse, tomatoes glowing like tiny red lanterns.

You find a working vending machine somehow still plugged into nothing. It dispenses ice-cold Dr Pepper and a single perfectly ripe mango. The sign on it reads "THANKS FOR NOT BEING A DICK."

Eventually you reach the edge of what used to be a city. There's a little girl there—maybe nine—wearing a gas mask two sizes too big, drawing on the cracked pavement with the charred end of a stick.

She looks up at you.

"Took you long enough," she says, voice muffled behind the mask. "I've been waiting since Tuesday."

You sit down beside her. The ground is warm.

"What are we waiting for?" you ask.

She pulls off the mask. Her face is yours, but younger. Eyes bright in a way yours haven't been in years.

"For you to remember that even after everything ends," she says, "you can still choose to sing."

The wasteland blooms around you like someone flipped a switch. Trees burst from concrete. Birds that definitely shouldn't exist anymore explode into flight. The coyotes are now wearing tiny party hats.

You wake up with the taste of mango still in your mouth and "Sweet Caroline" stuck in your head.

And for the first time in a long time, the real world doesn't feel quite so post-apocalyptic.
