• You are at your workplace, and suddenly, you are given a project with an impossible deadline. Your boss is waiting for
  you to start, and you have one hour, one office desk, and three coffee-stained deadlines from last quarter staring at
  you like a judge. The clock ticks louder than the keyboard clicks.

  You open the task tracker, scan the requirements, and the screen blurs for a second—too much. Then you do the only
  practical thing: cut the mountain into stairs. You split the work into three chunks, assign what’s urgent to the team,
  what’s critical to yourself, and what’s “nice-to-have” to the void. You write the first draft in 20 minutes, strip it
  down to what actually matters, and run it twice, then three times, while the team sends in partials from lunch-hour
  chaos.

  At 50 minutes you have a version that works, ugly but alive. At 57, you patch one edge case. At 59, you present it to
  your boss, who nods like nothing could go wrong. At 60 minutes exactly, an email lands: “Great work—client approved.
  Need this live by Friday.”

  The room is silent.

  Then your phone buzzes again: “Can you do one more thing?”
