You start to walk, because walking feels like doing something, and the street obliges by being long.

The buildings are the color of old teeth. They lean over the sidewalk with the mild curiosity of people at a parade. I check my pocket for the invitation and find instead a folded paper napkin with a phone number written on it in handwriting I recognize as my own but from a much earlier draft of me — the loops too generous, the sevens uncrossed. There is no phone. There has never been a phone. This does not strike me as a problem so much as a genre.

I ask a woman at a fruit stand where the meeting is. She says, "Which one," and I understand she isn't asking. She is holding a persimmon and turning it slowly, the way you'd turn a globe to find a country you've only read about. Behind her the fruit is arranged by weight, then by sadness. I want to tell her I'm in a hurry but the sentence comes out as *thank you*, which she accepts, and I move on carrying something I didn't buy.

The city has a subway. I know this the way you know things in dreams — not from evidence but from a kind of installed certainty. I go down. The stairs are wet and the tiles spell a word in a language I read fluently until I look at it. On the platform a man in a suit is explaining to no one that the trains run on time in both directions, which he says as though it were a comfort. The train arrives. It is my grandmother's kitchen, moving.

I sit at the table. The clock over the stove has fifty minutes left, then forty, then it has hands but no numbers, then it is a plate. She is at the sink with her back to me and she says, without turning, *you'll be late*, and I say *I know*, and she says *you were always going to be*, and the relief that opens in my chest is so large it embarrasses me. Outside the window, stations go by: a courtyard, a hospital, a field of parked cars with their headlights on in daylight.

Somewhere above the city the meeting is happening. I can feel it happening. Twelve people at a table with a document, and one chair pushed slightly back, and nobody looking at it. They are getting along fine. They are getting along so well that I understand, with the slow horror that in dreams is indistinguishable from peace, that the chair was never assigned to me, that I invented the appointment as a way of having somewhere to be, that this whole city was raised overnight to give me a reason to hurry through it.

The train stops. The doors open onto the top of the stairs I came down, and I climb out into the same street, and the woman with the persimmon says, "There you are," as if I'd only stepped away for a moment.

I ask her what time it is.

She says, "It's fine."

And in the dream that is a full answer, and the light comes down the avenue sideways and gold, and I walk in the direction I was already walking, which turns out to be the direction I was going.
