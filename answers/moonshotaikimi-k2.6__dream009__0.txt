You wake up in a completely different era, with no modern technology around. As you explore your new surroundings, you realize you need to **remember the word before the sun clears the ridge.**

The air tastes of iron and woodsmoke. No hum of electricity lives in the walls—only the wind, threading itself through gaps in the mud-and-wattle. Your hands, when you hold them up, are calloused in ways your waking hands never were, wearing someone else's history. You push through a doorway hung with cured hide and step into a village that seems to exist in the breath between Bronze and Iron, where the roofs are thick with thatch and the paths are beaten earth, gleaming with recent rain.

You don't know the language, not at first, but then you do—a strange, submerged knowledge bubbling up like a spring. You know the oven-shaped kiln to your left will burn too hot by midday. You know the woman grinding grain by the longhouse is missing two fingers. You know the child watching you from the goat pen will not survive the winter unless—

Unless *what?*

That's when the urgency grips you. It isn't just that you're lost. You were *sent.* The dream has a purpose, and your modern mind is the key, even if these ancient hands must turn the lock. You need to find the traveler from the north, the one with the amber bead at his throat, and tell him—tell him *what?* The word is there, a silver fish darting at the bottom of a dark pool. If you can catch it before the bells ring, before the dream shifts again, you might change the pattern. You might wake up. Or you might save something worth more than waking.

A horn sounds. The ground trembles, not from machinery, but from hooves. You turn, and the word is almost on your tongue.
