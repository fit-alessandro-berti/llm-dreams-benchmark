  You are about to take the final exam for a course that determines your future career. As you sit down to start the
  exam, you notice the questions are not printed on paper. They are floating just above the desk in shifting ink, like
  words on a lake, and each one reads in a different voice.

  Before you is a seat with your name already filled in, but your answer sheet is blank except for one line at the top:
  Write what comes next.

  The first question appears:

  “Describe a failure you are afraid to own.”

  You begin to write, and the room changes. The clock hands melt into thin silver vines around the room. The proctor’s
  face blurs into your own. Every student in the hall turns and becomes someone you have known and once disappointed—
  your teacher, your parent, your future self.

  Another question hovers up:

  “If success is granted, what will you sacrifice?”

  You look at your pencil, and the lead has become a key. In your hand it grows warm, then cold, then warm again, as if
  choosing between turning a lock and drawing a line.

  The final question is last and hardest:

  “What do you want to remember from this exam when you wake up?”

  That is when the desk rises from under you, the room becomes a staircase, and the pages turn into doors. You realize
  you are being asked to leave, not pass. So you answer carefully:

  I want to remember that I didn’t know the answers, and that I still wrote anyway.
