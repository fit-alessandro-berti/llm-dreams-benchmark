You wake up in a completely different era, with no modern technology around. As you explore your new surroundings, you realize you need to find out what year it is — and this seems, at first, like a simple thing to ask.

But the dream has arranged it so that no one will answer you.

The village is built of grey timber and thatch, and there is woodsmoke everywhere, and the smell is so specific and so *correct* that some part of you understands you have never actually smelled woodsmoke before, not really, only the idea of it. A woman is beating a rug against a fence post. When you ask her the year she says, "The third since the flooding," and goes back to beating, and dust comes off the rug in slow clouds that never settle, that hang in the air like they're waiting for something.

You need to find out what year it is. You need to find shoes, because you look down and your feet are bare and the mud is very cold and very intimate. You need to remember why you came here, because you're increasingly certain you *came* here, that this wasn't done to you.

There is a man selling eels from a barrel. You ask him for directions to the road and he says, "Which road? There's the one you took." He has your grandfather's hands. Not his face — his hands, the knuckles like walnuts, the thumbnail with the white mark. He notices you noticing and covers one hand with the other, embarrassed, as though you've caught him wearing something borrowed.

You need to tell someone about the future. This becomes urgent all at once, the way things do. You need to warn them, or teach them, or at least prove yourself — and you open your mouth to explain electricity and find that you can't. You know that it goes through wires. You know that it comes from somewhere and that people argued about it once. Your knowledge is a photograph of a book. You could describe the cover.

Penicillin: mould, somehow.
Flight: wings, but stiff ones.
The internet: everyone, all at once, complaining.

The villagers gather, patient and polite, waiting for you to finish. You realize they have gathered for this before. There is a bench worn smooth where they sit.

You need to leave. You start toward the treeline and the ground tilts pleasantly, the way a boat does, and you understand that the village is on the back of something and always has been, and that the flooding three years ago was simply it rolling over in its sleep.

At the edge of the wood there is a stone with a date carved into it. This is what you needed. This is the whole errand. You kneel and brush away the moss with your thumb and the numerals are perfectly clear, deeply cut, unmistakable — and they are a year that has never existed and never will, a number with too many digits in the wrong order, and reading it feels like biting foil.

Behind you, someone is calling you by a name you had as a child and had forgotten until precisely now.

You turn around to answer.

The woodsmoke smell goes first. Then the cold in your feet, which becomes a sheet kicked loose in the night. You lie still for a while in the dark, trying to hold the number, and you can feel it going, and you think: *just the first digit, just keep the first digit* —

and somewhere a boiler ticks, and the streetlight through the curtain is the wrong colour for any century at all.
