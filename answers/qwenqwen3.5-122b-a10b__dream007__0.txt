You decide to stop speaking and simply reach out your hand.

As your fingers extend, the air between you thickens, turning into a warm, golden mist. The shouting stops, not because you asked it to, but because the sound itself begins to float away, turning into small, glowing moths that drift toward the ceiling. The room you are standing in loses its corners; the walls breathe and expand until you are no longer in a house, but standing on a bridge made of glass over a deep, starry ocean.

Your friend’s face softens, their anger dissolving into a look of recognition. They don't say "I'm sorry," and you don't say "I forgive you." Instead, the object of your disagreement—a heavy, jagged stone you were both holding—shatters into light. It rains down on the glass bridge, chiming like tiny bells.

You realize that the argument was never about the stone, but about who was afraid of the fall.

Your friend takes your hand, and for a moment, your skin feels like water. You look down into the ocean below and see your own reflection sleeping in your bed. The bridge tilts, not to fall, but to slide you gently back into the sheets. You wake up with the taste of salt on your lips and the lingering, quiet certainty that you are still friends.
