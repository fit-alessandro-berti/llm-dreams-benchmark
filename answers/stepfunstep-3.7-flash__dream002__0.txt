You feel a storm approaching and the first thing that hits you is the sudden, suffocating silence. The chickadees that were flitting between the birch trunks ten seconds prior are gone, the squirrel that was chattering on the oak branch above the path has vanished, even the soft gurgle of the creek you’ve been tracking for the past hour cuts out. The warm, honeyed sunlight bleeds from the sky in seconds, replaced by a flat, bruised purple haze that tastes like iron on the back of your tongue—sharp, metallic, the smell of ozone thick in the air.

The first peal of thunder rumbles low in your bones before you hear it, so deep it makes the acorns still clinging to the oak above you rattle. A single fat raindrop hits your cheek, cold as a river stone, then another on your nose, then the sky opens up. You fumble for the hood of your flannel, but before you can yank it up, you realize the rain isn’t cold. Every drop that hits your skin leaves a faint, wispy golden glow, like someone crushed a thousand fireflies into the storm clouds.

You stumble half-blind through the downpour, tripping over a gnarled hemlock root, and only then notice the little moss-covered bench tucked under the tree’s overhang you’d missed a hundred times on walks through this forest. A dented steel thermos sits on it, steam curling up through the rain, and when you unscrew the lid, the smell of chamomile and honey hits you so hard your chest aches—your grandma used to make it exactly this way, before she moved to the coast, before you forgot what it smelled like to be unapologetically safe.

The thunder stops sounding menacing. It rumbles like a contented house cat, and the wind howling through the pines sounds like a choir humming under their breath. The creek you’d been walking toward glows soft aqua now, and tiny fish with bioluminescent lanterns strung along their sides swim lazy circles in the current, unbothered by the rain. The little red fox you’d spotted darting across the path an hour earlier trots over, shakes the rain off its russet fur, and curls up on your boots, its tail flicking slow and warm against your ankles.

You sit there long after the rain slows to a fine, glimmering mist, sipping tea that never runs out, and you realize you don’t want to wake up. The forest feels like it’s holding you, like the storm isn’t something to hide from, but something it’s been waiting to show you. When you finally do blink awake, you’re curled up in your own bed, the window cracked open to let in the smell of damp pine, and there’s a single, glowing golden dandelion seed stuck to the cuff of your jeans—proof, you think, that the forest didn’t let you leave empty-handed.
