…every group seems to know something you don’t.

The first circle of people is laughing too hard at a joke that hasn’t been told yet. They make space for you before you even ask, smiling as if they recognize you, but when you look closer their faces keep subtly changing—coworkers becoming cousins, strangers becoming old classmates, then back again. You ask if they’ve seen the one person you know, and they all point in different directions without stopping their laughter.

You move deeper into the party.

The rooms are larger on the inside than they should be. A dining room becomes a hotel lobby, then a greenhouse lit by chandeliers that drip warm rain instead of light. Somewhere, music is playing, but every time you follow it, it ends up behind you. You pass a group sitting on a staircase suspended in midair, speaking in whispers that sound exactly like your own thoughts. One of them pauses and says, “You’re close,” though you never asked aloud.

You start recognizing details instead of people: the color of your friend’s coat on the back of a chair, their voice around the corner, the shape of their hand disappearing through a doorway just before you get there. Each time, you hurry, and each time the space stretches—hallways lengthen, doors lead to balconies overlooking parties inside other parties, all of them full of guests who turn to look at you as if they’ve been expecting you all along.

Then you find a quieter room.

It’s almost empty except for a long table covered in glasses filled to different heights, each one humming a single note. At the far end sits your friend, finally, exactly as you remember them, calm and unmistakable amid all the shifting strangeness. Relief rushes through you. You cross the room quickly, afraid they’ll vanish too.

But when you reach them, they look up with a warm, puzzled expression and say, “There you are. I’ve been trying to find you.”

Before you can answer, everyone in the house falls silent at once.

Your friend stands and offers you their hand. “Come outside,” they say.

You follow them through a door that should lead to a garden, but outside is a moonlit shoreline. The party glows behind you in the windows of a house that is now somehow very far away and impossibly tall. Waves roll in without making any sound. On the dark water, hundreds of little lights drift like lanterns, except each one looks like a memory you almost remember.

Your friend points to the horizon.

“There,” they say, “is the thing you forgot.”

You try to ask what they mean, but your mouth fills with the taste of birthday cake and sea salt, and the sky begins to brighten—not like morning, but like someone turning a page.

Then you wake up with the strange certainty that, somewhere, the party is still going on without you.
