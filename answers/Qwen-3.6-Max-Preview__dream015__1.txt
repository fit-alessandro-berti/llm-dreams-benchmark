...plant a garden.

It sounds absurd, doesn’t it? In a world where the sky is permanently bruised with ash and the air tastes of copper, digging into the hard, irradiated earth feels like an act of madness. But you remember the smell of rain on dry soil from before the Collapse. You remember green.

You find a patch of land behind the ruins of an old library, sheltered by a collapsed concrete overhang that blocks the worst of the acidic winds. Your hands are calloused and scarred, wrapped in rags to protect them from the jagged debris, but they move with gentle precision. You don’t have seeds—those were lost decades ago—but you have found something else: a single, dried sunflower head tucked inside a plastic bag in a survivor’s abandoned pack. It was likely meant for food, but the kernels are too shriveled to eat. They are perfect for planting.

Day after day, you tend to this small square of dirt. You collect dew in scavenged bottle caps to water it. You shield it from the scorching noon sun with pieces of rusted metal. The other survivors watch you with suspicion, then curiosity, then a quiet, desperate hope. They see you kneeling in the dust, whispering to the ground as if it could hear you.

Weeks pass. Nothing happens. The wind howls. Raiders patrol the perimeter. Hunger gnaws at your belly. But one morning, when the gray light filters through the haze, you see it: a tiny, fragile green shoot breaking through the cracked earth.

It isn’t much. It won’t feed anyone. It won’t stop the raiders or clean the air. But as you stare at that speck of life, vibrant and defiant against the backdrop of death, you realize what you’ve been doing all along. You weren’t just trying to grow a flower. You were proving that the world wasn’t finished yet. And for the first time in years, you smile.
