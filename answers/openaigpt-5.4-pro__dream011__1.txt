…written in your own handwriting.

Not just the answers—the questions, too.

At first you think it’s a trick of panic. The page trembles in your hands as if your pulse has climbed into the paper. Then you read the first prompt:

**1. Explain why you abandoned the thing you loved first. Show your work.**

You flip to the next page.

**2. If success arrives wearing the face of your childhood fear, do you let it in?**

**3. Draw the room where you became who you are. Label all exits you did not take.**

Around you, the other students are already writing furiously, but their pens make no scratching sound. When you glance sideways, their desks are empty. They are writing on the air, on nothing, on invisible sheets that seem to accept every answer without protest. The professor stands at the front of the room with no face, only a pair of glasses suspended over a tie.

You look back at your test and the margins begin filling themselves in.

*Be honest,* your handwriting says.

The clock above the board has too many hands. One points to noon, one to midnight, one spins backward so fast it becomes a pale silver fan. You try to remember what course this is—law, medicine, engineering, whatever future was supposed to be safest—but the title on the exam booklet keeps changing.

**Applied Regret.**

**Advanced Becoming.**

**Introduction to Everything You Were Too Afraid to Ask.**

Your pencil feels heavy, almost warm, like a living thing waiting to see what you will confess. So, because this is somehow worse than failing, you begin.

For question one, you write about the piano, or painting, or astronomy, or the language you stopped speaking because no one around you understood it. As the words leave you, the classroom walls peel back, revealing all the versions of your life stacked behind them like theater sets: the apartment where you studied too hard, the summer job you took instead of the chance you wanted, the train station where you nearly left, the doorway where you almost said yes.

For question two, you hesitate. The paper darkens, then becomes reflective, and in it you see yourself in interview clothes, in hospital scrubs, in a suit, at a drafting table, on a stage, in every life that might still be yours. They all look tired. They all look hopeful. They are all waiting for the same answer.

For question three, your hand moves on its own. You draw your childhood bedroom. The crack in the ceiling. The window that stuck in winter. The carpet where you sat deciding, much too young, what kind of person would be easiest to become. Then you label the exits:

**Obedience.**

**Ambition.**

**Fear.**

**Love.**

**Delay.**

**One day.**

When you finally look up, the faceless professor is standing beside your desk. He places a sealed envelope on top of your exam. Across the front, in red ink, is written:

**RESULTS AVAILABLE ONLY AFTER YOU WAKE.**

“I haven’t finished,” you whisper.

“You have,” he says, though he has no mouth.

The room begins to fill with wind. Papers lift. Chairs slide backward. The other students dissolve into chalk dust. The backwards-spinning hand of the clock snaps off and falls, ringing like a small bell when it hits the floor.

Your exam booklet flips to the final page on its own.

There is only one question left.

**4. Who told you this exam determines your future career?**

The silence after that is enormous.

Then, from somewhere far away—through the classroom, through the wind, through the strange bright terror of being almost understood—you hear an alarm clock begin to ring.

And just before everything vanishes, you realize the answer is not on the page.

It is in the fact that your hand, for the first time in the entire dream, stops shaking.
