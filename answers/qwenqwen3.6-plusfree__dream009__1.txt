A weaver's shuttle clicking like a metronome. The way neighbors share bread without counting change. You realize you don't need a map, or a phone, or a way back. You need to unlearn the hurry. You need to let your senses recalibrate to a world where presence is the only currency that matters.

A stray dog falls into step beside you. An old man on a wooden bench offers a nod that feels like recognition. You sit on a sun-warmed stone wall, and for the first time in years, your mind isn't racing ahead. It settles. The sky softens into violet and amber. Somewhere, a lute begins to play a tune you've never heard but somehow recognize.

And that's when you understand: dreams like this aren't about escaping time. They're about returning to it. You close your eyes, breathe in the slow air, and let the era wrap around you like a well-tailored coat. When you open them again, you won't know if you'll wake in your own bed or step further into this one. But it doesn't feel like a choice anymore. It feels like a homecoming.
