In each reflection, you see your friend, but aged, younger, smiling, crying—different versions of them from moments you’d both forgotten. You reach out, and your reflected hand meets theirs in the glass. The anger in your chest doesn’t vanish, but it softens, edges blurring like watercolor.

Then, from the far end of the corridor, your friend steps out—not the person you were arguing with, but a child version of them, holding a crumpled drawing. They look up, not with blame, but with confusion. “I just wanted you to see,” they say, their voice small.

You wake up then, or perhaps you step *into* the next scene: you’re both sitting on a rooftop at dawn, not speaking, just watching the sky lighten. The argument feels distant now, like a story you heard about someone else. You realize the thing you were fighting over wasn’t the point—it was the fear of losing the connection beneath it. And in the dream-logic, that fear has already been soothed, not by winning, but by witnessing.

You turn to them. They’re already looking at you, and without a word, you both know: some dreams aren’t about the fight. They’re about the quiet after, and the choice to stay.
