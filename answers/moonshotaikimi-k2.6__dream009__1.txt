…you need to **remember the ending before it fades.**

The air smells of woodsmoke and iron, not electricity. Your hands—when you hold them up—are calloused in places your real hands have never been, and the tunic scratchy against your skin is homespun, not synthetic. You have stumbled out of a lean-to into a village of thatch and timber, where people move with the unhurried certainty of a time before clocks. A woman carrying a yoke of water buckets glances at you, not with suspicion, but with expectation, as if you belong here. As if you have always belonged here.

It is the expectation that frightens you most.

You walk toward the center of the settlement, past a smithy where the bellows wheeze like a sleeping beast, past children chasing a hoop with sticks. The sky is too wide without telephone wires to slice it. The silence is too deep without engines. And then you see the stone circle on the hill beyond the fields—seven stones, not eight—and your chest tightens with a dreamer’s terrible certainty: *you know what happens next.*

In your other life, the one with screens and soft beds, you read about this place. You read about the fire. About the drought that followed the eighth stone falling, about the village starving, about the name that was erased from every map. You know the exact angle of the sun when it will happen. You know the date.

But here, in the dream, knowledge is slippery. The more you try to grip the details, the more they smear. Was the fire in three days? Three weeks? Did it start in the grain store or the chapel? You can feel the information evaporating like morning frost, replaced by the mundane logic of this body—hunger, thirst, the ache in your calves from walking in leather boots.

A horn blows. People begin gathering in the square, looking up at the hill where the seventh stone leans, precarious. They are waiting for the priest. They are waiting for the ritual that, in your memory, marks the beginning of the end.

You realize you need to **reach the eighth stone before they do.**

Not to save them—though you want to—but because the stone is the door. It is the only solid thing in the dream, the only object that does not belong to this era or yours, but to the space between sleeping and waking. If you can touch it before the story plays out to its tragic end, you might carve a new ending into its face. You might write, in the language of your own time, a warning that will still be there when you wake up.

Your legs carry you faster than they should, through wheat that whispers, up the hill that grows steeper with every step. Behind you, the villagers begin to sing a low, droning chant. The sky bruises purple. The seventh stone casts a shadow that points directly at your feet, and at the empty socket in the grass where the eighth stone should stand.

You are almost there. The chant is rising. The dream is thinning.

You reach out your hand.

And you need to **remember your name**—because if you speak it when you touch the stone, the words will stay. They will wait in the empty socket for archaeologists to find, for you to read in a textbook years from now, for the dream to complete its circle. Your fingers brush cold rock.

*What is your name?*

The chant stops. The world holds its breath.

You open your mouth to speak—
