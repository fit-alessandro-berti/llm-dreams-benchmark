...the challenges become less about skill and more about surrender.

The arena, once a sterile stadium of white light, dissolves into a forest where the trees are made of glass. The other competitors, faceless figures in grey suits, begin to fade like mist, leaving me standing alone before a massive, floating door that hums with a low, cello-like vibration. There is no panel to knock on, no handle to turn.

I realize then that the test was never about proving I was capable. It was about proving I was willing to let go of the need to be chosen.

I close my eyes and simply step forward, expecting to fall, but instead, I walk through the door as if it were made of water. Inside is a small, cozy room filled with books that have no titles. In the center sits a desk with a single, blank sheet of paper and a pen that glows with a soft, warm light.

A voice, sounding like my own but older and kinder, whispers, *"The job is not to be hired. The job is to begin."*

I sit down. I pick up the pen. As soon as the nib touches the paper, the ink doesn't flow black; it flows gold, and the words I write don't appear on the page—they float into the air, becoming stars in the room's ceiling. I realize the "dream job" was never a position of employment. It was the permission to create without fear of failure.

I write the word *Wake*.

The room dissolves into light, and I feel the sensation of falling upward. My eyes open. The ceiling of my bedroom is just a ceiling, but the feeling of the golden ink remains in my fingertips, and I know, with absolute certainty, that I am ready to start.
