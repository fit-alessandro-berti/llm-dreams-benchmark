You are walking through a peaceful forest when suddenly the weather changes dramatically. You feel a storm approaching and the soft chirp of wren chicks from a maple hollow cuts off mid-trill, replaced by a low, rumbling gust that twists the pine branches overhead into gnarled fists. The warm, honeyed scent of blooming clover shifts to sharp, metallic ozone, and your boots crunch on loose, damp soil that’s already gone slick underfoot.

A fat, cold raindrop beads on your wrist, then another, and before you can reach for your backpack’s rain cover, the sky splits open—first a flash of searing white lightning that turns the forest into a black-and-white negative, then a crack of thunder so loud it makes your teeth ache. Tiny hail stones pelt your shoulders, tumbling off the brim of your hat, and you spot a narrow cave mouth half-hidden by ivy and moss a few paces away, tucked into the side of a mossy granite outcrop.

You bolt for it, scrambling through the curtain of ivy just as the rain becomes a roaring sheet. Inside the cave, the storm fades to a distant murmur, and your eyes adjust to the dim light filtering through the entrance: there’s a pile of soft, dried bracken in the corner, a smooth river stone polished smooth by water sitting on the cave’s floor, and even a cluster of glowing ghost fungus clinging to the back wall, casting a soft, blue-green glow over everything.

Just as you bend to run your fingers over the fungus, a soft rustle comes from the bracken pile, and a red fox cub pokes its head out, its fluffy tail tucked over its tiny body to stay warm. It tilts its head at you, and when you hold out a hand, it trots over and curls up in your palm, warm and soft. The storm rages outside for what feels like an hour, but inside the cave, time slows, quiet and still—like the forest is catching its breath, tucked safe from the downpour.

When the hail and wind finally die down, the fox cub scampers back to its bracken nest, and you step outside to find the forest glowing: every leaf is strung with dewdrops that catch the fading twilight, and a double rainbow arcs over the treetops, its ends dipping into the nearby creek that’s swollen with rainwater. A small herd of deer steps out from the underbrush, pausing to glance at you with soft, gentle eyes before fading back into the trees, like they’re thanking you for keeping their little one safe. You keep walking, the air now fresh and sharp with pine and petrichor, and the forest feels more alive, more bright than it did before the storm rolled in.
