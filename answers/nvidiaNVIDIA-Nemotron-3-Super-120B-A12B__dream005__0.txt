

You push through a knot of people laughing too loudly at a joke no one else seems to hear—their smiles fixed, eyes glazed as they pass a single shrimp cocktail back and forth like a sacred object. The air smells suddenly of ozone and old library books. You stumble into a quieter cluster near a towering fern: three figures in identical grey suits standing perfectly still, not breathing, their faces smooth and featureless except for tiny, pinpoint holes where mouths should be. They don’t turn as you approach, but you feel their attention like a weight on your shoulders. You whisper your friend’s name—*just a sound, really*—and the suit on the left tilts its head 180 degrees, slow and jerky, until it’s staring at you upside down. Its hole-mouth exhales a puff of cold air that smells like rain on concrete. You stagger back, heart hammering, and spill your drink. The liquid doesn’t wet the floor; it beads up and rolls away like mercury, vanishing into the cracks between the tiles.

Panic makes the room tilt. You close your eyes, pressing palms to your temples, trying to remember what your friend looks like. Was it the scar above their eyebrow? The way they tuck their hair behind their left ear when nervous? The images feel like smoke. When you open your eyes, the fern is gone. In its place stands a long banquet table groaning under dishes made of liquid light—jellied constellations, soufflés that pulse like slow-motion supernovas. People glide along it in silence, serving themselves with tongs made of fused glass. They don’t eat; they merely hold the glowing food to their chests, where it sinks into their fabric like stars being absorbed into velvet. One figure turns toward you—a woman in a silver dress whose face shifts between your mother’s, your first teacher’s, and a stranger’s with kind eyes. She doesn’t speak. She simply lifts a spoonful of nebula-jello and offers it to you. You shake your head, backing away, and bump into something solid.

You freeze. It’s *them*. Your friend. Right there in the crowd, wearing that faded blue shirt you know so well, laughing with their head thrown back—*real* laughter, crinkles at the eyes, the slightly crooked front tooth you’ve teased them about for years. Relief floods you, so intense it makes your knees weak. You open your mouth to call out, to shove through the last few feet between you…

But as you take a step forward, the light shifts. The banquet table flickers. The silver-dressed woman is now handing the nebula-jello to *your friend*—who accepts it with a serene smile, their blue shirt subtly shifting to match the woman’s silver. Your friend’s eyes meet yours over the woman’s shoulder. Not with recognition. Not with surprise. With a quiet, sorrowful *understanding*, as if they’ve been waiting for you to see this all along. And in that instant, you know: you weren’t lost in the crowd. You were the one who stopped seeing them. The gathering wasn’t outside you—it was the noise you’d been making inside your own head to avoid the quiet truth that you’d been alone here all along.

You don’t call out. You turn instead, not to flee, but to walk slowly toward the edge of the room, where the walls bloom like slow-opening nightflowers, releasing a scent of wet stone and distant thunder. The crowd doesn’t notice you go. Behind you, the laughter continues—bright, hollow, and utterly indifferent. For the first time since you entered, you don’t feel afraid. You feel strangely light. The search is over. Not because you found them. But because you finally stopped needing to.

*(The dream dissolves not with a jolt, but with the quiet sensation of sinking into cool, deep water—no struggle, no surface, just the soft pressure of dark holding you.)*
