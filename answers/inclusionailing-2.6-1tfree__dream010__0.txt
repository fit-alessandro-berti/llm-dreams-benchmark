At first, the challenges feel like ordinary contest problems: coding riddles, timed design sprints, live debates. But the room begins to breathe. The walls are made of a honey-colored paper that thrums whenever someone hesitates. I notice the proctors have no faces—only polished brass where features should be, reflecting versions of myself that look exhausted or triumphant or afraid.

The second round asks us to build a bridge with materials that change substance based on how we speak. When I whisper, the ropes turn to silk; when I argue, the beams harden into stubborn iron. Across the chasm, my opponent (who looks like a childhood friend I haven’t seen in years) builds with laughter, their span shimmering like water. I choose silence, and my bridge becomes glass, terrifying and clear. We meet in the middle, and the glass doesn’t break. Instead, it sings, a note that unknots the tightness in my chest I didn’t know I was carrying.

By the final challenge, the competition has stopped being a contest and become a pilgrimage. The grand prize—my dream job—materializes not as a title or a salary, but as a door floating on the horizon, woven from questions I haven’t learned to ask yet. To reach it, I must leave the path that looks like success. The last obstacle is a garden of mirrors, each one showing a future I could have: comfortable, praised, safe, small. I choose a mirror that doesn’t reflect but absorbs, swallowing my image and giving back only the sound of wind through a city I’ve never visited but recognize as home.

When I step through the door, there is no ceremony. Only a wide, quiet desk with a blank ledger and a window that opens onto weather I can taste—sleet and orange blossoms at once. The work begins not with a clock but with a hunch: to unmake the story that competition is the only way to earn belonging. The room behind me dissolves into birds. I pick up the pen; the page learns my hand, and for the first time, my dream job learns my name.
