But as first mate Roark leans into the line, his face glints through the sheets of rain and I freeze— I know that face, that faded blue streak in his hair, that scar above his left eyebrow from the time I crashed my mom’s minivan into a mailbox senior year. Roark isn’t some stranger I signed on with; he’s me, 17 years old, angry and scared and convinced I’d never amount to anything that didn’t end in a wreck.

I twist to check on the cabin boy, who’d slipped on the drenched deck and was clinging to the rail, and that’s me too— 10 years old, scuffed sneakers, a scraped knee crusted with dock mud, the same kid who’d hidden in the library for a week after bullies tore up my first drawing of a ship. Every face staring up at me, begging for guidance, is a version of me I’d left behind, convinced I’d outgrown them, that they were dead weight to ditch to stay afloat.

The storm howls louder, and through the black clouds I spot a shoreline glowing gold, strung with all the things I’d abandoned to get here: my half-written college thesis, the golden retriever I’d had to rehome when I moved cross-country, the guitar I sold to pay my first month’s rent, every path I didn’t take stretched out like a safe harbor. For a second I want to turn the wheel toward it, to outrun the storm and curl up with all the what-ifs that felt easier than the mess I was in now.

But then I look at the crew huddled on the deck, 10-year-old me crying into 23-year-old me’s jacket, 17-year-old me still gripping the line like his life depended on it, and I realize the sea isn’t out to sink us. The storm is all the guilt I carried for leaving those parts of myself behind, all the doubt that I wasn’t captain enough to steer us through. I don’t need to run for the shore of what if. I yell, loud enough to cut through the gale, “Lower the sails! Batten down the hatches! We don’t run from this— we ride it out, together!”

The whole ship goes still for a beat, then the crew moves, not as panicked strangers, but as parts of the same whole. The rain slows, the waves soften from rabid whitecaps to gentle swells, and for the first time I can read the name blazed into the ship’s bow, the one I couldn’t remember when I woke up on the bridge: Unfinished. The sun crests the horizon, and 10-year-old me pulls a crumpled chocolate chip cookie from his pocket, passes it around, and 17-year-old me snorts, the first real laugh I’ve heard from that version of me in over a decade. I’m still holding the wheel, the sea stretching out ahead of us full of unknowns, but for the first time I don’t feel alone. I wake up ten minutes later, my hand curled around the edge of my mattress like it’s the wheel, and for the first time in years, I don’t feel like I’m treading water alone.
