The air grows heavy, charged with something older than electricity. The trees don't sway—they *lean*, as if bowing to a verdict only they can hear. Their leaves flip silver-side up in unison, a million tiny palms offered to something descending.

You smell it before you see it: wet iron, pine sap, and something floral rotting sweet beneath the loam. The temperature doesn't drop so much as *thicken*. Your breath hangs visible, though it was summer a moment ago.

A single drop hits your cheek. Warm. Thick. Not rain.

You wipe it away—your fingers come back stained the color of dried roses.

The canopy fractures open. Not with lightning, but with *light*: pale, directional, sweeping the forest floor like a searchlight. Where it touches, the undergrowth doesn't burn—it *accelerates*. Ferns unfurl in time-lapse, mushrooms bloom and sporulate in seconds, vines race toward the beam like addicts to a vein.

The beam finds you.

Your shadow stretches, detaches, and begins walking *away* from you—deeper into the woods, moving with purpose you didn't give it. It doesn't look back.

Behind you, the path you came from has vanished. Only the beam remains, and the thing your shadow went to meet.

Somewhere, a bell tolls once. Or maybe it's the sound of a tree splitting its own bark to grow faster.

You realize you're not cold anymore. You're *hollow*—cleared out for something to fill.

The light pulses. Once. Twice.

A voice, not in your ears but in the marrow of your femurs:

**"The offering was never the blood. It was the witness."**

What do you do?
