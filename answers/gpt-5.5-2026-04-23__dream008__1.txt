Your boss taps their watch, but the watch has no hands. “We need this by yesterday,” they say.

You nod confidently, even though your desk has become a kitchen table, and your computer is now a toaster. You press the lever, hoping a presentation will pop out. Instead, two slices of toast rise up with charts burned into them: one labeled “URGENT” and the other labeled “DO NOT PANIC.”

Everyone in the office is staring. Someone whispers, “They’re starting.”

So you take a deep breath and begin typing on the toaster. The keys appear one by one, glowing under your fingers. Each time you press one, the office stretches taller, the ceiling drifting farther away until the fluorescent lights look like stars.

Your boss leans over your shoulder. “Excellent,” they say. “But can you make it more… blue?”

The entire room turns blue.

The deadline, which had been hovering in the air like a storm cloud, suddenly becomes a tiny paper airplane. It circles your head once, then lands gently in your hand. On it, written in your own handwriting, are the words:

“You don’t have to finish everything to begin.”

You look up, and the office is empty now. Quiet. Peaceful.

The toaster dings.

Inside is a perfect, completed project—warm, golden, and somehow stapled.

Your boss reappears, takes one look, and smiles. “Right on time.”

Then the floor turns into an escalator, carrying you upward into a bright morning sky, and just as you realize you’ve forgotten your shoes, you wake up.
