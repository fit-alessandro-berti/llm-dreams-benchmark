**...stop. I decide to** not argue anymore.

The word hangs in the thick air between us—a collective puff of exhaled, frustrated certainty. The argument had built up so quickly, layer upon layer, until it felt less like a conversation and more like a pressure cooker about to explode. Dust from the bookshelf settled on the polished hardwood floor, and even the gentle rhythm of the refrigerator hum seemed to amplify the silence, making it feel brittle, ready to shatter.

I look at your face. It is contorted, not by anger, exactly, but by a profound exhaustion—the kind that comes from fighting something you desperately want to believe in. Your eyes are red-rimmed, and there’s a sadness flickering across your expression that is far more painful than the actual disagreement.

I want to dismantle every point you’ve made. I want to point out every flaw in your logic, every instance where you misunderstood my intention. My mouth moves, forming the words, sharp and defensive, but before they leave my lips, something shifts.

It starts with the light.

The fluorescent glow of the living room seems to dim, not gradually, but abruptly, like a curtain being drawn over the sun. It becomes a sickly, bruised twilight, and the air cools instantly, smelling of ozone and rain that hasn't fallen yet.

I feel my muscles slacken. The adrenaline that had been pumping through me, fueling the fight, suddenly drains away, leaving me weightless, anchored only by the gravity of the deep, painful understanding that we are going this far apart, over this thing.

I don’t walk away. I can’t. Instead, I take one single step backward, and the floor beneath my heel feels strangely soft, like walking on thick velvet. As I take that step, the edges of the room begin to bleed. The bookshelves elongate, the geometric patterns of the rug soften, and the familiar outlines of the furniture begin to waver, as if viewed through heat haze.

I keep looking at you, not to defend myself, or to explain, but simply to see you in this altered space, in this moment where the rules of reality seem suspended. I search your face, desperate to find the version of you that existed before the disagreement—the version of you that remembers the quiet, comfortable nights and the effortless laughter.

And then, I realize that we are standing in a space that isn't our living room. It is a liminal space—a threshold carved only by our suspended resentment. The argument hasn't ended; it has merely elevated to a symbolic plane, where the truth is not found in words spoken, but in the sheer, unsettling weight of the silence.

I don't know how to break the tension, because the disagreement has been replaced by a far more unsettling form of conflict: the realization that the argument isn't about what we said, but about the fragile, irreplaceable scaffolding of *us*. I simply stand there, suspended between the memory of shouting and the quiet terror of the silence, waiting for the world—and you—to tell me which way to fall.

And then, a sound. It's faint, high, like a single glass chime struck off-key, and the light flickers back to normal, revealing that we are still in the living room, the scent of dust and cooling air replacing the ozone. The silence remains, heavier and more complex than before.

You are the only one who seems to notice. You don't speak, but you slowly lift your hand, not in a gesture of defeat, but in a gesture of question, pointing first to the bookshelf, then to your chest, and finally, meeting my eyes.

The dream hasn't ended. It has merely paused, forcing us to face the wreckage of what we thought we knew.
