...and the challenges keep changing shape, the way they only do in dreams.

In the first round, you must write your résumé on the surface of a lake with your fingertip. The words hold somehow, rippling but legible, and the judges—three owls wearing reading glasses—nod approvingly at your bullet points.

The second challenge takes place in an endless office where the floors are made of trampoline. Your task is to lead a team meeting, but every time you make a good point, you bounce slightly higher. A contestant next to you says something brilliant and launches toward the ceiling, never to be seen again. You feel guilty for celebrating, but you're through to the finals.

Between rounds, you're given lunch in a cafeteria that is also somehow your childhood kitchen. Your grandmother is there, and she tells you the secret to winning: "They aren't looking for the best candidate. They're looking for the one who's *paying attention*." You don't know what this means, but you write it down on a napkin that immediately folds itself into a paper crane and flies away.

The final challenge is announced over a loudspeaker that sounds like your own voice: *"For the last task, you must do the dream job. Right now. Begin."*

You look around. The other finalists are frozen, waiting for instructions that never come. But you remember the napkin. You stop waiting for someone to explain the rules and simply start working—though the work itself is wonderfully strange. You're arranging clouds into the shapes of people's unspoken hopes. You're answering letters that were never sent. You're untangling a ball of light, and somehow you know exactly which strand to pull.

The judges appear one by one, and they're all versions of you—older, younger, tired, hopeful. They don't score you. They just watch, and one of them mouths something you can't quite hear.

The loudspeaker crackles. *"The winner is—"*

And that's when you wake up.

But here's the strange part: your hand is moving, as if still mid-task, still pulling that thread of light. And all day, you can't shake the feeling that somewhere, in some conference room just past the edge of sleep, they're still holding your prize—waiting for you to come back and claim it.
