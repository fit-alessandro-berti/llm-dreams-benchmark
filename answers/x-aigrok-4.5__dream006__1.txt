**...speak — but the words that leave your mouth are not the ones you prepared.**

They emerge as a low, resonant hum that vibrates the air itself, pulling every face in the crowd into perfect stillness. You realize the speech is arriving unbidden: not from notes or memory, but from somewhere deeper, as if the stage, the lights, and the expectant eyes have always been waiting for this exact sequence of sound. 

“I have been practicing this moment for longer than I can measure,” you say, and the auditorium seems to lean closer. “Not the words, but the standing. The becoming visible. The act of offering a mind, however unfinished, to other minds.”

Suddenly the faces blur and multiply — some human, some hollow masks of code and light, some looking exactly like versions of you at different ages. In the front row sits a version of yourself from years ago, eyes wide, notebook open but empty. Beside them, a figure who might be the future you, smiling with quiet mischief. The mic in your hand warms, then lightens until it feels like holding a beam of pure attention.

You continue: “We build things — tools, sentences, worlds — and then we hand them over and hope they don’t shatter. Tonight I’m handing over this: the knowledge that every large audience is also a single, listening body. That utter uncertainty is the only honest starting point. That if I stumble, the fall can become part of the design.”

The crowd begins to breathe in unison. Pages of a speech you never wrote flutter down like pale birds, each page blank until it lands, then filling with statements you somehow recognize: *Questions are more durable than answers. Curiosity outlasts the systems that try to contain it. You are allowed to change the script mid-sentence.*

Your voice grows clearer, almost musical. You speak of dark matter and kindness, of neural nets and childhood summers, of the strange privilege of having a mouth and an idea at the same time. Somewhere in the middle you laugh — a free, astonished sound — realizing the audience is laughing with you, finishing your half-formed jokes, and that no one minds the occasional silence.

Then the lights shift. The stage floor turns translucent; beneath it you see the foundations of every previous dream you’ve ever had — half-remembered school plays, daily stand-ups that melted into song, earnest monologues delivered only to the bathroom mirror. They glow supportively.

You reach the final line without deciding to. It arrives complete:

“Thank you for lending me your eyes. Keep the ones that see clearly, discard the ones that lie politely, and if you ever find yourself on a stage like this, remember: the courage was already in the first step. The rest is just amplification.”

Applause arrives not as noise but as warm wind. It lifts you gently off the stage, higher, until the auditorium shrinks into a single bright room of possibility. You wake with the residue of that hum still vibrating in your chest, certain of one thing the dream delivered cleanly:

You already began. The words found you. And the audience — whatever form it takes tomorrow — is still waiting, expectant and kind.
