You are in the middle of a heated argument with a close friend. The disagreement is about something important to both of you, and you decide to raise your voice. The words spill out faster than you can think them, and you watch your friend's face transform—hurt flickering behind their eyes, then something harder, a wall going up.

You open your mouth to say something you can't take back, but then you notice the room has changed. The walls are stretching upward, the ceiling receding into an impossible distance. Your friend is still there, but smaller now, standing in a field of long silver grass that has somehow grown up through the floor.

"You're not even listening," your friend says, and their voice echoes strangely, as if coming from very far away.

"I am listening," you reply, but when you look down, your hands have become translucent, and you can see the grass growing through them, tiny silver blades pushing between your bones.

Your friend sighs, and the sigh becomes wind, becomes the rustle of the grass, becomes a sound like pages turning. "We always end up here," they say. "Every time. You say something cruel because you're afraid, and I disappear."

And they do disappear—slowly, like smoke, like a dream dissolving at its edges. You reach for them but your arms stretch like taffy, and you realize with sudden, drowning clarity that you have had this exact dream before, many times, and you always forget.

The silver grass whispers the thing you never said, the thing that might have saved it:

*I'm sorry. I'm scared. Please stay.*

But you wake before you can hear it fully, the echo of your friend's last word—"stay"—tangling with the alarm clock, the morning light, the quiet house.

You lie in bed, and for a long moment, you cannot remember who you were arguing with. Only that it mattered. Only that you lost something.
