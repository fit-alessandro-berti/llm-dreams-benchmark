...plant a garden.

You find the seeds in a rusted tin buried beneath the floorboards of an abandoned farmhouse—tomato, bean, marigold, squash. Their paper packets have long since rotted to dust, but the seeds themselves sleep on, patient as stones.

The soil fights you. It's gray and lifeless, churned with broken glass and the white ribs of old bones. You spend weeks hauling water from the creek in plastic jugs, your shoulders burning, your hands splitting open at the knuckles. You piss in the compost heap because nitrogen is nitrogen and nothing goes to waste. You sing to the dirt, a tuneless humming, because your grandmother once told you plants grow better with music and you have nothing left to lose.

The first sparrow doesn't return until the marigolds bloom.

Orange. Impossible orange. You weep into your palms, dirty fingers pressed against your face, and the bird tilts its head on the fence post as if to say *yes, this too.*

By harvest, the tomatoes taste like sunlight made solid. You eat the first one standing in the row, juice running down your chin, seeds catching in your beard. The flavor is so bright it hurts—*alive, alive, alive*—and you understand then that this was never about survival.

It was about remembering how to be soft in a world that only rewards hardness.

A child finds you there, later. Barefoot, silent, eyes like flint. You hand her a warm tomato without a word. She bites. Juice stains her chin. She smiles, and the expression rearranges her whole face into something unrecognizable.

Hope, you think. *It tastes like dirt and sugar and the color orange.*

You wake with soil under your fingernails.
