...and the architecture begins to lose its sense of gravity. 

One group is seated around a long, mahogany table that stretches toward a horizon that shouldn't exist. They are eating fruit that glows with a soft, internal light, and as they laugh, the sound doesn't come from their mouths, but from the chandeliers hanging above, chiming like crystal bells. I try to ask them if they’ve seen my friend, but when I speak, my words emerge as colorful bubbles that float away and pop silently against the velvet curtains.

I move on, passing a circle of dancers who are moving in such extreme slow motion that they appear to be frozen in mid-air. Their clothes are made of shifting smoke, and as I brush past them, I feel a chill that smells like ozone and old library books. I catch a glimpse of a familiar silhouette—the exact height, the specific way they tilt their head—and my heart leaps. I lunge forward, calling their name, but as I reach them, the person turns around. Their face is a smooth, featureless mask of polished silver, reflecting only my own wide-eyed expression back at me.

The hallway begins to bend, the walls curving upward until the floor feels like the side of a canyon. The music shifts; the chime of the chandeliers becomes a low, rhythmic thrumming, like the heartbeat of the building itself. The guests are no longer people, but shadows cast by objects that aren't there. 

Then, the floor beneath me softens. The marble turns to warm, dark water, and instead of sinking, I find I am walking on the surface, each step sending ripples of violet light across the room. I look up, and the ceiling has dissolved into a vast, swirling nebula. There, suspended in the starlight, I see them. They are standing on a floating fragment of the ballroom, looking down at me with a smile that feels like a memory I can't quite grasp. 

I reach up, stretching my fingers toward the sky, and just as the distance between us vanishes, the gravity flips, and I begin to fall upward into the stars.
