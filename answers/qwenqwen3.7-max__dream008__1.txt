"We need this assembled into a working timeline before the sun sets," your boss says. But when you look up, your boss’s face is completely smooth, like an unpainted mannequin, and his voice sounds like it's echoing from the bottom of a deep well. 

You try to pick up a gear, but your fingers have turned into soft, uncooked spaghetti. They bend and flop uselessly against the cold brass metal. A familiar, heavy dream-panic sets in. You glance at the clock on the wall to check the time, but the numbers are melting, dripping down the drywall like black ink. The hands are spinning backward so fast they blur into a solid, humming circle.

"Tick tock," the mannequin-boss whispers, though he has no mouth. He begins to grow, his head pushing up through the acoustic ceiling tiles, showering your desk in white dust and flickering fluorescent light. 

Desperate, you sweep the gears onto the floor, thinking you can assemble them with your feet, but the gray office carpet has suddenly turned into a shallow, freezing ocean. The gears sink into the dark water, glowing faintly blue as they drift away like mechanical jellyfish. 

You stand up to run, but your rolling office chair is now a small wooden rowboat. The fabric cubicle walls fold flat like cardboard, revealing that your workplace is actually a vast, endless salt flat under a purple twilight sky. 

Your boss, now a towering giant made entirely of stacked manila folders, points a massive, silver paperclip at you. *"The deadline,"* he booms, his voice shaking the clouds. 

You look down at your hands. They are normal again. But instead of holding a tool to fix the clocks, you are holding a single, perfectly ripe peach. With the sudden, unquestionable clarity that only exists in dreams, you realize the impossible project was never about the gears. The project was to eat the peach before it bruised. 

You take a bite. It tastes like static electricity and childhood summers. The giant folder-boss dissolves into a flock of white pigeons, the salt flat turns to mist, and you begin to fall upward into a bright, blinding light, waking up just as your real alarm begins to ring.
