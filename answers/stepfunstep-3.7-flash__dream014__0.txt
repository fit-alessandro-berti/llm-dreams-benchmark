The city hums around you—honking cabs, a busker’s off-key guitar chord, the clink of coffee cups from the sidewalk café behind you—but their small, huddled circle feels like a pocket of warm light cut loose from the noise.
The woman at the front of the group, her knit scarf slipping off one shoulder and a paper bag of warm cinnamon rolls sticking out of her canvas tote, steps forward first. “We saw you circling that same mural three times, honey,” she says, and her voice is soft, like the lullabies you half-remember from childhood. She pulls a still-warm roll out of the bag, the sugar crust crinkling as she hands it to you, and you realize your stomach had been growling so loud you’d almost forgotten it.
The guy next to her, skateboard slung over his back and a faded vintage band tee peeking out under his flannel, grins. “I know every crack in the sidewalk in this neighborhood. Where you headed?” You fumble for the address of the little used bookstore your grandma used to drag you to every Saturday when you were small—the one with the calico cat that always sat in the front window, the one you haven’t found in ten years, not since the neighborhood got rebuilt and all the old storefronts vanished.
The teen at the back of the group, library books stacked so high under one arm they’re almost hiding their face, pipes up before the skateboarder can answer. “Oh! I walk past there every day on my way to the library. It’s on that little side street no one ever notices, past the laundromat with the flickering neon sign.” They pull a crumpled, coffee-stained map out of their backpack, and when they point to the spot, you recognize the faded chipped blue sign you’d been searching for, tucked between a taqueria and a vape shop you’d never looked twice at before.
The walk there feels like floating. The woman tells you she used to buy her first poetry book at that exact store when she was 16, the skateboarder says he’s skated past the window a hundred times but never noticed the cat, the teen shows you the pressed four-leaf clover they taped to the inside of their library card. You point out the stray orange cat napping on the fire escape you’d passed three times without seeing, and they all coo like they’re looking at a long-lost friend.
When you turn the corner onto the little side street, the bell above the bookstore door jingles before you even step inside. The calico cat is right where you remember, stretched out on the windowsill, and the faded blue sign is still there, half-peeling at the edges. You turn to wave your thanks, but the group is already halfway down the block, the woman waving back, the skateboarder doing a little kickflip that makes the teen laugh so hard they almost drop their stack of books.
You step inside, the smell of old paper and lavender dryer sheets washing over you, and the jumble of lost, alone feelings you’d been carrying all day melts away. When you look down at your hand later, you still have a smudge of cinnamon roll sugar on your thumb, and you swear you can still hear the busker’s guitar and their laughter mixing together on the wind, even though the street is quiet now.
