When you touch the wood, it isn’t wood at all, but warm skin, pulsing with a rhythm that matches your heartbeat, or perhaps your heartbeat has slowed to match its own.

You bring your ear to the lock and hear not the sea, but a conversation in a language you haven’t spoken since childhood, spoken by a voice that is unmistakably yours yet layered and strange. The metal of the clasp begins to soften, melting into liquid silver that pools in your palm and evaporates into a scent—lavender, or old books, or the precise smell of rain on hot asphalt from a summer day you can’t quite place.

The box grows lighter, then weightless, and the intricate patterns lift off the surface like moths, circling your head in a halo of glowing script. You realize, with the sudden certainty that only comes in dreams, that the box was never locked to keep you out, but to keep something *in*—something that has been waiting for you to stop looking for a key and simply admit that you already know what’s inside.

So you say its name, though you didn’t know you knew it, and the lid dissolves like sugar in water. Inside, there is no darkness, only an expanding horizon, and a small wooden key lying on a bed of velvet that is the exact color of your mother’s favorite dress, or maybe the sky just before a storm. You reach for it, and your hand passes through, because the box has become a doorway, or you have become the box, and the patterns are now etched across your ribs, humming a lullaby that will vanish the moment you wake, leaving you with the lingering suspicion that you left something running—something important, still breathing—in the other room of the dream.
