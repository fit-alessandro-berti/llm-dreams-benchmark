You wake up in a completely different era, with no modern technology around. As you explore your new surroundings, you realize you need to find water—the sun beats down unmercially on a parched landscape, and your throat feels like sandpaper. But as you scan the horizon, you notice something that doesn't belong: a shimmering, translucent archway hovering a few feet above the ground, its edges rippling like heat haze.

Curiosity pulls you forward, and when you step through the arch, the dry plains vanish. You're standing in a lush medieval market square, the air thick with the smell of woodsmoke and roasting meat. Merchants cry their wares, children chase a stray dog, and minstrels strum lutes on a wooden stage. You check your pockets and find a few unfamiliar coins, along with a neatly folded note in elegant handwriting: "Trust the clockmaker. Time is not what it seems."

The dream shifts—always deeper, always stranger—until you find yourself in front of a crooked shop squeezed between a bakery and a tannery. A sign above the door reads "Elias Thorne, Horologist and Philosopher." Inside, the walls are covered in intricate timepieces, their hands sweeping backward, forward, or in spirals. An old man with kind eyes looks up from his workbench and smiles as if he's been expecting you.

"You're the one who walks through the next," he says, his voice echoing as if from far away. "We've been waiting quite a while. But first—would you like some tea? The pot's been steeping since Tuesday last."

Your head spins. Every time you try to ask a question, the dream twists again, and you find yourself somewhere else: a moonlit garden where the roses whisper secrets in a language you almost understand; a vast library with books that write themselves as you watch; a battlefield where soldiers made of clockwork march beneath a blood-red moon. Through it all, the archways keep appearing, each one slightly different, each one pulling you further into the labyrinth of the dream.

Eventually, you realize you're not just a passive wanderer—you're piecing together fragments of a story that the dream itself is trying to tell you. And it all seems to center on a key hidden in the gears of a great celestial clock, somewhere between moments of waking and the deep, uncharted territory of sleep.

Just as you reach for the key, the dreamer stirs. You feel the pull of consciousness, like a tide receding. The world blurs, the clockmaker's shop fades, the archways collapse into pinpricks of light. You wake in your bed, gasping, fingers clutching at the blanket. On your nightstand, where no clock was before, sits a small silver pocket watch. Its hands are frozen at the exact moment you opened your eyes.

Outside your window, the real world spins on. But you remember the note, and you wonder, briefly, before logic takes hold: *Trust the clockmaker. Time is not what it seems.*
