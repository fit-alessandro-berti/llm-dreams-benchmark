• ...You start to panic, then notice a tiny brass compass sewn into the seam of your jacket pocket, one the meeting card
  in your other hand insists should have come from somewhere familiar: “Twelve minutes, Old Quarter, Blackwater
  Clocktower.”

  Every street sign is written in a script you almost recognize. You follow the sound of a distant bell tower and a
  rhythm of footsteps that seems to sync with your heartbeat. As you turn onto a narrow lane, every building is a maze
  of mirrors and windows reflecting the same face: yours, older and stranger by each glance. At the end of the lane, a
  black taxi idles with its lights off. The driver never raises his head, just points to the back seat.
  Inside the cab is a map pinned to the dash with a single glowing pin. The city outside rewrites itself with every turn
  —bridges appearing, alleys folding, crowds thinning—until the car stops in front of a narrow iron gate. No numbers on
  it, only one word etched into the metal: TIMEKEEP.

  You step out and the city falls silent. The gate opens by itself, and a staircase of warm, breathing stone leads down
  into the meeting hall below. At the far end, the conference table is already set. Your meeting isn’t about work or
  deadlines. It’s about remembering how to wake up.
