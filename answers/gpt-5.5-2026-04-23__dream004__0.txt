pick up a brush that seems to be made of moonlight.

The moment your fingers close around it, the air goes silent. The shifting colors pause, hovering like curious birds. The canvas waits.

You touch the brush to the surface, and instead of paint, a doorway appears.

At first it is small—just a blue rectangle trembling at the edges—but then it stretches taller than you, its frame blooming with vines, clocks, feathers, and tiny glowing eyes. Behind it you hear the sound of waves, laughter, and someone whispering your name from very far away.

You step through.

On the other side is a beach beneath an upside-down sky. The ocean is above you, rolling gently across the heavens, and stars swim through it like silver fish. Beneath your feet, the sand is warm and covered in footprints that appear before you take each step.

A fox made of stained glass sits nearby, polishing its tail with a cloud.

“You’re late,” it says.

“For what?” you ask.

“For the part where you remember.”

It points toward the horizon, where a city is rising and falling like a chest breathing in sleep. Its towers bend into impossible shapes, and every window reflects a different version of you: a child, an old person, a bird, a flame, a shadow with bright eyes.

You walk toward the city, and with every step, the world repaints itself. The sand becomes grass, the grass becomes pages, the pages become mirrors. The fox follows, humming a song you almost know.

At the city gate, you find the blank canvas again.

Only now it is no longer blank.

It shows you standing there, brush in hand, about to paint the next world.

You look back at the fox. “What should I make?”

The fox smiles, and its glass body fills with sunrise.

“Something you’ll want to wake up inside.”

So you lift the moonlit brush, touch it to the canvas, and paint a door shaped like your own heart.

It opens slowly.

And just before you step through, you realize the entire dream has been painting you, too.
