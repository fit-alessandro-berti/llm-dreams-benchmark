The sky is bruise-purple, folding over itself like wet velvet, and each wave that strikes the hull sounds less like water and more like a door slamming in a house I’ve never seen. The crew’s faces blur in the lantern-light—some are men I’ve sailed with for years, others are strangers who wear my father’s eyes, my childhood teacher’s voice. They do not speak; they only watch.

I open my mouth to give an order, but the wind tears the words away before I can shape them. So I stop trying to command the storm and instead begin to sing. It is an old song, one I do not remember learning, low and slow, with a rhythm like oars dipping into black water. At first the gale swallows it, but then the ship itself seems to lean in, listening. The waves, still enormous, no longer crash—they roll, they breathe. The rain turns warm, falling in silver strings.

I tell the crew, “We are not here to conquer the sea. We are here to be carried through it.” The words feel true, even if they are not advice. One by one they leave the rails and take up tasks that make no practical sense: mending a net that has no holes, charting a constellation that only appears when you close your eyes, feeding breadcrumbs to a gull made of fog. The storm does not end, exactly, but it changes its mind about us.

By dawn—if it is dawn; the horizon has melted into something peach-colored and humming—the sea is glass. We drift through a city of sunken spires, their bells tolling underwater, and I realize the ship has no destination because the dream has no map. The crew gathers around me, not waiting anymore, only present. I place my hand on the wheel and feel it turn by itself, steering us gently into a sky that has opened like a curtain.

Then the deck softens beneath my feet, the salt smell becomes the smell of sheets, and I begin to wake, carrying with me the sound of bells and the certainty, already fading, that I have been here before.
