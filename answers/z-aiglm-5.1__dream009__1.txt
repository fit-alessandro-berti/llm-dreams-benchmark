The air hit different here—thick with woodsmoke and animal musk, carrying the distant lowing of cattle across rolling green hills. Rough wool scratched against skin that remembered only soft cotton and climate control. Calloused fingers that had never known true labor flexed instinctively, testing their new limits.

The village sprawled below the hilltop where consciousness had returned: a cluster of thatched roofs huddled together like frightened sheep, smoke curling from countless hearths into the pearl-gray morning sky. No power lines scarred the horizon. No engines thrummed. Just wind through tall grass and the distant rhythmic clang of a blacksmith already at work.

Movement drew the eye—a woman hauling water from the communal well, her stride unhurried despite the bucket's obvious weight. She moved with an economy of motion that spoke of years performing this exact task. Behind her, a boy chased chickens through the mud with more enthusiasm than skill.

The sun climbed higher, indifferent to one displaced soul's crisis. Hunger gnawed—real hunger, sharp and demanding, not the polite request for a snack remembered from another life. Thirst followed close behind. The well sat in the village's heart. Those people held knowledge necessary for survival.

First contact would determine everything.
