...suddenly, a group of kind strangers offers to help you find your way, and you accept. Their hands appear in your periphery—not solid, but luminous, like threads of moonlight woven into human palms. They don’t speak your language, yet their gestures are a universal map: palms facing upward to say *"we lift,"* fingers pointing not to a destination, but to the spaces between buildings—*the gaps where the city breathes*.

You walk with them, and the crowd parts not around you, but *through* you, as if you’ve become a temporary seam in the fabric of the street. Their touch leaves warmth where your pockets once held only hollow echoes. A bakery’s scent shifts from longing to memory; a bus stop transforms into a small galaxy of waiting lights.

One of them presses a folded paper into your hand. Unfolding it, you see not words, but a sketch of your own face—older, kinder, standing in this same street, now holding out hands to someone else. The realization hits gently: you were never lost. You were only forgetting how to be found.

The strangers dissolve into the crowd like chalk drawings washed by rain, leaving you at a crosswalk where the signal changes not with a beep, but with a sigh. You step forward, not knowing where you’re going, but certain the path will appear beneath your feet—because you are no longer walking alone. The city hums, and for the first time, it sounds like a lullaby.
