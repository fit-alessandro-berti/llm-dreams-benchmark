The cracked asphalt is stubborn, but you scrape away the debris with a bent piece of rebar, your hands raw and blistered. You find a single packet of seeds in the pocket of a long-dead stranger—tomatoes, impossibly optimistic. You work by the sickly light of a perpetual gray dusk, watering them with filtered rainwater you collect in an old hubcap. The wind carries the distant howls of things that used to be human, but you keep going.

At first, nothing happens. Then one morning a single green shoot pushes through the dust like it's arguing with the apocalypse. You laugh for the first time in months—dry, cracked, but real. Other survivors start appearing at the edges of the lot, drawn by the ridiculous sight of someone gardening in hell. A woman with a scarred face offers a rusted watering can. A kid brings a half-buried shovel. Soon the "garden" is a stubborn little patch of green defiance, ringed by scavenged tires and broken street signs.

The dangers don't vanish. Raiders come one night and you fight them off with the rebar and a lot of screaming. The soil is poisoned in places and two of the plants wither. But every morning you check the tomatoes like they're the last good idea left in the world.

One evening, as the sun bleeds orange through the toxic haze, you sit among the tiny vines and realize the dream has shifted. The ruins feel less like an ending and more like a blank page. You wonder what else could grow here if you just kept showing up.

You wake up with dirt under your fingernails and the faint taste of something green on your tongue.
