You start to run—though not in panic, but with the eerie calm of a dreamer who knows the rules don’t apply. The skyline pulses like a living thing: towers of glass and bone twist into each other, their windows blinking in Morse code you almost understand. The streets are paved with cobblestones that whisper names—your mother’s, your childhood dog’s, the name of a book you never finished.

A streetlamp leans toward you, its glow honey-gold, and whispers, *“Turn left at the clock that doesn’t tick.”*

You turn—and there it is: a brass clock embedded in the side of a bookstore, its hands spinning backward. You reach out. The moment your fingers brush the cold metal, the city inhales.

Buildings fold inward like origami. The sky becomes a ceiling of velvet, stitched with constellations that rearrange into your childhood doodles. A man in a suit made of falling leaves appears beside you, holding a door handle where a door should be.

“Ah,” he says, smiling without lips. “You're early.”

“Early?” you whisper. “I’m late!”

He laughs, and the sound is wind through autumn trees. “In this city, time isn’t a line. It’s a party you arrived at before you decided to come.”

He turns the handle—not a door, but a *memory*—and behind it: a room. Soft light. A chair. A single envelope on the table, sealed with wax shaped like your fingerprint.

You open it.

Inside: a single sentence.

> *“You were never late. You were becoming.”*

Then you wake.

But your alarm clock is gone.

And the scent of falling leaves lingers on your pillow.
