I grip the wheel with white-knuckled hands, salt spray stinging my eyes as the ship pitches violently beneath us. The wind howls like a living thing, tearing at the tattered sails, and my crew—faces pale, eyes wide—clings to the rigging, waiting for my word.

“Hold fast!” I roar over the thunder. “Secure the lines! We ride this beast, not fight it!”

Lightning cracks the sky in half, and in that blinding flash I see it: the sea itself is splitting open ahead of us, revealing not rocks or reef, but a vast, glowing chasm that spirals downward like a drain. The crew gasps. Some drop to their knees. I feel the dream-shift in my bones—the storm was never the enemy. The storm was the doorway.

I throw the wheel hard to starboard and laugh, wild and certain. “We go through!” 

The ship lurches, nose diving into the glowing whirlpool. Water doesn’t crash over the deck—it parts around us like silk. We plunge through color and sound and memory: my childhood lighthouse, the first kiss on a pier at midnight, the day I first felt the wind choose me. The crew stares in awe as the chaos becomes a tunnel of stars.

We burst out the other side into perfect, glassy calm. The sky is velvet black and full of new constellations that spell my name. The sea is mirror-still, reflecting every star twice. A single island rises ahead—emerald cliffs, a golden beach, and at its center a lighthouse whose beam pulses in the exact rhythm of my heartbeat.

I turn to my crew, every face now shining with the same strange peace I feel.

“We made it,” I tell them. “Because we stopped trying to conquer the storm and chose to become it.”

They cheer, but their voices are already fading, becoming wind and wave again. The lighthouse beam sweeps across the deck one last time, warm as a hand on my shoulder.

I wake with the taste of salt on my lips and the echo of thunder in my chest, smiling into the dark of my bedroom like I’ve just been handed a secret map to every storm I’ll ever face.
