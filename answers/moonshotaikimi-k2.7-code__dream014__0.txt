...and you follow them down a street you somehow hadn’t noticed before. The buildings seem to lean in gently, their windows glowing with warm amber light, and the noise of the city softens into a low, comforting hum, like the purr of a great sleeping cat.

One of the strangers—an older woman with silver-threaded braids and a coat the color of rain-soaked earth—slips her hand into yours. Her palm is warm, papery, and faintly smells of cinnamon. “You’re not lost,” she says, her voice calm and certain. “You’ve only forgotten where you were going.”

Another stranger, a young man with paint stains on his fingers and a bicycle basket full of books, points up. You look and see that the street signs are written not in words, but in feelings—small brass plaques that read *Relief*, *Curiosity*, *Belonging*, *Home*. The wind changes direction, and somehow you know which one to follow.

As you walk, the others talk to you—not with questions, but with gifts: a folded paper map that unfolds into a bird and flies ahead to show the way; a small stone that pulses gently when you hold it, reminding you that you are real and here and wanted; a song hummed by a child in the group that makes the traffic lights turn green just for you.

You pass a fountain in the middle of the square, and its water rises upward instead of falling, gathering into a bright doorway. The strangers stop at the edge and smile at you.

“We can’t go through with you,” says the woman, squeezing your hand once before letting go. “But we can wait right here until you’re sure.”

You step forward. The water is cool but not cold. Beyond it, you can see a version of the city that is quieter—your own street, your own window, a light left on. You understand now that being lost was only the first part of the dream. The second part is realizing that help can appear from nowhere, that hands reach out, that you are allowed to be guided.

You turn back to thank them, but they are already scattering into the crowd, becoming ordinary again—just faces in a bustling city. Yet something in their eyes, in the way one of them waves, tells you they remember too.

You walk through the doorway of water. You wake up with the taste of cinnamon on your tongue and the sound of a bicycle bell fading, already half-believing that if you ever feel lost again, someone, somewhere, will offer you their hand.
