You start to walk, then hurry, then almost run, though every street seems to bend away from the direction you choose.

The city is impossibly large. Glass towers lean over narrow alleys. Trains pass silently above your head on rails that disappear into clouds. Storefronts glow with signs in languages you almost understand, and every clock you pass shows a different time. One says you are early. One says you are late. One has no hands at all.

You reach into your pocket for your phone, but find only a folded paper map. When you open it, the streets rearrange themselves like living veins. A red dot pulses somewhere near the center.

**MEETING — 12:00**

There is no address.

A woman selling oranges from a silver cart notices your panic.

“You’re going the wrong way,” she says.

“Do you know where the meeting is?”

She smiles as if the answer is obvious. “Everyone knows where it is. But no one arrives there by knowing.”

Before you can ask what that means, a bell rings from somewhere deep in the city. The crowd around you begins moving all at once, flowing into the subway entrances, down side streets, through revolving doors. You are swept with them into a station made of blue tile and warm rain. Water drips upward from the floor to the ceiling.

A train arrives with no driver and no destination displayed. The doors open. Inside, every seat is occupied by someone who looks vaguely familiar: an old teacher, a childhood neighbor, a friend you have not seen in years, a version of yourself from a photograph you forgot existed.

You step in.

The train accelerates without sound. Through the windows, the tunnels give way to open sky. The city spreads below you like a circuit board, bright and endless. Your map unfolds again in your hands, but now it is blank except for one sentence:

**Bring what you forgot.**

Your heart sinks. You do not know what you forgot.

At the next stop, the doors open into a library. Not a station that looks like a library, but a real one: shelves rising higher than buildings, ladders moving on their own, books whispering as their pages turn. A receptionist sits behind a marble desk, typing on a typewriter that produces tiny white birds instead of paper.

“I’m here for a meeting,” you say.

She looks at you over her glasses. “Naturally.”

“Where is it?”

“Top floor. Or bottom floor. Depends on what you remember.”

“I don’t remember anything.”

“Then you’d better start.”

She hands you a key made of black thread.

You take the stairs because the elevators are full of mirrors, and in each mirror you are wearing different clothes, carrying different worries. As you climb, fragments return to you: a room with sunlight on the floor, the smell of rain on pavement, someone laughing at a kitchen table, a promise you made and never kept.

With each memory, the staircase changes. Carpet becomes stone. Stone becomes grass. Grass becomes a narrow bridge over a canal where paper lanterns float beneath you like small moons.

At the end of the bridge stands a door.

It is ordinary. Wooden. Slightly scratched. The kind of door you might pass every day without noticing.

The thread-key dissolves into your palm, and the door opens.

Inside is a conference room with a long table. The chairs are empty except for one at the far end.

Someone is waiting there.

At first you think it is a stranger. Then you see it is you, older or younger or perhaps only more awake. This other you gestures to the chair opposite.

“You made it,” they say.

“I don’t know what I was supposed to bring.”

“Yes, you do.”

You look down. In your hands is the blank map. But it is no longer blank. It has become a small mirror, and in it you see the city behind you: every wrong turn, every impossible station, every face that tried to help you. None of it was a delay. All of it was the way.

The clock on the wall strikes twelve.

“What is this meeting about?” you ask.

The other you leans forward.

“Whether you’re ready to wake up.”

For a moment, the city goes silent. Even the rain stops climbing. The towers outside the window soften at the edges, as if painted in water. You feel the meeting, the map, the panic, the streets, all loosening around you.

You want to ask one more question, but you cannot remember what it is.

The other you smiles gently.

“Don’t worry,” they say. “You’ll remember on the way.”

Then the room fills with morning light, and the city folds itself into the sound of your own breathing.
