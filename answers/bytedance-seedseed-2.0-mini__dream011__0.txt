You notice the questions are scrawled in smudged, warm sepia ink that bleeds slightly into the thick, recycled paper, as if the writer had leaned too close while crying or laughing mid-question. The first one sits front and center—no multiple choice bubbles, no formulaic prompts, just a doodle of a rusted bicycle basket underneath the prompt: *Name three times you chose vulnerability over being right, and how each one taught you to show up for the person you want to be*.

Blinking hard, you flip the page, and the questions warp in your peripheral vision, shifting from the organizational behavior frameworks and client negotiation tactics you crammed for last night to the tiny, messy moments you’d tried to stuff into the back of your mental closet: one asks you to calculate how many minutes you spent hiding under your bed during your parents’ divorce instead of studying for your first driver’s test, another wants you to list every houseplant you ever tried to keep alive and killed, scrawling in the margin that this is the true test of your long-term care for things that matter.

The ballpoint pen in your hand starts to glow faintly, like the fireflies you trapped in mason jars as a kid, and you glance up to see the proctor—who you’d sworn was your stern college advisor just a second ago—now bent over your desk, her face softening into your grandma’s, who passed away when you were 16, holding a crinkled bag of the lemon drops you used to sneak from her pantry cabinet. A faint voice, not your own, hums from the back of the exam hall: the career you’re meant for isn’t the one you studied for—it’s the one you kept stitching together between the moments you thought no one was watching.

Suddenly, the exam papers dissolve into dandelion fluff, swirling up into the fluorescent lights until the hall is filled with floating white seeds. You’re standing in the gnarled cherry tree from age 7, the faint sting of that old tree-branch scar on your palm, and below you, your 30-year-old self leans against a rusted bicycle basket, holding a handwritten sign that says *We got the job*—the one you never even sent a resume for, the one where you teach kids to draw their own palm scars and tend to wobbly window box herbs. You reach out, and a dandelion seed sticks to your finger, and you know you don’t need to fill out a single answer. The real question was never printed on the page at all.
