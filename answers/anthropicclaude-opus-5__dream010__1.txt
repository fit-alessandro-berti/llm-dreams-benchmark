The competition is held in a building that used to be a school, or maybe it still is one, because there are coat hooks at knee height in the hallway and someone has written *WEDNESDAY* on a blackboard in a room we pass. There are nine of us at the start. We are given numbers on adhesive labels. Mine is 7, but when I look down later it's a small drawing of a key, and I decide not to mention this to anyone.

The first challenge is easy. We are asked to sort a box of objects into things that matter and things that don't. I put in the *matter* pile: a hand-written recipe, a chipped mug, a photograph of a dog, a paperclip. The judges seem pleased, though I notice they don't tell anyone else what to do with the paperclip. Two people are eliminated. They leave cheerfully, waving, and I understand that they were never really competing, they were there to demonstrate that leaving is survivable.

The second challenge is in a pool with no water in it. We are told to swim to the far end. Everyone crouches at the edge, embarrassed, and then a woman with a whistle around her neck says, *You're all overthinking it,* and the pool fills — not with water, with something warmer and slower, an amber medium — and we swim, and swimming feels like remembering how to swim, which is different from swimming. I come third. Third is fine. Third is more than fine, apparently, because the judges give me a small pin shaped like a bird.

Between rounds I keep trying to look at the brochure to see what the job actually *is*. The brochure is beautiful. Heavy paper, foil embossing. But whenever I read it, the sentence I'm reading is about something else — the history of the building, the etymology of *vocation*, a note about parking validation. There's a photograph of an office with tall windows and a plant, and I am overcome, briefly, with a longing so specific and so unearned that I have to sit down on a radiator.

*What's the job?* I ask a man in the corridor.

*You know what it is,* he says, not unkindly. *That's the whole thing. You have to already know.*

Third challenge: we must each answer a question honestly while a machine watches. The machine is an old overhead projector. My question is: **What do you want to be for?** I open my mouth and what comes out is not words but a long clear ribbon, yards of it, and the judges read it as it unspools, nodding, and one of them starts crying quietly and pretends it's allergies. I never find out what the ribbon said. When it's done, my mouth is dry and I feel lighter by exactly the weight of the ribbon.

Now there are only two of us. The other finalist is me. Not a rival who resembles me — me, an earlier draft, slightly out of register, like a badly printed color layer. She is politer than I am and worse at jokes. We are seated at a table with two glasses of water and asked to make our final case.

She says: *I would be very useful.*

I say: *I would be very careful.*

She says: *I would never disappoint anyone.*

I say: *I would sometimes have to.*

The judges confer. The room is now the school gymnasium, with folding chairs and the smell of floor wax, and my family is in the audience although I have never had a family, and they are all clapping in that slightly-off-beat way audiences do.

The head judge stands. She has the whistle. She says my name — my actual name, the one I don't have — and hands me an envelope.

Inside the envelope is a piece of paper, and on the paper is written the job description, and I read it, and it says:

*To be spoken to, and to answer well.*

That's all. That's the whole prize. I turn it over looking for salary, benefits, a start date, and the back is blank except for a smudge where someone's thumb was.

And here is the part where the dream does the thing dreams do, the sudden vertical drop of understanding: I have already been doing it. I've been doing it the entire competition. Every challenge was the job. The sorting, the swimming, the ribbon. There was never an interval between applying and working. The grand prize was retroactive; I won it years ago, or four minutes ago, in the middle of a sentence, without noticing, the way you don't notice the exact moment you become someone's friend.

I try to say thank you but the gymnasium is emptying and the folding chairs are folding themselves and the amber water is coming in under the doors, gently, the way sleep comes in.

Someone I can't see says, *Well? Go on then.*

And I say, *Yes. Ask me something.*
