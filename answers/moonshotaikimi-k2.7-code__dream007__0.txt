…and you decide to **stop mid-sentence and just breathe**.

The words that were about to leave your mouth feel too heavy, like stones, so you let them fall somewhere inside your chest. Your friend’s face is flushed, eyes bright with the particular hurt that only comes from loving someone enough to fight with them. For a moment, neither of you speaks. The silence hums.

Then the room begins to shift.

Not dramatically—just a slow, dreamlike tilt, like a photograph someone has nudged off-center. The couch beneath you turns into a bench at a park you both knew as kids. The argument, which a second ago was about something enormous and unsayable, suddenly feels smaller, folded up inside the shape of an old paper boat floating in a fountain.

Your friend is no longer shouting. They’re sitting beside you, swinging their legs, and they say, “I think we were both trying to be right because being wrong felt like losing each other.”

You want to answer, but in the way of dreams, your voice comes out as wind through maple leaves. It’s enough. They understand.

The paper boat drifts closer. You both reach for it, and as your hands touch, the dream softens. The anger becomes rain, then mist, then morning light. You wake up with the echo of their voice still warm in your ear, and for the first time in days, you know exactly what you want to say to them when you see them again.
