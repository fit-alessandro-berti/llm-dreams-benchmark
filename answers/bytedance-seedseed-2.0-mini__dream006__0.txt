For a split second, a familiar dream panic pricks at your chest: you glance down, half-expecting to find you’ve forgotten to pull on pants, but instead spot your third-grade best friend in the front row, her hair streaked with the same electric blue she dyed after you two snuck into a beauty supply store at 10, waving so hard her pom-pom bracelet clinks against the podium edge.

When you adjust the mic, the wooden stand hums softly, like a contented cat, and you notice the crowd isn’t just the neighbors and city council members you prepared for—some of the listeners have soft, iridescent feathered collars fanning over their shoulders, their eyes glinting like polished river rocks. The formal remarks you typed up last night about urban green space grants vanish from your mind entirely, and instead, you start talking about the time you got lost in this exact park 16 years ago, how a stranger with a cardboard sign and a bag of peppermints walked you to the nearest bus stop when you were too shy to ask for directions.

A small kid in the front row raises a crumpled crayon drawing of a peppermint sprouting wings, and you laugh until your sides ache, the sound mixing with the soft hum of the crowd leaning in. Mid-sentence, the black carpet under your shoes softens into a field of clover, each leaf glowing with tiny, pulsing green light, though you don’t remember stepping outside onto grass. You don’t notice how much time has passed until the blue-haired friend taps your arm, handing you a mason jar of frosty lemonade scented with mint, and you realize half the crowd has softened into the faces of people who’ve lifted you up over the years: your high school art teacher, the mailman who left you extra Christmas cards, the barista who remembers your order even on your worst days.

You wrap up without glancing at your notes, saying that the big speeches don’t matter half as much as the small, quiet kindnesses people pass out like peppermints and lemonade, and the crowd erupts in cheers that sound like wind chimes tangled in tree branches. As you step off the stage, the clover carpet vanishes back to plain black fabric, and you wake up right as the kid with the crayon drawing skips up to press the smudged paper into your palm, its edges still sticky with the glue he used to mount the glitter accents.
