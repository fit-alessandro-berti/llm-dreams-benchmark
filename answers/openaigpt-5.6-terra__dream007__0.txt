You turn toward the door, but the room has changed. It is no longer your friend’s apartment. The walls stretch upward into darkness, papered with old messages the two of you have sent each other—every joke, apology, and late-night confession fluttering softly like leaves.

Your friend calls your name, but their voice comes from far away.

You reach for the doorknob. It is warm, almost beating beneath your hand.

When you open the door, you step into a train station flooded with moonlight. Every departure board displays the same destination:

**HOME**

A train waits with its doors open. Inside, every seat is occupied by a different version of you: younger, older, crying, laughing, asleep. One of them looks up and says, “You don’t have to win.”

Behind you, your friend is standing on the platform now. They are holding the small, important thing you had been arguing about, though in the dream it has become a glass bird with a crack running through its chest.

You want to speak, but the station clock begins ticking backward.

Your friend lifts the bird between you and whispers, “I didn’t want you to leave.”

The train doors start to close. You wake just before deciding whether to step inside or turn around.
