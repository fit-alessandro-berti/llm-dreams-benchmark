The wind screams like a banshee through the rigging, and the deck heaves beneath my boots like a living beast trying to shake me free. Rain lashes horizontal, stinging my face as I grip the wheel with white-knuckled hands. The mast groans — *that sound* — the deep, wooden groan that every captain knows means the ship is fighting itself.

"Captain!" My first mate, whose name I suddenly can't remember — Marcus? Martin? — stumbles toward me, half-swallowed by spray. "The sails are tearing! We need to—"

"reef them *now*!" I roar, though my voice is already raw. "Every hand on deck!"

He hesitates, rain streaming down his face, and in his eyes I see what I already know: he's wondering if this is it. If this is the storm that takes us.

I open my mouth to give an order — something heroic, something noble — but the wheel **kicks** hard to port, and the whole sea seems to rise up on our starboard side. The ship tilts. Tilts more. *Too much.*

I hear the cargo break free below. I hear someone scream. I hear the ocean laughing.

And the thing is — the thing nobody tells you about being captain — is that I have no idea what to do. I'm just a man gripping a wheel, pretending the fear in my chest isn't about to swallow me whole.

What do I do, sailor?
