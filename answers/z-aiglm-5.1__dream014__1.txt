The strangers smile with faces that shimmer slightly, the way people do in dreams — familiar yet unplaceable. They gesture for you to follow, and their footsteps make no sound on the wet pavement. The neon signs overhead blur into streaks of color, painting the sidewalks in ribbons of blue and gold.

"This way," one of them says, though their mouth doesn't move. The voice comes from everywhere and nowhere, gentle as wind chimes.

You walk together through alleys that twist impossibly, streets that curve upward like roller coasters, past storefronts selling things you can't quite name — bottles of moonlight, jars of forgotten melodies, maps drawn in disappearing ink.

And then the crowd parts, and you see it: a small door set into a wall covered in ivy. You somehow *know* — the way you only know in dreams — that what's inside is exactly where you're meant to be.

You reach for the handle. It's warm.

"You were never really lost," the strangers whisper behind you. "You just forgot how to look."

The door opens to soft, golden light, and you step through, feeling the strange, wonderful certainty that you are, at last, home.
