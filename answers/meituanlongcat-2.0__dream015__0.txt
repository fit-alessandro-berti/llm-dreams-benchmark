Despite the hardships, you decide to cultivate a single, defiant patch of green in the hollowed ribcage of a collapsed skyscraper.

You wake on the cold, shattered marble of what was once a lobby, the taste of copper and dust coating your tongue. The world outside is a sprawl of rusted metal and biting wind, a graveyard of a time before. Resources are a ghost story whispered among survivors. The rain, when it comes, is acidic, eating through tire-tread soles and leather jackets in a season. 

But today, it is quiet.

You climb the skeletal elevator shafts, hand over hand on rusted rebar, ascending forty stories. You reach the penthouse level, where the roof has long since caved in, leaving a jagged opening to the bruised sky. There, in the shadow of a fallen Picasso hung by a single wire, is your sanctuary. 

It’s a makeshift greenhouse built from shattered windows and salvaged plastic sheeting. It shouldn't work. The soil is a hand-mixed slurry of crushed drywall and the irradiated dirt you’ve scraped from the cracks in the pavement. The water is condensation you trap from the pipes, collected in a rusted coffee tin. But it is alive.

You dig your fingers into the warm, dark earth. You plant the seeds—a dented tin of heirloom lettuce and tomato seeds you found in an abandoned library next to a book on hydroponics. You water them with the last of your clean water, a drink you should have taken for yourself. 

A sound echoes from the stairwell below. The metallic clatter of a makeshift weapon, the guttural shout of a raider scavenging the upper floors. Your heart hammers against your ribs. Dangers are everywhere, and you are alone, high above the world with a blaster that has two shots and a heart full of hope.

You press your back against the crumbling wall, raising the heavy steel pipe you carry for a weapon. The footsteps grow louder, vibrating through the floor. A shadow falls across the greenhouse plastic. The intruder pushes through the door, eyes wild, skin scarred by the sun. He sees you, a tangle of desperation, and raises a rusted machete.

Then his eyes drop. He sees the tiny, fragile seedlings erupting from the dirt—vibrant, impossible green against the grey death of the city. 

He freezes. The machete wavers. In this world, water is worth a bullet, and bullets are worth a life. He is calculating the cost of killing you versus the value of the dirt, the seeds, the sheer, stupid miracle of life you’ve created. 

You don't attack. Instead, you slowly kneel. You cup your hands and offer him the last half-canteen of clean water. 

"Drink," you say, your voice raspy from disuse. "And I'll show you how to make it grow."

The machete clatters to the floor. He takes the water. He drinks. He stays. It isn't an alliance built on strength, but on the shared, stubborn refusal to let the world die. 

You are still dreaming. But for the first time, the nightmare is blooming.
