…speak, but no words come out—only a soft cloud of silver moths, fluttering from your mouth and rising into the bright stage lights.

The audience doesn’t seem surprised. In fact, they lean forward as if this is exactly what they came to hear.

You look down at your notes, but the paper has turned into a map of a city you somehow recognize from childhood, though you’ve never been there before. The streets shift gently in your hands like sleeping snakes. Somewhere in the back row, a clock begins ticking louder and louder, until it sounds like footsteps.

Then, without deciding to, you step off the stage and into the map.

Suddenly you are walking through narrow streets made of red velvet curtains. Doorways open as you pass, and in each one sits a different version of yourself: one laughing, one crying, one very old, one still a child, all of them waiting for you to continue the speech. Above you, the sky is the ceiling of the auditorium, and the stars are the audience’s watchful eyes.

You clear your throat and try again.

This time, your voice comes out as music.

Every sentence becomes a different instrument: apologies on a violin, forgotten memories on a piano, hope in a brass fanfare so bright it makes the buildings tremble. The people in the city stop what they’re doing and listen. The different versions of you step from their doorways and begin following behind, not threateningly, but as if they’ve been waiting a very long time to hear what you have to say.

At the center of the city stands a podium made of tree roots and moonlight.

You climb to it.

The crowd—the real crowd, the dream crowd, the many yous, the strangers, the moths now circling like living confetti—falls perfectly silent.

And then you finally say the one sentence you were apparently meant to say all along:

“I’m here.”

The room exhales.

The audience rises to its feet, but instead of applause, thousands of paper birds lift from their hands and fill the air, carrying the ceiling away with them. Beyond it is dawn, enormous and golden, spilling into everything.

And just as the first warm light reaches your face, you wake up with the feeling that you were deeply understood by someone—though you can’t remember who.
