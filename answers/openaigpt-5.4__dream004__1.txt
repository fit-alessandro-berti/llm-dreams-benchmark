…as you come closer, the canvas is no longer blank.

At first it only seems to be breathing.

Its surface rises and falls like a sleeping chest, white becoming pearl, then silver, then a color you almost recognize from childhood but cannot name. Around it, the world keeps slipping between forms—a staircase turns into rain, the rain hardens into glass birds, the birds unfold into blue triangles that drift upward like smoke.

You lift a hand toward the canvas.

Before your fingers touch it, a small circle appears in the center. Then a line. Then a doorway painted in strokes so wet they glisten. The doorway opens inward, though it is flat, and a wind pours out carrying the smell of oranges, thunder, old books, and the moment just before someone says your name.

Something in you understands: the canvas is waiting for you to remember what to make.

You look down and find that in your hand is not a brush, but a handful of light. It drips through your fingers in ribbons—gold, violet, green, and a deep red that sounds like distant bells. When you touch the canvas, the colors do not stay where you place them. They bloom outward, turning into hills with checkered shadows, rivers shaped like question marks, trees with square leaves and circular trunks. A sun appears in the corner, then decides it would rather be a fish and swims slowly across the sky.

The more you paint, the more the world around you responds.

Mountains lean in to watch.
A cube rolls past and laughs.
A flock of eyes flutters from one horizon to another.

Then, in the lower corner of the canvas, you paint a small house without meaning to. It has one round window and a crooked door. The instant you finish it, all the shifting colors in the landscape pause. Every shape holds still, as if listening.

The door of the painted house creaks open.

From inside steps a figure made of unfinished sketch lines, soft and flickering at the edges. You cannot tell if it is a stranger or someone you have always known. Its face changes each time you try to focus—friend, sibling, teacher, child, your own reflection older and kinder than you expected.

It points not at the painting, but at your chest.

There, beneath your ribs, a tiny frame has appeared, and inside it is another blank canvas.

You wake with paint on your fingertips that disappears before you can be sure it was ever there, and with the strange, luminous certainty that somewhere, just beyond waking, the house is still waiting for you to come back and finish the sky.
