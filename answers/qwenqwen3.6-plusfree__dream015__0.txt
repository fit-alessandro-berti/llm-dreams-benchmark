Despite the hardships, you decide to plant a pocket watch in the cracked concrete of what might have once been a park. 

As your fingers press it into the dust, the gears don't tick. They sigh. The air thickens, slow and heavy like warm honey. From the buried face, vines of spun copper and fractured glass spiral upward, blooming not with flowers, but with moments: a streetlamp flickering on at dusk, a bicycle leaning against a sunlit wall, the exact cadence of someone humming your name. 

The ash-storms part like heavy curtains. Creatures with too many joints and hollow eyes linger at the tree line, but as you walk past them, they settle into statues of rust and bone. In this dream, attention is gravity, and you've finally chosen to look toward something that isn't just survival.

You keep moving. The ruins soften at the edges. The few precious things left in your satchel don't multiply into rations or ammunition—they become seeds, brass keys, open doors. Each step leaves a faint impression of grass, then moss, then damp soil. You notice the sky is no longer the color of bruised iron, but the pale gold of an hour before dawn that refuses to end.

Somewhere deep in the dream-logic, you understand: scarcity was a rule of the waking world. Danger, a language spoken by fear. Here, you are not scavenging to stay alive. You are remembering to stay real.

And so you kneel again, dig another watch, another photograph, another splinter of a music box. You plant them all. The earth answers in green. The dream doesn't end. It simply wakes you up, gently, carrying the scent of petrichor on your skin.
