...plant a garden.

It seems absurd at first—digging through cracked concrete behind the husk of an old library, scraping together soil from beneath rusted cars. But your hands move with a strange certainty, the way hands do in dreams, and the seeds you find in your pocket (you don't remember putting them there) seem to glow faintly, like embers.

The first sprout appears overnight, impossibly fast. A tomato plant, then sunflowers taller than the broken streetlights, their faces turning to follow a sun that hangs strangely close in the ash-grey sky.

Others begin to arrive. A woman with a dented kettle who knows how to make tea from dandelions. A boy who hasn't spoken since "the event" but who hums while he waters the beans. An old man who claims he was once a botanist, or maybe a baker—his story shifts each time he tells it, the way stories do in dreams.

The dangers don't disappear. At night, you hear things moving in the ruins beyond the garden wall—a wall that you don't remember building, but which is somehow always there when you need it. Scavengers come, hungry and hollow-eyed, and instead of fighting, you find yourself handing them squash and saying, "There's more if you help dig."

Strangely, they always do.

The garden spreads in ways gardens shouldn't. Vines crawl up the skeletons of skyscrapers and bloom. You find apples growing from what used to be a parking meter. Someone swears the river is running clear again, and when you go to check, you see your reflection—older than you remember, or maybe younger, and smiling.

On the last night before you wake, the whole community gathers around a fire in the garden's center. The mute boy finally speaks. He says only one word, pointing at the green sprawl that has swallowed the dead city:

*"See?"*

And you do see. That's the secret the dream has been whispering all along—the apocalypse was never the end of the story. It was the part where the soil got turned over.

You wake with dirt under your fingernails. Probably just from yesterday. Probably.
