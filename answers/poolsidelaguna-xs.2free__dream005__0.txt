
You're weaving through the crowd, your heartbeat syncing with the thumping music. The first group is a circle of people dressed as vegetables — a giant radish offers you a lift, but his stem is too long to fit through the doorway. You duck just in time as he rolls away, laughing with a voice like maracas.

The next cluster is playing an invisible piano. Their fingers dance through the air, and you can almost hear the melody, sweet and aching, like a song your grandmother hummed. When you step closer, the pianist — a woman with eyes like folded paper — whispers, *“They’re not really here. None of us are, not really.”* She points behind you, where figures seem to shimmer and fade.

You turn to see your friend, but it’s not quite them. It’s a version made of light and laughter, waving but already beginning to dissolve. “Found me!” they call, but their voice splits into a chorus of echoes. Then they’re gone, leaving behind a single silver key that hums against your palm.

The room shifts. Walls become windows, and outside, instead of the street, you see a library with books that flutter like birds. You follow the key’s soft glow through doorways that lead nowhere and staircases that spiral into sky. Each step deeper into the dream brings you closer to waking — or maybe deeper into the dream. The choice isn’t yours anymore.

Would you like to wake up, or keep dreaming?
