and you look down and your hands aren't quite your hands. The keys on your keyboard are soft, like little loaves of bread, and every time you press one it sighs.

Your boss is standing right behind you. Only, it’s not quite your boss. He’s wearing your boss’s shirt, but his face keeps flickering — boss, then your 4th grade math teacher, then your mom. He taps his wrist. He doesn’t have a watch. He has a little analog clock growing out of his wrist, and the hands are spinning wildly.

“The client needs it by yesterday,” he says, perfectly reasonably. He drops a folder on your desk. It’s incredibly heavy. You open it and inside is a single sheet of paper that says:

**PROJECT: Build an entire city. Out of clouds. Due: in 5 minutes.**

You want to say that’s impossible, but when you open your mouth, no sound comes out. The office air is suddenly too thick, like water.

You try to stand up to explain, and you realize you’re not wearing shoes. In fact, the carpet under your desk isn’t carpet anymore — it’s wet, cool grass. It’s growing fast. The cubicles around you are stretching upward into enormous redwood trees. The fluorescent lights overhead have become a pale, buzzing sun.

Everyone else in the office is typing calmly, as if this is normal. You look closer — they’re all typing on the same bread-keyboards, but their fingers are passing right through the keys.

The clock on the wall — the big one — is melting. The numbers are dripping down like paint. 3, 2, 1... 6...

Panic spikes. The deadline. Five minutes. You have to start.

So you do. You reach into the folder and grab a handful of cloud. It’s cold and electric and it laughs in your hands. You start stacking it. It doesn’t fall. It floats.

Your boss — your mom — your teacher — leans over and whispers, “Oh, finally. You remembered.”

And in that second, you remember. 

This is a dream.

The second you remember, the rule breaks. The heaviness lifts. You don’t have to build the city *in* the office. You *are* the city. You look out the window that wasn’t there a second ago, and outside there is no parking lot — there is just open sky.

You push your chair back, and it lifts off the grass. You’re floating too, now, holding a fistful of cloud-city, and the impossible deadline doesn’t matter because you have all the time in the world, because time here drips like paint and spins like a wristwatch.

You laugh, and your boss laughs back with your mom’s laugh, and you fly right out the window holding your project — which is now just a perfect, small, glowing ball of light, completely finished.
