…stop talking.

Not in surrender—just in exhaustion. You turn away, step out into the hallway, and suddenly the hallway is impossibly long, lined with doors that pulse like they’re breathing. Behind each one, you hear fragments of your own voice repeating the same argument, louder, sharper, meaner.

Your friend appears at the far end, not angry now—just sad—and says, “Pick one.”

You open the nearest door. Inside is the two of you as kids, laughing over something small and stupid. Another door: the two of you years later, helping each other through heartbreak. Another: tonight, but with both of you listening instead of interrupting.

When you turn back, your friend is beside you, holding out a folded note. It reads: *Do you want to be right, or do you want to understand me?*

The hallway starts to flood with warm light. The doors dissolve. You finally say the thing you couldn’t say in the argument: “I’m scared of losing you.”

Your friend exhales, shoulders dropping. “Me too.”

Then the floor becomes a quiet lake, and you’re both standing on the surface, arguing no more—just looking at your reflections ripple together as morning begins to leak into the sky.
