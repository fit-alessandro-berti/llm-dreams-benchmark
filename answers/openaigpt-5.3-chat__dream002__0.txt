You feel a storm approaching and the forest seems to notice before you do. The wind slips through the trees in long, uneasy breaths, turning the leaves so their pale undersides shimmer like warning flags.

The path you were following fades into roots and moss.

A low rumble moves through the ground rather than the sky. The air smells like rain and cold stone. Somewhere deeper in the forest, a branch cracks—not falling, just bending, as if something large brushed past it.

The light changes.

It isn’t darker exactly. Instead the colors grow strangely vivid: the green of the moss, the black of the wet bark, the silver shine of the first raindrops. They fall slowly at first, heavy and spaced apart, tapping the leaves like quiet footsteps.

You realize you are no longer alone.

Ahead, between two ancient trees, stands a small wooden door set into the trunk of a massive oak. You are certain it wasn’t there a moment ago. Warm golden light spills from the edges of its frame, flickering gently against the wet forest floor.

Thunder rolls again, closer now.

The storm gathers above the canopy, but around the door the air feels calm, expectant—like the moment before someone speaks.

The handle turns slightly on its own.

And from inside, you hear someone softly say your name.
