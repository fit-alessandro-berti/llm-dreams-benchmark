No one in the audience seems surprised. They lean forward as if this is exactly what they came to see.

You look down at your note cards, but the words are gone. In their place is a map of a city you somehow recognize—a city made of memories: your childhood bedroom glowing in one corner, a rainy bus stop in another, a hallway from a school you haven’t seen in years stretching off the edge of the page.

So you begin to speak without the cards.

Your voice comes out as music first, soft and echoing, and every sentence you say turns into something visible in the air: a silver staircase, a drifting umbrella, a clock with no hands, a door standing by itself in a field of stars. The audience nods along, understanding everything, even the parts you don’t understand yourself.

As you talk, the stage grows wider beneath your feet until it becomes a shoreline. The front row is now seated in little wooden boats, gently rising and falling. The spotlight above you becomes a moon. Somewhere far back in the darkness, someone laughs warmly, like they’ve been waiting a long time for you to remember this speech.

Then you realize the speech is not about success, or fear, or any ordinary thing.

It is about how every person in the crowd is also you at a different age, listening to hear what happens next.

The oldest version of you sits in the back, smiling.
The youngest is in the front row, swinging their legs, eyes bright.

You keep speaking, and with every word the room grows lighter, until the ceiling disappears entirely and the audience, the stage, the boats, the moon all begin to float upward together.

And just before you wake, you hear yourself say, with perfect calm:

“I was never unprepared. I was only arriving.”
