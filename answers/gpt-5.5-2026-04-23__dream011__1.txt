…written in your own handwriting, but you don’t remember writing them.

The first question reads:

**1. What did you want to become before everyone told you what was practical?**

You look around. The lecture hall stretches impossibly far, rows of desks fading into mist. Everyone else is writing quickly, their pencils scratching like rain, but their faces are blurred, as if the dream hasn’t finished drawing them.

You turn back to the paper.

**2. Name one door you walked past because you were afraid it would open.**

Your pencil feels heavy. The clock above the chalkboard has no hands, only a slow blinking eye. At the front of the room, the professor is there and not there—sometimes your old teacher, sometimes your parent, sometimes a stranger in a suit.

You flip the page, hoping for equations, definitions, something you can memorize.

Instead, the final question covers the entire sheet:

**3. If you fail this exam, who told you your life was over?**

The room goes silent.

You realize no one is watching you. No one is grading. The other students are only shadows, bent over blank pages. The professor smiles gently and points to the door behind the chalkboard, a door you’re certain wasn’t there before.

You stand up, leaving the exam unfinished.

As you open the door, warm light spills in, and you hear the sound of waves, traffic, laughter, music—every possible future happening at once.

Then you wake just before stepping through, with the strange certainty that the exam was never about passing.

It was about choosing.
