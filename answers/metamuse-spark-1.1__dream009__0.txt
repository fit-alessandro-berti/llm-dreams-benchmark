You wake up in a completely different era, with no modern technology around. As you explore your new surroundings, you realize you need to **remember your name before it finishes being woven.**

---

You wake up on a floor, not a bed. Packed earth, cold under your back, covered in a thin layer of clean straw. The air smells like woodsmoke and sea salt and wet wool.

Above you is not a ceiling, but a high, dark timber roof, held up by massive blackened beams. Light comes in slanting from a single high window - no glass, just oiled parchment, glowing amber.

Someone is singing outside. A woman's voice, low and repetitive, like a work song.

You sit up. You are wearing rough linen. Your hands are dirty. Your mouth is dry. Your phone isn't in your pocket, of course. There is no pocket.

You stand, and your body feels wrong - lighter, like you've been sick for a long time, or like someone turned the gravity down slightly.

You push through a heavy wool curtain and step outside.

It's a village. But not a movie-set village. It's too muddy and too real and too functional. Low wattle-and-daub houses with thick thatched roofs. A muddy lane full of chickens and children with no shoes, even though it's cold. Far off, past the houses, you can see the ocean, grey and endless. No power lines. No contrails. No sound of a car, a plane, a hum. The quiet is so deep it hurts your ears.

No one looks at you strangely at first. And then you realize you understand them. You're hearing words that should be foreign - Old English, Norse, something like that - but your dream-mind translates it cleanly into meaning.

*"...the tide's turning..."*
*"...she'll want the blue thread..."*

And that's when you walk a little further to the center of the village green, where the big hall is. Inside, a loom, enormous, takes up the whole center of the room. Three old women are working it. Not weaving cloth.

They are weaving a picture.

And as you get closer, your stomach drops like an elevator, because you recognize the picture.

It's you. From this morning. In your actual bed, in your actual apartment. Curled around a pillow, mouth open, with the blue light of your laptop still glowing on your desk.

The weaving is half finished. Your sleeping face is there. Your room is there. But below your body, the threads are loose, tangled, unfinished. Gray wool hanging like fog.

One of the women looks up at you and she is not surprised to see you. She smiles with no teeth and holds out a shuttle made of bone.

And you realize with absolute, sudden dream-certainty - the kind of logic that only makes sense while dreaming - that you need to weave the rest.

You need to weave yourself awake. Every detail. If you get it wrong, if you forget how your blanket looked or what your street sounded like, you won't have anywhere to wake up to.

Your hands remember before you do. You sit at the loom. The wool is warm.

You start with the blue thread.
