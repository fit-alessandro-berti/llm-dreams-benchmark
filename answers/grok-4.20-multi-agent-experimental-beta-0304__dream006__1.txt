and you begin to speak, but the words aren't yours—they're coming from the mouths of every person you've ever disappointed, echoing back at you in perfect unison. The microphone melts into taffy between your fingers. 

Suddenly the stage floor turns to warm sand and you're barefoot, toes curling as the audience starts sprouting feathers and lifting off their seats in slow motion, becoming a murmuration of glowing owls that circle the auditorium once before settling back down as your old high school teachers. One of them (Mrs. Alvarez, the one who failed you in algebra) raises a single wing and whispers, "We were always the dream, dear. You were the speech."

The lights flicker like fireflies. Your notes fly out of your hands and reassemble themselves into a single white dove that lands on your shoulder and says, in your own childhood voice, "Just improvise... like the time you told Mom the dog ate your report card."

You open your mouth again.

This time, music comes out instead of words—your favorite song from 1997, but every lyric is replaced with the sound of rain on a tin roof. The audience sways. They know every note. They're crying in that way people only cry in dreams: beautifully, without shame, like it's the most natural thing in the world.

And for one perfect, impossible moment, you aren't afraid of forgetting anything.

You wake up smiling, with the faint taste of taffy still on your tongue.
