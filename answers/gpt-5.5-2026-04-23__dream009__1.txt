You wake up in a completely different era, with no modern technology around. As you explore your new surroundings, you realize you need to find out not just *where* you are, but *when* you are.

The air smells of woodsmoke, rain-soaked earth, and horses. Narrow streets twist between stone houses with sagging roofs, and lanterns glow faintly in the early morning fog. People pass you in wool cloaks and leather boots, speaking in a dialect you can almost understand, though certain words slip past like fish in a stream.

You check your pockets by instinct, searching for your phone, keys, anything familiar—but there is only a small brass coin, warm in your palm, stamped with the face of a king you’ve never seen.

A bell rings somewhere in the distance.

The sound pulls everyone toward the town square, and you follow. At the center stands a wooden notice board covered in parchment. One freshly nailed page bears a symbol that makes your breath catch: a spiral inside a circle—the same mark you noticed carved into the headboard of the bed where you woke.

Below it, written in careful ink, are the words:

**The stranger arrives before the eclipse. Bring them to the clockmaker.**

Your heart begins to pound.

Before you can step back, an old woman beside you grips your sleeve. Her eyes are sharp, too sharp, as if she has been waiting for this moment her entire life.

“You’re late,” she whispers.

“I don’t know what’s happening,” you say.

“No,” she replies, glancing up at the darkening sky, where the sun is already being swallowed by a shadow. “But you will.”

She presses something into your hand: a tiny hourglass, its silver sand falling upward.

Then the bell rings again—not from the tower, but from somewhere beneath the ground.

And suddenly you understand.

You didn’t fall into the past by accident.

You were sent here to stop something from being invented too soon.
