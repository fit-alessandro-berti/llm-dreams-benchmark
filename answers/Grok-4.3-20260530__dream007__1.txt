You decide to stop shouting and instead reach out—not with words, but with a strange, sudden impulse. You grab your friend’s hand and pull them through the nearest door, which wasn’t there a second ago. It opens onto a vast, starlit meadow that smells like childhood summers and burnt sugar. The argument—whatever it was about—still hangs in the air like smoke, but the ground beneath your feet is now soft moss that pulses gently with each heartbeat.

Your friend looks at you, eyes wide, half-angry, half-laughing. “This isn’t fair,” they say. “You always do this in dreams—turn everything into magic instead of fixing it.”

You smile, because you know they’re right. The disagreement was important: something about trust, about whether you both still believed in the same future you once sketched on napkins at 3 a.m. But here, in the dream, the stars rearrange themselves into the exact map you both drew years ago. Constellations spell out the promises you made and the ones you quietly broke.

You decide to walk the glowing path together. With every step, fragments of the fight dissolve into the grass—shouted accusations become fireflies, old hurts turn into soft lanterns that bob along beside you. Your friend’s grip on your hand tightens, not in anger now, but in recognition. “I was scared too,” they whisper, and the meadow answers with a low, warm hum that feels like forgiveness.

At the center of the field stands a single tree whose branches are made of every conversation you’ve ever had. You both climb it without speaking. Higher and higher, until the argument is just a faint echo far below. From the top you can see the whole dream-world: your waking lives, the roads you’ve taken apart, the ones you might still walk together.

You decide, finally, to let go of who was “right.” Instead you press your forehead to your friend’s and say the only thing that matters in a dream like this: “I’m still here. Let’s figure the rest out when we wake up.”

Your friend nods. The tree blossoms with light. The meadow folds in on itself like a closing book.

And just before the dream ends, you both laugh—the same laugh you used to share when the world was smaller and everything still felt possible.

You wake up with your phone in your hand, the argument’s last text still glowing on the screen. But the anger has cooled into something softer. You type three words you didn’t know you needed to say:

“I miss us. Coffee?”

The dream lingers in your chest like a secret map. You smile, already knowing they’ll reply yes.
