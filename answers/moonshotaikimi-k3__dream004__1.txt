...and as you approach, you realize the canvas isn't blank at all — it's just waiting. The colors of the shifting landscape begin to drift toward it like leaves toward water, peeling themselves off the trees and the sky, swirling into the fabric.

You reach for a brush, but there is none. Your fingers remember how to paint on their own. You touch the canvas and color pools beneath your fingertips — a blue that sounds like distant singing, a red that tastes of cinnamon and childhood summers.

You paint a door. Of course you do. The moment the outline is complete, it swings open, and through it you see a room you almost recognize — the kitchen from a house you lived in as a child, except the windows look out onto an ocean of stars.

You step through. The canvas closes behind you and becomes a wall, then the memory of a wall, then nothing.

In the kitchen, someone is humming. You can't see them, but you know the tune — it's a song your grandmother used to sing, though you can no longer remember her face. The kettle is boiling, but instead of steam it releases small silver fish that swim through the air and out the starry windows.

You sit at the table. On it rests a cup of tea that is exactly the temperature of forgiveness. You drink it, and for a moment you understand something enormous and important — but the understanding is made of water, and it slips through your fingers when you try to hold it.

Then the kitchen begins to fold. The walls bend inward like the pages of a book being gently closed. You are not afraid. You think: *this is how all rooms end.*

When the folding stops, you are standing before the canvas again. It is blank — or perhaps it was always blank, and the painting is still inside you, waiting for your hands to remember it.

Somewhere far away, an alarm clock is warming up its voice.

The colors slow. The shapes settle.

You wake with paint on your fingers that isn't there, and the strange, sweet certainty that somewhere, a door you made is still standing open.
