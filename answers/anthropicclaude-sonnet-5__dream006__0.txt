Strangely, the crowd nods along, murmuring in approval, as though they understand perfectly.

You glance down and realize your notes have turned into a small flock of paper birds, which lift off the podium and flutter out over the audience. People reach up to catch them, unfolding the paper to read messages that aren't quite the speech you prepared—more like fragments of memory: a faint image of a childhood home, a laugh you recognize but can't place, a color you loved as a kid.

The stage lights shift, pulsing gently in rhythm with your heartbeat, and you notice the audience's faces blur slightly, features softening like wet paint, until they resemble people you've known throughout your life—old friends, teachers, family, strangers who once smiled at you on the street. They watch you with warmth, waiting.

You feel a strange, calm confidence, the fixed idea that whatever you say, or don't say, is somehow exactly right. You take a breath, and the shimmering ribbons of your voice grow brighter, spelling shapes in the air—maybe words, maybe just feelings—and the audience begins to applaud, not for what you said, but for the fact that you stood there and let it unfold.

As the applause rises, the stage beneath your feet begins to soften, turning into something like sand or cloud, and you realize you're sinking gently, not falling, just sinking, as if the dream is folding you back into itself, ready to shift into another scene entirely.
