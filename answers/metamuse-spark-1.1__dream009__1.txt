You wake up in a completely different era, with no modern technology around. As you explore your new surroundings, you realize you need to **remember how to be human again without a machine telling you how.**

The bed beneath you isn't a bed. It's a stuffed pallet of straw and wool on a cold, uneven wooden floor. The air smells like woodsmoke and damp earth and baking bread — so real it shocks you. Somewhere, a rooster crows. No — a dozen roosters.

You push open a low, heavy wooden door that creaks on leather hinges. Outside is a village waking up. Thatched roofs heavy with morning dew. Muddy lanes. Women in long, undyed linen carrying buckets from a well. A boy herding goats stares at your strange clothes - your t-shirt and sweatpants suddenly feel like a costume from the future.

There's no hum. That's what hits you first. No fridge hum, no AC, no distant traffic, no phone buzzing in your pocket. You pat your pockets automatically. Empty. The silence is so complete you can hear your own blood.

And then the need hits you, urgent and dream-clear:

You need to get to the top of the hill before the bell stops ringing.

You don't know why. You don't know what hill. But you can hear it now — a deep, bronze bell from somewhere above the village, slow and patient. *Bong... Bong... Bong...*

So you run. Barefoot, because your socks are already soaked through with mud. Past the well where the women stop talking to watch you. Past a blacksmith whose hammer rings out of sync with the bell. Your lungs burn. The air is too clean, too cold.

The dream logic starts to bend. The hill gets steeper the closer you get. The lane turns into a staircase carved from white stone that wasn't there before. The bell gets louder, but also slower, like time itself is stretching.

At the top, there is no church. There is a huge old tree — an oak that must have been growing for a thousand years — and under it, an old woman sits at a low table, grinding ink. She doesn't look up.

On the table is a single piece of paper. And on the paper, in your own frantic, modern handwriting, are the words:

*Don't forget to look up.*

You look up.

The sky above the village isn't the sky you know. It's impossibly deep, violet-blue, and crowded with stars even though it's morning. You can see the whole Milky Way swirling lazily above the thatched roofs. You have never seen so many stars in your life. 

The bell stops. The old woman finally looks at you and smiles, like she's been waiting.

And you understand the other thing you needed — not to get back, not to find your phone, not to escape.

You needed to stop trying to get somewhere, and just stand there for a second and witness it.

Then your real alarm starts to ring, very faintly, from somewhere far beneath the straw and the mud and the stars, and the tree begins to dissolve into your bedroom ceiling.
