The storm doesn’t roar like any real storm, either. It hums with all the things you’ve put off: unwritten texts, missed anniversaries, the quiet fear that you’re steering everyone you love nowhere. The wheel under your palms isn’t even oak, you realize, it’s stacked with all the half-finished books and abandoned journals you left on your bedroom shelf, creaking like they might split apart under the wave rearing up 10 stories high, glowing silver at its frothing crest.

You don’t yell to tack, like a captain is supposed to. You reach into the oilskin coat slung over your shoulders, and your fist closes around something warm: a mason jar full of glowworms you caught in your backyard as a kid, the ones you grieved losing for months. You yank the cork out and hurl the whole jar into the oncoming wave, and the glowworms burst open like a million tiny stars, stitching through the black clouds and silver water until the storm unravels, soft as a knit blanket.

When the sea calms, a shore rises right ahead—not a sterile, mapped port, but a stretch of land stitched from all your favorite unvisited places: your grandma’s apple orchard bleeds into the summer camp lake you swam in as a teen, your best friend’s old pink bike leans against a oak tree right at the water’s edge. The crew whoops, piling off the ship to run to the people and things they’ve missed, and you stay at the wheel for a minute, watching them go. The sea laps gentle at the hull, and it murmurs just for you: I wasn’t trying to sink you. I was trying to show you where you’d forgotten to dock.
