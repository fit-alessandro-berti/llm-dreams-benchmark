The light in the sky doesn’t dim the way it should—it *spreads*, a sudden bruised glow blooming behind the clouds, as if the storm is being painted from the inside out.

At first it’s only distant thunder, far away and polite. Then the sound arrives all at once, not booming but *talking*—low syllables that vibrate in your ribs. The leaves around you begin to turn as though the air has become a spinning bowl, and every acorn and twig lifts and hangs for a heartbeat in midair before drifting down again in the wrong direction.

You take a step, and the ground feels slightly delayed, like it’s catching up with your foot. The path you were sure of is still there, but it’s rearranging itself around your ankles—roots sliding, stones shifting positions—until your footprints no longer match where you’ve been. The forest holds its breath.

Then lightning strikes.

Not the kind that cracks the sky—this one drops through the trees like a ribbon unspooling, and where it lands, the bark briefly becomes translucent. You can see *another forest* underneath, layered like pages: trunks and branches overlapping, seasons stacked on top of each other. In that brief transparency, you glimpse figures moving between the layers, not quite people, not quite shadows—shapes that look back with the curiosity of something waking.

The rain arrives sideways, striking your shoulders like thrown handfuls of cold beads. As it hits, the water doesn’t simply soak in. It beads up and *floats*, hovering in front of your face in shimmering clusters, forming words that drift and rearrange themselves.

You try to read them, but the letters slide away, slipping into patterns that resemble symbols you almost remember. When you blink, the symbols settle into a single sentence—clear as if someone has traced it onto the air.

**“Choose which way you want to be remembered.”**

Your mouth goes dry. You search for the source of the message—some magician of clouds, some voice lodged in the thunder—but there’s nothing. Only the storm, the forest, and the feeling that you’re being addressed not as a body, but as a story.

A gust surges through the trees and peels the canopy back, revealing a sky too large to belong above you. It’s filled with rolling fog-waves and pale lights, like lanterns floating under invisible water. Somewhere in that height, you realize you can hear your own breathing, but it’s not synchronized with yours. It’s breathing ahead—like it already knows the next part of the dream.

The storm intensifies, and now you can hear footsteps too, following at a distance. You turn and see a narrow corridor of clearing ahead where the rain doesn’t fall. The ground there is dry and oddly smooth, as if it’s been polished by time. Along its edges, the trees stand in perfect alignment, their branches forming a doorway-like frame.

Behind you, the forest presses closer. Between the trunks, shadows gather into silhouettes that begin to resemble you—different postures, different ages, different expressions. Some look afraid. Some look relieved. Some look as though they’ve already stepped through that doorway and survived.

The corridor of dry ground pulses once, inviting and impatient.

You realize you’re at the beginning of something, and whatever you do next will decide how the storm “writes” you.

So you step toward the doorway—

—and the rain follows, but it changes behavior. Instead of falling, it rises, spiraling around your arms and shoulders like a cloak being put on you from the inside. The words in the air return, but now they’re not asking. They’re waiting.

As your foot crosses the threshold, the sky above the corridor splits with a soft, impossible sound—like a page turning.

On the other side, you’re no longer in the forest.

You’re standing in a place made of twilight and paper-thin sound, where every remembered moment hovers in front of you as glowing fragments: the taste of a childhood apple, the exact shade of light in a kitchen you haven’t visited in years, a goodbye you never said out loud. They float in the air like fireflies caught in a jar, and you can feel them tugging at you, trying to align into a single sentence.

The storm’s thunder becomes a clock. The lightning becomes a pen.

And you understand: the storm didn’t approach because it was angry.

It approached because it was ready to *finish* the dream—ready to seal it into memory—ready to decide what kind of ending your mind will keep.

You open your mouth to answer the question the storm has been asking, and when your voice finally comes out, it doesn’t sound like you.

It sounds like every version of you that ever tried to be brave.

The corridor exhales, waiting for your choice.
