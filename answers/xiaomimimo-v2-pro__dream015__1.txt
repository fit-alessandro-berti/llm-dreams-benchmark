I find myself standing in a vast, ash-gray plain under a sky choked with perpetual twilight. The skeletons of cities jut from the horizon like broken teeth. The air tastes of dust and old metal. Resources are whispers—scraps of food, sips of contaminated water, fragments of shelter. Dangers lurk not just in the scavengers and feral machines that roam the ruins, but in the silence itself, in the slow poison of the land.

Despite the hardships, I decide to **head east, toward the rumored "Glass Gardens."**

I heard a dying traveler murmur about it before the light left their eyes—a place where the old world’s arrogance, in the form of a shattered bio-dome, had become its redemption. Supposedly, sealed vaults of soil and pure water remained, where things still *grew*. Most dismissed it as a myth, a fairy tale for the hopeless. But what else is there to cling to?

My journey is a silent ballet of survival. I move at dusk and dawn, using the skeletons of highways as cover. I filter rainwater through charred cloth, and share my meager finds with a one-eared cat that has been following me for three days, whom I’ve named "Rust."

We navigate a canyon of collapsed apartments, careful of the "Weepers"—zones where leaking chemical tanks cause hallucinations. In the hollowed-out shell of a library, I don’t find food, but I find something else: a single, water-stained botanical encyclopedia. I tear out the pages on root vegetables and hardy grains, tucking them into my jacket like a sacred text.

The final stretch is the hardest. A flat, open expanse of cracked earth, devoid of cover, guarded by a pack of rusted, canine-like drones. I can’t go through them. So, using the last of my clean water and a shard of mirror, I create a distant flash of light—a false signal—drawing them away. Rust and I sprint, lungs burning, across the killing ground.

And then, we see it.

A jagged spire of iridescent glass, half-buried in the earth, catching the faint light like a prism. The entrance is a airlock, miraculously intact. With trembling hands and the knowledge from a diagram in my stolen book, I work the manual release. The door groans open.

Inside is a cathedral of green. Condensation drips from glass panes onto soft, dark soil. Hardy ferns uncurl in the damp air. In the center, under a fractured skylight, a single, stunted apple tree clings to life, bearing three small, red fruits.

Rust purrs, weaving between my legs.

I don’t know if this is a new beginning for humanity, or just a beautiful place to survive a little longer. But I take out the botanical guide, kneel in the real, living dirt, and begin to read. I will learn. I will plant. I will build a gate to keep the horrors out, and a nursery to coax life in.

I decide to become a gardener in the ruins of the world. Not just to survive, but to tend to a promise, buried but not dead.
