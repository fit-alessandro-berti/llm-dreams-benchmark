Your pen feels impossibly heavy, and when you try to write your name, the ink pools into a tiny black lake that reflects a version of you already walking out of the testing hall, smiling.

You glance around. The other students are answering calmly, their pencils scratching out music instead of words. The clock on the wall isn’t moving forward — it’s counting the number of breaths you’ve taken since you realized you’re dreaming.

The proctor, who has your face from ten years in the future, leans down and whispers: “The exam isn’t about what you know. It’s about whether you remember that this isn’t the room where your future is decided.”

Suddenly the walls of the exam hall turn transparent. Beyond them you see all the other versions of yourself: one teaching, one building something in a garage, one laughing with people you haven’t met yet, one failing spectacularly and getting back up anyway. 

The test paper dissolves into a blank sheet. At the top, in your own handwriting, a single prompt appears:  
**“What question would you ask yourself if you weren’t afraid of the answer?”**

Your pen is light now. And you realize you’re already passing.
