You begin to speak, but the words refuse to stay in your mouth. They spill out as warm, glowing marbles that bounce across the stage and roll into the audience's laps. Each marble hums with a different truth: one sings about forgotten childhood wishes, another whispers the exact coordinates of a star no telescope has ever found, a third simply repeats "you are enough" in every language that has ever existed.

The crowd doesn't clap. They *melt*. Not in horror— in delight. Their edges soften like warm wax, colors swirling together until the entire auditorium is one vast, living painting of every face you've ever loved and every stranger you've ever passed on the street. They begin to hum in harmony with your marbles, and the sound lifts you off the stage. Your feet leave the floor. You're floating now, but backwards— falling upward through the ceiling, through clouds that taste like your grandmother's perfume, through a sky that's actually the inside of your own skull.

Suddenly you're standing in front of the *real* audience: every version of yourself that ever existed. Baby-you in the front row with a juice box. Teen-you with acne and too much hair gel. Future-you with silver hair and a quiet smile, holding a glowing notebook labeled "The Speech You Were Always Meant to Give."

Future-you steps forward, hands you the notebook, and says, "It was never about what you were going to say. It was about who would be listening when you finally stopped being afraid to say it."

You open your mouth to reply—

—and wake up with your heart racing and a single glowing marble still resting in your palm, slowly dissolving into morning light. 

The dream is complete. But the speech, it seems, has only just begun.
