You start to run, but your feet make no sound against the pavement, which has begun to shimmer like wet oil beneath a sky the color of old television static. The street signs are in a language you almost recognize—letters that slide off the metal and crawl into storm drains, whispering as they go. You reach into your pocket for your phone and find instead a heavy brass compass whose needle spins wildly before settling, pointing not north, but directly at your own chest, throbbing in time with your heartbeat.

You follow it anyway.

The avenue opens into a plaza where fountains flow upward into clouds shaped like filing cabinets, and bronze statues turn their heads to track your passage. At a café table that flickers in and out of existence, a woman waves you over. She has the face of your second-grade teacher, but her eyes are the exact shade of a conference room you once hated. "The meeting is where the shadows go to sleep," she says, her voice coming from the sugar bowl, and presses into your palm a ticket printed on a drying leaf.

The clock tower at the plaza’s center strikes thirteen. You realize with a drop in your stomach that the hour has both already passed and not yet arrived—time here is folded like origami, and your meeting exists in all the creases at once.

You board a tram that glides on beams of moonlight, its destination board flashing between your childhood home address and a room number you’ve never seen but somehow remember from a previous life. The other passengers are all wearing your clothes. None of them have faces, only smooth swirls of marble, yet one of them hands you a briefcase. It is warm and pulses gently, like a second heart.

The tram deposits you at a building with no doors, only mirrors. You step toward your reflection and the glass turns to mist. Inside, the elevator has no buttons, only a dial tuned to the frequency of your anxiety. It rises sideways.

The conference room is infinite, filled with thousands of empty chairs facing a single podium where a version of yourself is already speaking, gesturing gracefully at charts made of constellations and shifting weather patterns. The audience—those faceless passengers, the statue from the plaza, the woman from the café—turns toward you. Not with accusation, but with a terrible, patient relief, as if they’ve been waiting for you to arrive so the dream can finally begin.

When you open your mouth to apologize for your lateness, no sound comes out. Only a swarm of luminous moths that spiral upward, arranging themselves into a map that points not toward an exit, but deeper into the city, where the real meeting, you now understand, has always been about to start.
