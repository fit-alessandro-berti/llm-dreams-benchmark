...the sky turns the color of a bruise — purples and greens folding into each other like oil on water. The trees begin to sway, but not with the wind. They sway inward, leaning toward you, their trunks creaking in a rhythm that sounds almost like breathing.

You try to run, but the ground has softened. Each step sinks slightly, as though the forest floor has become something between soil and skin. Leaves spiral upward instead of falling, spinning slowly like music boxes.

Then the rain begins — but it falls upward. Drops rise from the earth toward the darkening canopy, pulling tiny specks of light with them. You realize each drop contains a memory: a kitchen from childhood, the sound of someone laughing, the weight of a hand you once held. They're not your memories, but they feel familiar.

The storm is above you now, but instead of thunder, there's a deep hum — like a cello string the size of the sky, vibrating once. The trees go still. The upward rain freezes mid-air, thousands of suspended luminous droplets, each one a small world.

You reach out and touch one. It's warm.

And then the forest speaks — not in words, but in the sudden understanding that everything around you has been awake this whole time, watching with something like tenderness. The storm isn't coming *for* you. It's been waiting *with* you.

You exhale.

The droplets fall — gently now, downward, as they should — and as they touch the ground, small flowers open where they land. Not ordinary flowers. These are impossible colors, colors you've never seen before and can't name, and they bloom in spirals that follow the pattern of your footprints.

The sky clears from the center outward, like an eye opening.

You walk on, and the forest is different now — or maybe you are.
