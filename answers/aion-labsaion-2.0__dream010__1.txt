The final challenge is not what you expected. Instead of a presentation or a test, you are led into a vast, empty room with a single, old-fashioned rotary telephone on a pedestal. The judge, a figure with a face that shifts like smoke, says, "The last task is to have a conversation with your future self."

You pick up the receiver. The line crackles. A voice, familiar yet aged, speaks: "You already have the job. The competition was never about proving yourself to them. It was about you believing you deserved it."

As the voice fades, the walls of the room dissolve into the bustling floor of an office you've never seen but somehow know intimately. Your competitors are there, smiling, handing you a cup of coffee. One says, "You start on Monday." But as you take the cup, you realize it's filled with sand, grains spilling through your fingers like time. You look up and you're back in your childhood bedroom, the alarm clock blaring. For a second, you hold the knowledge of the job, the office, the future, warm and solid in your chest. Then you wake.
