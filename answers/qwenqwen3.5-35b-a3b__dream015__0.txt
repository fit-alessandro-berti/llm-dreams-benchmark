Despite the hardships, you decide to plant a seed.

It is a strange choice, for you are not a farmer; you are a scavenger. The world around you is a tapestry of rusted metal and grey ash, where the wind whistles through the skeletal remains of skyscrapers like a mournful flute. There is no soil, only pulverized dust, and no water, only a salty mist that stings the eyes. Yet, in your pocket, you feel the warmth of a single, impossible acorn.

You kneel on a piece of broken concrete, ignoring the jagged rocks that press against your knees. A figure approaches from the smoke, their silhouette shifting between a scavenger and a ghost. You expect a weapon drawn, a demand for the meager supplies you cling to. But in the dream, the logic is fluid. The figure simply reaches out a hand, and their skin is transparent, revealing gears of gold spinning beneath the muscle.

"You know it won't grow," they say, their voice sounding like rustling leaves.

"I know it might," you reply.

You dig a hole in the hardpan dust. It shouldn't be possibleŚthe earth is too deep and frozenŚbut the ground yields to your fingers like soft clay, warm and smelling of rain that fell a century ago. You place the seed in the dark, cover it, and press the earth down.

As soon as your palm leaves the dirt, the world inhales.

A tremor runs through the horizon, not violent, but rhythmic. From the grey dust beneath your hand, a shoot erupts, unfurling leaves the color of emerald glass. They grow with terrifying speed, spiraling upward, drinking the ash from the air and turning it green. The sky, usually a bruised purple, begins to bleed into a soft, golden dawn.

The figure steps back, fading into the smoke. "That is not survival," they whisper. "That is resurrection."

The acorn becomes a sapling, then a tree, its branches heavy with fruit that glow like tiny lanterns. As you watch, the grey ruins around you do not vanish, but they soften. The rusted cars bloom with vines; the cracked pavement is filled with wildflowers. The danger remainsŚthe shadows still lengthen, and the silence still holdsŚbut the fear is no longer the master of the dream.

You reach up and catch a glowing fruit before it hits the ground. It tastes of sunlight and forgotten memories. The tree hums a low vibration that settles in your chest.

Then, the dream wavers. The ground beneath you dissolves into a stream of water that flows upward, defying gravity. The sky cracks open like an egg, and you feel the cold rush of a real morning air against your face, the smell of laundry detergent and exhaust replacing the metallic taste of dust.

You wake up gasping, but your hands are clean, and for a moment, on the window sill of your room, a single green sprout pushes its way through the potting soil, defying the season, defying the logic of the waking world. You smile, realizing the choice you made in the dream was never about the seed. It was about remembering that the world can still be grown.
