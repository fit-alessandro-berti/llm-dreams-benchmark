First, you stumble on a circle of people passing a mason jar full of glowing fireflies that hum half-remembered lullabies, the ones your grandma sang you that you can never quite place when you’re awake. One holds the jar out to you, and a firefly lands on your wrist, scribbling a tiny, smudged heart on your skin before it flies back. You don’t stop to talk; your chest aches too sharp with the need to find the one person you came with, so you just smile and slip away.

Next, a cluster of folks hunched over a folding table, folding origami cranes that unfurl the second they’re finished into handwritten postcards—no addresses, just lines of unspoken things: I miss the way we used to get lost in bookstore mazes. I’m sorry I didn’t call when my mom died. One slides a blank postcard toward you, and you back away, heart thrumming, because that’s the exact card you’d write to your person, if you ever found the words to say all the quiet gratitude you’ve never voiced.

The space shifts again, and suddenly you’re in a dim, quiet hallway lined with potted ferns that drip stardust instead of water. At the end, leaning against a doorframe, is your person. They’re holding two mugs of steaming cocoa, and they wave you over, like they hadn’t moved an inch the whole time you’d been wandering. They tell you they’d waited for you to work your way through the halls, that everyone at this gathering is only there to find one person they came with, that all the groups you passed were strangers pausing for a minute of soft connection before they kept looking for their own person too. The firefly’s heart glows warm on your wrist as you sit down next to them, and for the first time all night, you don’t feel lost at all—you feel like you were always meant to wander, just to end up there.
