They move through the crowd as if the city is parting just for you. One of them carries a lantern, though it is somehow both midnight and morning; another hums a tune you feel you’ve known all your life. The traffic softens into waves, the neon signs blur into constellations, and the streets begin to twist into places that don’t exist on any map you’ve ever seen.

They lead you through a narrow alley where vines grow from brick walls overnight, across a bridge suspended over a river of reflected stars, and into a quiet square where all the clocks have stopped at the exact same second. In the center stands a small door painted your favorite color.

“This is yours,” one of the strangers says, smiling as if they have been waiting a very long time to return it.

When you open the door, you do not find a room. You find every version of yourself you thought you had lost: the child who was fearless, the dreamer who believed in impossible things, the tired soul who only wanted to be understood. They look at you kindly, not accusingly, and step aside.

Beyond them is a path lit by paper lanterns floating in the air like patient moons. You turn to thank the strangers, but they are already fading into the city’s glow, waving as though they were never strangers at all.

Then you realize something strange: you are no longer lost.

The city is still vast, still noisy, still alive—but now it beats in rhythm with your own heart. You step forward onto the lantern-lit path, and somewhere in the distance, just before you wake, you hear a voice whisper:

“You were always on your way.”
