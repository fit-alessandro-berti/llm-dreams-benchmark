The groups shimmer and shift—first a cluster of old classmates, then a circle of tall, faceless people in gray suits, then children laughing in a language I almost understand. I call out the name of the one person I know, but my voice comes out as a ribbon of silver smoke that curls into the shape of a moth and flutters away.

The floor beneath me becomes a murmuring crowd of hands, fingers interlacing like a carpet, and I stumble into a grand ballroom where everyone wears masks of my own face. The music is the sound of water over stones, and the chandeliers are made of frozen laughter. I move through the masked crowd, and each one whispers a secret I’ve forgotten, a memory of a memory.

Then, at the far end, I see them—the person I know—standing by a window that opens onto a starless sky. They turn, and their face is a soft, glowing question mark. “You’re late,” they say, but their voice is like a warm breeze smelling of baking bread. “I’ve been here forever, and we’ve never met.”

Before I can answer, the gathering dissolves into a flock of doves, the building folds itself into paper, and I’m falling upward through a garden of clocks, all ticking backward. The dream ends not with a resolution, but with the quiet understanding that I was searching for the one person who could show me I was never really lost—just dreaming of a party I was always hosting in my own mind.
