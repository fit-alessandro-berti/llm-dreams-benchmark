You wake up in a completely different era, with no modern technology around. As you explore your new surroundings, you realize you need to **remember who you were before the dream swallowed you whole.**

The ground beneath your feet is packed earth, not asphalt, and the air smells of woodsmoke, horse sweat, and baking bread. Stone buildings lean toward one another across a narrow lane, their shutters painted in colors too bright to be real—cobalt, ochre, moss green. People pass you in woolen tunics and long cloaks, chattering in a language that almost sounds familiar, like a song you knew as a child but forgot the words to. A bell rings somewhere high above, and pigeons burst from a tiled roof in a gray flurry.

You pat your pockets. No phone. No keys. No wallet. Just a small brass token, warm to the touch, stamped with a symbol you don’t recognize: three spirals curling into one another. A child stops beside you, wide-eyed, and presses a scrap of paper into your hand. On it, in handwriting eerily like your own, are the words: *Find the clockmaker before the sun turns backward.*

The sky is already beginning to bruise at the edges, though it can’t be noon. You know, with the absolute certainty of dreams, that the token is a key, the clockmaker is waiting, and somewhere in this strange old city is the door that will either send you home—or make you forget you ever had one.

So you start walking. The streets shift under your feet like the pages of a book turning in a draft you cannot feel, and somewhere a clock strikes thirteen. You are not afraid. Not yet. You are simply moving, because in dreams, the only wrong choice is standing still.
