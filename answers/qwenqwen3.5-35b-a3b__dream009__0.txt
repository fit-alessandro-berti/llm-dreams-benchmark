You wake up in a completely different era, with no modern technology around. As you explore your new surroundings, you realize you need to **find the clock tower before the hands start moving backward.**

The air tastes of coal smoke and unripe plums. You are standing on cobblestones that hum with a low vibration, the kind you feel in your teeth more than hear. The sky is a bruised shade of violet, and the sun—a large, dim coin—sits just above the horizon, refusing to rise. There are people in the street, but they are walking sideways, their eyes closed, whispering numbers that don't make sense. You realize you don't know your name, and the panic in your chest is cold, sharp as a shard of glass.

You walk toward the town square, drawn by the sound of a distant chime that seems to echo from a decade earlier. A shopkeeper watches you, holding a lantern that contains not fire, but a swirling storm of blue light. He nods once, as if expecting you, and points a gloved finger toward the spire in the distance.

As you run, the buildings begin to shift. The Victorian facades melt into stone ruins, then back again. Your boots feel like they are made of water, heavy and impossible. You reach the base of the tower, and the door is locked with a mechanism that requires a key made of light. You look at your own hand, and the reflection in your palm shows an ancient symbol, glowing faintly. You realize the clock isn't there to measure time—it's there to keep the dream awake.

You reach for the symbol, and the vibration in the stones turns into a silence so loud it hurts. The world dissolves into white noise.

You wake up, gasping for air. Your phone is glowing on the nightstand, buzzing with notifications from this century. But in your pocket, you find a single, cold plum, smelling faintly of coal smoke.
