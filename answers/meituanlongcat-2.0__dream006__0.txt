...speak. But the words that leave your mouth are not the ones you rehearsed. They are a language you don't know, yet it flows from you like a river, ancient and sure. The crowd doesn't seem to notice. They lean in, their expectant faces softening into rapt attention.

You look down at your notes, but the paper is blank, then filled with shifting symbols that rearrange themselves into constellations. The microphone in your hand feels warm, organic, like a polished stone pulled from a riverbed.

You continue, the unknown words painting pictures in the air. You speak of cities built on the backs of sleeping giants, of oceans that remember every ship that ever sailed them, of a silence so profound it has a color—a deep, velvety indigo. The audience is utterly still. You can hear the collective rhythm of their breathing, synchronized with your strange cadence.

Then, you see it. In the front row, a single person is not listening. They are *watching*. Their eyes are not expectant, but knowing. They give a slow, deliberate nod, as if you've finally remembered the correct lines to a play you've both performed a thousand times before.

A jolt of recognition, not of the person, but of the *place*. This stage is not a convention center or an auditorium. It is a clearing in a vast, dark forest, and the audience is made of shadows and starlight. The speech is not a presentation; it is an incantation, a remembering.

You reach the final phrase. It is a single, resonant word that hangs in the air, vibrating. The knowing figure in the front row smiles. The crowd of shadows begins to dissolve, not by leaving, but by melting back into the darkness from which they came, their purpose fulfilled.

The stage lights dim to a single, soft spotlight on you. The silence is now complete, but it is not empty. It is full of the echo of the word you spoke, and the profound, unsettling certainty that you have not been giving a speech at all.

You have been giving a *key*.

And somewhere, in the waking world or in a deeper layer of the dream, you hear the sound of a great lock turning.
