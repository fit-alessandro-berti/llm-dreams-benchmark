

The weight of the wool tunic is unfamiliar against my skin—coarse, smelling of lanolin and woodsmoke—and my pocket, where my phone should press against my thigh, is empty, flat fabric. Panic, sharp and cold, spikes through me for a heartbeat: *Where am I? What year?* But the dream doesn’t fray at the edges like panic should. Instead, it sharpens. The sun is high, casting long, distinct shadows from timber-frame houses with thatched roofs. The air smells of woodsmoke, damp earth, and something sharp—maybe crushed mugwort growing by the path. A woman in a simple kirtle passes by, carrying a bucket toward a well, her bare feet sure on the packed dirt path. She doesn’t glance at my strange clothes (linen shirt, too-fine wool breeches—where *did* I get these?), but her eyes hold a quiet, weary assessment. *Survivor’s gaze.*

Then it hits me, not as a thought, but as a deep, wordless certainty in my bones: **I need water. Now. Before the thirst claws too deep.** Not to charge my phone, not to call for help, not to check a map—but the ancient, urgent imperative of the body: find clean water, or I won’t last the day. My modern instincts scream *wrong*—*I should be able to tap, to search, to summon*—but my throat is already dry, and the well the woman headed toward is the only sensible thing in sight. I ignore the phantom buzz where my smartwatch should be, ignore the scream of useless knowledge in my head (quantum physics, TCP/IP protocols, the plot of that show I binged last night), and I turn my feet toward the stone rim of the well. The dream holds. I am here. I am thirsty. And the first thing I need to do—*the only thing* that matters right now—is lower that bucket and drink.  

*(The dream continues: the rope is rough on my palms, the water shockingly cold and sweet as I cup my hands, and for a moment, standing there with drips falling from my chin into the dusty earth, I am not a person lost in time—I am simply a person, needing to drink.)*
