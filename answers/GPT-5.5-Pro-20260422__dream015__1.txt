…**plant a garden.**

At first, it feels absurd. The soil is cracked, the sky is the color of old ash, and every distant sound could be wind, metal, or something hungry moving through the ruins. You have one dented canteen, three packets of seeds, and a rusted shovel you found in the skeleton of a hardware store.

Still, you choose a patch of earth beside a collapsed train station and begin.

Each day, you return before dawn. You clear glass from the dirt. You drag sheets of metal into a crooked fence. You collect rainwater in broken satellite dishes. At night, you sleep inside the station ticket booth, listening to the world scrape and howl beyond the walls.

Then, one morning, a green shoot appears.

It is tiny. Fragile. Almost ridiculous.

But it is alive.

Others begin to notice. A child from the tunnels leaves a jar of water by the fence. An old mechanic fixes the station pump without saying a word. A woman with a rifle stands guard one evening, pretending she is only passing through. Strangers come with seeds, tools, stories, and names they had stopped using.

The garden grows.

So does the settlement around it.

The train station becomes a shelter. The platform becomes a market. The tracks become roads. People paint signs again. Someone teaches songs. Someone builds a bell. Someone laughs so hard one afternoon that everyone stops working just to hear it.

Years seem to pass in the strange, elastic way they do in dreams.

Then one night, you climb to the roof of the station and see lights flickering in the distance—not fires, not lightning, but other gardens. Other settlements. Other stubborn little points of green and gold pushing back against the dark.

You realize the world did not end all at once.

And maybe, you think, it does not have to begin again all at once either.

You wake with dirt under your fingernails and the feeling that something, somewhere, is still growing.
