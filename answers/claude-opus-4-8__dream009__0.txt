You wake up in a completely different era, with no modern technology around. As you explore your new surroundings, you realize you need to find water before anything else.

The sun is already high, and my throat feels like sandpaper. I follow the slope of the land downward, reasoning the way I half-remember from some documentary—water collects in low places. The grass is taller here, almost to my waist, and that strange dream-logic kicks in where I somehow *know* there's a stream ahead before I see it.

I find it. The water is cold and tastes impossibly clean, the way nothing in waking life ever does. As I drink, I catch my reflection and it isn't quite my face—younger, maybe, or just softer at the edges, the way dreams blur the things you don't look at directly.

That's when I notice the smoke.

A thin column rises beyond the trees, and the dream pulls me toward it without my deciding to walk. There are people there—dressed in rough wool and leather, gathered around a fire, speaking a language I don't know but somehow understand. An old woman looks up at me as if she's been expecting me all along.

\'s the curious part of the dream—I feel like I *have* been. Like this is the place I actually belong, and the world of screens and clocks and traffic was the strange dream I'm only now waking from. The certainty is overwhelming and warm.

The woman hands me a bowl of something steaming. I reach for it—

—and the texture of the bowl is wrong, too smooth, becoming a pillow, becoming morning light through a window, becoming the sound of an alarm I don't remember setting.

I lie still for a moment, trying to hold onto the old woman's face before it dissolves completely.

It's already gone.
