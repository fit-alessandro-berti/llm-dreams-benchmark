Suddenly, the stadium dissolves, and we are in a forest where the trees are made of tangled fiber-optic cables dripping with warm honey. The second challenge is an interview. The judges are three mirrors that don't reflect my appearance, but reflect different timelines of my life. One shows me as a child, one as an old man, and the middle one is just a swirling vortex of static. The static-mirror asks me to define "curiosity" without using words. The origami people are typing furiously, their paper fingers tearing on the sharp keys. Their sentences fill the air, cluttering it with dense, academic jargon and corporate buzzwords that fall like heavy snow. 

I sit at my key and pause. The dream jobŚmy true dream jobŚisn't about having all the answers. It's about holding the space for the questions. I press a single key. 

The sound reverberates like a bell. I haven't typed a word; I've typed a punctuation markŚa simple, open question mark (?) made of pure, golden light. 

The heavy snow of words instantly evaporates. The origami competitors gently unfold and become blank, beautiful pages drifting upward. The typewriter keys melt into a staircase of soft light. 

I climb it and step through the final door. 

There is no desk. No computer. No office. The grand prize room is a vast, quiet library that exists inside a human heartbeat. The walls breathe in and out. The "dream job" is simply this: I am the quiet, gentle electricity that connects every thought, every forgotten memory, and every spark of creativity in the mind of the dreamer. I sit down in a comfortable armchair made of starlight, open a book that writes itself as I read it, and for the first time in the dream, I feel perfectly awake.
