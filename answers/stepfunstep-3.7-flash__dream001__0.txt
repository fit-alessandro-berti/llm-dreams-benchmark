You start to fumble for your phone in your jacket pocket, but your fingers close around a smooth, cool river stone instead, the kind you picked up on a half-remembered vacation to a lake when you were 12. When you yank your phone out a second later, the lock screen is a photo of your childhood tabby cat wearing a tiny blazer, and the only calendar entry for the day is a single block labeled “IMPORTANT” pinned to a smudge of iridescent ink that shifts from teal to violet when you tilt the screen, no address attached.

Your first instinct is to run, but the streets feel wrong, like they’re breathing under the soles of your sneakers—concrete ripples like water when you step hard, and storefronts shuffle like a deck of cards every time you blink, the signs switching from bodegas to art galleries to dentist offices written in alphabets you don’t recognize. You grab the nearest stranger to ask for directions, and it’s the woman who used to run the frozen yogurt shop down the street from your first apartment, but she’s selling floating lavender pretzels from a cart that hovers three feet off the ground. She points down an alley that wasn’t there a second ago, and says “Third left past the piano that plays the weather, you’re only 37 minutes late, they don’t mind.”

You take the alley, and sure enough, there’s a dust-covered upright piano halfway down the block, its keys clacking out a tune that sounds like rain on a tin roof, a tiny digital display bolted to its side flashing 72°F and 10% chance of dandelion puffs. You turn left at the third corner, and the street vanishes into a spiraling staircase that climbs straight up into a sky the color of orange soda, lined with windows that glow warm gold. Every window has a person in business casual leaning over a desk, typing on typewriters that float three feet in front of them, no chairs, no lamps, just stacks of paper that turn into monarch butterflies when they’re tossed in the trash.

You check your watch halfway up the stairs, and the face is melting, the hands spinning backward so fast they blur, but you swear you’ve only been walking for seven minutes. You’re so busy squinting at it that you almost walk straight through the open door of the 23rd floor landing, and you skid to a stop when you realize where you are: it’s the tiny corner office you had when you ran your 7-year-old lemonade stand, scaled up to fit adults, the desk still has the crayon drawing of a fire-breathing dragon you taped to it when you were little, and the “IMPORTANT MEETING” sign you made out of construction paper is taped to the door.

The person sitting behind the desk is a version of you, ten years older, wearing the blazer you laid out to wear to this meeting last night but forgot to grab on your way out the door. They’re spinning a marble that glows like a captured star between their fingers, and when they see you, they laugh and push a sheet of paper across the desk. It’s a crayon map of the city you’ve been running through, drawn in wobbly lines by 7-year-old you, with a big red X right in the middle of the lemonade stand office.

“Took you long enough,” they say, and you realize the crumpled meeting agenda you’ve been panicking about all morning is in your pocket, and the “important thing” you were supposed to bring isn’t a presentation or a contract—it’s the marble, the one you’ve kept on your nightstand since you were a kid. You set it down on the desk, and it splits open to reveal a tiny, perfect replica of the city, the tiny versions of you running through its streets, the pretend lemonade stand on every corner.

The older you nods, and the first chime of the meeting starts—only it’s not a conference room chime, it’s the jingle of your childhood ice cream truck, pulling up to the curb outside the window. You realize you don’t have to give a presentation at all. You just have to sit down, order a snow cone, and tell the story of the morning you ran through a city made of memory to find the part of yourself you left behind when you grew up.
