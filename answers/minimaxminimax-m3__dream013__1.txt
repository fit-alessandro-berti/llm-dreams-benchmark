The puzzles grow stranger too—no longer simple riddles or logic grids, but vast interconnected webs of thought that span across entire rooms. You realize, with a chill, that the walls are lined with mirrors, and as you solve each puzzle, the reflections show you different versions of yourself—younger, older, paths not taken, choices unmade.

One chamber contains a clockwork heart suspended in glass, ticking in reverse. To pass, you must synchronize your own heartbeat with it, which means closing your eyes and forgetting that you're being watched by the maze itself. You can feel its attention on you now—a sentient hum in the corridors, whispering half-answers in a language that feels like your own forgotten memories.

Deeper still, the puzzles stop being puzzles at all. They become *questions*. The maze asks you things you cannot answer aloud, and somehow, you answer them anyway. *What did you leave behind? What did you never start? What is the shape of the thing you most regret?* The corridors open into vast cathedral-like spaces where the floor is made of unfinished sentences, and you must walk across them carefully, choosing your words as steps.

At the very center, you find not an exit, but a door that looks exactly like the entrance you came through. Standing before it is a figure made entirely of static—familiar in silhouette but impossible to see clearly. It turns to you and speaks without speaking, and you understand all at once: the maze was never a maze at all. It was a mirror, and you've been solving yourself this whole time.

You wake up.

The morning light is strange. You have a thought—**complete**—in your mind, and the feeling that if you don't write it down soon, the maze will take it back.

What did you remember?
