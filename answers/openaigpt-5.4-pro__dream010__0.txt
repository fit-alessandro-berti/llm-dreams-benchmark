…with each round, the rules become stranger.

At first, it feels almost normal. The competition is held in a glittering building made of glass and gold, and your name hangs in bright lights above a stage. Hundreds of contestants stand beside you, all dressed for different futures: lab coats, suits, chef jackets, flight uniforms, paint-stained overalls. At the far end of the room, on a pedestal under a single white spotlight, sits the prize: a sealed envelope containing the offer for your dream job.

You can feel, somehow, that your name is supposed to be inside it.

The first challenge is easy enough. You have to cross a hallway while answering questions about yourself. The floor moves like a treadmill, trying to pull you backward, while giant screens on the walls flash memories from your life—moments you were proud, moments you were embarrassed, moments you wish no one had seen. A voice from nowhere asks, “Why do you deserve this?”

You open your mouth to answer, but your voice comes out as birds. Small silver birds fly from your lips and circle the ceiling. Somehow, the judges nod as if they understand.

You pass.

In the next round, the room is a maze made of office cubicles, except every cubicle contains a different version of you. One version is confident and calm, typing furiously with perfect posture. Another is exhausted, rubbing their eyes under fluorescent lights. Another is younger, staring at the screen with huge hopeful eyes. They all look up as you pass.

“Don’t choose wrong,” one of them whispers.

You try to ask what that means, but the walls slide sideways and rearrange themselves. Somewhere ahead, a bell rings. Contestants begin disappearing one by one, not dramatically, but softly, as though erased by a sigh.

You start running.

The third challenge is impossible: you are told to present your greatest idea to a panel of judges seated far above you in a balcony hidden by clouds. The podium is on the edge of a swimming pool full of ticking clocks. Every time you pause too long, one of the clocks leaps from the water and shatters at your feet. You begin speaking anyway—about what you love, what you want to build, why this job matters to you, why it has mattered for so long.

As you speak, the room changes.

The balcony lowers.
The clouds part.
The judges are not strangers.

They are every person whose opinion ever mattered to you: a teacher who once praised you, someone who doubted you, a friend who believed in you before you believed in yourself, a family member who asked if this dream was realistic, an old boss, a younger version of you swinging their legs under a school desk.

They all watch in silence.

Your throat tightens. The clocks begin jumping faster.

Then your younger self smiles.

And suddenly the words come easily.

You talk until the pool goes still.

You pass.

By now only three contestants remain. You, a woman with stars drifting through her hair, and a man whose shoes leave footprints of grass on every floor. None of you speak. The building has grown too quiet for conversation.

For the final challenge, you are led to a door marked only with a mirror.

“Behind this door,” says the announcer, though now the voice sounds oddly like your own, “is the life you would have if you win. To claim it, you must enter alone.”

The woman goes first. The door opens, and for a moment you glimpse a skyline at sunset and hear applause. She steps inside and vanishes. The man goes next. Behind his door is birdsong, rain, and a long road shining in the sun. He also disappears.

Then it is your turn.

When you touch the handle, it is warm, as if someone on the other side has just let go.

Inside is not an office, or a studio, or a lab, or any place you expected. It is a long room lined with every unfinished thing you ever abandoned—half-written plans, unsent applications, ideas scribbled in notebooks, projects you loved but gave up on because you thought they weren’t good enough. They rise around you in piles like sleeping animals.

At the center of the room is the envelope.

You walk toward it slowly.

The closer you get, the heavier your feet become, until each step feels like walking through deep water. Whispering fills the room. Not voices from outside—your own thoughts, collected over years.

You’re too late.
You’re not ready.
Someone else wants it more.
What if you get it and fail?

The envelope trembles on its pedestal.

You stop just inches away.

Then, from somewhere behind you, comes the sound of those silver birds again—the ones that flew out of your mouth in the first challenge. They circle above your head, bright and restless, and with them come other sounds: the maze shifting, the clocks breaking, your younger self laughing, your own voice speaking clearly at the podium.

You realize the competition was never testing whether you were talented enough.

It was testing whether you could walk through your own fear without turning around.

So you reach out and take the envelope.

The entire room exhales.

The walls dissolve into morning light. The piles of unfinished things lift gently into the air, unfolding, mending, becoming ladders, bridges, paper airplanes, maps. The whispering stops. The envelope opens itself in your hands.

Inside is a single card.

It reads:

**WELCOME. YOU HAVE BEEN CHOSEN.  
PLEASE BEGIN.**

And just before you wake, standing there in that impossible golden light, you understand the strangest part of the dream:

The dream job was never waiting at the end of the competition.

It was waiting on the other side of your decision to believe you could step forward and claim it.
