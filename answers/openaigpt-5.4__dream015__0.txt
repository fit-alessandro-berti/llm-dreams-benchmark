…plant a garden.

At first it feels absurd.

The city around you is all broken teeth of concrete and rusted steel. Windows stare out like empty eyes. Wind drags ash through the streets in thin gray ribbons. Somewhere far off, metal bangs against metal in a rhythm the dead world seems to have memorized. Water is rationed by the swallow. Soil is poisoned in patches. Anything green is either guarded, stolen, or gone.

And still, you decide to plant a garden.

You find the place for it in the shell of an old rooftop courtyard, six stories above the cracked avenue. Once it had been decorative—benches, planters, maybe a little café for people who believed the future was a guarantee. Now it is a basin of rubble and wind, but it gets light, and light is rare enough to count as mercy.

You start small. A scavenged bucket. Two bent tins. A sack of dirt carried up stair by stair on shaking shoulders. You pry open old bags of potting mix gone hard as stone and break them apart with a knife. You sift glass from soil. You mix in compost made from things no one wants to think about for too long. A cracked blue barrel catches rain when the sky remembers how.

The seeds come from many places.

A handful of beans traded from a woman with a crossbow and one good eye. Tomato seeds dried years ago and tucked into a cookbook you found in the flooded remains of a library. Marigolds from a collapsed community center, their paper packets faded but intact. Potatoes with too many eyes. Onion bottoms. Scraps and remnants. Almost-nothings.

You press them into the earth as if tucking children into bed.

Below, the world remains what it is. Raiders move through the districts in painted trucks. Feral dogs roam in packs. At night, strange lights sometimes comb the horizon, and everyone goes quiet until they pass. Hunger makes people cruel in efficient ways. Fear makes them worse.

So you do not tell anyone at first.

Every morning before dawn, you climb to the roof. You carry your water in secret and pour it carefully, each drop a decision. You check leaves for blight, turn the soil, build little shades from wire and torn cloth. You speak aloud sometimes, though only to the plants.

“Grow,” you whisper.
“Please.”
“That’s enough for now.”
“You’re doing better than all of us.”

For a long time, nothing happens.

Then one morning, in a tin can by the east wall, a green hook lifts itself out of the dirt.

You stare at it as if it has spoken your name.

The first sprout is bean. The second is something uncertain. The third and fourth are tomatoes, frail and hairy, trembling in the wind like they regret being born into this century. But they are alive. Alive is enough.

Word gets out anyway. It always does.

A boy named Eli sees you carrying soil and follows at a distance. An old mechanic offers you a broken window frame that can be turned into a cold box. A woman from the clinic brings powdered minerals scavenged from abandoned stores. Someone leaves eggshells in a paper bag by the stairwell. Someone else leaves a note: If you can grow herbs, I can trade antibiotics.

Soon the roof is no longer yours alone.

People come quietly at first, as if entering a chapel. They kneel beside the planters and touch the leaves with rough fingertips. They ask what this is, and that is, and whether this one can be eaten, and how long until the others bear fruit. Children who have only known scarcity learn words like seedling, pollinator, compost, harvest. They laugh when worms appear. They give names to the crows.

You teach what you know, and invent the rest.

The garden changes the building before it changes the neighborhood. The stairwell smells faintly of basil. Buckets line the landings to catch leaks and rain. Broken mirrors are arranged to throw sunlight toward shaded corners. Someone paints the door to the roof a bright impossible yellow. Someone else hangs bells made from bottle caps, and when the wind moves through, the whole place sounds briefly like memory.

Then trouble comes, because of course it does.

One evening, smoke rises from the western blocks. By dark, armed men are moving street to street taking fuel, medicine, preserved food—anything portable, anything useful. Gardens are useful. Hope is useful too, but only to the people who have it, so they come for that as well.

You hear them on the stairs before you see them.

The community gathers on the rooftop in the blue light before night. There are not many weapons: a hunting rifle with five bullets, knives, hatchets, a length of chain, a nail gun with uncertain pressure. Behind you, the plants stir in their containers, soft as breath. In front of you, boots hammer upward through concrete dust.

And in the dream, something strange happens.

Not magical, not exactly. Just dream-true.

When the door bursts open, the rooftop is no longer small.

Rows of green stretch wider than they should, deeper than the roof could hold. Vines thread up old antennae and broken rebar, flowering under a bruised sky. Corn rustles where no corn had been yesterday. Sunflowers stand watch like sentries. The marigolds burn gold along the edges. Bees move through the air as though the world has never ended. The whole roof smells of wet earth and tomato leaf and life insisting on itself.

The raiders hesitate.

Everyone does.

Because for one suspended moment, all the ruins below are less real than this: a garden impossible in its abundance, fed by grief, labor, stubbornness, and all the tiny salvaged faiths of the people standing shoulder to shoulder around it.

One of the armed men lowers his gun first.

He looks not at you, but at a strawberry, red and small and shining from beneath a leaf. His face changes. You realize he is remembering something—summer, perhaps, or his mother, or a field from before. The memory hits him like a wound.

“No burning,” he says hoarsely to the others.

There is arguing. Threats. Hunger. Fear.

Then Eli, the boy, steps forward holding out a single bean pod in his open hand.

It is the smallest gesture in the world.
It changes everything.

Not all at once. This is still the end of the world, after all. People do not become kind just because they are offered food. Some still steal. Some still lie. Some come only to take. Violence does not vanish. Winter still comes. Disease still spreads. There are losses you do not wake up from easily, even in dreams.

But the garden survives.

And because it survives, other things do too.

Cuttings are carried across districts wrapped in damp cloth.
Seed packets become currency, then promises.
Bathtubs and shopping carts and old boots bloom with potatoes, spinach, peppers.
Rooftops green. Balconies flower. Courtyards turn into plots.
Children who grew up learning where to hide from gunfire now also learn when to thin carrots.
People begin mapping clean soil the way they once mapped danger.
A network forms—not of kings or armies, but of growers.

Months later, standing on the rooftop at sunrise, you look out over the city.

It is still broken.
But now it is streaked with green.

Through shattered windows spill trailing vines.
In gutted parking lots, rows of corn catch the light.
On top of buses, herbs ripple in the wind.
Someone, somewhere, has painted a wall with giant white letters:

WE ARE STILL HERE.

You kneel and press your hands into the warm dirt.

Under your fingers, roots thread through the earth in silence, finding one another in the dark, exchanging what they can, keeping alive what they touch.

And in the logic of dreams, you understand that this is how the world comes back.

Not all at once.
Not cleanly.
Not by force.

Leaf by leaf.
Seed by seed.
Hand to hand.
