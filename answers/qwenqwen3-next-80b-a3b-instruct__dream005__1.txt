You are at a large social gathering where you only know one person. As you try to find them, you encounter various groups of people and…  

…each group is made of people made of books.  

Not people who *like* books—*made* of them. Their skin ripples with printed text, their eyes flicker like page corners in a breeze, their voices hum with overlapping dialogue. A woman with a lace collar of handwritten letters whispers, “You’re looking for Eli, aren’t you?” before dissolving into a paragraph from *Pride and Prejudice*.  

You blink—and suddenly you’re standing beside a man whose torso is bound in ancient parchment, his face a single unopened chapter. He says, “Eli’s not here anymore. He became a footnote.”  

You follow the trail of whispers. A child runs past with a spine made of origami, giggling as her hair unfolds into a bibliography. You reach the garden—the center of the gathering—and there, beneath a tree made entirely of library cards, sits your friend…  

…except now Eli is a dictionary.  

Not just any dictionary. *The* Dictionary. Heavy, worn, the cover cracked with love. His pages flutter gently in a wind that doesn’t exist. Words drift from him like fireflies: *belonging, solitude, recognition, echo*.  

You reach out.  

The dictionary opens.  

On the first page, in your own handwriting:  

> “You always knew how to find me—even when I disappeared.”  

The crowd of book-people slowly turns to you. Their words quiet. One by one, they begin to glow—not with light, but with *silence*.  

And then, softly, as if the whole room has been holding its breath:  

*“Welcome home.”*  

You wake up—not from fear, but from a strange, warm ache in your chest.  

On your nightstand: a book you’ve never seen before.  

*The Dictionary of Dreams You Didn’t Know You Lost.*  

You open it.  

The first entry?  

*You.*
