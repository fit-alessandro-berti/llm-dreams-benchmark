Near the entrance, a circle of people in velvet jackets are laughing at a joke you somehow remember telling years ago. One of them turns and says, “There you are,” with such relief that you almost step into the circle. But when you ask whether they’ve seen the person you came with, their faces grow solemn.

“They were just here,” someone says.

“They’re always just here,” says another.

You move on.

The house is much larger inside than it was from the street. Rooms unfold into rooms: a kitchen full of strangers stirring empty pots, a library where every book has your handwriting in it, a balcony overlooking a garden that is underwater. Everywhere, music is playing, but it changes whenever you try to listen closely.

You catch glimpses of your friend ahead of you: their coat disappearing around a corner, their laugh rising from the next room, their hand lifting in a crowd as if waving you over. But each time you follow, you arrive too late.

In one room, people are dancing without music. In another, guests sit around a table wearing paper crowns, whispering secrets into cups and then drinking them. A woman you don’t recognize takes your arm and says, “You’re late for your speech.”

“I don’t have a speech,” you say.

She smiles kindly. “That’s what everyone says.”

She leads you to a small stage. The whole party turns toward you. Hundreds of faces wait, bright and expectant. At the back of the room, near a tall window, you finally see the one person you know. They are watching you with a calm expression, as if they have been waiting for you to notice them all along.

You step up to the microphone. It is warm, like it has been held by someone else for a long time.

“I’m looking for someone,” you say.

The crowd nods in perfect unison.

Your friend raises a hand.

But before you can call out, the walls of the room begin to breathe. The ceiling lifts away like a tent flap, revealing a night sky crowded with enormous, slowly turning stars. The guests continue watching you, but now they are made of shadow and candlelight.

Your friend walks toward the stage. With every step, the room grows quieter, until the only sound is the soft clink of glasses from somewhere very far away.

When they reach you, you realize they are holding your coat.

“You forgot this,” they say.

You take it, and inside the pocket is a small key you have never seen before.

“What does it open?” you ask.

Your friend looks past you, toward a door that has appeared at the back of the stage.

“You know,” they say.

You turn the key in the lock. The door opens not into another room, but into the same party from above, as if the entire gathering is inside a snow globe. You see yourself moving through the crowd, searching. You see all the strangers pointing you onward. You see your friend waiting patiently at the end of every path.

Then the globe tips, and the party begins to spill upward into the sky.

You grab your friend’s hand, but their fingers are suddenly paper, then smoke, then warm sunlight.

As you wake, you hear them say, very clearly:

“Next time, don’t look so hard.”
