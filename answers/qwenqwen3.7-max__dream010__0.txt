…the architecture of the arena begins to forget its own rules. 

The first challenge is a race, but the track is made of piano keys. Every time my foot strikes a white key, a memory of my childhood plays in the sky above me like a movie projection. I am running alongside my competitors, but when I look to my left, the person next to me isn't a stranger—it’s my third-grade teacher, running effortlessly in a business suit, while a giant, judgmental owl flies overhead holding a stopwatch. 

Suddenly, the piano keys turn to liquid, and I am falling. 

I don't hit the ground; instead, I land softly in a waiting room for the second challenge. The walls are breathing, expanding and contracting like lungs. The receptionist is a talking grandfather clock who tells me, in a voice that sounds exactly like my mother’s, that I am late for my own potential. 

The second challenge is an interview. I sit across from a panel of judges whose faces keep blurring, shifting between my friends, my parents, and strangers I’ve only seen in passing on the subway. They ask me a question, but instead of words, a swarm of glowing, blue butterflies erupts from the lead judge's mouth. I realize, with the absolute, unquestioning logic of a dream, that I have to catch the right butterfly to give the correct answer. 

I lunge and catch one. It dissolves into my skin, and suddenly I am speaking fluent, musical mathematics. The judges nod in profound approval, their blurring faces settling into wide, proud smiles. 

"You've passed the final round," the clock-receptionist chimes, rolling into the room. "You have won the dream job."

Confetti made of autumn leaves falls from the ceiling. The lead judge hands me a contract written on a massive, green monstera leaf. I look down at the title of my new position. It doesn't say "CEO" or "Lead Designer" or "Director." 

In shimmering, silver ink, it reads: **Architect of the Subconscious.** 

My duty, the contract explains, is to build the landscapes for other people's dreams when they sleep. I am to sculpt the flying cities, hide the monsters under the correct beds, and ensure the teeth-falling-out nightmares are kept to a reasonable minimum. 

I reach for a pen to sign the leaf, but the pen is made of melting ice. As the water drips onto the contract, the silver ink begins to run. The breathing walls of the room start to vibrate. A low, rhythmic thumping echoes from the sky—*thump, thump, thump*—which slowly morphs into the sharp, electronic blare of my waking alarm clock. 

The judges wave goodbye as they dissolve into mist. The leaf in my hands turns into my bedsheet. I open my eyes to the morning light filtering through my blinds, my hand still tightly gripping the fabric, my mind buzzing with the blueprints of a world I have to build tonight.
