You know the odds: the acidic rain, the roving bands of scavengers, the sun that burns like a bruise behind the smog. But as you pat the dry dirt flat, a strange, quiet defiance rises in your chest, replacing the hollow ache of hunger.

You realize that survival has been your only goal for so long that you’d forgotten how to build a future. 

Just as the sun begins to dip below the jagged, skeletal horizon of the skyline, you hear the crunch of boots on glass behind you. You don’t reach for your blade. Instead, you keep your back turned, watering the patch with the last of your canteen. 

"They won't grow," a gravelly voice rasps from the shadows. It’s a stranger—gaunt, eyes wide with the frantic light of someone who has forgotten how to be kind. 

You stand slowly, brushing the dust from your knees, and look him in the eye. "Maybe not," you say, your voice raspy from disuse. "But I'm tired of waiting for the end of the world. I’d rather see what happens if I start something instead."

The stranger freezes, his hand dropping from his holster. For a long, suspended moment, the only sound is the wind whistling through the hollow shells of skyscrapers. Then, he reaches into his pack and pulls out a dented tin cup of water and a rusted spade. He doesn't say a word, but he kneels three feet away from you and starts to dig.

In the middle of the wasteland, beneath a sky the color of a fading bruise, the two of you work in silence. It is a foolish, beautiful thing. And as you fall deeper into the dream, the sky begins to soften, turning from leaden gray to a faint, hopeful violet—and for the first time in an age, you aren't afraid of waking up.
