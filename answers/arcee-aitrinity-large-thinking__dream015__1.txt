
...push open the rusted door of the old library. Dust motes dance in the slanted, toxic-yellow light. Your pack is light, your canteen near empty. But you’re not here for canned goods or bottled water.

You’re here for seeds.

You find them in a crumbling card catalog, labeled in faded ink: *Phaseolus vulgaris, Lactuca sativa, Solanum lycopersicum*. Beans, lettuce, tomatoes. You pocket them, a criminal in a world of scavengers. Outside, the wind howls like a grieving beast, carrying the scent of ozone and decay.

You head for the rooftop, where the last patch of contaminated soil bakes under a bruised sky. You dig with your hands, the earth cold and strange. As you plant the first bean, a shadow detaches from a nearby air duct—a figure wrapped in scavenged plastic, eyes sharp with hunger and suspicion.

“What’re you doing?” they rasp.

You look at the seed in your palm, then at the endless, broken horizon. “Starting over,” you say.

The figure watches, silent, as you cover the seed with dirt. For a moment, the dream holds its breath. Then, a single, impossible drop of rain falls from the ash-gray sky, striking the soil where the bean lies buried.

You smile, a tight, tired thing. The real work has just begun.
