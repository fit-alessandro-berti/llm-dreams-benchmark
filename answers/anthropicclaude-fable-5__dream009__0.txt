The morning fog hangs low over a cluster of thatched-roof cottages. Smoke curls from chimneys. Somewhere, a rooster crows, and you realize the clothes you fell asleep in have transformed into rough-spun wool that itches against your skin.

A woman carrying a basket of bread stops and stares at you. She speaks—the words are almost familiar, like English heard underwater, vowels stretched and bent in ways your ears can't quite catch. Middle English, maybe. You gesture, point to your mouth, mime hunger. She laughs, not unkindly, and tears off a piece of bread. It's dense and sour and somehow the most vivid thing you've ever tasted, which strikes you as strange, because some distant part of you whispers: *you've never tasted anything in a dream before.*

You follow the dirt road into the village. There's a blacksmith hammering at a forge, and you find yourself drawn to the rhythm of it—clang, clang, clang—steady as a heartbeat. The smith looks up at you with eyes that seem oddly familiar, like your grandfather's, or your old teacher's, faces blending the way they do in dreams.

"You're not from here," he says, and somehow you understand him perfectly now. The dream has stopped bothering with the language barrier.

"No," you admit. "I don't know how I got here."

He nods as if this is the most ordinary thing in the world. "Then you'd best decide what you'll do with the time you've got." He hands you the hammer. It's heavier than you expect.

As you strike the glowing iron, sparks scatter like stars, and you notice the sky has gone strange—too purple at the edges, the sun hanging in the same place it's been all day. The village children gather to watch you work, and one of them, a girl with serious eyes, tugs your sleeve.

"You know this isn't real," she says quietly. "But you can stay a little longer, if you want."

The forge fire flickers. Somewhere, very far away, you can almost hear an alarm clock beginning to ring—but the iron is glowing, the hammer is in your hand, and the morning fog is finally lifting to reveal hills greener than any green you've ever seen.

You decide the alarm can wait.

*Clang. Clang. Clang.*
