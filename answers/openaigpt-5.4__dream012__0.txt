One by one, the crew moves with the kind of trust that only exists in dreams—swift, certain, almost like they already know what you are about to say. The youngest sailor, face pale with fear, meets your eyes, and you nod as if courage can be passed from one person to another. Somehow, it can.

Then, through the wall of rain, you see it: not land, not another ship, but a great golden light beneath the waves, moving with you. It pulses like a sleeping heart under the sea. Each time the ship rises on a swell, the glow comes nearer to the surface, outlining something enormous below—ribs of light, a curved spine, the shape of a creature or a city drowned long ago.

The storm does not feel natural anymore. It feels summoned.

You turn the wheel, though you do not remember deciding to. The compass spins uselessly, its needle trembling like it is afraid. Behind you, no one speaks. Even the thunder seems to pause, waiting.

The golden shape rises.

At first it is only a crown of luminous fins breaking the surface, then a vast head, scaled in shimmering patterns that look strangely like maps. Water pours from it in silver sheets. Its eyes are ancient and calm, larger than lanterns, and when they rest on you, the terror in your chest changes into recognition.

You have seen this before.

Not here, not at sea, but in another dream, years ago—standing as a child beside a dark window while rain ran down the glass, hearing a voice say, “When the storm finds you, follow what shines.”

The creature circles your ship without touching it, and where it passes, the waves flatten into black mirrors. In those mirrors you do not see the deck or the mast or your own rain-soaked face. You see impossible things: a house with all its lights on and no doors, a field of tall white grass bending under a moon too close to the earth, a table set for dinner in an empty room underwater.

The crew stares into the mirrors as if each person sees something different.

Then the creature opens its mouth—not to roar, but to sing.

The song is low and deep, older than language, and the storm begins to loosen around its notes. Wind unknots itself. Thunder rolls backward into the clouds. The sea, a moment ago wild enough to swallow the world, starts to breathe in long, slow waves. Your ship is no longer fighting the water. It is being carried.

Straight toward the horizon, where the clouds are splitting apart.

There, beyond the storm, stands an island that cannot exist. Its cliffs shine like polished obsidian, and from their tops grow trees with silver leaves ringing softly in the rain. At the island’s center rises a tower made of something pale and smooth, like bone or moonlight. At its highest window burns the same golden light as the one beneath the waves.

You know, with the unreasonable certainty that only dreams allow, that someone is waiting for you there.

So you do not order the ship away. You let the creature guide you.

Your crew no longer looks afraid. They move quietly, reverently, as though they too remember something they had forgotten. The youngest sailor begins to laugh—not because anything is funny, but because the laughter is the only sound big enough for wonder.

As the prow cuts into the calmer water, the sky above begins to clear. Stars appear between torn clouds, far too many stars, crowded and bright and watching. The creature dives once, sending a ring of gold across the sea, and disappears beneath the ship.

You sail on.

Closer now, you can see steps carved into the black cliff, leading from the shore up toward the tower. And at the top of those steps stands a single figure in white, motionless in the golden windowlight, waiting as if they have been waiting for a very long time—for the storm, for the ship, for you.

And just before the keel touches land, just before the crew can lower anchor, just before the figure lifts a hand in greeting, you wake with salt on your lips and the sound of that ancient song still echoing in your chest.
