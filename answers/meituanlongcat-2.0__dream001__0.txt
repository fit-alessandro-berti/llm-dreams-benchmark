The cobblestones beneath your feet are slick with a recent rain, reflecting a sky the color of tarnished silver. The buildings lean in, not threateningly, but curiously, their windows like half-lidded eyes. You check your pocket—a train ticket stub, a smooth blue stone, a key that feels too light. No map. No phone. Just the pressing weight of the hour.

You start to walk, choosing a direction at random. The streets are a labyrinth of impossible architecture: a staircase spiraling up the side of a bakery, a bridge made of woven light spanning a canal that flows with what looks like liquid clockwork. The air smells of ozone and baking bread.

Panic begins to prickle at the edges of your mind. You stop a figure cloaked in shifting newspaper headlines. "The meeting?" they whisper, their voice the rustle of pages. "It's where the forgotten things gather." They point down an alley that wasn't there a moment before.

The alley is narrow, lined with doors of every size and material—one is a slab of ice, another a curtain of beads, a third a simple, weathered oak. From behind the oak door, you hear a low murmur of voices and the clink of a teacup. This feels right.

You knock. The door swings open not to a room, but to a vast, circular library under a dome of swirling galaxies. In the center, around a table of polished obsidian, sit figures. They are you, but not you. One is you at seven, clutching a toy rocket. Another is you at seventeen, head bent over a textbook. A third is you, older, with kind eyes and hands that have built things.

"Ah," says the older you, gesturing to an empty chair. "You're right on time. We've been waiting."

The meeting, you realize with a start that melts the anxiety away, is with yourself. The forgotten ambitions, the past joys, the future hopes—they are all here, patient and present. The agenda is not a business deal, but a reconciliation. A remembering.

You sit. The child-you slides the blue stone from your pocket across the table. "You dropped this," they say. "It's for courage."

The clock on the wall has no hands, only a soft, pulsing glow. Time here is not a thief, but a tide. The hour you feared losing stretches out, generous and deep, filled with the quiet conversation of your own soul.

You wake not with a jolt, but with a slow, peaceful unfolding, the dream's lesson settling into your bones: the most important meetings are often the ones you have with the selves you've left behind. And you are, it seems, exactly where you need to be.
