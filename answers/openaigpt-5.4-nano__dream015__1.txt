…keep going anyway.

At first it’s just instinct—your body already tuned to survival: listen for footsteps, move low, breathe quietly, scan the broken street for anything that moves that shouldn’t. The sky is the color of old ash. Wind drags sand across the pavement in slow, choking sheets, and every sound feels too loud, like the world is waiting for you to make a mistake.

You could take the easy path. There’s a collapse of concrete ahead where someone has already stripped the place of useful things—no glass, no metal worth carrying, no warm pockets of safety. But you don’t go that way.

Instead you turn toward the half-buried building on the edge of town where the walls still hold patches of paint: a faded symbol, barely visible under grime. You remember it from stories—if not from the real world, then from the places you’ve dreamed before. It’s the kind of symbol people draw when they want to believe someone will come back.

Inside, the air smells like damp paper and rust. Your footsteps leave faint echoes that die quickly. There’s a stairwell choked with rubble, so you climb through a broken window instead, careful with your hands. Dust coats your palms as you crawl, and your knuckles scrape something brittle—old plaster, maybe, or bone that has dried into silence. You don’t look too long.

Then you find it: a locker half-swallowed by debris, its door jammed but not destroyed. The latch is still intact. You pry it free with a wrench you salvaged earlier, and the metal squeals like an animal waking up.

A rush of cold hits your face. Not temperature—purpose. The locker holds three things you can use immediately: sealed filter cartridges, a roll of waterproof tape, and a small notebook wrapped in plastic. The plastic is cloudy, but the notebook’s spine is uncracked.

You open it.

The pages aren’t a map or a manifesto. They’re schedules. Names. Coordinates. Small routines—weekly water checks, how to sanitize a canteen using ash and heat, what radio frequency to try when the storm is worst. Someone, at some point, decided the future wasn’t just for surviving—it was for staying ready.

At the bottom is a final line, written with ink that has faded but not surrendered:

*If you’re reading this, you’re not done. Find the others.*

Your stomach tightens, because the “others” are always the danger in this world. People who hoard. People who pretend to help. People who shoot first because fear needs somewhere to live.

But you don’t throw the notebook away.

You do the harder thing: you prepare.

You take the cartridges and tape, ration what you can, and then—because the notebook says you should—set up a simple signal system in the building’s upper room. You don’t have power. You don’t have a proper transmitter. So you build something from the wreckage: reflective strips angled toward an empty stretch of sky, a careful arrangement of metal that will flash when the sun hits it just so. You thread wire through a salvaged hinge. You practice the motion until it becomes automatic, like breathing.

Then you climb back out and walk, notebook held tight against your ribs.

The streets are quieter than they should be, but you learn quickly in this dream—silence isn’t the absence of danger. It’s the time between it.

A shape moves at the far end of an alley. Your hand goes to whatever you’ve been carrying—knife, improvised spear, anything sharp enough to remind the world you’re not defenseless. The shape pauses.

It’s not a person.

It’s an animal—thin, half-feral, eyes bright in the haze. It watches you like you might be food or a threat. It takes a step forward, then another, and you realize it’s moving not toward you but away from something behind you.

You turn slowly.

A hunter drone—one of the old kind, the kind that shouldn’t still work—sits on the corner of a roof, its sensors scoured but stubborn. A red light blinks, searching.

So that’s where the silence came from. The machine isn’t hunting you yet because it thinks you’re not worth the energy.

You could run.

Instead you whistle—softly at first, then louder, using the tone you remember from the notebook’s safety section. A series of notes that mimic a harmless animal call, something to draw the drone’s attention away from the ground.

It works. The red light swivels. The drone whirs, recalibrating, and the animal darts into the alley, vanishing.

Your heart hammers. You feel it in your throat, your wrists, your teeth. But you keep moving, because you made a promise when you opened that notebook: you’re not done.

You follow the coordinates written inside. They lead you to an overpass where the concrete has cracked like dried riverbeds, and under it—between two columns—there’s a door you almost miss. Not locked. Not broken. Waiting.

A rusted sign hangs crooked above it: an old name, letters missing, a partial symbol that matches the one on the building.

Inside, you expect a trap.

What you find instead is a room full of evidence that people were here—long enough to learn what works and long enough to leave notes for strangers like you. There’s a generator base, scavenged parts arranged carefully, a shelf of jars with labels that say things like *water + charcoal* and *bandage wash* and *candles (safe)*. A small radio sits on a table, wrapped in cloth like someone tucked it in for sleep.

On the floor, someone has left a message scratched into the concrete with a nail:

*You will come. Don’t let them tell you your job is only to live.*

You swallow hard. “Who’s ‘them’?” you whisper, and the sound of your own voice surprises you, like it belongs to someone braver.

You press the radio’s cloth away. Dust rises in a slow spiral. The screen flickers when you connect the salvaged power core you found earlier—barely. The radio’s speaker crackles, and for a moment it’s only static, the hiss of a world that refuses to end quietly.

Then a voice breaks through. Not clear at first—warped by distance, interrupted by interference.

“…anyone…answer…if you’re there, do not…repeat…do not rely on…just survive—”

The message cuts out.

Your hands shake as you write down the frequency and the time window the notebook suggests you try again. You realize the dream is doing something you didn’t ask for but desperately needed: it’s giving you not only a place to rest, but a role.

So you decide.

You’re going to be the kind of person the notes were written for.

You set up the radio properly, reinforce the connections with tape, and then you do what survival alone never covers: you reach outward. You take a stack of blank paper from a drawer, and on each sheet you write the same message in thick, careful letters:

*We’re not done. If you can hear this, meet at—*

You pause, because you don’t have an exact location yet. The city is ruins and memory.

You choose anyway. You name the overpass. You draw the symbol. You include the coordinates as best you can.

Then you make a second decision—one that feels dangerous in your bones.

You leave supplies behind.

Not all of them. Enough to say: someone can come and not die immediately. Enough to prove you understand scarcity without worshiping it.

You walk out into the ash-bright day with the notebook open in your hands, your signal mirrors covered and ready, and the taste of static still on your tongue.

The drone’s search has shifted. The animal is gone. The world is still cruel.

But the cruel part has changed shape.

Now you’re not just surviving in a wrecked world—you’re building a thread through the wreckage, a way for someone else to find you. A way for “the future” to be more than a word people used to pretend meant something.

You start walking toward the next coordinate, and as you move, you hear it—faint at first, like a distant birdcall cutting through wind.

Another whistle response.

Not yours.

Not random.

An answer.

Your mouth goes dry. You stop, listen again, and then—without thinking, without bargaining with fear—you follow the sound deeper into the ruins.

Because in this dream, once you choose to reach outward, the world has to respond.
