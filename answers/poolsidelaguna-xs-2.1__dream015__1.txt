...decide to take one last risk: to seek out a legend of a hidden underground bunker, said to hold the last cache of clean water and medical supplies before the collapse. Armed with a rusted crowbar salvaged from a graveyard of cars and a map scribbled on a torn notebook, you trek toward the skeletal remains of a city, where collapsed skyscrapers pierce the smog-choked sky like broken teeth.  

As you navigate the ruins, you encounter the *scars* of the apocalypse—flickering neon signs half-buried in ash, mannequins frozen in mid-scream, and the occasional flicker of movement in the shadows. You find a group of scavengers, gaunt and armed, who demand your supplies in exchange for safe passage. After a tense standoff, you barter your last can of beans for a map fragment, learning that the bunker lies beneath the old subway tunnels.  

But the tunnels are a maze of rusted tracks and flooded tunnels, and you’re not alone. A horde of *something*—hybrids of man and machine, their bodies fused with broken machinery—lurks in the darkness, drawn to the sound of your heartbeat. You fend them off with your crowbar and a flare, but not before one of your companions is dragged into the gloom.  

Finally, you reach the bunker’s entrance: a rusted door sealed with a biometric lock. The notebook’s scribbles reveal the code is a sequence of heartbeats—*your* heartbeat. As the door creaks open, you descend into a space untouched by time. There, glowing softly in the dark, are the supplies—and a message scrawled on the wall in a child’s handwriting: *“Forgive me. I’m sorry. It wasn’t supposed to end like this.”*  

The dream shifts. You’re no longer alone. A figure emerges from the shadows—it’s yourself, but younger, wearing a gas mask and a badge labeled “Dr. Elara Voss.” The truth crashes over you: you *are* the one who triggered the collapse. The bunker isn’t a sanctuary. It’s a prison. And the child’s handwriting? It’s your own, from before the world ended.  

The dream fractures, and you wake up—sweating, heart racing—in a real bed, the morning sun filtering through cracked blinds. But in your pocket, you find a small, rusted key, warm to the touch.  

The nightmare wasn’t over. It was just beginning.
