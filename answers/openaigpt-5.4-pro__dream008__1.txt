Your boss slides a folder across your desk. It is impossibly heavy. When you open it, instead of papers, there’s just a single blinking cursor in the middle of a blank page, waiting for you like it expects an apology.

Around you, the office changes in little wrong ways. The fluorescent lights hum louder, then begin ticking like clocks. The carpet ripples under your chair as if something enormous is breathing beneath the building. Your coworkers type furiously, but when you glance at their screens, they’re all just watching you.

“Go on,” your boss says, smiling too calmly. “You’re already behind.”

You start typing.

At first, the words come easily. Bullet points. Timelines. Charts. But every sentence you finish unravels itself and crawls off the page like a line of ants. The cursor keeps blinking, patient and judgmental. You try to stand up and ask for help, but your chair has grown roots into the floor.

Then the printer wakes up.

It coughs once, violently, and begins spitting out page after page after page. They pour onto the floor, over your shoes, across the office like snowdrifts. You pick one up. It’s your project, already finished—but every page is written in a language you almost understand. Your signature is at the bottom of each one.

Your boss is suddenly standing right beside you.

“This isn’t final,” they whisper.

You look back at the monitor, and now there’s a countdown clock in the corner of the screen: **00:00:59**.

You type faster. The keys soften under your fingers. They are no longer keys but piano keys, then teeth, then little white stair steps descending into darkness. The monitor stretches upward until it becomes a window. Beyond it, the sky is the exact color of copier paper.

“Fifty seconds,” says your boss, though their voice now comes from the office intercom, from your coffee mug, from the ceiling tiles.

You finally manage to open the folder all the way, and inside you find not instructions, but a tiny version of yourself at another desk, being handed the same folder by another boss. Tiny-you looks up, exhausted, and mouths, *Don’t start.*

But you already have.

The countdown hits **00:00:10**. Your coworkers stand, one by one, and begin applauding, slowly, as if you’ve done something terrible but impressive. The walls slide farther away. Your inbox multiplies into hundreds of inboxes. The phone rings. Every phone rings.

You take a deep breath and do the only thing that makes sense.

You hit **Send**.

Everything stops.

The lights go out. The applause cuts off. The printer falls silent. In the darkness, your boss says, in a voice that sounds almost kind:

“Great. Now do the second part.”

And that’s when you wake up—already tired, with the terrible certainty that somewhere, somehow, the deadline is still running.
