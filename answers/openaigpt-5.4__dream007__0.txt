…I decide to leave before I say something I can’t take back.

The room feels too small, like the walls are leaning in to listen. My friend is still talking—hurt, angry, desperate—but the words start to stretch and blur, as if they’re being pulled through water. I grab my coat from the back of the chair, though I don’t remember wearing one, and open the door.

Outside, it is night, but not a normal night. The street is lit by a violet dusk that never ends. The pavement shines like it has just rained, reflecting windows that don’t belong to any houses I know. I start walking fast, then faster, as if I’m trying to outrun the feeling in my chest.

Behind me, I hear my friend call my name.

I stop.

When I turn around, the street has changed. The house is gone. The whole neighborhood is gone. In its place is a train platform suspended over a black ocean. The waves below move without sound. My friend stands at the far end of the platform, impossibly far and yet close enough for me to see their face clearly. They don’t look angry anymore. They look sad, in the way people do when they’ve already accepted losing something.

Between us, a train slides in. It has no lights inside, only rows of empty seats and dark windows reflecting stars that aren’t in the sky. The doors open beside me with a soft sigh.

A sign overhead flickers:

LAST CHANCE

I know, in the strange certain way dreams make you know things, that if I get on the train, I will keep going and never have to finish the argument. I will never have to apologize, never have to explain, never have to hear what they were trying to tell me.

But I also know that if the doors close while we’re standing apart, something between us will become unreachable forever.

My friend raises a hand, not to wave, but like they’re asking me to wait.

So I do the hardest thing in the dream: I walk away from the open train and toward them.

The platform lengthens with every step. The ocean below begins reflecting memories instead of water—us laughing in a grocery store aisle, sitting on a rooftop eating takeout, the first time they told me a secret in a voice so quiet I had to lean in to hear it. Every memory trembles as if one wrong word could shatter it.

Halfway there, I realize I’m crying. Not the dramatic kind, just tears appearing like the dream had decided they belonged on my face before I understood why.

When I finally reach them, I try to speak, but my mouth fills with paper—thin scraps covered in all the things I meant and never said. I spit them into my hands. The pieces unfold by themselves:

I was scared.
I wanted to be right more than honest.
I thought if I left first, I wouldn’t be left.
I know I hurt you.

My friend reads them without touching them. The wind lifts the scraps from my palms and sends them out over the dark water, where they glow for a moment like lanterns.

Then my friend says, very softly, “That’s all I wanted you to say.”

The train behind us screams down the tracks and disappears into the dark without us.

For a second, everything goes still. The ocean becomes glass. The stars beneath it brighten. My friend steps closer, and I think maybe this is where the dream forgives us, where we hug and wake up relieved.

But dreams are never that simple.

They look at me with immense tenderness and say, “You can say it here. You still have to say it when you wake up.”

Then the platform begins to crack.

Light pours through the fractures, white and morning-bright. The wind rises. The ocean drains away into the sky. My friend reaches for my hand, and just before our fingers touch, the whole dream folds in on itself like a letter being closed.

And I wake up with my heart pounding, the argument still unfinished, but the apology finally clear in my mouth.
