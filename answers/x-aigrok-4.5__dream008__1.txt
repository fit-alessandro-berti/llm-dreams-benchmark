You freeze for a split second, the weight of that impossible deadline pressing down like a physical force. Your boss is standing right there, arms crossed, eyes locked on you, waiting for you to open the first file, type the first line, do *something*.  

You sit down at your desk. The keyboard feels wrong—sticky, oversized, the keys rearranging themselves into unfamiliar symbols every time you blink. You start typing anyway. The project brief floats in front of you in glowing red letters that keep changing: “Deliver a fully functional AI that can predict the future by tomorrow morning. No bugs. Presentation-ready. Also include snacks.”  

Your fingers fly across the keys and code appears, but it’s not code you recognize. It’s nursery rhymes, grocery lists, and fragments of old conversations. Every time you hit “save,” the document rewrites itself into something worse: a labyrinth of nested folders that open into other offices, other deadlines, other bosses staring at you with the same expectant look.  

Someone taps your shoulder. You turn and it’s your boss again—except now there are three of them, identical, all speaking in perfect unison: “Why isn’t it done yet?”  

You try to explain that the requirements keep shifting, that the clock on the wall is spinning backward and forward at the same time, that your coffee cup has turned into a small hourglass with sand that pours upward. They don’t care. One of them hands you a stapler that is also a telephone. It rings. You answer. It’s still your boss, now somehow inside the stapler.  

“Progress report,” the stapler-boss demands.  

You open your mouth and somehow, impossibly, entire chapters of documentation fall out. The pages flutter around the office like birds, forming temporary shapes—a bridge you once crossed, a childhood bedroom, a blank white void.  

Outside the windows the world has gone soft and watercolor. Colleagues walk past at strange angles, their faces melting into complete strangers and then back again. The fluorescent lights hum a song you almost remember.  

Your hands keep moving of their own accord, finishing slides that dissolve the moment you look away. The deadline clock on the wall now shows both infinite time and zero time simultaneously. Your heart is racing, yet part of you knows—deep in the dream logic—that if you just keep typing hard enough, keep believing hard enough, something will click into place.  

Suddenly the three bosses collapse into one again. This version smiles. It is not a comforting smile.  

“Almost there,” it says. “Just one more impossible thing.”  

And then, without warning, you are standing on the roof of the building. The documents you somehow finished are scattered like confetti in the sky. Your boss is gone. The deadline is gone. Only the quiet wind remains, and the strange realization that you were never late at all.  

You wake up.
