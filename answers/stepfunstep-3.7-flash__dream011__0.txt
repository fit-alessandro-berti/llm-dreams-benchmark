As you sit down to start the exam, you notice the questions are not printed in the neat, official font you expected, but scrawled in the smudged, loopy third-grade handwriting you haven’t used in 20 years, scrawled on the back of crumpled permission slips for field trips you don’t remember going on.

The first question asks: What flavor was the popsicle you shared with the kid who sat next to you on the bus the day you got your first F? You can taste artificial blue raspberry on your tongue, can still feel the stickiness of it on your palm, but you can’t remember the kid’s name. The second asks: What was the last thing your grandmother said to you before you forgot to call her back for three months? You can hear her soft, crackly voice over the phone line, saying “I saved you a jar of your favorite jam,” but you can’t remember if you ever went to pick it up.

The room is your old elementary school gym, the one with scuffed maple floors and basketball hoops too high for anyone under 12. The proctor is Mrs. Hale, your third grade teacher, but she’s wearing a blazer covered in embroidery that reads MIDDLE MANAGEMENT, KNOW YOUR PLACE, and she holds a stack of folders stamped with the name of the career you’ve spent years cramming for: the one you scribbled on your college application, the one your parents asked about at every holiday dinner, the one you’ve missed birthdays and skipped sunsets to study for. The person next to you is wearing your high school graduation gown, tassel still on the left side, staring at their own exam with the same confused panic you feel, like they’re you from a future you haven’t lived yet.

You flip the page, and the questions get sharper, less nostalgic, more cutting. Who did you let down last week when you chose to study instead of going to their art show? What was the lyric of the song you sang in the shower at 16 that made you decide you wanted this career in the first place? If you could trade your perfect exam score for one more hour with the person who taught you to love this work, would you?

Your pencil hovers over the first question when you look up, and the gym is gone. You’re in a sunlit, empty room with two sheets of paper on the table in front of you: one is the exam with the smudged, personal questions, the other is the pristine resume you spent months perfecting, every bullet point polished to match the job description you’ve been obsessing over for years.

A voice—no one’s voice, just the sound of your own thoughts, but deeper, warmer—says, “You can fill out the resume, and you’ll get the career you thought you wanted. Or you can answer the exam, and you’ll get the career that’s already yours, the one you’ve been building without noticing.”

The clock on the wall, which was a digital school clock a second ago, is now a cuckoo clock, the bird popping out holding a tiny, frayed version of the stuffed rabbit you lost when you were 7. You remember the kid on the bus now: his name was Leo, he had a bright orange cast on his arm from falling off his bike, and he gave you the last bite of his popsicle even though he’d been looking forward to it all day. You write that down, and the ink glows gold.

You wake up before you finish the rest of the exam, and for half a second, you can still taste blue raspberry popsicle. Your phone is buzzing on your nightstand, a text from your mom: I saved you a jar of strawberry jam, the one you like, from the farmers market. You stare at it for a long time before you type back, I’ll come pick it up tomorrow. For the rest of the day, every small choice feels a little heavier, a little more like it counts.
