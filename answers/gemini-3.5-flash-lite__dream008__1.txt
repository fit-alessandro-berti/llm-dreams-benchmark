Your boss—who has no face, just a shifting silhouette behind a polished oak desk—taps a single, long finger against the paperwork. The sound echoes like a gavel in an empty cathedral. 

*“Five minutes,”* they say, though no sound leaves their mouth; the words simply appear inside your skull, heavy and cold. *“We need the universe mapped, bound, and indexed by five minutes.”*

You look down at the desk. There is no computer, no keyboard—only a stack of blank, glowing white pages and a single, feather-light quill that feels impossibly heavy to lift. As you pick it up, the ink doesn't flow from a well; instead, the tip bleeds dark, swirling galaxies that immediately begin to evaporate into the recycled office air.

Panic threatens to swallow you, the classic dream-paralysis creeping up your spine, making your limbs feel like lead. You try to speak, to protest the absurdity of the timeline, but your jaw feels locked. 

Instead of fighting it, you take a deep breath. In the fluid logic of a dream, the rules don't apply. You don't need a computer. You don't need five minutes. 

With a sudden surge of surreal confidence, you don't write on the paper at all. You press the glowing quill directly against your own forehead. 

The entire office dissolves into a cascade of neon numbers and floating shapes. The boss’s desk crumbles into autumn leaves blowing away in an indoor gale. You are no longer sitting in a cubicle; you are floating in a boundless, starlit void, holding the entire project in the palm of your hand, already finished.

You smile in the dark, and for a brief moment, you realize you are dreaming—and you decide to fly.
