The wind arrives in a sudden, cold gasp. It sweeps across the forest floor, flipping the ferns so their silvery underbellies flash like coins. I try to hurry my pace, but in the strange logic of the dream, my feet feel light, almost weightless, barely brushing the damp moss. The trees stretch upward, their trunks twisting into impossible arches that form a living, wooden cathedral over my head.

Then, the lightning comes—not as a harsh flash, but as ripples of soft, electric blue that illuminate the veins in the leaves and light up the sky behind the dark clouds. There is no thunder, only the sound of a deep, resonant cello string vibrating somewhere beneath the earth.

Ahead, in a small clearing I hadn't noticed before, stands a door. It has no walls attached to it, just a weathered oak frame standing alone among the roots. Beneath its brass handle, a soft, warm light spills onto the grass. 

As the first heavy, impossibly warm drop of rain hits my hand, I reach for the handle. The moment the brass turns, the howling wind abruptly dies, replaced by a profound, breathless stillness. I step through the frame, and the forest, the storm, and the dark sky dissolve around me like ink in water, pulling me gently toward the edge of waking up.
