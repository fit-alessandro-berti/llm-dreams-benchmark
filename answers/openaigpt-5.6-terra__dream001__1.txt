You start to walk, certain that if you keep moving the city will explain itself.

The streets are impossibly narrow, then suddenly wide enough to hold an ocean. Every building has your name written somewhere on it—on brass mailboxes, in neon signs, scratched into elevator buttons—but none of the doors will open. You ask a passerby for directions. They smile apologetically and hand you a small brass key.

“For the meeting,” they say.

The key is warm in your palm. It begins to tug you through the city.

You follow it past a train station where every departing train is labeled *YESTERDAY*, across a bridge made of old calendars, and into a library with no books—only clocks ticking on endless shelves. At the center is a single elevator, its doors already open.

Inside, there are no buttons except one marked with your childhood address.

You press it.

The elevator descends, though the numbers above the door count upward: 12, 47, 103, 1000. The lights flicker. For a moment, you see yourself reflected in the polished walls—not as you are now, but older, calmer, wearing clothes you do not own.

The elevator stops.

The doors open onto a sunlit room with one long table. Everyone you have ever been sits around it: the frightened child, the tired student, the person you almost became, the version of you who took the other road.

An empty chair waits at the head of the table.

Your older reflection looks up and says, “You’re late.”

You apologize, but they all begin to laugh—not cruelly, but with relief.

Then the brass key melts in your hand, turning into a small paper note.

It reads:

**The meeting was never somewhere you had to find.  
It was the moment you finally arrived.**

And just as you sit down, your alarm begins to ring somewhere beneath the table.
