...stare at the blank document on your screen. The cursor blinks, a steady, mocking heartbeat. Your boss stands behind you, but their features have softened, their tie beginning to drift upward like a slow river against an unseen current. The deadline on the whiteboard isn't a date anymore, but a countdown of floating, glowing numbers that keep resetting to *00:01:00* and melting.

You try to type, but your keyboard is made of smooth river stones. You try to speak, but your voice becomes the hum of fluorescent lights. The office windows show not a city, but a vast, quiet desert under a twin-moon sky. A stack of reference books grows beside you, their spines reading in languages you almost recognize but can't quite place.

Then, you notice it. The *impossible* part of the task isn't the deadline. It's the subject. The project requires you to document a memory you've already forgotten, to design a blueprint for a bridge between two thoughts that have never met.

You stop fighting the dream logic. You close your eyes, pick up a pen from your desk that now feels like polished bone, and begin to draw not a report, but a map. You map the feeling of the countdown, the color of your boss's impatience (it's a sharp silver), the texture of the pressure (it's cold and smooth like obsidian).

You slide the map toward your boss. They look down, and their drifting tie settles. They don't speak, but you hear a voice in your head, clear and calm: *Ah. You understood. The deadline wasn't for the project. It was for you to stop trying to finish it.*

The numbers above you dissolve into dust. The twin moons in the window align into a single sun. You wake up at your actual desk, the morning light gentle, a single, perfectly ordinary coffee cooling in your mug. You remember the dream, and the strange certainty it leaves behind: that some tasks are not meant to be completed, but merely witnessed, and in the witnessing, transformed.
