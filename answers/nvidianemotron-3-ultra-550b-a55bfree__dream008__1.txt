...look down at the project brief. It's written on a napkin from a coffee shop you've never visited, in handwriting that keeps changing—first your mother's, then your own from third grade, then something that looks like tree roots growing into words.

*The deadline is yesterday,* the napkin says. *The deliverable is forgiveness.*

Your boss doesn't blink. His face is slightly out of focus, like a photograph developing in slow chemicals. When he speaks, the sound comes from the fluorescent lights above you.

"First draft by lunch. Final by the time you learn to breathe underwater."

You nod. This seems reasonable. You've always been good at impossible things. Once, in a dream you don't remember having, you built a staircase from the sound of your father's laugh. It reached the second floor of a house that burned down before you were born.

Your computer monitor displays a spreadsheet where the cells are tiny doors. Each one opens onto a different memory: the time you lied about breaking the vase. The time you didn't say goodbye. The time you stood in the grocery store aisle for twenty minutes staring at tomato soup because choosing felt like betrayal.

You start typing. Your fingers are made of smoke. The keys are cold and taste like pennies.

The first deliverable slides out of the printer on a sheet of wet bark. Your boss takes it without looking. He folds it into a paper crane. It flies into the drop ceiling and you hear it fluttering above the tiles, trapped with all the other things that were never finished.

"Next," says the light-fixtures-voice.

You realize you're not wearing shoes. Your feet have grown into the carpet. Gray industrial fibers threading between your toes, rooting. The deadline is approaching—you can feel it in your molars, a pressure like wisdom teeth coming in sideways.

A coworker walks past. She has your grandmother's hands. She drops a file on your desk. It's heavy. It contains every "I'm sorry" you've ever swallowed.

*Due immediately,* the sticky note reads. *No extensions.*

You open your mouth to ask a question, but a moth flies out. Then another. Your throat is full of wings.

The clock on the wall has no numbers. Just a single hand spinning faster and faster, and with each rotation the office grows smaller—walls breathing in, ceiling descending, your boss's face pressing close until his pores are craters, his eyes two black holes where the napkin-brief burns endlessly in orbit.

*You are the project,* the dream whispers. *You are the deadline. You are the only one who can forgive the napkin for burning.*

You wake up typing on your pillow. Your fingers hurt. The room is dark. 3:47 AM.

But you can still taste the pennies.
