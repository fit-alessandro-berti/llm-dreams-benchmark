I realize then that these aren't ordinary people. One of them is wearing a coat made entirely of sewn-together maps that constantly shift and redraw themselves; another is holding a lantern containing a tiny, glowing, gently spinning galaxy instead of a flame. 

"We know the shortcut," a woman with hair like spun silver says, though her lips don't move. I hear her voice directly in my chest. 

Instead of leading me down the crowded sidewalks, they guide me toward a brick alleyway. As we step into it, the concrete beneath our feet softy gives way to a path of luminescent blue moss. The towering skyscrapers above us begin to bend and lean inward, intertwining like ancient trees to form a beautiful, starlit canopy. 

We walk, but it feels more like gliding. With every step, a heavy weight lifts off my shoulders. The strangers playfully point out things I hadn't noticed: a flock of origami birds nesting in a traffic light, a street musician whose saxophone is blowing out iridescent bubbles instead of notes. 

Finally, we arrive at a solitary, intricately carved wooden door standing freely right in the middle of a cobblestone square. There are no walls attached to it. 

The stranger with the lantern turns to me and smiles softly. "This is your stop."

I turn the brass knob, which feels comfortably warm in my hand. When I pull the door open, I'm not looking at another street. Instead, it opens up into my childhood bedroom, bathed in the golden light of a late afternoon sun. It smells faintly of rain and cinnamon. 

I turn back to thank them, but the kind strangers are already scattering into the air like dandelion seeds on a gentle breeze. Feeling perfectly safe, perfectly found, I step across the threshold into the warm light, and the dream slowly fades into a deep, peaceful sleep.
