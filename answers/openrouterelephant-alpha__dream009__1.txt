...adapt. The air is thick with the scent of damp earth and unfamiliar blossoms. The sky is a bruised purple, hanging low, and the sun—if it is a sun—is a dull, orange coin. My modern clothes feel like a costume, stiff and impractical.

I find a village, not through grand architecture, but through the faint, persistent smell of woodsmoke. The people there are wary, their faces carved from the same weathered stone as the huts. They speak in a language that is a melodic hum, rolling off the tongue in ways my tongue refuses to follow. Communication becomes a dance of hands, of shared tasks, of mimicking the planting of seeds in the stubborn ground.

I need to learn their ways, their fears, and their hopes. I need to find a purpose here, or I will fade into the background, a ghost from another world forgotten by time. I begin by tending their crops, my clumsy hands soon learning the rhythm of the soil. In their eyes, I am not a god, nor a demon, just a stranger with useful hands. And in this forgotten era, that is enough. To survive, I must become more than a dream—I must become a memory they choose to keep.
