You begin to open your mouth, but no sound comes out. Instead, ribbons of silver light spill from your lips, swirling into the air like smoke, drifting toward the faces in the darkness. The spotlight above dims to a gentle twilight, and the heavy curtains behind you dissolve into a waterfall of falling leaves.

The audience, previously distinct figures in suits and dresses, begins to blur at the edges. Their faces soften, turning into constellations against the darkening sky. You realize the microphone in your hand is no longer plastic, but a glowing crystal that pulses with a rhythm matching your own heartbeat. They don't cheer; they breathe in unison with you, their exhales lifting you inches off the stage until you're floating among them. There is no need for a topic, for they already know what you are thinking, for you are thinking them. And then, just as the words take the shape of birds and fly away, the floor beneath you cracks open into a pool of liquid moonlight, and you step into the water, no longer afraid of sinking, because the dream has told you that you are made of air now.
