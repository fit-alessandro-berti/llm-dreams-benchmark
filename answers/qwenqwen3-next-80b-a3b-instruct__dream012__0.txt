You are the captain of a ship in the middle of a turbulent sea. Your crew looks to you for guidance as the storm worsens, and you—though your body trembles with exhaustion and your voice is hoarse from shouting orders—stand tall at the helm, eyes locked not on the crashing waves, but on the horizon where the sky has begun to *glow*.

Not with lightning. Not with dawn.

With *letters*.

Floating in the ether, immense and luminous, burning against the black clouds like celestial script:  
**“TURN WHERE THE WIND SINGS YOUR NAME.”**

No one else sees it. Not the first mate, bleeding from a gash on his temple, clutching the wheel. Not the youngest sailor, curled beneath a tarp, sobbing into his soaked journal. Not even the old mermaid-carved figurehead, whose glass eyes have long since cracked—but now, as the ship lurches sideways, they *blink*.

You know, with a certainty that bypasses reason, that you’ve heard that wind before.

It’s the same wind that blew through your childhood window the night your mother whispered, *“You were born to follow the quiet things.”*

You turn the wheel—not toward the storm’s eye, not toward safety, but into the heart of the hurricane’s dissonant roar, where the wind howls in unfamiliar tongues.

And then, impossibly…

The storm *listens*.

The clouds part not to reveal stars—but a path of glowing kelp, pulsing like veins, stretching beneath the waves. Sea creatures rise not in fear, but in reverence: whales with bioluminescent spirals across their backs, dolphins wearing crowns of coral, a school of fish spelling out your true name in shimmering dots.

The ship glides—not driven by sails or engines, but by the rhythm of a song only you can hear.

Your crew, one by one, feel it too. They stop trembling. They step out of the rain as if walking through warm rain. They begin to sing—off-key, broken, beautiful.

And you realize—

This was never a storm trying to sink you.

It was a gate.

And you weren’t meant to survive it.

You were meant to remember.

The ship begins to dissolve—not into water, but into feathers. Into fireflies. Into the scent of honeysuckle and salt.

You turn to your crew, your voice soft as a lullaby:

*“We were never lost. We were returning.”*

And as the last of the ship fades, you open your eyes—

Not on a deck.

But in a cradle.

And your mother, younger than you’ve ever seen her, smiles down at you, her eyes glowing with the same light as the sky.

Outside the window, the storm still rages.

But inside, there is only peace.

And the whisper…

*“Welcome home, Captain.”*
