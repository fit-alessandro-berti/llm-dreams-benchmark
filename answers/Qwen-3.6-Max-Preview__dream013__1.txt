The air grows colder, smelling faintly of ozone and old paper. The puzzles change from abstract logic grids to deeply personal riddles—questions you’ve asked yourself in the quiet hours of the night, equations representing relationships you couldn’t balance, mazes within mazes that mirror your own indecisions.

With each solution, a piece of the wall dissolves into light, revealing a glimpse of something familiar yet distant: a childhood home, a forgotten conversation, a path not taken. But as you solve them, you feel a strange weight lifting from your shoulders, only to be replaced by a growing sense of urgency. The maze isn’t trying to trap you; it’s trying to *organize* you.

Suddenly, you reach the center. There is no exit door, no final lock to pick. Instead, there is a single, floating mirror. As you approach, you see not your reflection, but the version of yourself who has already solved every puzzle, standing on the other side, calm and whole. They smile, extend a hand, and whisper, *"You don't need to find the way out. You just need to remember how you got in."*

And then, you wake up.
