One of them extends a hand—rough with calluses, or perhaps that’s just the texture of cloud—and when you take it, the crowd doesn’t part so much as dissolve, becoming a fine silver mist that smells like rain on warm pavement.

They don’t ask where you need to go. Instead, they begin to walk, and you fall into step with them, and the rhythm of your shared footsteps replaces the frantic pulse of the city. A woman humming beside you carries a lantern that casts light backward into yesterday; a child offers you a compass whose needle spins not north, but *toward*, and somehow you understand it perfectly. The skyscrapers begin to lean together above you like old trees in a forest, their glass facades rippling into bark, their neon signs softening into fireflies.

You turn down an alley that wasn’t there a moment ago, and it opens into a courtyard where the paving stones are maps of places you loved but forgot. The strangers stop at the fountain in the center. They don’t speak, but you hear them anyway: *You were never lost. You were just gathering the courage to remember.* 

One by one, they step back into the mist, smiling, waving, already fading. The fountain water rises to meet the sky, becoming light, becoming morning. You feel the warmth on your eyelids, hear the distant hum of your actual bedroom, and as you drift toward waking, you carry with you the weightless certainty that somewhere, in the bustling city of your chest, a group of kind strangers has built a permanent home for you to find whenever you need it.
