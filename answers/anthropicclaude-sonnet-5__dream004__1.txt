# The Dreaming Canvas

...you reach out, and your fingertips leave trails of liquid amber light. The canvas isn't blank anymore—it never really was. It was *waiting*.

The first stroke you make becomes a sound instead of a color—a low hum that ripples outward and turns the ground beneath you into rippling water made of glass. You don't question this. Here, logic bends like a held note.

You paint a second stroke, and this one becomes a memory that isn't yours: a childhood you never had, a house with rooms that breathe in and out like lungs. The walls exhale the smell of rain on hot pavement, though you've never smelled rain that way before.

The canvas grows larger the more you add to it, or perhaps you're shrinking. A spiral of impossible birds—their wings made of torn paper and static—lifts off from the corner of the frame and scatters into the sky above you, which is now the same canvas, which was always the same canvas, because in dreams, edges are suggestions, not rules.

Someone who might be you, or might be someone you loved once and forgot, stands on the other side of the frame. They're painting too, mirroring your strokes half a second behind, like an echo learning to catch up to its sound.

"We're not finishing it," they say, though their mouth doesn't move. "We're *remembering* it into existence."

You look down at your hands. They're not hands anymore—they're brushes, they're roots, they're something in between, dissolving into the canvas as it dissolves into you.

The last thing you paint is a door.

It opens onto the room where you're actually sleeping.

You wake up still holding the brush that was never there, colors fading from your fingertips like the last few seconds of a dream trying to hold on.
