You stand there for a full three seconds, your boots stuck to the linoleum (which you suddenly notice is made of pressed dried flower petals, because dream logic only lets you pick up details when you stop panicking) while your boss—who’s wearing a tie made of woven dandelion fluff, not the usual gray polyester he wears in waking life—taps the sunflower watch on his wrist. The petals fan out to show 14 minutes left.

Your desk, which was empty a second ago, is now buried under a tide of lost things: your middle school Hello Kitty pencil case, a stranger’s cracked phone case covered in cat stickers, a single wool mitten that smells like wood smoke, a stack of unopened love letters tied with red ribbon, a toy dinosaur that lights up when you squeeze it. They’re all glowing faintly, like they have tiny fireflies trapped inside them.

You reach for the mitten first, and when your finger brushes the yarn, you’re suddenly standing in a snowy parking lot, 12 years old, watching your grandma tuck the mitten into your coat pocket before you left for a school ski trip. You can smell her cinnamon perfume, hear her laugh when you promised you’d bring her back a snow globe. You forgot you lost that mitten the day she died.

When you blink back to the office, the mitten is gone from your hand, and out the window, you see an old woman walking down the sidewalk below, tucking a matching mitten into her coat pocket, smiling like she just remembered something she’d misplaced for years.

Oh. You get it now.

The project isn’t to sort the lost items. It’s to let the memories attached to them find their way back to the people who need them. You don’t need to sift through each one, or tag them, or file them. You just need to untangle the thin, glowing silver strings that are tied around every object, knotted together with all the other strings of lost things people have been carrying around.

You start pulling at the knots, your fingers moving faster than you’ve ever moved them before. Every time you untangle a string and let the object float out the window toward the person it belongs to, a little weight lifts off your chest—the weight of all the things you’ve lost too, all the memories you’ve tried to shove in the back of your brain because they hurt too much to hold.

12 minutes left when you untangle the love letters, and they float out the window to a guy in a suit two blocks over, who stops walking, presses them to his chest, and calls someone, his voice cracking.

9 minutes left when you untangle the toy dinosaur, and it zooms out the window to a kid on a bike who whoops so loud you can hear it through the glass.

4 minutes left when you get to the last knot. It’s tied around the half-written letter you sent to your best friend, the one you never finished because you were too proud to say you missed them after the fight. The string is wrapped around your own wrist, tight, like it’s been holding you here this whole time.

You pause. Your boss is leaning against the floating bookshelf now, petting his cloud puppy (he got a dog in this dream, obviously), not rushing you. The sunflower watch’s petals have stilled.

You untie the string.

The letter floats out the window, and a second later, your phone buzzes in your pocket. It’s a text from the number you haven’t dialed in three years: “Got your letter. I missed you too. Want to get boba Saturday?”

You laugh, and the sound makes the remaining floating items dissolve into sparkles, the desk clear, the office lights fading into the soft golden light of a sunset you can’t remember seeing in any waking life.

Your boss grins, and holds out a paper cup of warm sparkling water. “Good job,” he says. “Deadline’s met. You can go home now.”

You reach for the cup, and that’s when the alarm goes off.
