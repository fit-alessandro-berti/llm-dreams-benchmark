…find a way to belong before anyone notices you don’t.

The air smells of woodsmoke, wet earth, and horses. Your shoes sink into a road made of packed mud, and around you rises a town of timber-framed houses, crooked signs, and narrow windows glowing with candlelight. No engines. No wires. No distant hum of electricity. Only wagon wheels, church bells, and the murmur of people speaking in a language you almost understand.

You look down at yourself and realize, with a rush of panic, that you’re dressed wrong—far too wrong. Your clothes are strange here. Too clean, too fitted, too full of impossible seams and fabrics. People are beginning to stare.

You need to disappear.

A woman carrying a basket pauses in front of you. She studies your face as if she has been expecting you all along. “You’re late,” she says, pressing a rough wool cloak into your hands. “If the clocktower strikes nine before you reach the astronomer, it will happen again.”

“Again?” you ask, but she is already gone, absorbed into the crowd.

The cloak smells of lavender and dust. You pull it on and move through the market, heart hammering. The town square opens before you, and at its center stands a clocktower so tall it seems to pierce the clouds. Its hands are frozen at three minutes to nine.

Everyone in the square is avoiding looking at it.

A boy with ink-stained fingers tugs your sleeve. “You really don’t remember, do you?” he whispers. He places something cold into your palm: a brass key engraved with a crescent moon. “You hid it last time. Up in the tower. Before the sky opened.”

The sky opened.

You want to laugh, or ask questions, or wake up—but instead you run, because somewhere deep inside the dream you know this is true. You know there is an astronomer waiting in a room full of maps and gears. You know the town has lived this same day before. And you know that when the bell rings, something above the clouds will notice this place again.

You climb the spiral stairs of the clocktower two at a time. Halfway up, the walls begin to change. Stone gives way to wallpaper. The smell of smoke becomes the smell of your childhood home after rain. You hear your phone vibrating in a room that does not exist yet. You hear your own voice from years in the future saying, Hurry.

At the top of the tower, the astronomer is waiting: not old, as you expected, but young, with silver dust on his sleeves and one eye reflecting the night sky. He turns when you enter, unsurprised.

“You made it,” he says. “Then there’s still a chance.”

He unlocks a wooden box with your brass key. Inside is not a machine, not a map, but a small mirror black as water. When you look into it, you do not see your reflection. You see a city full of glass towers, electric light, and streets slick with rain. Your era. Your world. Empty.

“What is this?” you whisper.

“The door,” he says. “Or the warning. It depends what you choose.”

Outside, the frozen clock groans. The minute hand shudders forward.

The astronomer steps aside. “You can go back,” he says. “But if you do, you must remember what you came here to learn.”

“And what did I come here to learn?”

The tower trembles. Far above, something vast moves behind the clouds.

He smiles sadly. “That every age believes it is permanent. Every age is wrong.”

The bell begins to ring.

The sky splits open like a curtain, revealing not stars but countless watching eyes made of fire and time. The whole town falls silent. You raise the black mirror, and the glass turns bright, brighter, until it fills your hands like a second moon.

And just before you step through, you realize what you need to do:

not just survive this era,

but carry it back with you—
the fragility of it,
the wonder of it,
the knowledge that the world can be lost quietly, one ordinary day at a time.

Then you fall through light, through bells, through centuries—

and wake in your own bed, your phone buzzing on the nightstand, dawn at the window, your hand clenched tightly around a small brass key.
