written in a language you've never seen before — but somehow, you can read it perfectly.

The first question asks you to describe the taste of a color you've never seen. You hesitate, pen hovering, and then you realize your hand is moving on its own, writing something elegant and true about the way indigo sounds like a cello in a distant room.

You glance up. The exam hall is enormous — impossibly tall ceilings disappearing into mist, the kind of architectural space that only exists in dreams. Other students sit around you, but their faces keep shifting, like your mind can't quite decide who they are. Your best friend is there, except they're also your third-grade teacher, and somehow this feels normal.

Time moves strangely. You've been working for either four minutes or four hours when you turn to the next page and find the questions have changed. Now they're asking you things like:

*"At what point did you first understand that you were capable of failure?"*

*"Draw the room where you felt safest, and explain why the door was always open."*

*"If you could return one sentence to someone who has already forgotten it, whose voice would you hear?"*

You notice the ink is warm to the touch. Your answers aren't words anymore — they're becoming memories, rising off the page as small translucent shapes that hover briefly before dissolving. A butterfly made of regret. A doorframe made of laughter.

The proctor walks by. It's someone you know but can't name. They lean down and whisper: *"You've already passed. You've been passing your whole life. This exam is just to remind you."*

You look down. Your paper is blank. Or maybe it's full. You can't tell the difference anymore.

And then the bell rings — except it sounds like wind chimes, and you realize you're waking up, the question still unanswered, the feeling of that impossible language still warm on the tip of your tongue.
