…not words, but living things.

The letters squirm across the page like small black beetles, rearranging themselves every time you blink. You grip your pen—at least you *think* it’s a pen—only to find it has softened into a length of warm chalk that crumbles between your fingers. The first question reads:

**“Name the color of the sound your mother made when she was proud of you.”**

You glance around the lecture hall. The other students are bent over their desks in perfect, unsettling unison, their scratchings echoing like rain on a tin roof. Their faces are smooth and blank, like eggs. You look back at your own paper, and the question has already changed:

**“If Tuesday had a weight, how many of them could you carry before your knees gave out?”**

The clock at the front of the room ticks backward. The proctor—was there always a proctor?—is a tall figure made of folded exam booklets, pacing the aisles with a rustling sound. You try to raise your hand, but your arm moves in slow motion, sinking through the air like it’s honey.

You turn the page. There are no multiple choices anymore, only mirrors. Each reflective surface shows you at a different age: a child, an old person, someone you almost recognize but never became. The last mirror is simply the words:

**“What will you do with the one life you are sure is not a rehearsal?”**

You realize your desk is floating now. The floor has become a dark ocean, gentle and waiting. The other students are gone. The exam booklet drifts from your hands, its pages scattering into white birds that circle the room once, twice, then fly out through a window that was never there before.

You stand up. The chalk in your hand is gone. The proctor has folded themself into a single, unreadable page and blown away.

And just as you open your mouth to answer—not to the exam, but to the silence—you feel the familiar tug of morning. The birds dissolve. The ocean floor becomes bedsheets. The final question lingers for a heartbeat in the dark behind your eyelids:

**“Now that you have passed nothing, what will you begin?”**

Then you wake up.
