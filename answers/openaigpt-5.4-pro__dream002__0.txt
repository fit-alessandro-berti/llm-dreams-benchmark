…the air turns silver.

Not gray, not dark—silver, as if the whole sky has become the underside of a lake. The leaves above you stop rustling and begin to whisper instead, each tree speaking in a language you somehow almost understand. A low roll of thunder moves through the forest floor before it reaches the clouds, and the roots beneath your feet tremble like something enormous is waking up.

You look up just as the first raindrop falls.

It doesn’t hit the ground.

It hangs in front of you, suspended in the air like a glass bead, reflecting a tiny upside-down version of the forest. Then another appears. And another. Within seconds, thousands of raindrops are floating all around you, motionless, filling the woods with a glimmering, weightless rain.

Lightning flashes soundlessly.

In that white-blue light, you notice a path that wasn’t there before—a narrow trail of glowing stones winding deeper between the trees. You don’t remember deciding to follow it, but in dreams your feet always know before you do. As you walk, the floating raindrops drift aside to let you pass, brushing your skin with a cool, electric tingling.

The storm grows stranger.

The wind begins to move backward, pulling fallen leaves up from the ground and lifting them into the branches where they reattach themselves. Puddles un-form. Broken twigs knit together. Time is bending around you, rewinding the forest into some older, purer version of itself.

Ahead, in a clearing, stands a single tree taller than all the others. Its bark is midnight-black, but its branches are lit from within by a soft golden glow, as if stars are caught beneath the wood. At its base sits a small door, no taller than your knee, carved with symbols that shift each time you look at them.

The thunder speaks again—closer now—and you realize it is not thunder at all.

It is your name.

The little door swings open.

Warm amber light spills out across the roots, and from inside you hear distant music: a piano playing a melody you knew once as a child but forgot when you woke up too many mornings in a row. You kneel and peer inside, expecting darkness, but instead you see a vast hallway lined with windows. Through every window is a different sky: one full of green moons, one raining feathers, one with whales swimming slowly through clouds.

Then a figure appears at the far end of the hallway.

It is you—but older, calmer, dressed in a coat woven from storm clouds and constellations. The other you smiles as if this meeting has been expected for a very long time.

“You came just before the rain remembered how to fall,” they say.

You want to ask what that means, but the question melts away the moment it forms. Dream logic settles over everything like mist. Of course the rain can remember. Of course storms can call your name. Of course there is a version of you living in the heart of a tree, waiting patiently between lightning strikes.

The older you holds out a hand.

Behind them, the windows begin to brighten one by one. The floating rain outside starts to move again, slowly at first, then faster, until every drop becomes a thread of light descending through the forest. The storm is no longer frightening. It feels ceremonial, like the sky is opening not to destroy the world, but to reveal it.

You take the offered hand—

—and the moment your fingers touch, the entire forest rises into the air.

The trees lift like green towers. The roots loosen from the earth without breaking. The clearing becomes a great turning wheel of leaves and light, and you are carried upward through the storm, past the silver clouds, into a night sky so close you can hear the stars humming.

Far below, the forest folds itself into the shape of a sleeping deer.

Far above, a door appears in the moon.

And just before you step through it, with rain shining on your skin like tiny mirrors, you have the overwhelming certainty that this is the part of the dream you were always trying to get back to.
