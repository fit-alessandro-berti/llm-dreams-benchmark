The strangers aren't tourists or locals. They're... *familiar* in a way that doesn't make sense. One is an old woman with galaxies in her eyes. Another is a tall man whose shadow keeps laughing a second before he does. A third is a child who keeps offering you pieces of the sky like they're pocket change.

"Come on," the child says, pressing a warm shard of sunset into your palm. "Your real path isn't on any map. It's the one that was waiting for you to get lost enough to notice it."

They don't just point directions. They *fold* the city for you. Streetlights bend into friendly arches. Billboards rearrange their letters into inside jokes you didn't know you needed. The endless noise of traffic turns into a lullaby made of every song you loved as a kid.

You walk with them through alleys that smell like rain on hot concrete and forgotten dreams, and for the first time in forever, being lost feels like the beginning of something rather than the end.

Eventually you reach a quiet square where the buildings part like curtains. In the center is a single door that definitely wasn't there a moment ago. It has your name on it, written in the handwriting you had when you were seven.

The old woman smiles. "This is where we leave you. But not really. We were never strangers."

You turn to thank them, but they're already becoming fireflies, constellations, echoes.

You reach for the doorknob, heart hammering with something that feels dangerously like hope, and just as your fingers touch the brass—

You wake up.

But the warmth from the shard of sunset is still in your hand. And somewhere in the distance, you swear you hear a child's laughter mixed with morning traffic.
