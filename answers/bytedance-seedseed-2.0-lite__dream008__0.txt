Panic bubbles up, and you dart to the breakroom sock bin tucked by the copier, only to find it overflowing not just with mismatched ankle socks and pilled winter wool pairs, but with tiny, impossible things tucked into their toes: a mini sailboat that bobs on its own, a jar of fireflies that flicker like old Windows screensavers, the missing key to your first car, even a crumpled concert ticket from a show you’d always regretted missing. You grab a spool of thread someone left on the counter, but your hands keep slipping through the sock fabric, like it’s made of smoke, and you slump against the copier, convinced you’ll never finish.

Then a soft tap on your shoulder makes you jump. It’s the intern who quit last month, but they’re floating six inches off the linoleum, holding a blue raspberry popsicle that drips juice that evaporates before it hits the ground. “The deadline was never yours to meet,” they say, like they’re sharing a secret the office walls have whispered for years. “The deadline itself had a deadline. It ran out five minutes ago.”

You twist around to look back at your desk, and sure enough, your golden retriever-principal-boss is wagging their tail so hard their glasses slide off their nose, pointing at the sky visible through the breakroom window. A storm of floating neon post-it notes is pouring down, drenching the parade route outside, and a voice over the office speakers crackles to life: “Parade canceled. All socks may be repurposed into nap blankets.”

The pile of socks at your feet unfurl on their own, soft and warm, spreading across the floor for everyone to sit on. Someone passes you a popsicle that tastes like the sad office coffee you drink every morning, but somehow it’s sweet, and for the rest of the dream, no one asks you to finish anything ever again. You’re still laughing when you wake up, that weird, warm relief clinging to you even as your real alarm starts to blare for your actual workday.
