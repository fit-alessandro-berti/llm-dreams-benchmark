...decide to cultivate the one thing that still smells of the world before: a single, stubborn patch of wild lavender growing in the cracked concrete of a repurposed subway station.

It is irrational, I know. Every calorie burned pulling weeds is one less calorie for the long trek toward the coast, where rumor says the fallout has finally thinned. But there is a madness that sets in when the sky is perpetually the color of a bruised plum and the wind tastes of rust. I need color. I need a scent that isn't ash.

I spend my nights reinforcing the station’s rusted iron gates, listening to the skittering of the things that have inherited the ruins. They are fast, and they are hungry, and they don't understand why anyone would waste sweat on a plant that can’t be eaten. 

But yesterday, a stranger arrived. They were gaunt, their clothes held together by wire and prayer. They didn’t draw a weapon. They didn’t beg for water. They just stood at the edge of the platform and stared at the purple blooms, their cracked lips trembling.

"I thought they were all gone," the stranger whispered, their voice like gravel grinding on glass.

I realized then that this isn't just about survival. If we lose the memory of what is beautiful, then the sirens and the storms have already won. I offer the stranger a canteen of gray water and a small, ceramic shard to help dig a new irrigation trench. 

We work in silence as the radiator hums in the distance, a low, ominous thrumming that shakes the dust from the ceiling. We are tired, we are starving, and the world is ending—but for the first time in years, the air smells like peace. 

And in the dream, I know: if the lavender survives the night, perhaps we will too.
