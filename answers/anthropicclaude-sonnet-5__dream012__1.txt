The rain hits like static, and the mast groans overhead like something alive and afraid. The crew's faces are half-lit by lantern light, waiting—not for orders exactly, but for the *shape* of a decision, something to hold onto.

I grip the wheel, and it feels warm, almost pulsing, like the ship itself has a heartbeat that's syncing with mine.

"Hold the line!" I shout, though I'm not entirely sure what line I mean. The words just feel true in the moment, the way dream-logic often does—more about conviction than content.

A wave curls up beside us, too tall, glassy black and streaked with phosphorescent green, and for a second it just *hangs* there, defying whatever physics this dream has decided to keep or discard. The crew doesn't scream. They watch it too, strangely calm, like they already know something I don't.

Then Mira—my first mate, though I don't remember hiring her, she's just always been my first mate—leans toward me and says, "You know we're not actually running from the storm."

"What are we running from, then?"

She points, not at the horizon, but *down*, at the deck, at the ship itself. "It's underneath us. It's always been underneath us."

The wave breaks. Not over us—*through* us, like light through fog, and suddenly the storm isn't outside the ship anymore. It's inside my chest, churning, and I realize the ocean was never separate from me to begin with.

I wake up not knowing if I saved the ship or became it.
