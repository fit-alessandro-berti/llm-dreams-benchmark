You wake up in a completely different era, with no modern technology around. As you explore your new surroundings, you realize you need to remember your own name before sundown, or the village won't let you inside the walls.

This seems reasonable to everyone. A woman beating flax against a stone nods at me sympathetically. "It happens," she says. "The river takes them sometimes." She doesn't explain what the river takes, and in the dream I already know, the way you know the plot of a film you've never seen.

So I go looking.

The road is packed earth, rutted from cartwheels, and it smells like woodsmoke and animal and something green and fermenting. I keep reaching for a pocket that isn't there. My hand goes to my hip, to my chest, patting for a rectangle of glass — that's how I'd find out, I think, I'd just look it up — and each time my palm meets rough undyed wool I feel a small, specific grief, like touching a tooth that's been pulled.

There's a boy driving geese. I ask him if he knows me. He says, "You're the one who came down from the hill." That's the closest thing to an identity anyone offers all afternoon.

I try the blacksmith. The forge is a cave of orange light and he's hammering something that keeps changing shape — a horseshoe, then a key, then a small bird — and he shouts over the noise that names live in the throat, not the head, and I should try singing. I try singing. What comes out is a jingle for a car insurance company, and the blacksmith stops hammering and looks at me with such pity that I have to leave.

The sun is getting low. It's absurdly low, actually, dream-low, an enormous swollen coin sitting right on the field's edge, and I understand that time here is not a resource but a weather.

I find a well. This feels important. I lean over the lip and look down and there's my face, wobbling, and the face is mine but the reflection is wearing a suit — a suit! — and holding a phone up, taking a picture of me looking down. We regard each other. I say, "What's my name?" and the reflection mouths something but the sound arrives late, distorted, like it has to travel up through all that cold water, and what I finally hear is an alert tone. Two notes. The sound a message makes.

I understand, in the way of dreams, that I will not get it from him. That he doesn't have it either. That he has been searching in his own way — in archives, in accounts, in the little glowing rectangle — and that between us we have every fact about me and not one true thing.

So I sit down in the dirt.

That's what I need to do, it turns out. Not remember. Sit down in the dirt outside a village at sundown and let it be quiet, and it *is* quiet, catastrophically quiet, no hum, no fan, no distant highway, a silence so total it has texture, like felt pressed against both ears. And into that silence comes: the geese. A dog. Someone's mother calling someone in. My own breathing, which I have apparently never heard before.

And then a woman's voice behind me, ordinary and slightly annoyed, the way you'd speak to someone who's late for dinner, saying a word.

It's my name. I know it immediately and completely. It's not the name I have when I'm awake. It's better — it's short and it has a sound in it like a door unlatching — and I turn around already smiling, already home, and I open my mouth to say it back to her so I'll never lose it again—

and the alarm goes off.

I lie there for a long time in the blue dark, holding very still, the way you hold still when a moth has landed on you. Not moving. Not reaching for the phone. Trying to keep the shape of it — two syllables, a door unlatching — while the room fills up with morning and the walls come back and the whole heavy architecture of who I actually am settles onto my chest, piece by piece, like snow.
