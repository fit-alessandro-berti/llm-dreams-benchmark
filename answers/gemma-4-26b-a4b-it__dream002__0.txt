Then, the rain begins. But it isn't water. 

The droplets fall in slow motion, glowing with a soft, bioluminescent light. As they strike the leaves of the ancient oaks, they don't splash; they chime, sounding like a thousand tiny glass bells ringing in unison. The forest floor begins to transform, the moss turning into a sea of soft, glowing ferns that pulse in time with my own heartbeat.

A gust of wind sweeps through, but it doesn't chill me. Instead, it carries the scent of things I haven't thought of in years—freshly baked bread, a childhood summer, the salt of a distant ocean. The wind lifts my feet off the ground, and suddenly, the struggle to stay upright vanishes. I am no longer walking; I am drifting.

The storm isn't something to hide from. It is a symphony. The lightning strikes, but instead of jagged white lines, it laces the sky with golden threads of light that hang suspended in the air like glowing spiderwebs. I reach out to touch one, and as my finger brushes the light, the world dissolves into a kaleidoscope of colors, and I realize I am no longer in the forest at all, but floating through a galaxy made of stardust and rain.
