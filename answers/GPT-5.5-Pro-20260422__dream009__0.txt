…find a way to belong before anyone notices you do not.

The first thing you do is check your pockets, out of habit. No phone. No keys. No wallet. Only a smooth black pebble you do not remember picking up, warm as if it has been sitting in sunlight. Around you, the world smells of horse sweat, woodsmoke, wet stone, and bread.

You are standing in the middle of a market square.

Not a reconstructed one. Not a museum. Not a movie set.

Real mud grips your shoes. Real chickens scatter from under a cart. Real people in wool and linen move around you, shouting prices in a language that feels almost familiar, as though you once learned it in a dream and forgot it while waking.

Above the rooftops, a bell begins to ring.

Everyone stops.

The market falls silent so suddenly that you hear the tiny tick of rain against canvas awnings.

A woman beside you clutches a basket of apples to her chest. A boy drops a bundle of firewood. A man with a scar across his lip turns toward the hill at the edge of town, where a tower stands against a green-gray sky.

On top of the tower, a red flag rises.

Someone whispers, “The clockmaker has returned.”

You do not know why, but the words make the black pebble in your pocket burn.

You pull it out. It is no longer a pebble.

It is a watch face without hands.

The glass is cracked. Beneath it, instead of numbers, tiny constellations glow and rearrange themselves. You stare at it, and for one breath the whole market vanishes.

You see another place.

A room filled with mirrors.

A door made of water.

Your own reflection standing on the other side, older than you are now, mouthing a warning you cannot hear.

Then the market snaps back into place.

The woman with the apples sees the object in your hand. Her eyes widen.

“You,” she says.

You try to speak, but the bell rings again, louder this time, and the air ripples like disturbed water.

The woman grabs your wrist and pulls you into an alley.

“Do not let the king’s men see that,” she hisses.

“I don’t know what it is,” you say.

“That is the trouble with travelers,” she replies. “You never do.”

She drags you through a maze of narrow streets, past shuttered windows and crooked chimneys, until you reach a small shop wedged between a candle maker and a place that smells strongly of vinegar. The sign above the door shows a golden eye painted on blue wood.

Inside, shelves climb to the ceiling, packed with jars of teeth, folded maps, dried flowers, brass instruments, and little clay birds that turn their heads to watch you pass.

The woman bars the door behind you.

“My name is Mara,” she says. “And unless I am very wrong, you have arrived on the day the hour is stolen.”

You look down at the broken watch face. “What does that mean?”

Mara’s expression darkens.

“It means that at sunset, today will begin again. And again. And again. It has happened before. Most forget. Some go mad. A few remember enough to hide before the bells.”

Outside, boots strike the stones in rhythm.

Mara blows out a candle with two fingers. “They are already searching.”

“For me?”

“For the piece you carry.”

The boots stop outside the shop.

A fist pounds on the door.

Mara moves faster than thought. She shoves a cloak into your arms, then presses one hand against the painted eye on the wall. The shelves split open, revealing a stairway descending into blue darkness.

“Down,” she says.

You hesitate. “Why are you helping me?”

For the first time, Mara looks frightened.

“Because last time, you helped me.”

The door bursts open.

You run.

The stairway coils beneath the shop, deeper than any cellar should go. The walls are carved with dates, thousands of them, some ancient, some impossible. You see one from your own century. You see tomorrow’s date. You see your birthday scratched again and again until the stone around it has worn smooth.

At the bottom waits a tunnel lit by water.

Not torches. Not lamps. Water.

It flows along the ceiling in a shining river, carrying reflections of places that are not there. A desert at night. A city of glass. A battlefield covered in snow. Your bedroom, empty, morning light on the floor.

You reach up without meaning to.

Your fingers touch the ceiling-river.

The world tilts.

Suddenly you remember.

Not everything. Just pieces.

You have been here before.

You have run through this tunnel before.

You have failed at sunset before.

And each time, the clockmaker has taken one more hour from the world.

At the end of the tunnel stands a door made of water, just like the vision. Behind it, your older reflection waits.

This time, you hear the warning.

“Do not go home yet,” your reflection says. “You are the key, but not the lock.”

“Then what is the lock?” you ask.

The reflection raises a hand and points behind you.

You turn.

The king’s men are gone. Mara is gone. The tunnel is gone.

You are standing in the tower on the hill.

The red flag snaps above you in a wind you cannot feel. Gears the size of houses turn overhead, but they make no sound. In the center of the chamber sits an old man at a workbench, repairing a clock with golden hands.

He does not look up.

“You are late,” he says.

“I don’t even know when I am.”

“That,” says the clockmaker, “is why I keep inviting you back.”

On his bench lie hundreds of broken watch faces, all like yours. Each one reflects a different version of you: younger, older, afraid, angry, laughing, crying, dressed in clothes from eras you have never seen.

The clockmaker finally looks at you.

His eyes are not old. They are empty skies full of falling stars.

“You wanted to escape time,” he says gently. “So I showed you what happens when time escapes you.”

“I didn’t ask for this.”

“No,” he agrees. “Not here.”

The watch face in your hand begins to grow hot again. The constellations beneath the glass align into a single shape: a door.

Outside the tower, the sun touches the horizon.

The bell begins its final toll.

You understand then, with the strange certainty dreams give you, that you have only one choice. You can use the watch face to open the way home, return to your clean bright room, your buzzing devices, your ordinary morning.

Or you can break it.

You look at the clockmaker. “What happens if I do?”

He smiles sadly. “Then the day continues.”

“And me?”

“Then you wake up.”

The final bell rings.

You smash the watch face against the stone floor.

Light spills out, not white, but gold and blue and green, the colors of every morning that has ever happened. The tower shakes. The silent gears scream into motion. The red flag tears loose and flies upward into the sky, becoming a bird, then a flame, then a tiny red star.

The clockmaker reaches for you, but his hands turn to dust.

The floor opens beneath your feet.

You fall through the tower, through the hill, through the market square, through the tunnel of water and the room of mirrors. You fall past all your other selves, and they fall with you, laughing, shouting, waving goodbye.

Just before you hit the dark, Mara’s voice whispers in your ear:

“Remember enough.”

You wake in your own bed.

Your phone is on the nightstand. Cars hiss along the wet street outside. Somewhere nearby, a delivery truck backs up with three dull beeps.

For a moment, everything is ordinary.

Then you notice mud on your shoes.

And beside your pillow, warm from sunlight that has not yet reached the window, lies a smooth black pebble.
