...and the canvas is the only still thing in the world.

Everything else keeps deciding to be something else. The hills breathe in and become ochre, breathe out and become a color I have no name for, a color that tastes like the inside of a bell. Trees fold themselves into hexagons and unfold as birds. The birds are triangles, then they are the idea of birds, then they are gone, leaving a sound behind like a comma.

But the canvas doesn't move. It stands on an easel made of something that might be driftwood or might be a sleeping animal's ribs. Its white is patient. It has the quality of a held breath.

I look for a brush and find one in my hand, which is strange, because a moment ago my hand was a small doorway. The brush has no bristles. It has a tip made of the dark you see when you press your eyes shut.

I touch it to the canvas.

And the world stops changing.

Everything — the hills, the folding trees, the color-that-tastes-like-a-bell — all of it goes still and waits, the way a room goes quiet when someone says *I have something to tell you*. I understand, in the way you understand things in dreams, without being told: whatever I paint, the world will become. This is the only fixed point. This is where the shapes come from.

The pressure of it is enormous and completely weightless.

I try to paint a house. But my hand paints a door, opening. I try to paint my mother's face and the brush makes a long slow curve like a road going over a hill at dusk, and somehow that *is* her face, more than a face would have been. I paint a word I can't read. I paint the smell of rain on hot stone. I paint something small in the corner that I immediately want to apologize for.

And behind me, the landscape obeys. I don't turn around. I can feel it happening — the great soft sound of a world rearranging itself into the shape of my attention, like a crowd of people all turning to look the same direction.

When I finally step back, the canvas is covered edge to edge, and I still can't tell what it is. It's not a picture. It's more like a face I've seen a thousand times and never once looked at.

I turn around.

The landscape has become a room. My room. The one with the crack in the ceiling shaped like a river delta, the lamp with the burn mark, the window with morning starting behind it, thin and grey and honest. Nothing is changing. Nothing is folding into hexagons or breathing out impossible colors. It is all so ordinary that I want to weep.

*Oh,* I think. *This is what I made. Out of everything.*

And then, from the canvas behind me, a very small sound — a brush being set down, gently, by someone finishing.

I don't look. I know that if I look, the whole thing starts over.

Instead I lie down on the bed that is already there, in the room I apparently painted, and I let the grey light come in, and the last thing I think before waking is: *how kind of me, to give myself somewhere to land.*
