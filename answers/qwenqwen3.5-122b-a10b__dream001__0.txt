You start to run, but the pavement ripples like water beneath your shoes, slowing your pace without stopping you. The buildings around you are not made of stone or steel, but of stacked books and old photographs that shift as you pass, rearranging their facades like a deck of cards being shuffled by an invisible hand.

You check your wrist, but the watch face is blank, save for a single eye that blinks in time with the city's heartbeat. Panic flares, hot and bright, but then the air grows cool and smells of ozone and old rain. A streetlamp ahead flickers, not with electricity, but with a soft, golden moth that flutters down and lands on your shoulder. It whispers without sound: *The destination is not a place, but a state.*

You stop running. You realize the meeting isn't scheduled for an hour from now; it is happening the moment you decide to arrive.

The street dissolves into a long hallway lined with mirrors. You walk through them, and with each reflection, you shed a layer of worry—your coat falls away, your shoes turn to light, your hurried thoughts quiet into a hum. At the end of the hall, there is a single door. You push it open.

Inside, the room is empty except for a table and two chairs. One chair is occupied by a figure with your face, but wearing a calm you haven't felt in years. They smile, pour a cup of tea that steams in the shape of a question mark, and slide it toward you.

"You're late," they say, but their voice sounds like home. "But you're just in time."

You sit. The clock on the wall begins to tick backward, and the dream fades into the warm, heavy feeling of waking up, leaving you with the lingering sense that the meeting was never about the work, but about finally finding yourself in the crowd.
