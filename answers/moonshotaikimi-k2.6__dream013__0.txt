…the walls stop being walls. They become the negative space between your thoughts, solidifying only when you blink. The puzzles no longer sit before you in stone and brass—they invade your reasoning itself. You encounter a door that unlocks only when you forget what you are looking for; a staircase that ascends only while you are lying down in your mind; a riddle whose answer is the sound of your own name spoken backwards by someone who loves you.

Concentration becomes dangerous. The harder you focus, the more the corridors multiply, branching into probabilities rather than directions. You pass a version of yourself who is still solving the first puzzle, eternally struggling with a mechanism of moonlight and gears. You wave, but your other self does not see you—they are trapped in the urgency you have already outgrown.

At the heart of the maze, there is no final puzzle. There is only a desk, and sitting at it is the part of your mind that has been constructing the labyrinth all along. They look up from their blueprints—impossible maps drawn in water and ash—and smile.

“You were never meant to solve it,” they say, sliding a blank sheet toward you. “You were meant to realize that the maze solves *you*.”

As you touch the paper, the ink bleeds upward into your fingertips. The corridors collapse inward, not with violence but with relief, like a long-held breath finally released. You carry the maze out with you—not as memory, but as structure, a new folding in your brain where once there was only straight line.

Then the alarm clock is a color you have never seen before, and you are awake.
