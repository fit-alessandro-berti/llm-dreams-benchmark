You feel a storm approaching and **the birds fall silent in the highest branches as if holding their breath for you.** The sky does not turn black, but rather shifts into a deep, liquid purple that ripples like dark water. 

The wind rushes through your clothes with a gentle pressure rather than force, smelling of ozone and old books. Instead of running, you stop walking. You notice that the ground beneath your feet is no longer dirt; it is made woven from silver thread, glowing faintly with every gust of breeze.

A lightning fork strikes a great oak tree right ahead, but instead of burning it, it ignites the bark into living flame that dances harmlessly along the trunk, illuminating a path you had not seen moments before—a pathway paved with glass flowers. The rain begins to fall, but as droplets touch your skin, they dissolve instantly into warm light. 

You realize you do not need shelter. You walk through the downpour, and where your footsteps fall, small bridges of woven leaves appear to connect the stormy bank with the sunlit side. When the thunder finally rumbles, it sounds like someone humming a lullaby rather than a warning. The storm does not break upon you; it breaks open the world around you, revealing that the forest was never outside, but within the vast, swirling memory of who you are becoming when you wake.
