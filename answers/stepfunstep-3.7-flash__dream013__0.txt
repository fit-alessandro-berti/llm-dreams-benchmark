You are in a maze filled with complex puzzles. Each puzzle requires intense concentration and logical thinking to solve, and as you move deeper into the maze, you notice the puzzles stop testing only your logic, and start testing the parts of you you’ve spent years learning to ignore.

At first, the chambers near the entrance are straightforward: sliding tile puzzles where the tiles are fragments of the lullaby your mom sang when you had nightmares as a kid, cipher locks that require you to translate the secret code you and your best friend made up in 7th grade, mazes drawn in chalk that rearrange only when you blink. The air smells like burnt ozone and peppermint, the walls are frosted glass that flicker with half-remembered scenes: the time you fell off your bike and scraped both knees, the opening line of the book that made you want to write, the face of the stranger who smiled at you on the bus the day you moved to a new city. You solve them easily at first, the way you solve problems in waking life: break them into steps, rule out impossible answers, push through the frustration when a lock won’t turn.

But the deeper you go, the more the rules bend.
By the third ring of the maze, the puzzles don’t have clear right answers. A door stands before you carved with the question: What’s the name of the person you were too scared to say goodbye to when you left home? You rack your brain, but the name won’t come—until you realize the door isn’t listening to your voice, it’s synced to the rhythm of your heartbeat, and it swings open the second you stop trying to force the memory and let yourself feel the ache of that goodbye you never got to give.

The next chamber has a table piled with scattered photographs, each one a moment you’ve tried to forget: the test you failed that made you switch majors, the text you deleted before you could hit send, the night you walked home in the rain because you were too proud to call someone to pick you up. The puzzle here is to arrange them in order, but no matter how you stack them, they keep sliding back into the wrong order—until you stop trying to organize them, and just sit with them, let the embarrassment and regret sit in your chest instead of pushing it down. The second you do, the photos glow, and a door opens in the back wall.

You realize after a while that the maze isn’t trying to trap you. It’s sifting through the layers of stuff you’ve packed away: the little fears you ignore to get through the workday, the unspoken dreams you told yourself were silly, the apologies you never got to make. The puzzles get easier the more honest you are with yourself. A riddle asking for the one thing you’d save if your house was burning down doesn’t need a clever answer—you just say it out loud, and the wall in front of you dissolves into dandelion fluff.

When you reach the very center, there’s no treasure, no grand exit sign, no final boss to defeat. There’s just a small wooden stool, and a notebook on it with your name scrawled on the cover in the handwriting you had when you were 10 years old. You open it, and every page is filled with all the ideas, questions, half-finished stories and silly jokes you’ve forgotten over the years—stuff you thought you’d lost forever.

You sit there for what feels like hours, flipping through the pages, and you realize the maze wasn’t something you were supposed to solve and escape. It was something you built for yourself, a place to go when the noise of waking life got too loud, to remember all the parts of you you leave behind when you rush through the day.

Then you hear a sound, far off: the alarm clock you set for tomorrow morning. The walls of the maze start to fade, the notebook dissolves into mist in your hands. You’re not sad to leave, because you know the maze will be there next time you need it—waiting, with its puzzles and its frosted glass walls, ready to help you find the parts of yourself you’d forgotten.
