You feel a storm approaching and the air turns to mercury—heavy, silver, pressing against your eardrums until the world seems to hold its breath. The canopy above begins to rustle in reverse, leaves lifting their pale undersides not to the wind, but to something older. Thunder doesn’t crack; it *unzips*, a long slow tear in the sky that leaks not rain, but static. The bark of the oaks begins to peel upward like old wallpaper, revealing not wood beneath, but the deep bruised purple of twilight itself.

You run, but your feet sink into moss that has turned to velvet, then to water, then to something like the skin of a drum. Each step sends a low vibration through the forest floor. The trees are no longer rooted—they’re wading, great wooden legs striding through the rising mist, rearranging themselves into a labyrinth that pulses with your heartbeat.

Lightning forks down, but it moves too slowly, a bright fracture hanging in the air, and where it touches the earth, flowers made of glass bloom instantly, chiming. You realize the storm isn’t coming *for* you—it’s coming *from* you, exhaling from the center of your chest where the dream has been hiding its true weight.

You stop running. You open your mouth to speak, and the wind rushes in, filling you with the smell of ozone and forgotten names. The rain finally arrives, but it falls upward, returning to the wound in the sky, stitching it closed with threads of liquid silver. The forest settles, the trees lowering themselves back into place, their leaves now the color of your eyes. The storm leaves behind a clearing you don’t remember, and in its center, a door made of the same storm-cloud grey, waiting for you to decide whether the dream has ended or only just begun.
