The impossible deadline—scrawled in neon pink marker at the top, “done by sunrise”—glows so bright it bleeds through the paper, and when I look up, my boss’s usual stern sharpness softens at the edges, their outline blurring into the office’s flickering fluorescent lights like a watercolor left out in rain. They don’t bark demands, just tuck a stray dandelion fluff behind my ear and murmur, “It’s not just spreadsheets and client decks, you know. You’ve been carrying the parts of this project that matter most for months.”

Before I can ask what that means, my desk unsticks from the scuffed linoleum, rolling across the office floor to the beat of a childhood song I can’t quite name, its drawers flying open to spill not staplers and sticky notes, but the half-finished poems I’d scribbled in meeting margins, the tiny sketches I’d hidden in my laptop’s secret folder, the handwritten thank-you notes from clients I’d stuffed away thinking they didn’t count. Each piece floats into the air, stitching itself into a glowing, wobbly tapestry that wraps around the drab cubicle walls, painting them in the sunflower yellows and ocean blues I’d always wished my office could be.

When the first real streak of sunrise bleeds pink through the 12th-floor windows, right on the dot of the deadline, the project settles into a neat, perfect portfolio on my desk—not because I’d rushed to check boxes, but because it was always just me, piecing together the parts of myself I’d buried under “urgent” work tasks. My boss grins, hands me a slice of the vanilla birthday cake I’d bought for myself the week prior and forgotten about, and says, “Told you you had it. Now wake up to eat it before it goes stale on your kitchen counter.”
