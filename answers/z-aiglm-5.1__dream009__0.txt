You are dreaming. Can you complete the following dream?

You wake up in a completely different era, with no modern technology around. As you explore your new surroundings, you realize you need to find water before the sun climbs any higher.

The air tastes different—cleaner, sharper, like stone and wet moss. You're lying on packed earth outside a low stone structure with a thatched roof. Your clothes are rough-spun, scratchy against your skin, and your feet are bare. The last thing you remember is falling asleep in your bed, the glow of your phone screen still warm against your face.

Now there's nothing. No hum of electricity. No distant highway. Just birdsong and the rustle of wind through tall grass.

You stand, legs unsteady, and look around. Rolling hills stretch in every direction, dotted with small farm plots and scattered animals—goats, you think, or maybe sheep. In the valley below, smoke rises from a cluster of humble dwellings. A village.

Your throat is dry. Genuinely dry. You swallow, and it hurts.

You start walking toward the village, but slowly. Something feels wrong about rushing. The grass is wet with dew, cold between your toes. You notice your hands—are they your hands? They're calloused in places you don't recognize, the nails dirt-caked. A leather cord binds your wrist, strung with a single bead of blue stone.

A sound behind you. Footsteps.

You turn. A woman stands there, middle-aged, broad-shouldered, holding a clay jug against her hip. She says something—a string of words you shouldn't understand, but somehow, impossibly, you do.

"You slept long. The well is this way."

She doesn't wait for you to answer. She just walks, expecting you to follow.

And you do.

The dream continues—
