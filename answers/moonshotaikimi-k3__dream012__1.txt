...and you grip the wheel with hands that somehow know exactly what to do, even though you've never sailed a day in your life.

"Steer into the wave!" you shout, and your first mate—who is, you realize without surprise, your third-grade teacher—nods gravely and rings the ship's bell.

The sea rises like a wall of dark glass. Inside it, you see the silhouette of a whale, enormous and unhurried, watching you through the water as if it has all the time in the world. Then the wave folds over the ship—but instead of crushing you, it passes through the hull like cold light, and suddenly you are sailing through the night sky, stars foaming around the bow like phosphorescent surf.

The crew cheers. Someone presses a compass into your hand, but its needle points only downward, toward a warm glow beneath the clouds. You understand, with the strange certainty that only dreams allow, that this was always your destination.

You turn the wheel. The ship descends slowly, like a falling leaf, and the storm becomes a distant silver ceiling above you. Your crew gathers at the rail, faces lit from below, and you realize they are unafraid because *you* are unafraid—that this was the whole of your duty, and you have done it.

Then the bell rings again.

It's your alarm clock. The sea drains away through the floorboards of morning, and you're left holding a fading certainty: somewhere, somehow, you steered them safely through.
