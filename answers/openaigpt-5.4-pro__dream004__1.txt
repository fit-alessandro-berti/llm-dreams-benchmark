A brush appears in your other hand—though you do not remember picking it up—and when you lift it, it drips not paint, but moments: a laughing face, the sound of rain on a window, the feeling of running downhill as a child. You touch the brush to the canvas, and the memories bloom into color.

A single blue line becomes a river suspended in the air. A circle of amber opens into a second sun. A jagged streak of silver turns into a staircase made of moonlight, climbing upward into a sky filled with floating doors. Every stroke you make changes the world around you. Mountains fold themselves into paper cranes. Trees sway and dissolve into flocks of emerald fish that swim through the clouds. The ground beneath your feet becomes a mosaic of moving eyes, all blinking slowly, peacefully, as if they have been waiting for you.

Then you realize something stranger still: the canvas is painting you back.

With every image you create, a new detail appears on your own skin—constellations across your wrists, tiny gardens growing along your sleeves, windows opening in your shadow. You are becoming part of the dream, and the dream is becoming part of you.

In the distance, a figure made of shifting glass watches from the edge of a lavender desert. When it steps closer, you see that it has your face, only older, calmer, lit from within. It says nothing. It simply points at the canvas.

At the center of all your wild colors, one blank space remains.

You hesitate, then press your palm into it.

Instantly, the changing world stills.

The colors stop running. The shapes hold their breath. The blank place fills not with an object, but with a doorway—a simple wooden door painted white, glowing softly around the frame. On it, in handwriting you somehow recognize as your own, are the words:

**Wake up when you are ready.**

You reach for the handle.

And just before the door opens, you hear the canvas whisper, like a voice from very far away and very close at once:

“Take one color with you.”

When you wake, your room is ordinary, the walls unmoving, the morning light pale and familiar.

But on the tip of your index finger, there is a small smudge of impossible blue.
