You are in the middle of a heated argument with a close friend. The disagreement is about something important to both of you, and you decide to stop yelling first, for the first time in the ten minutes you’ve been standing in the half-flooded community garden you planted together the spring after your shared best friend Lila died.

The argument has been sharp enough to leave your throat raw: Mia has been screaming that you care more about clinging to the patch of tomato vines and lavender bushes Lila loved than you care about her mom, who can’t afford the copay for the chemo that’s keeping her alive. You’ve been yelling back that the garden is the last place Lila is still tangible, that you made a promise to her you’d keep it running as long as you could, that you didn’t tell her you’d already won a $50,000 community grant that would cover her mom’s costs for two years and fund a free fresh produce program for the neighborhood because you were scared if you fixed both of your crises at once, you’d have to admit you’d been so busy clinging to the past you almost let her family fall apart.

When you hold up your hands to show her you’re not fighting, your flannel sleeve slips back, and she sees the edge of the crumpled grant letter sticking out of your jeans pocket. She goes quiet, rain dripping off the hem of her hoodie, and you fumble to pull it out, but when your fingers close around it, the paper dissolves into a cloud of dandelion fluff, the kind Lila used to blow on your birthday every year. The rain stops mid-fall, every droplet hanging suspended like glowing glass prisms, and suddenly the garden is full of the sound of Lila’s lopsided laugh, the one she let out when she tripped over a tomato vine and face-planted into a bucket of compost last summer.

You decide to tell her the truth you’ve been too scared to speak out loud, that you don’t love the garden because it’s a shrine, you love it because it’s the only place that still smells like Lila’s vanilla hand cream, the only place you can still hear her teasing you for being unable to tell a weed from a tomato plant to save your life. But when you open your mouth, the fluff swirls back into the shape of the grant letter, taped to the side of the old dented garden shed, and the first line reads Awarded to Lila’s Legacy Garden, for community food and health access, in honor of Lila March.

Mia laughs, wet and wobbly, and reaches out to brush a stray piece of fluff off your cheek, and suddenly Lila is standing at the edge of the tent, holding a mason jar of her famous strawberry jam and a plate of the chocolate chip cookies she baked every Sunday for the after-school program kids who help out in the garden. She grins, and you can hear her say clear as day, “Told you two would stop being idiots before the tomatoes rot on the vine.”

Then the dream softens, the sharp edges of the argument melting away: you’re sitting on the splintery wooden bench you built together, passing a jar of lemonade around with Mia, her mom, and the row of seven-year-olds who run between the tomato plants chasing a monarch butterfly. The air smells like wet dirt, lavender, and strawberry jam. You don’t have to make any big decisions for a while, not in this part of the dream. You just get to sit there, and the garden is still there, and Lila is there, and neither of you are angry, and for a minute it feels like everything is exactly how it was supposed to be, before you wake up.
