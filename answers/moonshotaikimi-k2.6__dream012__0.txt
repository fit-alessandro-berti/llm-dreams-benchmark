...stand at the helm with salt crusting my eyelashes, the wheel bucking in my hands like something alive. The sky has turned the color of old bruises—purple, yellow, sickly green—and the waves are no longer merely water but architected things, rising in impossible geometries, crests frozen into crystalline spires before collapsing into foam.

"Reef the mainsail!" I shout, though the wind tears at my words. "And bring me the glass."

Young Marisol scrambles forward, her oilskins shining like sealskin. When she hands me the spyglass, I don't point it at the horizon where the storm rages darkest. Instead, I raise it to the sky, searching for the star that shouldn't be there—the one I saw in the dream *before* this dream, burning cold and silver in the noon dark.

There. Just above the eye of the storm. A fixed point in the chaos.

The crew is watching me. Their faces are pale moons in the twilight, eyes wide with that particular terror that comes when the world unmoors itself from its own rules. I feel the old, familiar weight settle over me—not the burden of command, but the certainty that in dreams, the ship goes where the captain believes it can go.

"We're not running from it," I call out, my voice cutting clearer than it should through the howl. "We're sailing *through*."

"But the charts—" begins the first mate.

"The charts end at the edge of the known world," I say, spinning the wheel hard to starboard. The ship groans, timber singing, and for a moment we ride the spine of a wave so high I can see the curvature of the dream itself, the hazy border where sea meets sleeping mind. "This storm isn't trying to drown us. It's trying to wake us up too soon."

Lightning forks down, but instead of striking the mast, it braids itself into the rigging, illuminating the sails from within. They billow with a light that doesn't burn. The crew falls silent as the ship plunges forward, not downward into the trough but *through*—the waves parting like a curtain heavy with velvet.

The compass spins backward, then stops. The wind shifts from a scream to a whisper, carrying the scent of rain on hot pavement, of childhood bedrooms and morning coffee—scents from a world we're not ready to return to yet.

"Steady," I tell them, though my own heart is hammering against my ribs like a bird against glass. "Steady as she goes. There's calm water on the other side. I promise."

And the strange thing is, as the ship begins to rise—not on a wave, but on the slow, inexorable lift of dream logic moving toward dawn—they believe me. The wheel grows warm in my hands. The silver star above pulses once, like a heartbeat.

We sail on. The storm breaks not into sunlight, but into something softer: the hushed blue of deep water under a moon that hasn't risen yet, in a place that exists only between sleeping and waking, where I am still the captain, and they are still my crew, and the journey is far from over.
