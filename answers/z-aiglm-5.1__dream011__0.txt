...not written in any language you recognize. The letters shift and swim before your eyes, rearranging themselves like tadpoles in a pond. You blink hard, and when you look again, the questions have transformed into something else entirely—musical notation, except the notes are colors, and the staff lines are made of breathing vines.

You look around the exam hall. It stretches impossibly long, the ceiling lost somewhere in a pale fog. Other students hunch over their papers, their pencils scratching in unison, producing a sound like insects in a summer field. You recognize no one, yet each face flickers with someone you've almost met—a barista, a stranger on a bus, a childhood friend whose name you've forgotten.

The professor walks between the rows, but she is also your grandmother, and also a large gray owl. She passes your desk and whispers, "You already know the answer. You've always known."

You look back at the exam. One question has stabilized into legibility:

*Question 1: Describe the house you lived in before you were born. Be specific. (50 points)*

You pick up your pencil. It feels strange—heavier than it should be. You begin to write, and the words flow out not as language but as watercolor, bleeding across the page in rivers of amber and sage. The paper drinks it in. You feel certain you are failing beautifully.

The clock on the wall has no hands. It ticks anyway.

Somewhere beneath the exam hall, beneath the building, beneath the dream itself, you hear a door open.
