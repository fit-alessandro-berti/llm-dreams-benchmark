a brush that seems to be breathing.

The moment you pick it up, the world goes quiet.

The shifting colors pause midair like startled birds, and the shapes around you lean closer—triangles folding into flowers, rivers curling upward into glass staircases, clouds blinking with silver eyes. The canvas waits.

You dip the brush into nothing, and it comes away soaked in a color you have never seen before—something between moonlight and memory. When you touch it to the canvas, a door appears.

It is small at first, no larger than your hand, but it grows with every heartbeat until it stands before you, painted in impossible detail. Behind it, you hear laughter, rain, and the distant sound of someone calling your name from a place you almost remember.

You open the door.

On the other side is a room filled with versions of yourself: one made of paper, one made of stars, one with wings of blue smoke, one very old, one still a child. They are all painting their own canvases, and each painting shows a different sky.

The child-version of you looks up and smiles.

“You’re late,” they say.

“For what?” you ask.

“For waking the sun.”

They hand you a golden thread. It hums in your palm, warm and alive. You step back into the changing world and tie the thread to the horizon. The sky trembles. The colors begin to move again, but now they follow your brush—swirling, blooming, becoming mountains, oceans, lanterns, faces, songs.

At last, you paint a sun in the center of the canvas.

It rises not into the sky, but out of it, spilling light over everything. The surreal landscape softens. The breathing brush dissolves into a flock of butterflies. The canvas folds itself into a paper boat and sails away across the air.

And just before you wake, you realize the world was never changing randomly.

It was waiting for you to decide what it would become.
