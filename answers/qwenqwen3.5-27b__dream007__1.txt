As you turn on your heel, the floor beneath your feet doesn't feel like wood anymore; it feels like moss, soft and damp. You step toward the door, but the hallway stretches endlessly, the walls breathing in slow, rhythmic pulses. The sound of your friend's voice doesn't fade; instead, it echoes from the ceiling, vibrating through your chest like a low cello note.

You look back. They are still standing where you left them, but the room behind them has dissolved into a twilight ocean. They aren't angry anymore. They are holding a lantern made of glass, and inside the flame, you can see the image of the thing you were arguing about—it’s not a person or a place, but a moment in time that neither of you can touch.

You realize, with the sudden, sharp clarity of a dream, that the argument wasn't about the issue at all. It was about the fear of drifting apart.

You take a step forward, and gravity loosens. You aren't walking; you are floating toward them. The distance closes without you moving your legs. When you reach them, the lantern shatters, but the fire doesn't fall. It rises, turning into thousands of fireflies that swirl around your heads, warm and silent. You look into their eyes, and you know that whatever you were fighting over has already been forgiven, even before you said the words.

The fireflies begin to dim, turning into dust motes in a morning sun. The mossy floor hardens back into wood. The breathing walls stop. You feel the familiar, heavy sensation of your own mattress beneath you.

You open your eyes. The room is dark, but the feeling of warmth lingers on your skin, and for a moment, you can still hear the faint, melodic hum of their voice before the silence of the real world takes over.
