...decide to collect the echoes of what used to be.

I don't hunt for canned goods or scrap metal; instead, I wander through the skeletal remains of the cities, carrying nothing but a heavy, velvet-lined jar and a tuning fork. The wind whistles through the hollowed-out skyscrapers like a broken flute, and I realize that the world isn't just dead—it is quiet, and that silence is a weight I cannot bear.

I find a patch of rusted playground swings, and as the wind hits them, they creak in a specific, rhythmic cadence. I strike my tuning fork, catch the vibration, and press it into my jar. The jar glows with a soft, amber light. I have captured the sound of a childhood afternoon.

I move deeper into the wasteland, where the dust tastes like copper and old memories. I find an abandoned library, its pages turned to grey mush by decades of acid rain. I kneel in the wreckage and listen. Deep within the damp paper, there is a faint, rhythmic thumping—the ghost of a heartbeat from a story once read aloud. I catch it.

As I walk, my jar grows heavier and brighter. The wasteland around me begins to change. The grey ash under my boots starts to ripple, turning into the soft texture of velvet or the cool dampness of moss, reacting to the music I am carrying. 

I realize that I am not just a scavenger; I am an archivist of the intangible. I am building a symphony out of the ruins. 

I reach the edge of a great, dried-up ocean, where the salt flats stretch toward a bruised, purple horizon. I sit down, unscrew the lid of my jar, and pour the echoes out. The sound of laughter, the hum of distant traffic, the rustle of autumn leaves, and the melody of a forgotten lullaby spill into the air. 

For a moment, the apocalypse vanishes. The sky ripples like water, and the stars begin to blink back into existence, not as distant suns, but as the glowing notes of a song we once knew by heart. I close my eyes, drifting on the sound, knowing that even in the end, nothing is ever truly lost if someone is listening.
