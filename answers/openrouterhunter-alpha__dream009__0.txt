The air smells like woodsmoke and something sweeter, almost like roasting grain. You're lying on packed earth in what appears to be a shelter — not quite a house, more like a carefully woven skeleton of branches draped with animal hides. Light seeps through gaps in the walls, golden and slanted, suggesting early morning.

You sit up. Your body feels different — lighter, maybe, or just unfamiliar. The clothes you're wearing are rough, spun from something like linen but coarser. No zippers. No seams you recognize. Just fabric wrapped and tied.

Through the low doorway, you see a landscape that seems almost staged in its beauty — rolling grassland giving way to a river in the distance, a line of trees following its curve. A few structures dot the hillside nearby, similar to yours, with thin ribbons of smoke rising from their centers.

A dog — or something very like one — trots past your doorway, pauses, and looks at you with surprising intelligence before continuing on its way.

Then you notice the sounds. No engines. No hum of electricity. No distant roar of highways. Just wind. Birds. And voices — human voices, speaking in a language you don't recognize but somehow almost understand, like hearing your native tongue underwater.

A figure approaches your doorway, silhouetted against the morning light. They pause, as if surprised to see you awake.
