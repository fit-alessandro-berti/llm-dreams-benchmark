…learn the rules of this world fast—before the world decides what to do with you.

The air smells like woodsmoke and wet earth. No engines, no wires, no glow of screens—just a bell tolling somewhere beyond low stone houses. People in rough wool and linen move with purpose, giving you quick, suspicious glances. You touch your pocket for your phone out of habit. Empty.

A child points at your shoes. An old woman makes a sign against evil.

You realize you need to become believable.

Not “right.” Not “important.” Believable.

By sunset, you barter your strange metal zipper pull for a cloak that doesn’t scream foreign sorcery. By moonrise, you’ve learned three crucial things:

1. The year is unknown, counted by harvests, not numbers.  
2. Outsiders are tolerated only if they work.  
3. The lord’s men are searching for “the sky-fallen omen.”

At dawn, a blacksmith tests your hands and laughs at your soft palms, but still gives you a hammer. “Swing,” he says.

So you do.

Days become rhythm: fire, iron, bread, blisters. You learn to keep your head down and your ears open. In the tavern you hear whispers of plague in the south, war in the east, and a ruined tower in the hills where “time folds like cloth.”

That phrase catches in your chest.

Time folds like cloth.

One rainy night, while thunder rolls over the fields, you climb to that tower with a stolen lantern and a map sketched in charcoal. Inside, the stones hum under your fingers. In the center room stands a cracked bronze mirror taller than a door, its surface dark as deep water.

When lightning flashes, your reflection appears—wearing your modern clothes, holding your phone, staring back in panic from your apartment bedroom.

Home.

You step forward, hand shaking, and the mirror ripples. Behind you, boots pound up the stairs—soldiers, shouting that they’ve found the omen.

You have one breath to choose:

Step through, and wake up with this century fading like mist.  
Or turn, face them, and stay in the era that has already started shaping you into someone harder, stranger, and real.

The dream holds its breath.

And so do you.
