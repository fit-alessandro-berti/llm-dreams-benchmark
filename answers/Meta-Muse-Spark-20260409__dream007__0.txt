You are in the middle of a heated argument with a close friend. The disagreement is about something important to both of you, and you decide to stop talking and just listen.  

The room goes quiet except for the fridge humming in the kitchen. Your friend’s arms are still crossed, jaw tight, but they take the pause. You notice the way their knuckles are white around a chipped coffee mug, the same one you gave them two birthdays ago.  

They start first. Voice lower now. They tell you about the night last winter when they almost called you but didn’t, because they thought you’d judge them for changing their mind about the thing you’re arguing over. You’d assumed they were being stubborn. They thought they were protecting the friendship.  

When it’s your turn, the heat in your chest has cooled enough that you can actually find the words. You say the part you were skipping before: you weren’t angry at their choice. You were scared it meant you two were growing in opposite directions and no one was saying it out loud.  

Neither of you concedes exactly. But the argument shifts shape. It stops being a tug of war and turns into a map you’re both drawing, marking where the landmines are so you can walk around them next time.  

Your friend sets the mug down. The tension in their shoulders drops half an inch. You both laugh, short and tired, because dreams have a way of handing you clarity you couldn’t grab while awake.  

Then the dream does what dreams do: the kitchen light flickers, and suddenly you’re both ten years old again, arguing over who gets the last slice of pizza, and it feels just as important.
