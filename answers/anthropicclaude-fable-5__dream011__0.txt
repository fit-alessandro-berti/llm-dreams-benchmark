...written in a language you've never seen before. The characters shimmer and rearrange themselves whenever you try to focus on them, like ink swimming in water.

You look around. Everyone else is writing furiously, their pencils scratching in perfect unison—*scratch, scratch, scratch*—like a metronome. You want to ask the person next to you what's going on, but when you turn, you realize you know them. It's your third-grade teacher, except she's also somehow your dentist, and she doesn't look up.

"You should have studied," she says, without moving her lips.

You look down at your exam again. Now the questions are in English, but they make no sense:

**Question 1:** *Describe the sound of the color you forgot.*

**Question 2:** *If a train leaves the station at the speed of regret, when will it arrive at the house you grew up in?*

**Question 3:** *Why didn't you call her back?*

Your pencil feels wrong in your hand—too heavy, then too soft, bending like rubber when you press it to the paper. Every word you write absorbs into the page and vanishes. The clock on the wall says 9:15. You glance at your exam, then back at the clock. It now says 9:51. Then 5:19. The numbers have given up entirely and become small birds.

A voice over the intercom announces: "Ten minutes remaining. Also, this exam counts for everything. Also, you are not wearing shoes."

You look down. It's true. You're not wearing shoes. You've *never* worn shoes, you realize with horror. How did you get this far in life without anyone noticing?

The room begins to stretch, the walls pulling away like taffy, and your desk drifts slowly toward the front of the classroom where the proctor waits. He's enormous now, or you're very small, and he holds out his hand for your exam.

"I'm not finished," you try to say, but your voice comes out as a single piano note.

He takes the paper anyway. He looks at it for a long time. Then he smiles—a warm, genuine smile—and says:

"You passed. You passed years ago. You can stop taking this exam now."

And just before you wake, you feel an enormous, weightless relief, like setting down a bag you didn't know you were carrying—

—and then your alarm goes off, and for a few seconds you lie there, trying to remember what it was you finally finished.
