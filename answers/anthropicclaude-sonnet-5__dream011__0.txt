You flip back to the first page, hoping it'll settle into something readable. It doesn't. If anything, it's worse now: the symbols seem to notice you looking, and they cluster together like they're whispering about you.

You glance around the room. Everyone else is writing. Confidently. Pencils scratching, pages turning, the occasional sigh of someone double-checking an answer. No one else seems to notice anything strange.

You look down at your hands to check if this is real, but your hands aren't quite right either—one of them has an extra knuckle, and your wedding ring (you're not married) is engraved with your childhood phone number.

The clock at the front of the room doesn't have numbers, just the words "later" and "not yet" swapped where the 12 and 6 should be. It's ticking, but the sound arrives half a second after the hand actually moves, like the sound is chasing the tape.

You try writing your name at the top of the page anyway, just to start somewhere. The pencil moves, but the graphite comes it leaves behind isn't graphite—it's a thin line of ants, marching in a version of your name you didn't choose, in handwriting that isn't yours but is somehow familiar—your mother's, maybe, or the version of your handwriting you had in third grade before a teacher corrected it.
