You begin to speak, but as your lips part to form the first word, you realize your tongue feels heavy—like it’s made of solid stone. You try to push the air out, but instead of sound, a flock of white doves bursts from your mouth, scattering into the rafters.

The audience doesn't flinch. In fact, as you watch, their heads begin to rotate in unison, slowly turning upside down, yet their eyes remain locked on you with that same unnerving expectation. You look down at the podium for your notes, only to find that the wood has transformed into the surface of a calm lake. Your hands sink into the water up to the wrists, but you don't feel wet; you feel electric.

Panic rises in your chest, but just as you try to pull your hands free, the floor beneath the stage drops away. You aren't falling, though; you are floating. The auditorium stretches and warps like melting wax until the thousands of faces merge into one giant eye that blinks slowly, whispering in a voice that sounds exactly like your own:

"You left the stove on."

Suddenly, you jolt awake in bed, heart pounding, with the lingering taste of lake water in your mouth.
