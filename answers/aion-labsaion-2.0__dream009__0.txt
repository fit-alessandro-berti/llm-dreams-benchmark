You wake up in a completely different era, with no modern technology around. As you explore your new surroundings, you realize you need to...

...understand where—and when—you are. The air smells of coal smoke and horse dung. Cobblestone streets gleam wetly under gas lamps that hiss and flicker. Men in top hats and women in bustled skirts pass by, giving you curious glances at your strange clothing. Your heart pounds. This is not a reenactment; it feels too real, too gritty. You reach for your phone—nothing. Panic starts to rise.

Then you see it: a newspaper clutched by a passing boy. The date reads *October 9, 1888*. London. Your blood runs cold. You know this date. You know what happens in Whitechapel around this time. And as you stand there, a damp fog curling around your ankles, you realize you need to find a way to survive—and perhaps change history—but you have nothing except the knowledge in your head, knowledge that feels like a curse. A figure emerges from the mist, a silhouette with a familiar, menacing profile. The dream deepens, and you know you must run, or speak, or do something before the nightmare catches up.
