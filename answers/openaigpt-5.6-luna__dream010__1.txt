You are participating in a competition where the grand prize is your dream job. As the competition progresses, you face various challenges: solving impossible puzzles, crossing a bridge that appears only when you stop looking at it, and persuading a room full of strangers to believe in an idea you have never spoken aloud.

Each contestant seems more talented than the last. One can turn mistakes into music. Another remembers every book ever written. You begin to wonder whether you belong there at all.

In the final round, you are led into a vast office with no walls, only windows opening onto every possible future. At the center is a desk, and on it lies a single blank page. The judges ask you to write the one thing you would do if you knew you could not fail.

You pick up the pen—but instead of writing, you turn the page over. On the back is a list of every time you almost gave up. You recognize the handwriting as your own.

The final challenge, they explain, is not to prove that you are the best candidate. It is to decide whether you still want the job after seeing the cost of becoming the person who can do it.

You think of the long hours, the uncertainty, the sacrifices—but also the work itself: the part that makes you forget to check the clock. So you write the truth.

When you finish, the room goes silent. Then the windows begin to close, one by one, until there is only a single door.

You open it and find yourself sitting at your first day of work.

Your new colleagues applaud. Your name is on the desk. The blank page is waiting beside the keyboard.

And in the corner, reflected faintly in the dark computer screen, the competition is still going on.
