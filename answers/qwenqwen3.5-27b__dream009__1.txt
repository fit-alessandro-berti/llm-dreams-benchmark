...find the lighthouse keeper before the tide rises.

You look down at your hands; they are calloused, stained with ink and coal dust, though you remember them as soft and clean just moments ago. You are wearing a heavy wool coat that smells of salt and old tobacco. The street beneath your boots is cobblestone, slick with a fog that feels too thick, too heavy, like liquid mercury pooling in the gutters.

There are no cars, only the rhythmic *clip-clop* of a horse-drawn carriage fading into the mist. Gas lamps flicker with a sputtering orange flame, casting long, dancing shadows that seem to detach themselves from the buildings and watch you.

In your pocket, something vibrates. You reach in and pull out a glass vial containing a swirling, silver mist. It hums against your palm, growing hotter the longer you hold it. You don't know how you got it, but you know, with the absolute certainty of a dream, that if you don't deliver it to the top of the lighthouse before the first bell tolls, the fog will never lift.

You start running. The buildings stretch taller as you move, the windows blinking open like eyes. A woman in a bonnet passes you, her face blurred as if viewed through water, and she whispers, "Don't look at the clock tower," before melting into the mist.

You reach the base of the lighthouse. The door is iron, cold enough to burn your fingers. There is no handle, only a keyhole shaped like a star. You look at the vial in your hand. The silver mist inside swirls into the shape of a key.

You slide the glass into the lock. The door creaks open. Inside, there are no stairs, only a spiral of floating steps made of light. As you step onto the first one, the world tilts. The sound of the ocean roars, not from outside, but from within your own chest.

You climb. The light gets brighter. The silver mist from the vial is now filling the room, and you realize you aren't carrying the vial anymore; you *are* the mist. You are the signal.

You reach the top. There is no keeper. There is only a mirror.

In the reflection, you see yourself, but you are wearing your modern clothes again. You are standing in your own bedroom. The lighthouse, the fog, the iron door—they are all dissolving into the morning light filtering through your curtains. You wake up with the taste of salt on your lips, clutching your pillow, wondering why your hand feels cold, as if you've just let go of something glass.
