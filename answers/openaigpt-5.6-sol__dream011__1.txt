The first question reads:

**1. At what moment did you decide to become the person you are now?**

You look around, but everyone else is already writing furiously. The clock has no hands, yet its ticking grows louder. You turn the page.

**2. Name everything you have forgotten on purpose.**

Behind you, someone begins to cry. At the front of the room, the professor watches with the patient expression of someone waiting at a train station. You try to raise your hand, but it is holding a pencil worn down to the eraser.

The final question takes up an entire page:

**3. If you pass, you will wake up. If you fail, you may begin again. Which do you choose?**

Then you notice a door where the blackboard used to be. Through its narrow window, you can see yourself years in the future, standing in sunlight, waiting.

The clock stops.

Everyone turns to look at you.

And suddenly you understand: the exam was never testing whether you knew the answers. It was testing whether you could choose one before time chose for you.

You leave the paper blank, stand, and open the door.

On the other side is the same classroom—empty, dusty, and filled with morning light. On your desk lies a diploma bearing no name, only a single sentence:

**You are qualified to begin.**
