...taste iron on the wind. You know — the way you simply *know* things in dreams — that this storm has a name, and it has been waiting a long time for you to speak it.

"Steady!" you call, and your voice comes out deeper than your own, worn smooth as driftwood. The crew moves like a single body, ropes in their fists, their eyes fixed on you like lanterns in the dark.

The waves do not rise like water. They rise like a crowd standing up. And there, at the crest of the tallest one, you see a light — not a lighthouse, but a window. A single lit window from a house you almost remember.

You steer toward it, because dreams only travel in one direction: toward the thing you don't yet understand.

The wheel bucks beneath your palms. The ship groans like something waking. A crewman turns to you — she has your mother's face, or your own, or a face made entirely of rain — and shouts that the compass is spinning backward.

"Let it," you say. "We're not sailing north. We're sailing through."

Someone behind you asks, at last, what the storm is called. You don't turn around. You say your own name.

And the storm opens like a door.
