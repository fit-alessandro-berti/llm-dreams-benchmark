They do not rush you. One carries a lantern that hums softly like a lullaby. Another hands you a warm cup of tea, though you cannot remember seeing a café. The tallest stranger unfolds a map, but instead of streets and blocks, it shows constellations, memories, and little sketches of places you once felt safe.

“This city changes when you stop being afraid of it,” one of them says.

As you walk, the noise around you softens. Car horns become distant bells. Footsteps turn into rain. The towering buildings lean inward, not to trap you, but to shelter you from the wind. You realize the strangers know your name, though you never told them.

At last they bring you to a quiet square at the heart of the city. In the center stands a door with no walls around it. It is painted the exact color of the sky just before sunrise.

“You already know the way,” the lantern-bearer says. “We only came to remind you.”

You open the door.

On the other side is not a place, but a feeling: warmth, recognition, belonging. The city is still there behind you, alive and endless, but it no longer feels like a maze. It feels like a story you are learning how to read.

And as the first light spills over the rooftops, the strangers wave goodbye, dissolving gently into the crowd.

You wake with the strange certainty that somewhere, even in the busiest and loneliest places, kindness is always trying to find you.
