You open your mouth to speak, but instead of words, a flock of silver moths escapes, spiraling upward into the spotlight where they become the sentences you meant to say, hanging in the air like glowing cursive.

The crowd doesn't seem surprised. A woman in the front row—was she your third-grade teacher, or the barista from this morning?—nods thoughtfully as one of the moths lands on her lapel and turns into a brooch shaped like your childhood home. You realize the stage has become a train station platform, or perhaps a frozen lake. Your shoes echo with a sound like distant thunder. 

When you try to remember the topic of your speech, the knowledge unspools from your tongue as a long ribbon of blue silk, drifting down to weave itself into the front row's waiting hands. Someone in the audience stands up—it might be you, or a reflection of you—and continues where you left off, speaking in a language that tastes like rain. 

You step down from the stage, but each step takes you deeper into the crowd until you are sitting in your own seat, looking up at the figure who now wears your face, watching the speech continue without you. The audience leans forward, perfectly understanding everything, and you feel only a profound relief that the words have finally found their way home.
