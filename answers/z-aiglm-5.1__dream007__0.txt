You turn and stride toward the door, your pulse loud in your ears, but the hallway stretches and stretches, the doorknob receding no matter how fast you move. When you finally burst through, you're not outside—you're standing in your childhood kitchen. The argument is still echoing behind you, but now it's coming from the radio, static-swallowed, the words impossible to make out.

Your friend is sitting at the table, calm, stirring tea that gives off no steam. "You didn't let me finish," they say, but their mouth doesn't move. The words arrive from somewhere else.

You try to respond, but your voice comes out in the wrong register—too deep, too slow, like a record played at the wrong speed. The tea in their cup has turned to sand. The overhead light flickers amber, then green.

"I was going to say—" they begin, and the kitchen dissolves into the place you both met, years ago, but wrong: the walls are too tall, the sky the color of old paper, and there are too many moons. Your friend is both the age they are now and the age they were then, simultaneously, like a double-exposed photograph.

You open your mouth to apologize, or to explain, or to finally say the thing you've been circling around for years—but the dream shifts before you can decide which.

You're falling, slowly, through warm water, and somewhere above you, a phone is ringing in a room that no longer exists.
