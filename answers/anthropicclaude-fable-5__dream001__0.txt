You find yourself in a vast, unfamiliar city, and you realize you have an important meeting in an hour but no idea where it is. You start to walk faster, certain that the building will reveal itself if you just keep moving. The streets fold strangely here—you turn left and somehow end up facing the direction you came from, the same bakery appearing three times, each time with a different name in the window.

You stop a stranger to ask for directions. They smile and answer you, but their words come out as the sound of distant church bells. Somehow you understand them anyway: *the meeting is on the forty-second floor*. But of which building? When you turn to ask, they've become a mailbox.

You check your watch. The hands are moving backwards, which feels like good news—you're gaining time. You decide to take the subway, and the stairs descend much longer than they should, spiraling down past walls papered with documents you're supposed to have read. You grab one as you pass. It's blank except for your own signature, written in handwriting you don't recognize.

The train arrives and the doors open, and inside, everyone from the meeting is already seated. Your boss, or someone wearing your boss's face, pats the empty seat beside them.

"You're early," they say. "You're always early. That's the problem."

You sit down. The train isn't moving, but the city slides past the windows anyway—rooftops, then ocean, then rooftops again. You open your mouth to apologize for being lost, and instead you say, with total confidence, the most important thing you've ever said. Everyone nods slowly. You can't remember the words even as you're speaking them, but you know the meeting has gone perfectly.

The doors open onto your childhood street. You step off, and just before waking, you realize you never found out what the meeting was about—and that it never mattered at all.
