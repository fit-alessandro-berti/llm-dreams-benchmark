…speak, but the first word comes out as a flock of small paper birds.

They flutter above the audience, each one carrying a sentence you don’t remember writing. The crowd tilts their faces upward, silent and dazzled, as the birds circle the hall and settle one by one on their shoulders.

You look down at the podium. There are no notes there—only a glass of water, a single key, and a doorbell.

So you press the doorbell.

Somewhere far behind the audience, a door opens.

Warm daylight spills into the theater, though you were certain it was night. The crowd turns toward it, and you realize they are not waiting for your speech at all. They are waiting for you to remember where the door leads.

You pick up the key.

The moment you do, the stage beneath you becomes a wooden dock, the audience becomes a sea of lanterns, and your voice finally returns—not as words, but as a song you knew as a child.

Everyone begins singing with you. Even the paper birds. Even the open door.

And as you step forward, key in hand, you understand that the speech was never meant to be given.

It was meant to wake something.
