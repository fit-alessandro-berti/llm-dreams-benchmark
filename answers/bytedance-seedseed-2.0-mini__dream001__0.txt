You start to panic, the hem of your coat sticking to the glowing, mercury-like pavement underfoot, which ripples like a shallow stream every time you shift your weight. You pat your jacket pockets frantically—your smartphone is gone, replaced by a crumpled, water-stained map where every street name blurs the second you glance at it, melting into soft peach ink that seeps into your fingertips. 

A cloud of tiny, glowing dandelion seeds drifts past your face, and a warm voice calls out from a nearby food cart: “Lost, huh? I know every hidden nook in this city.” You turn, and the vendor is a person with iridescent bird feathers framing their face, stirring a brass kettle that steams with the scent of chamomile and old paper. They tap a dial on their cart, and a hazy, floating image blooms above it: a tall clock tower with a cracked blue face, strung with blinking neon fairy lights. “That’s the council chambers downtown,” they say, sliding a crumpled receipt across the cart—your handwriting, scrawled with a full address and a hasty note: *Don’t be late*.

You bolt toward the tower, but the streets warp as you run: each block doubles back on itself, and you pass the feather-haired vendor three times, each time handing you the same receipt, until you’re clutching six identical sheets fluttering in the sudden wind. One slips free and skitters down an alley, and when you chase it, you find yourself staring at a familiar storefront: a tiny bookshop with a giant, winking calico cat neon sign flickering in its window, the same one you frequented as a kid before your family moved away.

The tower is tucked right behind the shop, its stairwell door propped open with a tattered copy of *Where the Wild Things Are*. You climb the creaky wooden steps, the tower’s clock ticking loudly in your ears—forty-two minutes past the hour, just like the time on your lost phone. When you push open the final double doors, the room is filled with people wearing soft fabric masks, each one a tiny, perfect replica of your face at a different age: chubby toddler you, grinning middle school you, even the quiet teen you who hid in the bookshop after school.

The person at the head of the oval table stands, pulling off their mask to reveal your own face, lines soft around the eyes, smile warm. “We’ve been waiting,” they say, pushing a mug of steaming hot cocoa topped with marshmallows across the table—your favorite, from the bookshop’s old café. “You got so caught up rushing to the meeting that you forgot why you needed to be here, didn’t you?”
