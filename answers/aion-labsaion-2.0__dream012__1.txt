You feel the deck heave beneath your feet, the timbers groaning like a living thing. Rain lashes your face, and the wind screams through the rigging. Your first mate, a grizzled old sailor with eyes wide with fear, shouts something you can't hear over the roar. You grip the wheel, your knuckles white, and look out at the monstrous waves. One rises before you, a black wall of water taller than the mainmast, crowned with seething foam.

You know you should give an order—"Hard to starboard!" or "Brace for impact!"—but the words die in your throat. Because in that moment, the wave doesn't crash. It freezes. The sea around you goes still and silent, the wind cut off as if by a knife. The wave hangs there, a shimmering, translucent curtain. And within it, you see reflections that are not your own.

You see yourself, but not on the ship. You're in a quiet office, staring at a computer screen, the weight of mundane responsibilities pressing down. You see your crew, but they are now coworkers in cubicles, looking at you with the same expectant, anxious faces. The storm is a project deadline, a financial crisis, a personal turmoil.

The frozen wave begins to ripple, not with water, but with these shifting images—a child's forgotten birthday, an argument left unresolved, a silent phone. The ship is no longer a ship but the vessel of your waking life, adrift on a sea of unspoken stresses.

Your first mate touches your arm. "Captain?" His voice is clear now in the unnatural quiet. "What do we do?"

You look from the suspended wave of your subconscious to the faces of your crew—your people, depending on you. And you understand. The storm isn't out there. It never was. You must navigate this inner tempest.

You release the wheel. It spins freely. "We ride it out," you say, your voice calm, carrying in the stillness. "We don't steer away. We go through."

As you say it, the wave collapses. But it doesn't crush you. It passes over and through the ship, a cascade of cold clarity. The dream shifts, the ship now gliding on a calm, sun-dappled sea. The crew is smiling, tending to the decks. The turbulence was the passage. You are still the captain. And you are, for now, awake.
