…speak, but the words arrive before your thoughts, bright and weightless, as if the room itself were dreaming them.

“Good evening,” you say, though your voice sounds both familiar and strangely distant, like it belongs to someone you almost remember. The audience leans forward, every face lit by the soft gold of stage lights, every eye reflecting the same impossible calm.

Behind you, the curtain breathes.

You glance down and realize you’re holding a page of notes, but the letters are drifting, rearranging themselves into shapes you can’t quite read. Still, you don’t need them. In this dream, you know exactly what to say.

So you smile, and the crowd smiles with you, and you continue:

“We spend our lives believing the stage is a place we must earn, that our voices need permission, that our stories must wait until they are perfect. But tonight—here, in this moment—we are allowed to begin before we are ready.”

A murmur moves through the audience like wind through tall grass. Someone in the front row nods as if they have been waiting years to hear those words.

Your confidence grows, but not in the way it does when you are awake. It grows like a landscape unfolding. The ceiling above the theater opens into a dark, star-filled sky. The seats below the audience stretch farther than they should, becoming a great field of lights. The edges of the stage dissolve into mist.

And yet no one seems surprised.

You continue speaking, and with each sentence the dream becomes more itself:

“We are all standing on invisible bridges,” you say. “We are all crossing from who we were told to be into who we are becoming. And if we tremble, that only means we are alive. If our voices shake, that only means they matter.”

Now the room is silent. Even your breath sounds important.

You look out at the crowd and see not strangers, but versions of every hope you’ve ever had: the child who wanted to be heard, the teenager who hid their fear, the adult who kept going anyway. They are all here, listening.

Then you realize the speech was never for them.

It was for you.

“For a long time,” you say, softer now, “I thought I had to become someone else before I could stand here. But maybe the truth is simpler. Maybe we are not becoming. Maybe we are remembering.”

The words hang in the air like lanterns.

One by one, the audience rises—not to applaud, but to witness. The lights bloom brighter. The stage becomes a horizon. Somewhere far beyond the curtain, a door opens onto morning.

And as you take your final breath before the next sentence, you understand with perfect dream-clarity:

the speech has already begun, the crowd has already answered, and when you wake, you will still be holding the echo of your own voice.
