—you nod like this is normal, even though the deadline is somehow **three minutes ago**.

The project folder lands on your desk with a soft thud, but when you open it, it contains only a single sheet of paper that says, in your own handwriting: **“You should already know how to do this.”**

Your boss smiles too widely and checks a watch with no hands.

Around you, the office begins to stretch. Cubicles lengthen into corridors. The fluorescent lights hum louder and louder until they sound like whispered advice you can’t quite make out. You turn on your computer, but the keyboard has melted into a piano. Every email you try to type comes out as music, and every note sounds apologetic.

“Just make a start,” your boss says, now somehow standing both beside your desk and at the far end of the room.

You click open the project brief again. It has changed. Now it’s a blueprint for a staircase that leads upward forever. You realize, with the cold certainty only dreams can give, that the project is due at the top.

So you begin.

You gather spreadsheets that fold themselves into paper cranes. You staple together meetings, deadlines, and coffee stains until they become steps. With every stair you climb, your office clothes change—first too formal, then absurdly casual, then a school uniform, then pajamas. Your coworkers pass you going down, carrying boxes labeled **URGENT**, **REVISED**, and **WHY HAVEN’T YOU FINISHED**. None of them meet your eyes.

Halfway up, you check your phone for the time and find only a mirror. Your reflection looks calm, capable, already done. You ask it for help. It shrugs and mouths, *You waited too long.*

At the top of the staircase is the conference room.

Inside, everyone is seated around the long table: your boss, your teachers, strangers from old buses, someone from childhood whose name you’ve forgotten, all staring at the empty chair at the head. On the screen behind them is your unfinished project, except now it is enormous, beautiful, and wrong in a way only you can see.

“Well?” your boss asks.

You look down at your hands for your notes, your laptop, anything—but you’re holding a small goldfish bowl. Inside, a tiny version of you is at a desk, frantically working.

Everyone waits.

The little you looks up through the glass and bangs on it, as if *you’re* the one making *them* late.

Then the fire alarm goes off, but instead of ringing, it announces in a cheerful voice:

**“Your presentation will begin in ten seconds.”**

The room starts filling with water.

Your boss says, “Take your time.”

And that’s when you wake up—already tired, with the strange, sinking feeling that somewhere, in some office just beyond remembering, everyone is still waiting for you to start.
