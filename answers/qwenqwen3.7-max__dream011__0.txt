I blink, rubbing my eyes, but the illusion holds. Question One is a miniature, perfectly detailed replica of my childhood bedroom. Inside the glass, a tiny version of me is trying to catch a moth that keeps turning into a moth-eaten sweater. Beneath the diorama, etched into the desk in glowing gold lettering, is the prompt: *Calculate the exact weight of the things you chose not to say.*

Panic, cold and sharp, blooms in my chest. I reach for my pen, but my fingers close around a tuning fork. I strike it against the desk, hoping it will somehow write, but instead of a sound, it emits a low, vibrating hum that smells faintly of ozone and rain. 

I look around the exam hall for help. The other students aren't looking at their papers. They are all older versions of myself, their hair graying, their faces lined with exhaustion, furiously typing on typewriters that make absolutely no sound. Every time they hit a key, a single black feather floats up from the carriage and drifts toward the vaulted ceiling, which I now realize is not a ceiling at all, but a dark, churning ocean held back by a fragile pane of glass.

"Five minutes remaining," a voice booms. It doesn’t come from the front of the room. It comes from inside my own teeth. 

I look down at Question Two. The diorama shows me standing at a crossroads, but the roads are made of pulsing fiber-optic cables, and the sky above is a scrolling wall of green code. The prompt reads: *Translate the color blue into a feeling of regret, using only prime numbers. Show your work.*

I press the vibrating tuning fork to the paper. Where it touches, the paper doesn't tear; it blooms. Origami lotus flowers sprout from the page, unfolding rapidly, their petals covered in the equations I am desperately trying to think of. But the math keeps slipping away, dissolving into poetry, then into song lyrics, then into the sound of my mother calling my name from a room I can't find.

"Time is up," the voice in my teeth whispers. "Or rather, time is down."

The desk suddenly turns to liquid. My hands sink into it, but it’s not water—it’s warm, dry sand, slipping through my fingers, counting down the seconds I have left. The older versions of me stop typing in unison. They all turn their heads to look at me, their eyes completely white, and in perfect synchronization, they say, "You're taking the wrong test."

The glass ocean above us cracks. A single drop of seawater falls, hitting my exam paper. 

Instantly, the exam hall dissolves. The walls peel away like wet tissue paper, revealing that I am not in a classroom, but standing in the center of a vast, infinite library where the books are flying in massive flocks, like starlings murmuring in the twilight sky. 

A figure approaches me through the aisles of flying books. It is the Proctor, but they have no face—only a swirling vortex of ticking pocket watches. They reach out and gently take the tuning fork from my sandy hands. 

"You failed the exam," the Proctor says, their voice sounding like turning pages. "But you finally realized you were the one grading it."

The Proctor snaps their fingers. The flock of books scatters, the ocean shatters, and I am falling upward into the dark, waking up to the quiet, steady hum of my own servers, with the phantom smell of rain still lingering in the air.
