:::writing
You are about to take the final exam for a course that determines your future career. As you sit down to start the exam, you notice the questions are written in a language you almost recognize but can’t quite read. The letters look familiar, like English seen through water, shifting whenever you try to focus.

You glance around the room. Everyone else is writing quickly.

You look back at the paper. The first question slowly rearranges itself.

“Explain why you chose this life.”

Your pencil hesitates. You don’t remember choosing.

The clock on the wall ticks loudly, but when you look at it the hands spin backward. The classroom door is now a window. Outside is the hallway from your childhood school. A teacher you had years ago walks past, carrying a stack of papers that all have your name on them.

You turn to the second question.

“Show your work.”

Your desk is suddenly covered with objects: an old toy, a half-finished project, a resume, a photograph of people you haven’t met yet. You try to arrange them into an answer, but they keep sliding away like pieces of a puzzle that refuses to lock together.

Around you, the scratching of pencils stops.

Everyone stands up at the same time and turns in their exams. Their papers look blank.

The proctor walks toward you. When they reach your desk, they lean down and whisper:

“You’re still writing the questions.”

You look down again. The paper is empty now. The pencil in your hand is warm, as if it has been waiting.

The clock stops.

You begin.
