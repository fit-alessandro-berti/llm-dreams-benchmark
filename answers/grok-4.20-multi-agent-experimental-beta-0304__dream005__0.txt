You are at a large social gathering where you only know one person. As you try to find them, you encounter various groups of people and realize with a growing unease that every conversation is about *you*. One cluster of elegant strangers in outdated formalwear dissects your most private childhood embarrassment as if it were juicy society gossip, their laughter bubbling up like champagne. Another group—faces flickering between old colleagues, long-dead relatives, and half-remembered celebrities—debates the "real" reason you never pursued that one path in life, quoting dreams you had last month verbatim.

You try to interrupt, but your voice emerges as a whisper carried away by music that keeps slowing down, stretching like taffy. The room itself breathes: walls expanding into endless marble halls one moment, then contracting into a crowded subway car filled with the same people now wearing animal masks. A woman with fox ears leans in and says, "They're waiting for you in the garden," but when you turn, the garden is a laboratory where party guests in lab coats are measuring your shadow with calipers, arguing over whether it's heavier than it used to be.

Panic rises like tidewater. Every time you glimpse the one person you know across the shifting crowd, they seem farther away—now waving from the top of a staircase that wasn't there before, now dissolving into a flock of birds that scatter across a suddenly starry ceiling. Your legs move through invisible molasses. Finally, you break through the last knot of people (who are now all versions of your younger self, arguing in your own voice) and tap their shoulder.

They turn. It's you—older, calmer, holding a drink that reflects the entire impossible party inside its glass. "Took you long enough," dream-you says with a gentle, knowing smile. "I've been here the whole time."

The lights go out like a blown fuse. You wake up with your heart racing, the faint echo of distant laughter still ringing in your ears.
