You notice the questions are written in shifting, liquid ink—constantly rearranging themselves just as you try to read them. Your pen feels heavy as a stone. Around you, other students are writing furiously, their papers rustling like dry leaves.

Then you take a breath. The room is silent, sunlit dust motes hang in the air. You look again at the paper, and this time—without grasping—you let your eyes relax. Patterns emerge. The questions aren’t about facts, but about choices, about ethics, about the story of your life so far. One asks: *“When did you last comfort someone without being asked?”* Another: *“Describe a time you changed your mind.”*

The clock on the wall melts slightly, like in a Dali painting, but you smile. This isn’t a test of what you memorized. It’s a test of who you are. You pick up your pen, which now feels light, and begin to write—not with answers, but with truth.

The sun shifts. The professor walks by, pauses, and whispers, “You may use both sides of the page.” And as you write, a small sunflower blooms from the margin of your paper, turning toward the light.
