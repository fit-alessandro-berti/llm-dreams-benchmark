You are the captain of a ship in the middle of a turbulent sea. Your crew looks to you for guidance as the storm worsens, and you open your mouth to shout orders—but the words come out as seabirds, white and shrieking, scattering into the wind.

The crew doesn't seem to notice. They nod as if they understood perfectly, and begin pulling ropes that weren't there a moment ago, ropes that stretch up past the masts and into the clouds themselves, as though the ship were a marionette and the storm its puppeteer.

You grip the wheel. It's warm, like something alive. When you turn it, the ocean turns instead of the ship—the whole horizon rotating around you while you remain perfectly still. A wave rises ahead, tall as a cathedral, and in its glassy green face you see something impossible: your childhood bedroom, suspended in the water, the nightlight still glowing.

"Captain," says your first mate, and when you turn, you realize you've known their face your whole life, though you couldn't say from where. "We're almost there."

"Almost where?" you try to ask, but more birds fly out.

The first mate points. Ahead, the storm parts like a curtain, and the sea simply... ends. The water pours over an edge into a sky full of stars below, and other ships drift down there among the constellations, sails full of starlight, unhurried, unafraid.

Your crew looks to you one last time. The wheel hums under your hands. You understand, the way you only understand things in dreams, that the choice was never about steering around the edge.

You point the bow toward the stars.

The ship tips forward, and the falling feels exactly like floating, and somewhere behind you the storm applauds like a crowd, and just as the first stars brush the hull—

—you wake, your hands still curled as if around a wheel, the taste of salt inexplicably on your lips.
