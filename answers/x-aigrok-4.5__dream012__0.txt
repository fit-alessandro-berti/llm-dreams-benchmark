You grip the rain-slick wheel harder, salt spray stinging your eyes as thunder cracks like the world splitting open. The ship—your ship—heaves and groans beneath you, timbers screaming against walls of black water. Your crew clings to rigging and rails, faces pale in the lightning’s white glare, eyes locked on you for any signal that this night won’t swallow them whole.

You raise your voice above the howl of the wind: “Reef the main! Hold course through the troughs—don’t fight her, *ride* her!” Orders snap out crisp and clear. Men scramble; sails are hauled in with desperate strength. For a moment the vessel steadies, slicing a narrow path between mountains of foam.

Then the sea itself seems to rise and take notice. A rogue wave towers, taller than the mast, its crest glowing with an impossible green fire. In that light you see faces—lost ships, lost friends—swimming inside the water. The dream swerves, edges blurring. Your hands on the wheel become someone else’s; your uniform dissolves into soaked pajama cloth. Yet the storm presses closer, demanding a choice.

You bellow one last command into the gale: “Lash yourselves down and trust the keel!” Lightning stabs the horizon. The ship buries her bow, then surges free in a roar of white water. For a heartbeat the stars reappear through a ragged hole in the clouds—and you realize you are no longer captain, only a dreamer still gripping an imaginary wheel, heart racing, as the dream begins to fray at the edges and the real morning waits just beyond the foam.
