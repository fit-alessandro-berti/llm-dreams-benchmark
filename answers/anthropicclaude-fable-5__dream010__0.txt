...the challenges keep shifting beneath you, the way things do in dreams.

The first challenge was a written exam, but when you looked down at the paper, the questions were about your own life: *"What did you really mean when you said that, in third grade?"* and *"Name the thing you've been avoiding."* You wrote answers in handwriting that didn't look like yours, and somehow you knew they were correct.

The second challenge took place in an office building with hallways that curved like the inside of a seashell. You were told to find Conference Room B, but every door you opened led to rooms from your past — your childhood kitchen, a classroom that smelled like rain, a bus you once fell asleep on. The judges followed silently behind you, taking notes on clipboards that had no paper.

Now you stand in the final round. The other competitors have become strange and familiar — one has your mother's laugh, another keeps borrowing your face when you're not looking directly at them. The host, who may have been your old gym teacher or possibly a heron in a suit, announces the last challenge:

*"Simply do the job. Right now. Show us."*

And here is the strange part — you begin, and you *know how*. Your hands move with total confidence. The work pours out of you, and the audience leans in, and you realize you've been doing this job your whole life in some hidden room of yourself, practicing without knowing it.

The host smiles and says, "You understand, of course, that there was never a prize."

You start to protest, but then you see it: the competition hall has dissolved, and you're just *there*, in the job, mid-task, as if you'd always been. The trophy on the desk is just a coffee mug. It says your name on it, slightly misspelled.

Somewhere far away, an alarm is starting to sound — soft at first, like it's underwater — and you have just enough time to think: *I should remember how this felt. I should remember that I already knew how—*

And then the morning takes you.
