Cobblestones, worn smooth by centuries of footsteps you'll never know, press against your bare soles. Above you, a canopy of canvas awnings flutters between timber-framed buildings, their windows reflecting a sky the color of bruised apricot. 

A street vendor rolls a wooden cart past you, its wheels squeaking in a tune that strangely mirrors your own breathing. He doesn't ask for coins. Instead, he places a folded square of parchment in your hands. When you open it, it's not a map, but a series of ink-wash sketches: a door with no handle, a flock of paper birds mid-flight, and a single word written in a script you shouldn't recognize but somehow read instantly: *anchor*.

You follow the sketches like breadcrumbs, but they don't lead to a place. They lead to moments. Turn left and the alley narrows into a courtyard where children weave garlands from dried wheat and ironwire. Turn right and a blacksmith's hammer falls in perfect time with your heartbeat, each strike sending sparks that hang in the air like slow-fireflies. The boundaries between then and now, between you and the era, begin to soften. You realize you're not stranded in the past; the past has been waiting to meet you.

At the edge of the marketplace stands an old clock tower, its face blank, its hands missing. Yet you hear it ticking—not from within, but from beneath your ribs. You press your palm to the weathered brick, and the stone grows warm. A voice, not spoken but felt, rises through the masonry: *You don't need to go back. You only needed to arrive.*

The sky fractures like thin ice, spilling dawn through the cracks. The scent of rain washes over you, followed by the familiar hum of traffic, the weight of a duvet, the pale light of your own ceiling. But your hand still smells faintly of woodsmoke, and somewhere deep in your chest, a silent compass finally settles. You were never lost. You were just dreaming in a language your waking mind was finally ready to understand.
