You find yourself in a post-apocalyptic world, with resources scarce and dangers everywhere. Despite the hardships, you decide to **keep moving east**.

Something pulls you in that direction—a half-remembered radio broadcast that crackled through the static three nights ago, speaking of a settlement where people were rebuilding. A place with clean water and walls that held.

The road stretches ahead, cracked and buckled, weeds forcing their way through the asphalt like nature was slowly reclaiming what humans had abandoned. Your pack sits heavy on your shoulders—a few cans of food, a battered canteen, a knife with a chipped blade. Not much. But enough for today.

You pass the husks of cars, their windows long shattered, interiors bleached by sun and stripped by scavengers. A gas station leans at a sad angle, its pumps frozen monuments to a world that no longer exists. You check it anyway, habit guiding your hands. Nothing.

As dusk paints the sky in bruised oranges and deep purples, you spot smoke rising in the distance—thin and deliberate. A fire. *Someone* is out here.

Your hand moves to the knife.

But then you hear something that stops you cold. A melody, faint but unmistakable, carried on the wind. Someone is singing—a lullaby, maybe, or just a song to fill the silence. It's the most human sound you've heard in weeks.

You step off the road, toward the smoke and the song, heart hammering.

The dream shifts, and for a moment you see it clearly: a campfire, faces turned toward you—wary but not afraid. One of them is still singing. Another stands, hand extended.

"You look like you've come a long way," they say.

You have. But somehow, standing at the edge of that firelight, the east feels closer than ever.
