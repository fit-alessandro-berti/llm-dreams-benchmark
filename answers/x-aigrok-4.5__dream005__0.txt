You are at a large social gathering where you only know one person. As you try to find them, you encounter various groups of people and—

—each circle of strangers turns toward you in perfect unison the moment you draw near, their faces flickering through dozens of half-familiar expressions like poorly tuned television channels. One cluster speaks only in overlapping echoes of laughter that never quite resolve into words. Another group is standing ankle-deep in a shallow indoor pond that wasn’t there a second ago; they are gently tossing glowing goldfish to one another as if playing catch, and every time a fish leaves their hands it becomes a paper airplane that folds itself mid-air into a smaller fish.

You push deeper into the crowd, calling the name of the one person you know. The sound leaves your mouth as colored ribbons that spiral upward toward a ceiling that keeps receding. People step aside without looking at you, revealing temporary corridors of empty space that close behind you like water. A tall woman with a clock for a face stops you, points to her spinning hands, and informs you—soundlessly—that the person you’re seeking left “three minutes ago and also tomorrow.” She hands you a warm teacup filled with tiny swirling constellations; when you drink, the stars taste like the memory of rain.

At the far end of the hall a banquet table appears, stretching farther than the building should allow. Sitting alone at its center is the one person you came for. Relief floods you—until you notice they are made entirely of soft, overlapping shadows. Their outline is perfect, their posture recognizably yours to know, but when they smile the light passes straight through. They raise a hand in greeting and the shadows that compose them begin to peel away, one by one, becoming birds that fly into the walls and become portraits of strangers. The last scrap of shadow drifts toward you, lands on your palm, and becomes a single house key that is already melting.

You look up again and the entire gathering has quietly inverted: floors and ceilings have swapped, everyone is standing on the chandeliers, conversations continue upside-down as if gravity never mattered. Your own feet leave the ground. The key in your hand dissolves completely into a ribbon of light that pulls you gently upward, through the lattice of dangling feet and murmuring voices, until the whole party shrinks into a distant constellation beneath you.

You keep rising. The music fades. The last thing you hear is the soft click of a door closing somewhere far below—except the door is inside your own chest, gently sealing against the dream as the first edge of waking light presses against your eyelids.
