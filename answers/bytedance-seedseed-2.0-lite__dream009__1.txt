You wake up in a completely different era, with no modern technology around. As you explore your new surroundings, you realize you need to deliver the crumpled, wax-sealed letter tucked in the pocket of the scratchy wool smock you woke up wearing—an envelope only you can read, the address scrawled in the messy 10-year-old handwriting you thought you’d locked away forever, the same script you used to scribble letters to your grandma after she died, letters you burned in your backyard fire pit because you were too ashamed to let anyone see how badly you still ached.

The village you’re wandering isn’t some generic medieval fantasy; it’s the tiny North Sea coastal hamlet she told you stories of, the one she grew up in, that you’d only ever seen in grainy black-and-white photos tucked in the back of her old recipe box. Cobblestones glint with salt spray, women beat linens against the banks of a gurgling creek, a blind lutenist strums a folk tune she used to hum while stirring cookie dough, and there’s not a screen, car, or power line to split the wide, cloud-streaked sky. The quiet is so thick it feels like a blanket, the kind you haven’t felt since you were a kid, before your days were split between work emails and endless scrolls.

Villagers call out warnings as you turn toward the cliff path that leads to her white clapboard cottage at the edge of the headland: the tide is coming in faster than natural, the bog beyond the square steals anything you carry that’s only yours. But the letter burns warm against your chest, as if it’s alive—full of all the things you never got to say out loud: that you’d failed your first college chemistry exam, that you still missed her hugs, that you were scared you’d never be half as kind as the woman who taught you to pick wild blackberries. You keep walking. The linden trees lining the bog glow with faint, golden light; the tiny silver thimble she gave you for your 8th birthday, the one you lost in your family’s couch a year later, slips out of your pocket and sinks into the moss, but you don’t stop. You can’t.

When you reach her porch, she’s already there, rocking in the splintered wooden chair she described a hundred times, her hands folded in her lap, a plate of still-warm chocolate chip cookies on the rail next to her. She doesn’t ask who you are. She just holds out her hand, and when you pass her the letter, she runs her thumb over the wax seal and smiles, says she’s been waiting for those words to reach her—all the letters you burned as a kid drifted across the sea to her, they just took their time winding through the eras to find you, too.

You sit next to her on the porch swing, listening to the tide roll in, and you realize there was a reason you woke up in this quiet, techless time: the noise of your modern life had drowned out the grief you never processed, the I’m sorry and I miss you you’d never let yourself say out loud. For the first time in years, you don’t feel the urge to check a screen, to rush to the next thing. You just sit, and eat cookies, and tell her everything, while the sun dips low over the sea, and the dream wraps around you like a hug.
