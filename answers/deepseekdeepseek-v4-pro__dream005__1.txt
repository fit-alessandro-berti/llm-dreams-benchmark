...each group seems to embody a different version of connection you’ve either longed for or lost. You weave between them, the air thick with murmuring and the scent of something familiar you can’t place—maybe your grandmother’s perfume, or rain on asphalt.

You first brush past a circle of people laughing silently, their mouths open in exaggerated joy but emitting no sound. They wear name tags that shimmer and change letters every time you blink. Yours says “ALMOST” for just a second before morphing into a mirror.

Then you stumble into a cluster arguing passionately about the rules of a game no one has ever heard of. They hand you a piece that feels heavy and warm in your palm, like a sleeping bird. “You’ve been holding onto that for years,” one says, and you realize it’s a key to a childhood home that doesn’t exist anymore.

Every time you spot your one person—across the room, beside the punch bowl, turning a corner—the layout of the house shifts. Doors become windows, stairs fold into themselves, and a long hallway turns out to be a painted backdrop that ripples when you try to lean against it. Your friend always has their back to you, talking to someone you can’t quite see, someone whose voice sounds exactly like your own.

Finally, you push through a velvet curtain and find yourself in a room made of all the living rooms you’ve ever been in, stitched together. There, your person sits on a sofa that is also a library and a garden. They look up and smile, unsurprised. “You’ve been trying to find me all night,” they say, “but I was just inside the conversation you didn’t finish, the one where you finally told the truth.” You open your mouth to speak, and the dream, like salt dissolving, changes its mind about where it wants to go next.
