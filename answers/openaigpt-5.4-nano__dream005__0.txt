A cluster of laughter moves like a school of fish, turning when you look at them and then slipping away. Somewhere near the center, someone calls your name—wrongly, like they’re reading it off a sign in a different language—and you follow the sound for a moment before realizing the voice is coming from everywhere at once. You start to walk in the direction you last saw your one person, but the room subtly rearranges itself around you: the same doorway, the same chandelier, the same stretch of polished floor—yet every time you pass a corner, the angle changes and you’re a little farther from where you meant to be.

You stop at a table set for people who aren’t sitting down yet. Condensation beads on glasses that never empty, and the air smells faintly of oranges and raincoats. When you pick up one of the invitations resting there, the paper is warm, as if it’s been held recently. The name at the top isn’t yours, but beneath it are sentences—small, polite reminders, like: *Be kind even if you can’t explain why.* *Keep looking; you won’t miss them forever.* You blink, and the words rearrange into a new set of instructions you almost understand.

Trying again, you drift toward a group that seems to be waiting for someone like you to arrive. They speak in a calm, practiced tone, like a rehearsal you’ve walked into halfway through. When you listen closely, their conversation contains no information—only the sound of meaning. You ask a simple question—where is so-and-so?—and they smile with sympathy that feels oddly rehearsed. “You’re closer than you think,” they say, and gesture toward a hallway you don’t remember being there.

The hallway is narrower than it should be, stretching farther into darkness than the building can hold. Along the walls are framed photographs of moments that almost belong to you: birthdays with missing faces, train platforms where the trains never arrive, a childhood room lit by a lamp you used to love. Each picture has one detail that repeats—the same person’s shoes, the same color of a scarf, the same flash of a familiar hand—always just out of full view.

You walk down the hall anyway. With every step, the lights behind you dim, and the social gathering fades into a distant, muffled applause. You reach a door with a brass handle that feels too light, as if it’s been waiting to be pulled from the inside. Before you touch it, you hear a soft, familiar sound—breathing, maybe. Or laughter held gently behind a mouth.

When you finally open the door, you don’t step into a different room. You step into the same gathering—but from a place you can’t access in waking life. It’s like standing on a balcony above the crowd, looking down through layers of atmosphere. Your one person is there, clearly visible now, turning their head as if they can sense you. They look relieved—almost, but their expression keeps shifting, as though your recognition is lagging behind the moment.

You try to call out. Your voice comes out as if underwater: *I’m here—* and then it trails into static. The crowd below reacts anyway. People begin to look upward, not directly at you, but at the *idea* of you, as if you’re a missing subtitle that they can finally read. The music changes without anyone touching a speaker. It becomes a tune you know from somewhere older than your memories, a melody that makes your chest feel both heavy and safe.

Your one person meets your eyes. They start walking toward you, but the distance between you measures itself in something other than feet—something like sincerity, something like fear. When they reach the edge of the balcony, their hand hovers a fraction of an inch from the rail. Their fingers tremble, and then they press their palm against the wood.

You press back instinctively. Their skin is warm, impossibly warm, like you’ve been holding it in your mind for years. In that contact, a truth rises up—not in words, but in the way your body understands it: the gathering isn’t looking for you to be social. It’s looking for you to choose what you want to find.

You realize you still haven’t decided whether you’re searching because you care… or because you’re afraid of being left alone in a room full of people.

So you do something small and brave. You speak clearly this time, letting your voice stop trying to sound perfect.

You say: “Tell me where you are, and tell me what you need.”

Their smile breaks open, not wide—honest. They answer without moving their lips, as if the meaning is transmitted through the air between your palms.

“You’re already here,” they say. “You’ve just been looking for me to rescue you from the crowd.”

The social gathering below swells—glasses clink, laughter brightens, then suddenly falls quiet, like a breath held in a theater. Everyone turns their attention to the two of you at once, not as an audience, but as witnesses. For a moment, you’re certain you could step down into the crowd and become ordinary with them, or you could stay on the balcony and keep the dream suspended in this shared contact.

Your hand begins to cool against the rail. The warmth fades as if your choice is making the dream decide what it will allow.

You feel time tighten. The music starts again, louder now, pulling at the edges of the room.

And in the panic of losing the feeling, you understand the last piece: the person you’re trying to find has never been separated from you by distance. They’ve been separated by attention.

So you choose.

You either let your grip loosen—stepping into the crowd, letting the search end in the messy truth of being seen—or you tighten your hold and ask for one more moment, insisting on the exact clarity you’re chasing.

Which do you do?
