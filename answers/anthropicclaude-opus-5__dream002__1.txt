...and the light goes strange before the sound does. The green of the leaves turns metallic, like the underside of a spoon. I know the storm is coming the way you know things in dreams — not by evidence but by *already having known*, the certainty arriving before the reason.

The trees begin to lean, all of them together, in a slow courteous bow, and I understand they are not bending in the wind; they are bending toward it, the way people turn toward someone entering a room. The path under me softens into something like piano keys, and each step makes a low note that the forest seems to be listening for.

Then the rain, except it falls upward. It rises out of the moss in silver threads, out of the puddles, out of my sleeves. I hold up my hands and watch water leave them and go up into a sky the color of an old bruise, and I think, quite calmly: *of course, it has to go back*. Someone taught me that once. I can't remember who, but the not-remembering has a shape, like a chair with the person just gotten up from it.

A door is standing in the middle of the clearing. No walls, no house — just a door, white paint peeling, a brass knob worn gold at one edge from being turned. Thunder arrives, not as noise but as a pressure behind my sternum, a hand pressing gently and insistently, and I know the storm is not weather. It's arriving to *ask me something*, and I don't have the answer, and the door is the only thing in the forest that isn't bowing.

I reach for the knob. It's warm, the way a stone is warm after the sun's already gone. Behind me, the whole forest inhales.

And on the other side of the door there is — of course — the same forest, the same clearing, the same door, but it's evening now and quiet and the rain is falling the correct direction, soft as static, and someone is standing there with an umbrella held out for me, patiently, as though they've been waiting a long time and don't mind at all.

I can never see their face. But in the dream I'm not frightened by that. In the dream, I say thank you, and I take the umbrella, and the handle fits my hand exactly, and I think: *ah — this one was mine all along.*
