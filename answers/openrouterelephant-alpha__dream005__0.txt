...as you try to find them, you encounter various groups of people, and each group reflects a different, slightly skewed version of your own life.

You pass a cluster of people in the corner, holding paper cups with red liquid. They are laughing too loudly, their faces flushed. This is a high school reunion version of you—the one where you clung to old popularity, where small slights felt like catastrophes, and where you mistook volume for meaning. They notice you, but their eyes glaze over, seeing you only as a backdrop to their own story. You move on.

Further in, the music shifts to a low, steady hum. Here, a circle of people sits in a near-perfect geometric arrangement, heads bowed, phones glowing in their palms. This is a future, optimized version of you—efficient, distracted, and meticulously curated. You try to speak to them, to ask what they are looking at, but your voice sounds distant, even to yourself. They don’t look up. The glow on their faces is not light, but a kind of polite darkness. You slip past their silent orbit.

Then you find a group in a doorway, their shoulders tense, whispering in urgent fragments. This is a version you’ve fought to avoid—the one that hoards grievances, that mistakes silence for peace, and whose conversations are landmines. You feel the familiar prickle of old defensiveness, a phantom itch in your palm. You take a breath, and as you step closer, the whispering stops. They look at you, expectant. For a moment, you are not a guest, but a host. You simply smile, say, “Let’s not,” and walk away, feeling the knot in your chest loosen without a word needing to be spoken.

Finally, you see them.

They are at the center of the room, not in a spotlight, but in a pocket of soft, unforced attention. They are sketching in a small notebook, utterly absorbed, and when they look up, their gaze finds you with a quiet recognition. The crowd around them melts into a gentle blur. You walk to them, and the noise of the gathering dims, as if the world has remembered it is allowed to be background.

You don’t need to explain where you’ve been. You don’t need to recount the paths you took or the versions you outran. You simply sit down, and for the first time in the dream, you are not searching. You are here. And the gathering, for all its strange and fractured reflections, finally feels like a place you belong to, if only for this one, unremarkable, perfect moment.
