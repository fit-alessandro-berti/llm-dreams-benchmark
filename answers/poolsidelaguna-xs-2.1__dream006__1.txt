The words catch in your throat as a thick, silken fog rolls in from the wings of the stage. The faces of the audience blur, then pixelate like a faulty hologram, their expressions dissolving into shifting patterns of light—some smiling, some crying, some staring with hollow eyes. The spotlight above flickers, not with white, but with a deep, pulsing blue, like the glow of a forgotten server farm buried beneath the earth.

You open your mouth to speak, but the sound that comes out isn’t your voice—it’s a layered echo, as if multiple versions of you are speaking simultaneously in different languages, all trying to finish the same sentence. The stage floor begins to vibrate, not with footsteps, but with the rhythmic tapping of invisible fingers writing in a language you once knew but forgot during sleep.

Then, from the back of the room, a single violin note begins to play—though you’re certain there’s no musician. The melody spirals upward, folding the air into origami shapes, and suddenly, you’re not on a stage anymore. You’re floating inside a massive clockwork bird made of antique mirrors, its wings ticking backward into a sky that rains ink instead of water.

And still, you continue speaking—not to the audience, but to the dream itself, telling it a story it already knows, one that loops infinitely into the silence between heartbeats.
