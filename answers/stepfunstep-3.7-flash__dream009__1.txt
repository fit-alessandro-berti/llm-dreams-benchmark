As you explore your new surroundings, you realize you need to find the locket you were twisting between your fingers when you fell asleep reading your grandma’s old journal, and you need to beat the coming storm to fetch the midwife before the babe in the cottage down the lane is born alone.

The sensory hit of the era hits you first: no electric hum, no phone buzz, just the burble of a stream, the far-off crow of a cockerel, the scratch of rough wool against your arms, and the sharp, sweet smell of wood smoke and clover. You’re standing in a hayloft, boots caked in mud you don’t remember putting on, and when you climb the rickety ladder down to the farmyard, a woman in a frayed mob cap and mud-splattered apron sets a chipped clay mug of buttermilk in your hands, calling you “love” like she’s known you your whole life. She tells you the midwife is needed at the heath cottage three miles off—her daughter’s in labor, the babe’s breached, and you promised you’d bring her back before the moon rises, though you have no memory of making the promise.

That’s when you pat your pockets and panic sets in: the locket is gone. It’s the only thing your grandma ever let you touch from her own childhood, the tiny silver trinket with the water-damaged photo of a girl with her same corn-silk hair, the one she said was the only thing she had left of the mother who died when she was a baby. You have that weird, unshakable certainty dreams give you: if you don’t find it, you’ll never get back to your own time, and you’ll never get to hear the end of the story your grandma never finished telling you, about the Shropshire farm she grew up on, the midwife who delivered her, the locket she lost when she was six.

You take off running down the dirt lane, mud squelching in your boots, the wind picking up and spitting cold rain. Half a mile down the path you spot the midwife’s cottage, smoke curling from its thatch roof, and when you burst through the door, she’s sitting by the hearth polishing a tiny silver locket that glints exactly like the one you lost. She looks up, and her face is your grandma’s—wrinkled, the same faint scar above her left eyebrow, the same crinkle at the corners of her eyes when she smiles.

“Took you long enough,” she says, holding the locket out to you. “Your gran’s been waiting for you to come get this. The babe’s fine, by the way—girl, named Elspeth, after her own gran, after me. She’s the one who’ll pass this down, line after line, till it gets to her, then to you.” She tucks a sprig of hawthorn into your pocket, the same kind your grandma always grew by her front door back home. “The need wasn’t just to fetch me, love. It was to come get the story she never got to finish: the one about how the locket saved her ma’s life, the night she was born, when the storm flooded the lane and I had to wade through the stream to get to her. You take that back, and you tell her I never forgot her. Tell her the story doesn’t end with her.”

The last thing you hear before the world tilts and the hayloft fades is her laugh, the same warm, cackling laugh your grandma had when you’d sneak her extra biscuits when you were little.

You wake up in your own bed, the locket warm in your closed fist, the sprig of hawthorn crushed into your sweatpants pocket, your grandma’s journal open on your chest to the last page, where a note in her looping handwriting you swear wasn’t there before reads: *I knew you’d come looking. The bridge is made of stories, not time.*
