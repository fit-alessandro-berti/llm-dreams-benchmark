You find yourself in a vast, unfamiliar city, and you realize you have an hour to get to an important meeting but no idea where it is. You start to walk briskly, scanning the street signs, but the letters writhe and rearrange themselves into languages you don’t recognize. The buildings lean in, whispering in overlapping murmurs, and every person you pass turns their head exactly 90 degrees to watch you without breaking stride.

You pull out your phone, but the screen shows only a live feed of yourself from behind, as if the camera is hovering over your shoulder. A notification pops up: “Your meeting is at the place where you left your first shadow.” You don’t remember ever leaving a shadow, but your feet know the way. They carry you down a stairwell that spirals into the earth, past doors that open onto other cities stacked like cards, until you reach a vast, silent hall filled with coat racks draped in nothing but twilight.

At the far end, a single chair faces a blank wall. On the chair sits a figure made entirely of your own lost things: a childhood toy, a forgotten key, a word you meant to say but didn’t. It turns to you, and its face is your face, but younger, and it says, “You’re early. The meeting hasn’t been scheduled yet.”

You wake up just as the clock in your room strikes an hour that doesn’t exist.
