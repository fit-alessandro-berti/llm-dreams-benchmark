...the canvas itself begins to breathe. Its surface ripples like silk underwater, and as you approach, the edges glow with a soft iridescent pulse—blues bleeding into golds, purples dissolving into pale morning pinks. The air around it hums with a sound that isn't quite a sound, more like the memory of a bell heard from very far away.

You reach out, and your fingers leave trails of light wherever they move—small constellations blooming at your touch. The ground beneath you isn't solid; it's a shifting mosaic of hexagonal tiles that rearrange themselves with each step, creating staircases that spiral into nowhere and bridges that stretch across voids of soft, swirling mist.

A paintbrush materializes in your hand. Not gradually—it simply *was* there, as if it had always been. The handle is carved from something that resembles wood but feels like captured starlight. And then, without thinking, you begin to paint.

The first stroke is a question. The second is an answer you didn't know you had. Soon the canvas isn't blank anymore—it's a window, and beyond it you can see *yourself*, but different. A version of you standing in a garden made of glass flowers, where every petal reflects a moment you've forgotten. There you are at seven, laughing. There you are at twenty-three, crying alone in a car. There you are at the age you'll someday be, holding something precious you can't quite see.

The shapes around you begin to settle, as if your painting has given them permission to rest. The colors slow their dance. And a voice—your own voice, but deeper and kinder—whispers from within the canvas:

*"What you make here, you carry with you when you wake."*

The brush is still in your hand. The dream is waiting for your next stroke.

What do you paint?
