You are feeling lost and alone in a bustling city. Suddenly, a group of kind strangers offers to help you find your way, and you blink back the hot prickle of unshed tears, too shocked—and a little wary—to speak at first; you’d spent the last hour darting between honking taxi cabs and weaving food carts, your phone dead and your memory of your cousin’s apartment fuzzy around the edges, and you’d been taught never to talk to strangers in crowded cities. But their smiles are soft, no sharp edges or hurried agendas, and when the older woman tucks a crumpled printed map into your free hand, you let your guard slip.

The cluster of strangers closes in soft and slow, none jostling or pressing too close: a teen with a neon-patterned skateboard slung over their shoulder, a barista with a flour-dusted apron and a grin that crinkles the corners of their eyes, an older woman carrying a woven tote stuffed full of library books. The teen taps a section of their phone’s map, nodding toward a narrow alley half-hidden by a stack of glowing billboards. “Most tourists skip this shortcut,” they say, and before you can even stammer out a thank you, the barista tucks a free paper cup of steamy hot cocoa into your hands, the warmth seeping through the disposable sleeve into your numb, cold fingers.

Together, they wind through the alley: the older woman points out a cluster of wild climbing roses tumbling over a weathered brick wall, the teen rambles about their favorite local skate park just a block over, and the loud, overwhelming hum of the city fades to a soft, gentle background buzz. Halfway through the shortcut, a street musician perched on a stoop plays a slow, wobbly version of the lullaby your mom used to sing when you got lost at the grocery store as a kid, and a warm tear slips down your cheek before you can stifle it; the barista presses a crumpled napkin into your palm without a word, their touch light and kind.

When you round the final corner and spot the peeling blue awning of your cousin’s apartment building, you turn to thank the group properly—but they’re already fraying at the edges, the kind of faint that happens only in dreams: the teen’s skateboard glows pale before vanishing into the air, the barista’s grin dissolves into a soft, golden haze, the older woman’s library books tumble into a shower of floating cherry blossom petals that drift away into the bustling street below. You wave anyway, your chest light and warm for the first time all day, and when your cousin bursts out of the building, laughing and pulling you into a tight, familiar hug, you can’t quite remember the strangers’ faces anymore—only the warmth of their cocoa, the sound of their laughter, the way the city that once felt like a cold, unforgiving maze suddenly felt like a place full of quiet, caring people.
