You quicken your pace, but the ground beneath your feet feels unstable—almost alive—as roots seem to coil and shift beneath the soil. A low, rumbling growl echoes through the forest, not from the storm, but from something deeper in the woods. The air grows thick with the scent of wet earth and ozone, and you realize with a jolt that the storm isn’t just weather—it’s a living thing, a force that’s been waiting for you.

Then, the first raindrops fall. But they’re not water. They’re cold, metallic, and when they hit your skin, they sting like tiny needles. The forest groans around you, and the path ahead splits into three: one leading to a crumbling stone bridge, another into a cave mouth glowing with eerie blue light, and the last disappearing into a thicket of thorns that seem to pulse with a slow, rhythmic breath.
