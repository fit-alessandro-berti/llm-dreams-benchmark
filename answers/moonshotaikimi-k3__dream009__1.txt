You wake up in a completely different era, with no modern technology around. As you explore your new surroundings, you realize you need to find the bellmaker before the first frost.

You don't know how you know this. You don't even know what a bellmaker looks like, or why the frost matters. But the knowledge sits in your chest like a stone—heavy, certain, immovable.

The village around you smells of woodsmoke and wet wool. People pass you without suspicion, which strikes you as strange until you glance down and see you're dressed like them: rough-spun tunic, leather boots caked with mud that somehow feels like *your* mud, from *your* walking. In your pocket, your fingers close around a small iron key you've never seen before, attached to a cord you apparently braided yourself. You know this the way you know your own name—except you can't quite remember your name right now. Only the key, the bellmaker, and the frost.

You ask a woman drawing water where the bellmaker lives. She looks at you strangely. "You're standing in front of his house," she says. "You live here. You *are* the bellmaker."

And that's when you remember: the bell isn't finished. It hangs in the tower, half-cast, its voice trapped inside it like a held breath. When the first frost comes, something will need to be woken—or warned, or called home—and only the bell can do it. You've spent years dreaming you were someone else, someone from a far-off time with glowing boxes and humming wires. But the frost is coming now, and there's no more time for dreaming.

Your hands already know the work. As you climb the tower steps, you feel the shape of the bell in your palms, the exact heat the bronze must reach, the words that must be spoken as it cools.

You only hope you remember them correctly.

Outside, the first snowflake falls.
