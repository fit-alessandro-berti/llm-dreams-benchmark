The golden haze of late autumn sun gilds the edges of a park three times larger than the actual city park you live near, string lights strung between oak trees hung with hundreds of tiny folded paper cranes that rustle even when there’s no wind. The air smells like popcorn, jasmine, and something you can’t name—burnt sugar and old books, maybe. You’re clutching the two matching straw sunhats you and Lila, your oldest friend, bought at the festival entrance ten minutes prior, and you reach for her hand to point out the glittery kinetic sculpture across the lawn when your fingers close on empty air. That’s when you realize you’re alone, the only person you know lost in a sea of laughing strangers in cropped tie-dye hoodies and knitted cowls.
The first group you encounter is a circle of teens passing around a Tupperware of neon yellow cookies that glow faintly in the dimming light. You kneel down, ask if they’ve seen Lila: the one with the faded Orion constellation tattoo on her left wrist, the one who collects vintage 1970s postcards and sings off-key to 2000s pop in the car. A boy with a septum piercing nods, says, “Yeah, she traded us a postcard of the Grand Canyon for a pack of those glow sticks over by the food trucks. She said she was heading to the Ferris wheel, but watch out for the cotton candy guy—his stuff changes color when you lie, he’ll make you tell him where you’re going even if you don’t want to.” When you thank him and stand, the cookies in the Tupperware have turned into folded paper notes, each scrawled with a single word: lost, found, key, s’more. The one you pick up says wait, but when you glance back, the Tupperware is full of cookies again.
Next you wander past a cluster of people in wool sweaters and fuzzy socks gathered around a fire pit burning bright, weirdly blue, roasting marshmallows that smell like lavender. An older woman with silver hair braided with ribbon holds a perfectly golden marshmallow out to you. “We just saw your friend,” she says. “She was talking to the man who sells wind chimes made of old keys. She said she’s looking for the art installation that glows pink, but the path to it only shows up if you’re humming the song you used to sing in the car when you were 10.” You don’t mean to, but the opening bars of the Wiggles’ “Hot Potato” fall out of your mouth before you can stop them, and suddenly the grass under the fire pit ripples, a narrow dirt path glowing faintly pink winding off into the oak trees. You walk, and the fire pit’s crackle fades into the sound of a portable speaker blaring that exact Wiggles song—you haven’t heard it since Lila made you sing it at your 9th birthday party, when you were scared of the dark and wouldn’t go into the basement to get the cake.
The path opens into a small clearing under a cherry blossom tree blooming bright pink even though it’s late October. Lila’s standing under it, a stack of vintage postcards in one hand, waving, holding a cotton candy that glows bright purple—your favorite color. You start running, but a group of people in Victorian lace and pinstripe suits carrying trays of lemonade block your path. A woman in a frilly parasol holds out a frosty glass. “You can’t go that way yet,” she says. “You haven’t finished your lemonade. It changes flavor depending on what you’re missing most.” You take a sip, and it tastes like the iced coffee Lila always brings you when you’re sick, extra oat milk, dash of cinnamon, a little too much sugar, just how you like it. “She’s been waiting for you,” the woman says. “She saved you the last cotton candy that changes to your favorite color.”
When you look up, Lila’s standing right in front of you, grinning, holding the purple cotton candy and a postcard of the exact park you’re in, postmarked 1997, with a note scrawled on the back in her messy handwriting: I knew you’d find me. You reach for the postcard, and the whole world tilts.
The cherry blossom tree snaps back into the park’s ticket booth, the Victorian crowd becomes festival staff checking wristbands, the blue fire pit is a portable speaker blaring top 40, the paper cranes on the oak trees are promotional flyers taped to metal poles. Lila’s holding a regular, not-glowing cotton candy, and she’s laughing, saying, “Dude, I’ve been looking for you for 20 minutes, the headliner is about to start.”
