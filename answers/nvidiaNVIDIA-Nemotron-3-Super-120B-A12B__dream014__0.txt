

You are feeling lost and alone in a bustling city. Suddenly, a group of kind strangers offers to help you find your way, and you realize, with a jolt that feels both shocking and deeply familiar, that they aren’t offering directions at all.

They don’t point to a street sign or name a landmark. Instead, the woman with the woven scarf and eyes like worn riverstone places her cool palm gently on your forearm—not to guide you, but to *anchor* you. Her touch carries the scent of rain on dry earth and something faintly herbal, like crushed mint. The teenager with the paint-splattered jeans and a laugh like wind chimes doesn’t ask where you’re headed; he simply points upward, not to a building, but to the way the late afternoon sun catches the edge of a distant cloud, turning it into liquid gold. He says, softly, “Look up when your feet forget the ground.” The older man, whose hands tell stories of labor and repair, doesn’t hand you a map. He produces a smooth, warm stone from his pocket—pale quartz veined with silver—and presses it into your closed fist. “Hold this,” he murmurs, his voice like gravel under footsteps, “when the noise inside gets louder than the noise outside. It remembers the quiet.”

As they step back into the flow of the crowd—toward a bakery smelling of cardamom bread, toward a street violinist playing a tune that makes your throat tighten, toward a child chasing bubbles near a fountain—you don’t feel guided *out* of your loneliness. Instead, the loneliness itself shifts. It doesn’t vanish, but it becomes porous, like fog touched by sunlight. You feel the weight of your own breath again, the rhythm of your pulse against the quartz stone warming in your palm. The city hasn’t changed—sirens wail, horns blare, people rush—but suddenly, you are *in* it, not apart from it. The strangers weren’t showing you a path *through* the crowd; they were reminding you that you were always standing on solid ground, surrounded by unseen hands steadying the air around you. You open your fist. The stone is still warm. You take a step forward, not because you know where you’re going, but because, for the first time in a long while, you trust that the ground will hold you—and that you are, inexplicably, not alone.

(You wake with the quartz stone cool and real on your nightstand, and the echo of a violin in your ears.)
