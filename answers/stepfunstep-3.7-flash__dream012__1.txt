You clamp a hand to the dented steering wheel of our minivan-turned-sloop, salt and bioluminescent indigo sea foam slick under your palm. The wind howls so hard it strips the flannel off your shoulders, and the waves—each one tall as a two-story house, glowing with that faint, pulsing blue that smells like the jello your grandma used to make for church potlucks—crash over the deck in sheets that hiss when they hit the wood. The crew is pressed close around you: your old high school calculus teacher, Mr. Henderson, is clinging to the hockey stick that serves as our mast, his tweed jacket soaked through; the pizza delivery kid who always threw in extra garlic knots when you were broke and cramming for finals is huddled by the sail, which is stitched together from the tattered hood of your old winter coat, frantically bailing iced coffee out of a hole torn in the hull. All of them are staring at you, eyes wide, because ten minutes ago a rogue wave swallowed our compass whole, and the radio that used to play your college breakup playlist is sparking, dead.

For a second you stand frozen, the same way you froze when your boss called you last week to tell you the transfer to the coast fell through, the same way you froze when you found the stack of unread love letters tucked in the back of your closet before you packed up your childhood bedroom. The storm is screaming now, pink lightning arcing across the sky, leaving trails of glitter that stick to the railings and melt into slush that tastes like the flat root beer you spilled at your 10th birthday party.

Then you remember the half-forgotten rule your grandma whispered to you after that nightmare you had when you were seven, the one where the floodwaters rose up to your bedroom ceiling: the sea doesn’t care about starboard or port, about sail trim or anchor chains. It wants the things you’ve been keeping tucked away, the little stupid true stories you’re scared no one will care about.

You kick off your sneakers, climb up onto the dented roof rack that serves as our crow’s nest, and you start yelling the first thing that comes to your head, loud enough to cut through the wind: the story of how you tried to set up a lemonade stand in your driveway when you were 9, and your golden retriever Buster drank all 12 jugs, then chased a squirrel up Mr. Henderson’s oak tree and got stuck for three hours while the whole block laughed and brought you hot dogs to eat instead.

The crew blinks. Mr. Henderson snorts, then laughs so hard he snorts. The pizza kid starts cackling, bailing iced coffee so hard he sloshes it all over his shoes. The wind dies down, just a little. The indigo waves slow, lapping gentle against the hull now, like they’re listening.

You climb down, grinning, and realize the storm wasn’t angry. It was just waiting for you to stop trying to steer, stop trying to outrun all the loose ends you’ve been dragging around for years. The horizon splits open like a curtain pulling back, and instead of open ocean you see the cracked asphalt of your childhood driveway, the screen door of your house slamming as your mom yells that meatloaf is ready, Buster trotting down the drive towards you, tail wagging so hard his whole back end wiggles.

Mr. Henderson tosses you your old varsity jacket, the one with the hockey patch frayed at the sleeve, and the pizza kid passes you a slice of garlic knot, still warm. You don’t need the wheel anymore. The minivan-ship drifts slow and easy across the glittery, story-tasting water, towards the porch light that’s flickering on, warm and gold.

Just as your boot touches the edge of the driveway, the sound of your phone alarm blares through the dream, sharp and real. For a second you’re still holding the steering wheel, still smelling lemonade and burnt toast and wet dog, and you swear you can hear Buster’s bark fading behind you as you blink awake, the sheets twisted around your legs, the rain tapping against your window like it’s been trying to get your attention the whole time.
