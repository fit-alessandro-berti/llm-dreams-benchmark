**You realize you need to start a fire before nightfall—or freeze, starve, or get taken for a witch.**

You wake up on hard-packed earth under a sky already sliding into bruised purple. No phones. No lights. No plastic. No faint electric hum of anything. Just the smell of woodsmoke, animal dung, wet leaf mold, and distant cooking fire. You’re wearing the same clothes you fell asleep in—soft modern fabric that already feels like a neon sign screaming “stranger.” Around you: timber-framed huts with thatched roofs, muddy tracks instead of roads, people in rough wool and linen, a blacksmith’s hammer ringing somewhere, and the low grumble of oxen. No metal machines. No glass screens. No convenience.

You explore carefully. Bundle of drying herbs hung from a doorway. A well with a wooden bucket and rope. Chickens scratching. A woman in a coif and apron eyeing you the way people eye a dog that might bite. Children point and whisper. You don’t speak the local dialect fluently enough; scraps of what sounds like Middle English or early something-or-other float past, half-familiar words twisted by centuries. Your modern knowledge is suddenly both treasure and liability: you know germ theory, crop rotation basics, simple metallurgy, how gears work, why scurvy happens, the lever and inclined plane, how to make soap from ash and fat. But none of that helps if you can’t keep your hands warm tonight or convince these people you’re not possessed.

You need flint, steel (or good pyrite), dry tinder, kindling, and a sheltered spot. Fast. Darkness is coming and with it cold that bites through your thin layers. Survival checklist in your head:

- Catalog every useful thing you can scavenge or trade for without drawing more suspicion: water, food that won’t kill you (no raw meat or questionable greens), warmer outer garments, a knife or something edged, cordage, a few copper coins if you can find any dropped ones.
- Start a reliable fire using rock-and-steel spark technique or a bow-drill if you can improvise the parts. Keep the embers alive.
- Blend. Mute the synthetic colors. Smear a little mud and ash if needed. Mirror posture and speech patterns as best you can. Trade labor for keep—hauling wood, squinting at sick livestock you can actually help with basic hygiene advice, fixing a broken cart axle with proper lashing and a wedge instead of waiting for the millwright.
- Long game: decide whether this is a one-way trip. If you stay, introduce carefully gated knowledge—better water filters of sand and charcoal, three-field rotation hints, boiled linen for wounds, simple solar stills later. If you want out, watch for patterns that feel too symbolic or looping (dreams love those); search for a door that shouldn’t be there, a book that writes your modern name, a place where the light looks wrong, or the constant low pulse of “this isn’t permanent.”

Night settles. You scrape sparks onto a nest of dried grass and shredded bark inside a hollowed log. The first reluctant glow becomes orange steady flame. Shadows leap. The cold retreats a little. Someone leaves a crust of dense bread and a clay cup of weak ale near the edge of your fire circle—payment for the quiet stranger who knows how to coax heat from stones. You eat. The bread is grit and yeasty dullness. You watch the stars, unpolluted and violently bright, and map constellations you still somehow remember.

In the dream the fire crackles louder than it should. Faces of the villagers blur at the edges. One of them looks almost like someone you know from waking life. The smoke twists into the shape of a phone screen that refuses to light. You feel the decision forming: stay and build something lasting with pre-industrial hands and post-industrial mind… or run toward whatever threshold will spit you back into the timeline of dishwashers and Wi-Fi. Either way, you need that fire. You need to keep it going through the black hours. You need—

—and the dream holds you there a moment longer, hands cupping warmth, mind already racing through levers, seed-saving, charcoal for filtering, stories you’ll tell tomorrow that sound close enough to local legend that no one lights a pyre. The era waits. Your next move belongs entirely to you.

(Then the dream softens, the fire becomes the glow of your own room when you finally surface, but the sensation of real sparks and real cold sticks to your fingers a while after.)
