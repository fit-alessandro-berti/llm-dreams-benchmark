Panic flares for a split second, but as I look closer, the stiff paper melts into cool pools of water, slipping through my fingers and splashing onto the wooden floorboards. 

I look back up at the audience, intending to apologize, but the auditorium has changed. The rows of seats are now stretching endlessly into a twilight horizon. The people aren't wearing suits and dresses anymore; they are wearing elaborate masquerade masks made of autumn leaves and stained glass. 

I open my mouth to speak, but no sound comes out. Instead, a thick, silver mist rolls from between my lips. It smells heavily of old library books and approaching rain. The mist cascades down the steps of the stage, drifting over the front rows. 

The audience doesn't seem to mind. In fact, they lean forward, inhaling the mist, nodding as if it makes perfect sense.

Suddenly, the stage beneath my feet is no longer wood, but a soft, mossy forest floor. The microphone stand has sprouted leaves and transformed into a tall, weeping willow tree. I reach out to hold it, and gravity loses its grip on me. I begin to float, drifting up toward the ceiling—which has peeled away to reveal a night sky where three enormous moons are rotating like gears in a clock.

Down below, the audience stands in perfect unison. They begin to applaud, but the clapping makes no sound. Instead, every time their hands meet, a flock of white doves bursts into the air, flying up to meet me in the sky as I fall upward into the dark, feeling the sudden, jolting sensation of waking up.
