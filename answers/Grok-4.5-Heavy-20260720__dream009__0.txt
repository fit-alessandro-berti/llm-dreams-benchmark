You wake up in a completely different era, with no modern technology around. As you explore your new surroundings, you realize you need to blend in immediately before the stares turn into accusations of witchcraft or worse.

The air is thick with woodsmoke, livestock, and damp earth. Muddy paths wind between timber-framed houses and thatched roofs under a sky empty of planes or satellites. People in rough woolen tunics and cloaks speak a language that feels like a distant cousin of English. Your jeans, sneakers, and the silent black rectangle in your pocket mark you as something unnatural.

You quickly hide the phone and trade your most obvious modern clothes for a coarse tunic and cloak. Night is approaching. Without clean water, antibiotics, or any of the invisible safety nets of your old life, every cut and every meal becomes a calculated risk. You must relearn the ancient skills—how to purify water, which plants heal, how to start a fire with flint—while deciding the harder question: how much of the future you carry should you share?

As dusk settles, an old woman by the well looks at you with knowing eyes and murmurs, “Ah. The dreamer has returned under the third full moon. This time, will you stay?”
