You start to walk briskly down the sidewalk, but the street names keep changing every time you look back at them. The buildings stretch taller than they should, their windows catching a light that doesn't seem to come from any sun you can locate in the sky.

You approach a woman waiting at a bus stop. "Excuse me," you say, "do you know where the meeting is?"

She looks at you and smiles patiently. "You're already late," she says, though you know you have an hour. "Everyone is waiting in the room with no doors."

You thank her anyway and keep walking. The city shifts around you—a bakery becomes a library becomes a parking garage becomes a park where children are flying kites shaped like clocks. You check your watch. The hands are moving backward now, but somehow you still feel the pressure of time.

You turn a corner and find yourself in a lobby. It feels right somehow. A receptionist who looks like someone you went to high school with but can't quite place waves you toward an elevator. "Floor seven," she says. "They've been expecting you for years."

The elevator is made of glass, and as it rises, you can see the whole city spread below you—impossibly vast, shimmering, somehow familiar, though you've never been here before.

The doors open onto a long hallway. At the end, a door waits.

You wake before you reach it.
