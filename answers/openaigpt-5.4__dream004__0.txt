…I realize it isn’t waiting for paint. It’s waiting for a decision.

The canvas floats upright in the middle of the shifting world, its edges breathing softly, expanding and narrowing like it is alive. Around me, the sky changes from violet to gold to a color I can only feel in my teeth. Trees fold into spirals. Rivers become ribbons, then ladders, then flocks of silver triangles that rise without sound.

When I reach toward the blank surface, it ripples.

Not with water, but with possibilities.

A circle appears for an instant, then becomes a doorway. A line stretches across the center and turns into a horizon. A handprint blooms in cobalt blue, though I have not touched it yet. The canvas seems to know me before I know what I want from it.

I look down and find that in this place, I have no brush.

Instead, I am holding a small piece of dawn, warm and glowing, and in my other hand, a shadow shaped like a feather.

So I begin to paint with those.

Where the dawn touches the canvas, mountains rise—transparent at first, then dense with flickering forests made of emerald glass. Where the feather-shadow passes, night pools in the valleys, and stars open like flowers in the dark. I draw a door in the side of one mountain, and immediately I hear laughter behind it, familiar and distant, like a memory from a life I almost lived.

The world around me notices.

The changing colors slow. The restless shapes pause in mid-transformation, watching. Even the sky holds its breath.

I paint a path of impossible color leading to the door—an orange that tastes like music, a blue that smells like rain on stone, a white so bright it hums. As the path lengthens, it slips off the canvas and unfurls at my feet, becoming real.

That is when I understand the rule of this place:

Anything imagined with enough tenderness can cross over.

So I paint more carefully.

A small house with round windows full of evening light.
A tree whose leaves are made of handwritten letters.
A lake that reflects not faces, but secret hopes.
A chair beside the lake, already warm, as if someone I love has just stood up from it and will return any moment.

With each image, the blankness recedes, and the dream-world becomes less chaotic, less random. Not fixed, exactly—still fluid, still alive—but listening now. Becoming.

At last, in the center of the canvas, I paint a figure.

Not perfectly. Not with certainty. Just honestly.

A person walking toward me through all the changing colors, carrying their own unfinished sky.

As soon as I complete the last line, the figure lifts its head.

And steps out of the canvas.

It is me, and not me—older, quieter, with constellations threaded through my hair and paint on my hands. They smile as though we have been expecting each other for a very long time.

“You can keep wandering,” they say, their voice full of wind and turning pages. “Or you can begin.”

“Begin what?” I ask.

They glance at the painted door in the mountain, now glowing softly in the shifting dusk.

“Waking,” they say.

Then they hand me the canvas, though it is somehow still larger than the world, and the moment my fingers close around it, everything—the rivers of ribbons, the glass forests, the flower-stars, the impossible path—folds inward into one bright point of color.

I step through the door.

And on the other side, morning is painting me.
