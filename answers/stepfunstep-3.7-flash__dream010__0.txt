The competition is held in the derelict Grand Alhambra Opera House, the one that burned down in 1927 and was never rebuilt—except in the dream, its velvet curtains are still plush red, the chandeliers drip with honey instead of crystal, and the audience seats are filled with shadowy figures who clap when you walk in, their hands making no sound. The host, a woman with hair the color of dried lavender and a locket that glows like a captured firefly, tells you the grand prize is your dream job: Memory Weaver for the city’s forgotten archives, the person who stitches together the frayed, half-lost memories of people who can’t hold onto them on their own.

The first challenge is laid out on the stage: a rusted steamer trunk overflowing with physical manifestations of lost memories. You have to sort them, the host says, but the catch is you can’t ask the owners a single question. You lean in, and the memories hum under your fingers: a chipped blue marble that thrums with the scrape of training wheels and a kid’s whoop when they first rode a bike alone; a sticky cherry popsicle stick that smells like summer fair cotton candy and hums with the quiet, fluttering sound of a first kiss; a crumpled concert ticket that reeks of cigarette smoke and regret, its hum thready and faint. You sort them easily at first, matching the marble to the 7-year-old in the front row picking at her cuticles, the popsicle stick to the teen in the back with a fade haircut who’s biting his lip like he’s trying not to cry. But the concert ticket doesn’t fit anyone. You hold it for a long time, and suddenly you realize its hum matches the locket around the host’s neck. She smiles, and says the first test was never sorting—it was learning to recognize the memories people carry that they don’t even know belong to them.

The second challenge takes you to the Cloud Library, where shelves float suspended in mist, and every book is an unwritten ending to a dream someone gave up on. Your task: pick one book, write the ending its original dreamer deserved, without changing a single word of the first three chapters. You pull a book bound in faded denim off a low shelf, and the first three chapters tell the story of Lila, a girl who planned to be a custom kite maker, who dropped out of art school when her dad got cancer to work 12-hour shifts at a gas station, whose hands are calloused from pumping gas and haven’t held a paintbrush in 12 years. You write the ending where she makes a small, bright yellow kite for her dad on his last day in hospice, and he sits in his wheelchair on the hospital lawn and lets it go, and it tugs so hard on the string it almost pulls his IV pole over, and he laughs so hard he cries, and after he’s gone, she opens a little kite shop in the old laundromat down the street, and every kid in the neighborhood gets a free kite when they move in, and the last line you write is “The wind never forgets the things you think you’ve lost.” When you close the book, it glows warm in your hands, and the host nods from the edge of the cloud shelf. “That’s how you weave,” she says. “You don’t fix the past. You make the ending worth the hard parts.”

The final challenge takes you to the Hall of Closed Doors, a long corridor lined with every door you’ve ever shut in your life, each one glowing faintly with the memory of what you left behind. The rule is simple: open one door, take one thing from the other side, bring it back. If you take something that isn’t yours, you lose. If you take something too heavy to carry, you won’t make it back out. You open the first door and it’s your 10-year-old bedroom, and on the bed is the stuffed red fox you gave away when you decided you were “too old” for stuffed animals, the one you told all your secrets to when kids at school made fun of you for liking to draw. You pick it up, and it’s so heavy with all the tears you cried into its fur that your arms shake, and you set it back down gently. The next door is the apartment you shared with your first love, and on the coffee table is the half-finished love song you wrote for them, the one you never finished because you were too scared to say you were sorry for the fight that made you move out.
