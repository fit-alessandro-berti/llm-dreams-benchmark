...you sit down at your desk, but your computer won't turn on. You press the power button again and again, and each time, the screen flickers with something that isn't quite your desktop—a photograph of a house you lived in as a child, a spreadsheet full of words instead of numbers.

Your boss is standing behind you now. You can feel them there without turning around. "It's due in an hour," they say, though earlier you're certain they said a week. You don't argue. In here, deadlines work like that—they collapse the moment you look directly at them.

You decide to write the project by hand instead. You find a pen, but the paper keeps absorbing the ink before words can form, like writing on the surface of water. Your coworkers walk past your desk, and you notice they're all carrying finished versions of your project, bound and laminated. One of them is someone from high school you haven't thought about in fifteen years. They nod at you sympathetically.

The office begins to rearrange itself. The hallway to the conference room is longer than it should be, and your desk is now somehow on the fourth floor, though you work on the second. You realize, with that strange dream-certainty, that the project isn't really a project at all—it's a test, and everyone knows the answer but you.

"Almost time," your boss says, and now they're sitting in your chair, and you're the one standing.

You open your mouth to explain—that you never got the instructions, that the computer wouldn't start, that nobody told you—but what comes out instead is a single, simple thought:

*Wait. I don't even work here anymore. I left this job years ago.*

The fluorescent lights hum louder. The walls go soft at the edges.

And somewhere, far away, your alarm begins to ring.
