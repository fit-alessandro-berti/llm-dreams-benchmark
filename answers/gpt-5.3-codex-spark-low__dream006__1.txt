  You are about to give a speech to a large audience. As you step onto the stage, you see the crowd looking expectantly
  at you, and you begin to—

  You open your mouth, but your voice emerges as a soft bell ringing underwater. At first, only the people in the front
  row tilt their heads, puzzled, then the whole hall quiets into a held breath. The lights above flicker in rhythm with
  their silence. On the giant screen behind you, your notes turn into constellations, and each word you try to speak
  becomes a tiny star before your eyes.

  You smile, because panic is already dissolving into wonder. “Before we begin,” you say, and your voice suddenly
  deepens, steady as thunder, “we need to remember what everyone in this room has forgotten.” The stage floor warms
  beneath your shoes. A single microphone stand stretches into a branch, then two, then four, and at its base, roots
  spread into the wood of the platform, pulling up the smell of rain, soil, old paper, and salt.

  As you speak, the audience changes—not in face, but in posture: shoulders drop, hands unclench, spines straighten.
  People who looked like strangers begin to seem like versions of yourself at different ages. A woman in a red coat from
  row six nods as if she already knows the ending. A boy in a gray hoodie lifts his chin and mirrors your breathing.
  Somewhere high above, the rafters open like pages, revealing a sky that is not a ceiling at all.

  You read from no notes now, just from the warmth in your chest. You tell them about the first time they were brave,
  the last time they were hopeful, and every time they thought they were alone. You say there is no such thing as a
  speech that changes nothing—only people who don’t know what to do with what they already feel after hearing it.

  The applause begins before the speech is done.

  In the front of the room, the first row starts clapping. By the time you reach the aisle, the whole hall is standing,
  not because it is expected, but because the place around you has changed into something larger than a room: a field in
  wind, a street in dawn, a hospital corridor at 3 a.m., a classroom, a kitchen, a shoreline. Every person is standing
  somewhere they needed to stand, and now they have nowhere else to do it except up.

  You keep speaking, though you no longer hear the words. They are no longer for the room—they are the room, and the
  room is listening, and somewhere beyond the lights, just past the stage edge, the dream opens. You step toward it, and
  the applause becomes a tide.

  Then you turn back once, raise your hand. “Thank you,” you say, and it feels like the word is being spoken through
  everyone in the hall, at once. The lights go dark.

  You wake up with your mouth full of the taste of rain and a certainty you cannot name: tomorrow, you will not let the
  silence win.
