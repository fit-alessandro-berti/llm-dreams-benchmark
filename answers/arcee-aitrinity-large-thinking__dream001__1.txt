
...hurrying down a street that seems to rearrange itself as you walk. The buildings are tall and glassy, reflecting distorted versions of yourself that move even when you stop. A clock tower looms ahead, its hands spinning wildly—11:50 glows in neon red, then flickers to 10:15, then to 12:00.

You pull out a crumpled piece of paper from your pocket. It has an address written in a handwriting that isn’t yours: *The Athenaeum, 4th and Gray*. But the street signs here are in a language of shifting symbols, and the numbers on the buildings keep changing.

A woman in a long coat passes you, her footsteps silent on the pavement. “You’re late,” she says without looking at you, and then she’s gone, dissolved into the crowd.

You break into a run, but the city seems to stretch and warp. The air smells like rain and burnt sugar. Up ahead, you see a park—but the trees are made of rusted metal, their leaves chiming like broken glass in a wind that doesn’t touch your skin.

Is this the right way? Do you turn back, or keep running toward the impossible park?
