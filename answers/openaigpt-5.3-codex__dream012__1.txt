…step onto the rain-slick deck, gripping the wheel with both hands as lightning splits the sky like torn silk.  
The mast groans, sails snapping wild, but your voice rises above the wind—steady, impossible, certain.

“Trim the mainsail! Keep her nose into the waves!”

They move because you move first.  
Because in this dream, fear is loud—but command is louder.

A wall of water crashes over the bow, knocking you to one knee. Salt burns your eyes. Thunder answers from somewhere beneath the sea, as if the ocean itself is speaking your name.  
Then, through the black churn ahead, you see it: a circle of calm water, glowing faintly silver, impossible and still.

“Hold course,” you whisper, though no one can hear it. Or maybe everyone can.

The ship climbs one last monstrous wave, shuddering like a living thing, and plunges into the silver calm.  
At once, the storm falls away.

The clouds part in a spiral above you. Stars pour through.  
Your crew stares, drenched and trembling, and then they begin to laugh—first one, then all of them—because they are alive, because the night is suddenly warm, because your ship is no longer in any ocean they know.

Floating in the quiet water around you are lanterns, hundreds of them, each carrying a tiny flame that never goes out.  
One drifts to your hand. Inside, instead of fire, you see a memory: yourself as a child, folding a paper boat and setting it in a puddle.

When you look up again, the horizon is gone.  
There is only light, and the ship, and your crew waiting for your next command.
