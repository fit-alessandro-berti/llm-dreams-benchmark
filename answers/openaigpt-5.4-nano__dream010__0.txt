As the competition progresses, you face various challenges and—somehow without ever fully waking—your dream job begins to feel less like a prize and more like a place you’ve already been.

First comes the orientation, held in a room that seems both familiar and newly built: the fluorescent lights hum like distant insects, and the chairs are arranged in concentric circles as if you’re waiting for an eclipse. A panel of judges sits behind a desk made of glass shelves. Inside the shelves are your old resumes, stacked like fish scales—each one shimmering with a different version of you. Every time you try to read one, the words slide away into unfamiliar handwriting.

“Tell us,” the lead judge says, voice smooth as water, “who you are in a sentence that can’t be revised.”

You open your mouth and feel the sentence already forming in your chest—until it fractures into smaller sentences, each one trying to leap forward like they’re racing to be chosen. You realize the challenge isn’t speed or eloquence.

It’s honesty.

So you don’t recite. You confess.

You say: “I am someone who wants to keep learning, even when I’m afraid it won’t be enough.”

The judges pause. One of them smiles like they’ve been waiting for that exact kind of imperfect answer.

Then the room changes.

The glass desk becomes a map. Lines bloom across it like veins: routes, detours, dead ends. The map is labeled in places you recognize—streets from childhood, the alley where you once lost a friend, the hallway where you practiced an apology you never delivered.

A new challenge card appears in your hand, though you didn’t pick it up. It reads: **THE WORK BEGINS WHEN YOU STOP PERFORMING.**

You think, *Easy.* And then, immediately, it becomes hard.

You’re asked to solve a problem that isn’t presented with words, but with sensations: a knot in your stomach that won’t loosen, a tightness behind your eyes that keeps blinking in Morse code. You try to translate it into logic, but the dream refuses. So you do something strange: you breathe until the knot becomes a shape you can hold. You stop asking, *What is the answer?* and start asking, *What is the question trying to protect me from?*

When you finally speak, the solution arrives like a door clicking open. The map reconfigures. A route lights up, and the judges nod as if the world just taught them something they forgot.

You move on.

Next comes a timed task—except time behaves incorrectly. Minutes fall backward like petals. Your watch counts down to zero, then keeps counting while your wrist grows heavier and heavier, as if the future is anchoring itself to you. You try to rush, but everything you touch turns into fog. When you give up the frantic grip, the fog gathers and reveals a solution you hadn’t thought of: you don’t sprint through the problem; you circle it, listen to it, let it reveal its edges.

When the clock finally stops, the number **7** appears on the glass shelves. A seventh resume slides forward on its own, and you feel the faintest tug, like a tide pulling you closer to shore.

You reach the next round: **THE INTERVIEW THAT CAN’T LIE.**

A microphone appears in front of you. It’s warm. You expect it to record your voice, but it records your *intentions* instead. Every time you say something safe—every time you try to make yourself sound impressive—the microphone catches and echoes back the part of you underneath: the doubt, the hunger, the desire to matter without becoming someone you’re not.

Your throat tightens. You want to try again and make it smooth. But then you remember: you’re dreaming. Smoothness doesn’t count here. Only truth does.

So you answer like a real person, not a polished candidate.

You tell them about the last time you failed and how you didn’t just survive it—you studied it. You tell them you want your dream job because you want to build something that makes other people feel seen. You tell them you’re afraid, and you explain exactly what you’d do if you were brave enough to be afraid *and* still show up.

The panel members don’t interrupt. They lean forward, as if they can smell sincerity.

One by one, their faces blur into reflections. In those reflections, you see versions of yourself: the one who could do the job well, the one who could do it badly, the one who would do it out of pride, the one who would do it out of kindness.

They’re not asking which you *are.*

They’re asking which you’re willing to practice becoming.

Then the final challenge arrives, delayed and enormous, like thunder rolling around the ceiling.

A doorway appears where the stage used to be. On the other side is a hallway that stretches too far to measure. At the top, a sign swings gently, reading: **FINAL SIMULATION: DAY ONE.**

You step forward, and the dream shifts from competition to rehearsal.

You’re wearing the uniform of your dream job—except it doesn’t feel like a costume. It feels like clothing you forgot you owned. The badge on your chest has no company name. It just says: **YOU**.

Day one begins with urgency. There are alarms, but no one sounds panicked. People move quickly, not because they’re afraid, but because the work matters. You watch them communicate with gestures and glances you don’t fully understand yet. Somehow, you know you’ll learn.

Your supervisor—someone kind, someone real—hands you a task packet. The papers are blank until you look at them with the right kind of focus. Then the text forms:

**The first thing you must do is choose what you protect.**

You stare at the lines. Your mind tries to list strategies: deadlines, metrics, scope, resources. But the dream won’t let you hide behind spreadsheets.

You realize they’re not asking about the job.

They’re asking about your ethics.

You think about all the times you’ve wanted to cut corners. You think about all the times you’ve wanted to overexplain just so nobody could misunderstand you. You think about how you’ve often tried to earn love with performance.

You ask yourself, quietly: *What would I do if no one could punish me for being human?*

And you answer.

You protect clarity, so others don’t have to guess what you meant. You protect people’s time, so the work doesn’t grind them down. You protect the truth, even when it’s inconvenient. You protect your own learning, so you don’t mistake certainty for competence.

When you say it out loud, the blank pages fill themselves with a plan that looks like your own notes—your own instincts, finally recognized as enough.

The hallway brightens. The ceiling stops stretching. The world steadies, like the dream is satisfied you didn’t just want the title—you wanted the purpose behind it.

You turn to tell your supervisor you’re ready.

But your supervisor smiles and says, “You’ve already done it.”

Because the dream is replaying something you haven’t remembered yet.

You look down at your hands and notice ink smudges along your fingers, like you’ve been working for days. A faint bruise on your wrist. A note tucked in your pocket that you don’t remember putting there.

The note is in your handwriting.

It reads: *If you wake up and try to shrink your desire, don’t be surprised when the job feels like a stranger. Your job isn’t a prize. It’s a mirror.*

The alarms fade. The competition judges dissolve into distant silhouettes. The glass shelves fold inward like closing eyes.

A final curtain of light drops between you and the door you entered through.

You realize the grand prize isn’t a contract or an acceptance letter.

It’s permission.

The doorway swings open, and you step through.

On the other side is an office that smells like coffee and new paper—your dream job, finally grounded. There’s a desk waiting. There’s a chair you fit into instantly. And when you sit down, a stack of documents appears—not tasks, but invitations.

You can choose what to start with. You can choose how to pace yourself. You can choose who to ask for help.

The monitor flickers on. A message appears in clean, familiar text:

**WELCOME BACK.**

Then the dream begins to loosen around the edges. The world softens, as if it’s packing itself away for later.

But before you fully wake, you notice one more thing:

On your badge, the word **YOU** has changed.

Now it reads: **WORKING DRAFT—IN PROGRESS.**

You smile, because it means your life isn’t a finished product. It’s something you keep writing, day by day.

And as the dream fades, you don’t feel cheated.

You feel—strangely—employed by your own becoming.
