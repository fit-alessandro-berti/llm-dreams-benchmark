Despite the hardships, you decide to **carry the glass sapling west.**

It was growing in the ruins of the old library, translucent branches chiming in the poisoned wind, and you know—with that absolute, unshakable certainty that only comes in dreams—that if you can reach the coast before the next blood-moon, it will take root in the salt and save what remains of the sky.

You wrap it in strips of faded map-silk, tucking it into the hollow of your collarbone where your heartbeat keeps it warm. The roads are treacherous: asphalt cracked into grinning teeth, buildings leaning together like whispering giants. You navigate by the hum of the sapling, which grows louder near clean water and falls silent when the mutated things—half-coyote, half-static—pad through the dry riverbeds.

At dusk, you meet the woman who trades in memories. She offers you a can of peaches for the glass leaf trembling at your throat. You refuse, though your stomach claws at your spine. She smiles, and her eyes are two different yesterdays, and she points you toward the subway tunnels where the sun still lingers, trapped in the third rail.

You walk. Time stretches and snaps. Sometimes you are barefoot; sometimes you wear boots made of thunder. The sapling grows heavier, or perhaps you are shrinking. Dangers everywhere—bandits with mirrors for faces, acid rain that sings lullabies, the suffocating silence of empty cities—but you press on, because in this dream, hope is not a choice but a compass, and the glass tree must be planted where the drowned bells ring.
