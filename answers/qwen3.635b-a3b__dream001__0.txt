You start to run, but the cobblestones soften like dough beneath your feet, each stride sinking you slightly deeper, as if the city is breathing against your pace. You reach for your phone—its screen ripples like a pond, reflecting a sky that's slowly turning the color of old parchment. The address on the screen keeps dissolving, reforming as street names, then your old school's hallway, then just your own voice repeating a syllable you can't quite catch.

You stop. Panic tightens your chest, but the moment you do, the noise of the city drops away. The buildings lean in, their windows becoming mirrors, then windows again, showing glimpses of rooms you've never visited but somehow recognize. A clock tower chimes, but the sound stretches thin, then snaps back like a rubber band. Time here doesn't pass; it pools.

A woman walks past you holding a compass that spins wildly, never settling. "You're looking for the wrong thread," she says, though her lips don't move. "Find the one that breathes." She steps into an alley that wasn't there a second ago, the brick seam closing behind her like a wound.

You close your eyes. The urgency unravels. The city stops fighting you. You realize you've been running against the current of this place, and dreams don't care about schedules. You just need to let go of the hour.

You open your eyes. The street stretches calm and clear. The meeting isn't a destination—it's a threshold. And thresholds only open when you stop trying to force them. You take one step. The ground holds. You take another. The air tastes like static and rain.

And then the city folds inward like a page turning. The urgency lifts. You wake not with a jolt, but with the quiet certainty that you were never lost—you were just being asked to remember how to arrive.
