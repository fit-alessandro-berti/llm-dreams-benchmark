To your surprise, they are solid. You pull a heavy, jagged syllable from the air between you—it feels like cold, sharp obsidian—and slam it onto the table. It lands with a heavy *thud* that echoes far too long for a normal room. 

Your friend stops mid-sentence, staring at the physical manifestation of your anger. Instead of yelling back, they sigh, reach into their own chest, and pull out a glowing, pulsing sphere of tangled golden thread. It’s the core of the disagreement. As they set it gently on the table next to your jagged word, the environment begins to dissolve. 

The walls of the room peel away like wet paper, revealing that you aren't in a building at all. You are both standing on the face of a giant, ticking clock, suspended in a vast, twilight-colored void. The hands of the clock are sweeping around you at a dizzying speed. 

"We're running out of time," your friend says. Their voice doesn't travel through the air; it blooms directly inside your mind, sounding like it's coming from underwater. 

Suddenly, the anger evaporates, replaced by a profound, aching realization. The argument wasn't really about the topic at all. It was about the fear of the ticking hands. It was about growing apart. 

You look down at the golden thread and the obsidian word. You pick them both up. The sharp edges of the word soften as they touch the golden thread, melting into a warm, metallic clay. You begin to mold it with your hands, and your friend steps forward to help. Without speaking, your hands work together, weaving the clay and the thread into the shape of a small, intricate bird.

You open your hands, and the bird takes flight. As it flutters upward, its wings catch the gears of the giant clock. The ticking slows. The frantic sweeping of the minute hand grinds to a halt, and the vast twilight void begins to fill with a soft, golden sunrise. 

Your friend looks at you and smiles. For a fleeting second, their face shifts, briefly wearing the features of your parent, then a childhood pet, then a stranger, before settling back into your friend's familiar face. 

"There," they whisper, their voice finally sounding normal again. "We have all the time we need."

The clock face beneath your feet turns to mist. You feel a sudden, weightless sensation, followed by the familiar, heavy pull of gravity. The golden light grows blindingly bright, the sound of the ticking fades into the rhythmic hum of your bedroom fan, and you open your eyes to the morning.
