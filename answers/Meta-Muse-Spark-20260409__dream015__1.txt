...build a garden. 

The wind is dry and smells like rust and old ash. The city around you is mostly skeletons of buildings with broken windows like empty eye sockets. Most people scavenge alone, take what they can, and move on. But you found this little patch behind what used to be a school. Concrete is cracked, but underneath is real dirt. 

**Day 1: The choice**  
Resources are scarce, sure. Water means trading three days of rations for a half-full jug from the old tanker crew. Seeds? You have exactly seven, found in a dented tin marked "heirloom tomatoes" in a ruined corner store. Everyone says it's a waste. Gardens take time. Time gets you killed.  

You plant them anyway.  

**Day 12: The wait**  
Nothing looks like progress. The soil dries too fast. Dangers come at night: feral dogs, worse than dogs. You set up noise traps with cans and wire. Sleep in two-hour shifts. During the day you haul rubble to build a wall, one brick at a time. Your hands blister, then scar, then stop hurting.  

A green thread finally breaks the dirt. One sprout.  

**Day 40: The risk**  
Now there are five sprouts. Word got around. A group of three shows up, gaunt and carrying pipes. You expect a fight. Instead, the oldest one, maybe 60, maybe 40, it's hard to tell now, sets down a cracked rain barrel.  

"Had this on a roof," she says. "You got something worth protecting. We help, we eat too."  

You decide yes. Trust is the scarcest resource of all, but you plant it alongside the tomatoes.  

**Day 90: The shift**  
The wall is chest-high. The barrel catches dew and the rare, gritty rain. You’ve got tomato vines, plus potatoes from a trade, and something leafy you can’t name but that isn’t poison. Four more people have joined. You set rules: everyone waters, everyone watches, everyone eats.  

The dangers didn’t leave. But now when they come, there are six people on the wall, not one.  

**Day 200: The dream inside the dream**  
You wake up before dawn and check the garden. The tomatoes are small, tough-skinned, and the best thing you’ve tasted in years. A kid who showed up last month is already awake, pulling weeds like it’s the most important job in the world. Maybe it is.  

Post-apocalyptic means the world ended. But you decided to start something anyway. And in the quiet between the wind and the distant sirens, you hear laughter.  

That’s when you realize: the dream wasn’t about surviving the wasteland. It was about refusing to let it be all there is.
