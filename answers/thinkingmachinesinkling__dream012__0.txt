Instead, you step to the bow and open your arms. The crew expects commands—shout, steer, strike the sails—but you begin to hum, a low, wordless tone that vibrates in your ribs and resonates through the planks beneath your feet. The storm does not calm; it *listens*. You realize the ship is not wood but memory, and the crew are not strangers but every version of yourself you have ever tried to protect.

You turn not away from the waves, but toward them. You tell the crew—without speaking—that the turbulence is not an enemy to conquer, but a language they have forgotten. One by one, they stop fighting the ropes and begin to move in rhythm with the roll of the deck. The sea, seeing itself reflected in your refusal to fear it, begins to fold inward like a closing eye.

The ship does not break. It transforms—mast becoming spine, sails becoming wings of light. You guide them straight through the heart of the storm, where the water turns to glass and the sky opens like a mouth speaking your true name.

You wake not to silence, but to the echo of that song still humming in your chest, and the strange, certain knowledge that you were never lost—only learning the shape of your own depth.
