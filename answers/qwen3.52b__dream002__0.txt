And then the air grows cold and sharp, turning your breath into thin ice on your lips. The leaves in the trees stop rustling and simply fall down like heavy raindrops landing on a canvas. Your heartbeat slows to barely a beat against the chest, as if every step you take now is weighed by the weight of a thousand storms waiting behind closed doorways. Suddenly, the sunlight in the canopy flickers into red and violet colors that dance around your eyes before vanishing again. You feel something shifting beneath the soles of your shoes—not your feet, but the memory of your own skin as it changes texture overnight. The forest is silent, save for a single, rhythmic sound like rain on a metal roof.

As if to test if you are remembering or falling into sleep, the path ahead suddenly disappears, replaced by open water that reflects your face perfectly. You stand still and watch the shadows in the air move with the wind, not moving the trees, but moving through you. The storm has turned into something else entirely—something ancient.
