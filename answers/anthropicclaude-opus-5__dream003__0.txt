...symbols worked into the wood — not carved, exactly, more like grain that decided to become writing. Whorls and loops. I run my thumb across them and feel the shock of recognition: they're my fingerprints. Enormous, the ridges wide as furrows, repeating around all six faces of the box in a pattern that never quite closes.

So it *is* mine. That's settled, in the way things get settled in dreams — without evidence, without argument, just a quiet click of certainty behind the eyes.

There's no keyhole. I turn the box over and over looking for one, and each time it comes back around, the box is slightly smaller, or my hands are larger, or the room has grown another window. Through one of them I can see the house I grew up in, but the wrong way round, as if seen in a spoon.

I try shaking it. Something inside shifts with a soft, heavy sound — sand, or feathers, or a sleeping animal. When I hold it to my ear I hear breathing, patient and unhurried, in time with my own. I stop breathing to check. The sound stops too. That should frighten me and it does, but only distantly, the way weather frightens you in another country.

A woman is beside me now. She has been there the whole time, and also she has just arrived; both are true. She might be my grandmother, or a librarian, or the box's owner before me. She says, without moving her mouth: *You are holding it closed.*

I look down. My fingers are laced tight around the lid, knuckles pale. I have been gripping it so hard my hands ache, and I didn't notice, because the gripping had been going on so long it had stopped feeling like an action and started feeling like a hand.

I make myself let go. It takes an embarrassing amount of effort — like unclenching a jaw you've held shut for years, like setting down a suitcase at the top of a hill.

The lid rises on its own, slowly, the way bread rises, and light comes out. Not dramatic light. Ordinary light. Late-afternoon, dust-in-the-air light, the kind that comes through kitchen curtains at four o'clock in autumn.

Inside there is nothing at all. Just the light, and the smell of a coat that has been out in the rain, and the sound of someone in another room saying my name — not calling for me, just saying it, mentioning me fondly in a conversation I'm not part of.

I begin to cry, and can't say why, and in the dream that's fine; the crying is not sadness but a kind of pressure equalizing, like a door opening between two rooms of different air.

When I look up, the woman is gone and the box is gone and I'm holding my own two empty hands, palms up, patterned with those same whorls and loops, which I now understand were never a lock. They were just the marks left by something I'd carried for a very long time.

Somewhere a bell, or a bird, or an alarm clock.

Then the ordinary morning, and the strange grief of forgetting, and all day the odd sensation of hands that feel unaccountably light.
