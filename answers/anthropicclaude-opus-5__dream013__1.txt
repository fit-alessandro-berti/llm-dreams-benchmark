...notice that the puzzles are getting easier, which is how you know something is wrong.

The first ones were honest work. A lattice of brass tumblers that had to be turned in an order determined by their own weight. A door with seven locks and six keys, where the seventh lock's key was the shape the other six made when laid end to end. You sweated over these. You loved them. There was a clean click in your chest each time a thing opened.

But deeper in, the answers begin arriving before the questions finish forming. You round a corner and there is a wall of shifting glyphs and your hand is already on the correct one, and the wall is already gone, and you are already walking. You try to slow down. You stop in front of a puzzle — a great iron sphere pierced with holes, humming — and you deliberately look away, you refuse to think about it, and the sphere unlocks anyway, blooming open like a fist relaxing, because *not thinking about it* was the solution and you performed it perfectly by accident.

This is the part of the dream where you get frightened.

You start to suspect that the maze is not testing you. You put your hand against a corridor wall and feel the grain of it, and it is the grain of a solved puzzle — the specific texture of an answer, dry and satisfied. The floor is answers. The ceiling is answers. Every wall you have ever passed was something you once figured out, laid down like sediment, and the walls ahead are thinner and thinner because you have not figured out very much yet, and behind you the corridor is choked, packed solid, a geology of certainty you cannot walk back through.

You come to a room with nothing in it.

You wait a long time for the puzzle. You examine the corners, the seams, the light source you cannot locate. You look for the trick. And then, in the way of dreams, you understand — not by reasoning but by simple arrival, the way you understand your own name — that the puzzle is that you are still looking, that some part of you has been holding a shape this entire time, gripping it, a clenched configuration of attention that has been mistaken for a self.

You are asked to set it down.

And you find that you cannot, quite, but you find also that you can *loosen* — one finger at a time, the way you'd release something you'd carried so long the carrying had gone numb — and as you loosen, the room does not open. Nothing opens. Instead the walls become suggestions, then rumors, then the pleasant idea of walls, and you are standing in an enormous soft dark with a great deal of unused thinking in your hands, like a person who has arrived at a party carrying a ladder.

Somewhere far off, a lock clicks — not opening. Closing. Gently. Someone tucking a house in for the night.

You think: *I should remember this one.*

You think: *this is the only one that was ever mine.*

And then the dream, which has been very patient with you, takes even that, and gives you back the brass tumblers, and the six keys, and the beautiful, exhausting, endless beginning.
