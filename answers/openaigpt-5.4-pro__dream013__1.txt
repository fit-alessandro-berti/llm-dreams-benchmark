At first, the puzzles are ordinary in the way dreams pretend to be ordinary: sliding tiles etched with constellations, doors that open only when you arrange shadows into the shape of a key, staircases that will not let you climb them unless you remember a sentence no one ever told you. You solve each one with a calm, impossible certainty, as though some hidden part of you has always known the answers.

But the deeper you go, the quieter the maze becomes.

The walls, made of pale stone veined with gold, start to breathe in long, slow rhythms. Torches burn with blue fire that gives off no heat. Your footsteps echo a second too late, as if someone behind you is repeating them. Every turn leads to another puzzle, but now the puzzles seem to know things about you. One asks you to weigh your happiest memory against your worst regret. Another presents you with three masksŚchild, stranger, and elderŚand demands that you choose the face that is truly yours. When you do, the masks laugh softly and crumble into sand.

Somewhere far ahead, water drips in a pattern that sounds almost like your name.

You reach a circular chamber at the heart of the maze. In the center stands a table of black glass, and on it rests the final puzzle: a cube made of mirrors. Each mirrored square reflects not your face, but a different version of youŚone afraid, one furious, one weeping, one smiling with eerie serenity. Written beneath the cube in silver letters are the words:

**TO LEAVE, ASSEMBLE YOURSELF.**

Your hands tremble as you touch it. The cube turns too easily, silently, its pieces sliding into place with a smoothness that feels alive. As you work, the reflections begin to align. The frightened version of you looks less frightened. The angry one softens. The weeping one lifts their head. The smiling one closes their eyes. Piece by piece, image by image, they merge until only one reflection remains.

It is you.

But older. Or younger. Or perhaps simply more awake.

The chamber shudders. The walls of the maze unfold like paper. Corridors peel upward into a sky filled with enormous floating keys and moons shaped like watchful eyes. The floor beneath you becomes a still lake, and you are standing on its surface. In the distance, where the maze should end, there is only a single open door glowing with warm golden light.

You walk toward it, though each step sends rings across the water that show brief scenes from your life: forgotten afternoons, almost-decisions, people you loved, words you never said. The light beyond the door grows brighter and softer at once, until it feels less like something you are approaching and more like something remembering you.

Just before you cross the threshold, you hear the maze speakŚnot in words, but in understanding:

Every puzzle was a question you had been asking yourself.

Every wrong turn was still a path.

Every wall was built by your own fear of what waits at the center.

You step through the door.

And instead of waking, you find yourself in a small sunlit room with an open window, white curtains moving in the breeze. On the table beside the bed is the mirror cube, now solved, and next to it a note in your own handwriting:

**You can leave the maze whenever you remember it was made by a dreamer.**

Then, very gently, the room begins to dissolve into morning.
