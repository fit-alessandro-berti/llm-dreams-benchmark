You start to run — but the pavement beneath you is soft, like carpet, and the buildings lean inward as if listening. Every street you turn down looks vaguely familiar, like a place you've been before but can't name. Your hands are empty. You're certain you had a piece of paper with the address written on it, but now your pockets hold only warm stones and dried lavender.

A woman in a long coat passes you. She doesn't walk so much as glide, her feet never quite touching the ground. You ask her for directions. She points toward a tower made entirely of clocks, each face showing a different time, and says, "It's already three o'clock there. It was always three o'clock there." Then she's gone — not around a corner, but simply *finished*, like a thought you were having.

You move toward the tower. The air smells like rain and old libraries. As you walk, the city rearranges itself: streets swap positions, a fountain appears and then drains into nothing, a cat watches you from a windowsill with eyes too bright to be real.

You find a staircase descending into the earth. A brass sign above it reads, in letters you somehow understand but cannot pronounce: *The Meeting Is Already In Progress.*

Your chest tightens — not with panic, exactly, but with the strange certainty that you are both late and exactly where you need to be.

You descend.

The stairs open into a vast room. Long tables stretch in every direction, lit by candles that flicker without heat. Dozens of people sit waiting, though some of them are shadows, some are made of paper, some are simply outlines drawn in light.

At the far end, a single chair faces you.

It is, unmistakably, your chair.

And on the table before it sits the piece of paper you lost — your name written on it in your own handwriting, though you don't remember writing it.

You sit down. The meeting begins. No one speaks. But somehow, you understand everything that is being said.

And then you wake up, and for a brief moment, you're not entirely sure which side of the dream you're on.
