...set out to find the source of the strange green light you've been seeing on the horizon every night since the world ended.

The ground crunches beneath worn boots—ash, glass, the bones of a civilization that used to hum with electricity and noise. Now there's only wind, and the occasional distant cry of something that might once have been an animal. I've been walking for three days, rationing water from a dented canteen, following that light like it owes me an explanation.

Tonight it's closer. Close enough that I can see it's not a fire or a flare—it pulses, slow and steady, like a heartbeat made of light. It comes from what used to be a city, now a jagged silhouette of collapsed towers and skeletal bridges.

I climb over a mountain of rubble, and that's when I see it: a garden. Impossible, glowing, green—not the sick green of radiation, but the green of *actual growth*. Vines wrapping around dead streetlights. Flowers blooming in cracks in the pavement. And in the center, a tree that shouldn't exist, its leaves emitting that same soft pulse of light.

There are others here already—maybe a dozen people, thin and wary, but their eyes have something in them I haven't seen in years. Hope, maybe. Or just curiosity strong enough to override fear.

An old woman near the tree looks up as I approach. "It started three months ago," she says. "One seed. We don't know where it came from. We don't know why it glows. But it's *growing*, and everything around it is growing too."

She holds out a small cutting from one of the vines, roots still moist with soil that shouldn't be fertile. "Take it. Plant it wherever you end up. Maybe it spreads. Maybe that's the point."

I take it, feeling something loosen in my chest—not quite hope, but its first cousin.
