**You realize you need to find food, water, shelter, and some way to understand (or at least survive in) the rules of this place before night falls.**

You push yourself up from the damp earth. The air smells of woodsmoke, wet leaves, and animals—rich and raw in a way no modern city ever is. Around you stretch rolling green hills dotted with thatched-roof cottages, stone walls, and a distant manor house with a high keep. No asphalt, no electric lights, no distant hum of engines or power lines. Just carts, oxen, rough-spun clothing, and the clatter of blacksmith hammers somewhere downhill. Judging by the clothing, the tools, the absence of anything post-gunpowder, you’ve landed somewhere in a medieval/late-feudal European-ish era.

A few villagers notice you (your modern clothes immediately mark you as alien, as does the bewildered way you stare). One older woman approaches carefully, muttering something that sounds like a dialect of English mixed with older words. You catch “stranger,” “lost,” “forest.” You try answering; English still works somewhat, enough that she points toward a rough track leading to a village square and mimes eating and sleeping. Hunger is already sharpening; your phone is useless brick, your watch still ticks the wrong century’s hours, and every modern assumption about safety nets has evaporated.

You need three things immediately:
1. **Camouflage and currency** – Trade something small and non-threatening (a glowing digital watch battery if you can pry it out? No—too suspicious. Better: knowledge of simple herbs, or labor). You strip off outer layers that look strangest and try to dirty the rest so you don’t scream “rich foreigner / witch.”
2. **Local knowledge** – Food won’t be store-bought. There’s a stream for water, but you’ll need to boil it over a fire you don’t yet have flint for. You trail the woman toward the village, listening hard, mimicking greetings, spotting where people gather (the well, the church or shrine, the alehouse).
3. **A story** – “I am a traveler from far lands whose ship wrecked / caravan was lost / who wandered sent by spirits.” Dreams rarely give perfect linguistic fluency, but the subconscious stitches enough meaning that you can negotiate bread, a straw pallet in a barn, and a warning about the local lord’s tax collectors and the woods after dark (wolves, bandits, or worse things people still believe walk).

As the sun drops, you sit by a smoky hearth in a drafty common house, chewing coarse bread and thin stew that tastes like salvation. A rambling storyteller recounts tales of kings, saints, and monsters; for a second the edges of the dream flutter and you almost remember the couch or bed you actually fell asleep on. Then a child asks where your home truly is, and you realize the real task isn’t just surviving the night—it’s deciding whether you want to claw your way back to the modern world… or whether some part of you is already learning the forge, the field, the old songs, ready to stay.

The dream continues as long as you keep exploring: secretive monks with manuscripts that might hold true maps, a blacksmith who could be convinced to teach you real steel-making, a neighboring village muttering about plague or invasion. Every choice you make (steal, help, hide, invent, run) warps the landscape a little more.
