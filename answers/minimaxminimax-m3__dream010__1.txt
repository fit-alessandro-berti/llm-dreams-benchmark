...and the challenges become increasingly strange, defying the logic of the waking world.

The first round is simple enough—a trivia contest, but the questions are about memories I haven't made yet. I answer with confidence I don't understand, and somehow I'm right. The judges nod approvingly, their faces shifting subtly between rounds—sometimes familiar, sometimes features I've never seen on a human.

In the second round, I'm tasked with solving a riddle posed by a child made entirely of static. The child giggles when I speak, and the riddle unfolds in three dimensions around me. I climb into it. At the center, there's a small door with my childhood handwriting on it. I open it and find myself passed to the next stage.

By the third round, the other contestants have thinned. Some disappeared during impossible tasks—one had to hold their breath for an hour while standing on a cloud, another had to teach a shadow to sing. I'm surprised I'm still here. My competitor now is a woman who looks like a version of me, but older, and she keeps slipping between reflections of the room.

The final challenge arrives in a sealed envelope. I open it with trembling hands. Inside is a mirror, and the mirror shows me the job—but the job is to keep dreaming. To stay in this space between sleeping and waking, where everything I imagine becomes briefly real. The salary is paid in moments of perfect clarity, and the benefits include a coat that keeps you warm in any weather, real or imagined.

The older version of me leans over. "You don't have to take it," she whispers. "You can wake up. The competition was never real."

But I look at the mirror again, and I see a room I've been building in my mind for years—a workshop with no ceiling, a desk that faces every direction, a window that shows me exactly what I need to see.

I take the job.

I wake up smiling, and I can still feel the coat around my shoulders.
