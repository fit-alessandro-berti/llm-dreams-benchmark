You are at a large social gathering where you only know one person. As you try to find them, you encounter various groups of people and the air itself feels thick with half-remembered conversations.  

The first cluster you pass is huddled around a floating chandelier made of glowing question marks. They’re arguing in overlapping whispers about whether the host is actually a time-traveling librarian who misplaced the guest list in 1973. One woman keeps repeating, “But if the past is just a deleted draft, does that mean we’re all footnotes?” No one answers. They simply nod in perfect unison, their heads tilting like synchronized metronomes.  

A few steps away, another circle is playing an invisible game of musical chairs where the chairs keep materializing under the wrong people. Every time the music (which sounds suspiciously like your own heartbeat) stops, someone laughs too loudly and the floor tilts. You catch a glimpse of your friend’s jacket across the room—navy blue with the small tear on the sleeve you remember from last summer—but the moment you move toward it, the crowd folds in like origami and the jacket dissolves into a flock of paper birds that scatter upward and become stars on the ceiling.  

You keep walking. A third group is taste-testing clouds that have been spun into cotton candy; they offer you a bite and it tastes exactly like the color blue. Their faces are familiar in that dream-way—everyone you’ve ever nodded to on the street but never spoken to. One of them leans in and says, very seriously, “You’re looking for the wrong version of them. The one you know is still buffering.”  

The room begins to stretch. Walls breathe. Someone hands you a glass of liquid starlight and tells you it’s “the 3 a.m. version of courage.” You drink it anyway.  

Finally you spot them—really spot them—standing beneath an archway that wasn’t there a second ago. They turn, smile with your own smile, and say, “I’ve been looking for you too.” Their hand reaches out. Your fingers almost touch.  

Then the entire gathering exhales at once. Every conversation collapses into a single soft chime. The people, the lights, the impossible food all fold inward like a paper fortune teller being closed. You are left standing in a quiet hallway that smells like rain and old books.  

Your friend is gone. Or maybe you were the one they were searching for.  

You wake up with the faint taste of blue still on your tongue and the strange certainty that somewhere, in another version of the night, the two of you finally found each other.
