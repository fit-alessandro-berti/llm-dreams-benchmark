...and you hesitate, the way you only do in dreams—where your feet know the rhythm of the sidewalk better than your mind knows the street names.

The woman with silver hair pressed into a neat bun holds out a paper map, its edges frayed, ink smudged at the creases. The young man beside her carries a lantern, though the sun hasn't set. The child—no older than seven—clutches a red balloon that doesn't float up but *pulls*, taut as a compass needle.

"North," the woman says. "Or what passes for it here."

You take the map. The streets rearrange themselves under your thumb—Churchill Avenue bending into a spiral, the subway station breathing like a lung, the park folding into a paper crane. The lantern light doesn't illuminate; it *remembers*. Cobblestones glow where your grandmother's footsteps once fell. A doorway pulses with the warmth of a joke you told fifteen years ago.

The child tugs your sleeve. "You don't follow the map," they whisper. "You follow the remembering."

The balloon *yanks* your wrist—gentle, insistent—toward a narrow alley where the buildings lean together, whispering in brick and ivy. At its end: a door painted the exact blue of your first bicycle. No handle. Just a keyhole shaped like a question mark.

The strangers don't follow. They never do. They fade the way kindness fades—into the bones of the city, into the next lost traveler's luck.

You press your eye to the keyhole.

On the other side: your childhood kitchen. Steam rising from tea. The sound of your mother's laughter, unrecorded, unarchived, *alive*.

The door swings inward before you can knock.

You wake with the taste of cinnamon on your fingers and the certainty, bone-deep, that you have never been lost. Only waiting for the right strangers to show you the door you've been carrying all along.
