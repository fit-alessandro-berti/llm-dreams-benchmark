…and the corridors keep changing their minds.

One moment I’m standing in a glass atrium where the walls are made of ticking clocks, the next the floorboards have become the deck of a ship sailing through a sea of ink. The other contestants blur at the edges—sometimes they have my face, sometimes they have no faces at all, just nametags that read *Potential* or *Almost*. I clutch my portfolio to my chest, but when I look down, it’s not paper anymore; it’s a living thing, breathing, its pages fluttering like moth wings.

**The first challenge** is the Maze of Credentials.

We are told to find our way to the center by following our own histories. But the walls are constructed from every resume I’ve ever half-written, every cover letter I’ve abandoned at 2:00 a.m. The words crawl across the drywall like ants. I reach out to touch a line that once read *Proficient in*—and the wall crumbles. A voice booms from the ceiling, or maybe from inside my ribcage: *You cannot reach the job by listing what you are. You must become the list.*

I stop trying to read the walls. Instead, I walk with my eyes closed, letting my footsteps write new sentences into the floor. The maze sighs and opens.

**The second challenge** is the Panel.

I enter a room that is simultaneously a boardroom and a forest clearing. The judges sit behind a table that stretches for miles. They wear masks of people I’ve disappointed, people I’ve admired, people I’ve never met but invented in my anxiety. They don’t ask questions. They simply hold up mirrors.

In the first mirror, I am too small. In the second, too loud. In the third, I am silent and fading. The contestant beside me—a woman made of smoke—turns to ash under the weight of her reflection. I realize I cannot break the mirrors, and I cannot polish them. I have to step *into* mine.

The glass is warm. On the other side, the judges are gone, and it is only me sitting at the long table, looking back at myself. I pour a glass of water for the empty chairs. The room fills with the sound of rain.

**The final challenge** has no rules.

We are brought to a vast, empty plain under a sky the color of static. The other remaining contestants immediately begin to build—towering sculptures of gold and light, perfect machines that sing, empires of sand and starlight. I look at my hands. My portfolio has dissolved completely, leaving behind only a handful of seeds.

I don’t know what they will grow. I kneel and plant them in the static-soil. Nothing happens. The other creations are magnificent, blinding. I can feel the judges watching, patient as geology.

Then, from my seeds, come vines. But they don’t grow upward toward victory. They grow sideways, bridging the spaces between the other contestants’ towers. They weave through the machines, holding gears together. They turn the sand empires into gardens. The structures don’t become mine—they become *connected*.

The sky clears. The judges stand up, but they are not judges anymore. They are the future colleagues I haven’t met, the problems I haven’t solved, the conversations waiting to happen. They don’t hand me a trophy. Instead, one of them—a figure with eyes like open doors—places a key in my palm.

It has no teeth. It is just a smooth piece of metal, warm from their hand.

“You don’t unlock the job with it,” they say, and their voice is the sound of the vines settling into place. “You keep it to remember that the door was already open. You walked through it when you chose to grow sideways instead of up.”

I wake up just as the key begins to melt, becoming a pen, becoming a pulse in my wrist, becoming the first gray light of morning filtering through the window—carrying with it the strange, stubborn certainty that somewhere, in some waking room, the vines are still growing.
