The canvas floats a few inches off the moss that shifts from neon green to lavender every three seconds, woven from the frayed edges of the low-hanging auroras that drift over the morphing hills—one minute the hills are sharp, glowing cubes, the next soft, wrinkled curves like folded silk. It has no frame, no stretched edges, just a soft, luminous surface that looks blank until you stare at it too long: then you catch the faint, shifting shadow of whatever you’re thinking about, like it’s listening.

When I reach out to touch it, my fingers pass through the surface like cool, still water. It tingles, the same buzz as when you touch a plasma ball, and every color in the world flares brighter for half a second— the indigo static that floats between the shifting shapes turns gold, the clover patches flash bright tangerine. A brush floats up out of a nearby dandelion puff then, its handle a curved dandelion stem, its bristles made of captured starlight that fizzes softly when I wrap my fingers around it.

I test it first, dipping the bristles in a puddle of the pale, honeyed yellow that’s the exact color of the lullaby my mom used to hum, and paint a tiny circle on the canvas. The circle doesn’t stay. It peels off the surface like a sticker, floats up, and blooms into a firefly the size of a teacup, its wings made of iridescent mother-of-pearl that shifts to match whatever color is closest to it. It buzzes around my head once, slow and warm, then flits off toward the cube-hills, and I realize the canvas doesn’t hold paint at all—it lets it out into the world.

I get bolder. I paint a long, loose spiral of the warm rose color of the scratchy blanket I slept with as a kid, and as I lift the brush, the spiral unspools from the canvas, twists up into the air, and hardens into a staircase that climbs straight up into the cotton-candy pink clouds that drift past. Each step is soft as moss, and when I step on the first one, it hums the first three notes of that lullaby, and the whole world shifts to match: the neon clover turns rose-gold, the silver static in the air softens to peach, the cube-hills melt into gentle, rounded mounds.

I climb a few steps, then turn to look back at the canvas. It’s still blank—until I notice the edges are now the exact pale yellow of the lullaby firefly’s glow, the same as the sky wrapped around the staircase. A ripple runs through the canvas surface like someone just tapped it from the other side, and a hand reaches through, made of the same shifting, morphing color and shape as the rest of this world, its fingers long and slender, nails painted with the glitter of the stars I used to paint the staircase. The figure it’s attached to pulls themselves through next, and their face is the shape of the first wave I ever saw crash on a beach, their hair is made of the auroras that weave the canvas, their eyes the blank, glowing white of the canvas itself.

They don’t speak—there’s no sound in this world except the hum of the steps and the distant buzz of the lullaby firefly—but they hold out a second brush, this one with a sunflower handle, bristles made of liquid rainbow. They nod at the canvas, then at the world around me, and I realize I’d had it all wrong. The canvas wasn’t something to paint the world with. It was a mirror. Every color and shape I’d seen shifting since I arrived was just the paint someone else had let out of this blank space long before I got here: the morphing hills, the clover that changes color, the clouds that taste like vanilla when you bite them. They were all someone else’s dream, spilling out through this quiet, waiting gap.

I reach for the sunflower brush, and as my fingers brush the figure’s hand, the world shifts one last time. The colors melt into each other so bright I have to close my eyes, and when I open them, I’m standing in front of my actual, physical canvas in my sunlit studio, the one I was working on before I fell asleep at my easel. The paint on my palette is the same pale yellow of the lullaby firefly, the same rose as the blanket staircase. The brush in my hand is the dandelion one I was using before I dozed off, but the bristles are still fizzing a little with starlight. Outside the window, the sunset is the exact same shifting indigo that tingled when I first touched the dream canvas, and I swear, for half a second, I see a teacup-sized firefly with mother-of-pearl wings buzz past the glass.
