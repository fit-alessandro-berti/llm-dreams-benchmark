...and each group seems to be performing a task you understand perfectly until you draw close enough to hear the words.

A circle of women in cardigans knit sweaters from the breath of the people standing nearest them. The yarn unspools from mouths in colors that don't exist—*the smell of rain on hot asphalt, the sound of a telephone ringing in an empty house, the texture of your grandmother's hands.* They nod at you as you pass. *Almost finished,* their eyes say. *Yours is the blue one.*

A man in a tuxedo stands alone beside a punch bowl filled with a liquid that reflects not the chandeliers but a different room entirely: your childhood bedroom, the street where you learned to ride a bike, the interior of a car you've never owned. He offers you a ladle. "Just a taste," he says. "It helps the evening go down." You drink. The punch tastes like the color of your first heartbreak.

The groups rearrange themselves when you blink. The knitters become a jazz band playing instruments made of bone and light. The man in the tuxedo is suddenly your third-grade teacher, Mrs. Callahan, wearing the tuxedo now, conducting an orchestra of floating teacups that pour steam into the shape of forgotten promises.

You check your pockets for your phone but find instead: a smooth stone that hums when you squeeze it, a key to a door you've never seen, a photograph of yourself sleeping in a bed that isn't yours. The photograph is *moving*—your sleeping self smiles, turns the page of a book you can't read.

The person you came with—*who was it again? Their face keeps dissolving into static whenever you try to hold it*—sent a text three hours ago or thirty years from now: *"By the fountain. Don't eat the hors d'oeuvres."*

There is no fountain. There are only hors d'oeuvres, circulating on trays carried by waiters with no faces, only smooth skin where features should be. The food screams when bitten. Tiny, polite screams, like the sound of a book dropped in a quiet library.

A woman with your mother's hands but a stranger's face catches your elbow. "You're looking for the exit," she says. "But this is the exit. Every room is the exit. The trick is remembering which life you meant to leave."

She dissolves into moths. They fly upward, spelling something in the air you almost read before they scatter.

The lighting changes. The walls breathe. Somewhere, a door opens—not into another room, but into *the moment before you fell asleep*, where someone is shaking your shoulder, saying your name, saying *wake up, wake up, you're dreaming, you're dreaming—*

But the dream doesn't break. It *thickens*.

And the person you came with finally steps from the crowd, wearing your face, smiling with your mouth, saying in your voice:

**"You took long enough. Now it's my turn to be you."**
