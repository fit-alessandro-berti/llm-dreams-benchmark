I try to run for shelter, but the air has turned the consistency of warm honey. My legs move in that familiar, frustrating slow-motion, each step taking immense effort while the ground beneath me softly hums a low, cello-like note. 

Then, the rain begins. But it isn’t water. It’s a downpour of glowing, translucent jellyfish, drifting down from the bruised sky. They don't sting when they land on my shoulders; they just dissolve into cold, smelling strongly of ozone and old paper. 

I spot a doorway standing completely alone among the trees—no walls, no house, just a weathered wooden door frame with a brass knob. Desperate to escape the strange rain, I push through the heavy air and turn the knob. 

I step through and suddenly I am indoors, but the ceiling is still the churning storm. I’m standing in an endless, grand library where the bookshelves are made of living, breathing coral. The books aren't bound in leather, but in woven leaves, and they flutter their pages like trapped birds. 

A figure is sitting at a mahogany desk in the center of the room. It’s a large, snow-white owl, but it’s wearing my grandfather’s tweed vest and reading glasses. The owl doesn't look up from the book it’s reading, but it speaks in my own voice.

"You're late," the owl says, turning a page. "The tide is coming in, and you haven't wound the moon yet."

I look down at my hands and realize I am holding a giant, rusted iron key. I suddenly understand, with the absolute, unquestioning certainty that only exists in dreams, that I need to lock the ocean. 

I turn back to the doorway to go find the sea, but the door is gone. In its place is a massive, ticking grandfather clock, and its glass face is slowly filling with dark, swirling water. The jellyfish rain begins to fall inside the library. 

Instead of panicking, a profound sense of peace washes over me. I sit down on the coral floor, open one of the leaf-bound books, and watch the water rise, knowing that when it reaches my eyes, I will finally wake up.
