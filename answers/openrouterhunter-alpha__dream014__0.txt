...and you feel something shift in your chest. Not relief exactly, but recognition—like remembering you've forgotten something important.

The strangers don't speak the same language as you, but that doesn't seem to matter. One of them, an older woman with paint-stained hands, takes your elbow gently and points down a narrow street you hadn't noticed before. It's lined with paper lanterns, though you can't tell if it's dusk or dawn—the sky is that strange color that only exists in dreams, somewhere between amber and violet.

You walk together. The others fall into step around you, not crowding, just present. Someone laughs at something you didn't catch, and the sound makes the tightness in your shoulders loosen.

The street opens into a courtyard with a fountain that's flowing upward instead of down. The water rises in slow spirals, catching light from nowhere in particular. The woman gestures toward it, and you understand—without words—that this is where you were trying to go all along.

But here's the strange part: you realize you don't actually know *why* you needed to be here. The urgency that drove you through those crowded streets has dissolved completely. You just feel... still.

One of the strangers sits beside you on the fountain's edge. They don't ask where you came from or where you're going. They just sit with you, watching the water defy gravity, and somehow that's enough.

You wake up with the sense that something was resolved, though you can't quite name what.

There's something tender about dreams like this, isn't there? The kind where the problem isn't solved so much as it stops mattering. Where strangers become companions without explanation. It makes me wonder if sometimes the feeling of being lost is itself the destination—just a space waiting to be filled with unexpected kindness.
