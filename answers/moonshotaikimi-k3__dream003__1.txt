You search for a keyhole and find none. Instead, the patterns begin to glow faintly beneath your fingertips, tracing constellations you almost recognize. You follow one with your finger and the box hums—a low note, like a cello string plucked in another room. And suddenly you understand: the box isn't locked at all. It's asleep.

So you do the only sensible thing. You hum back.

The note you offer comes from a song your grandmother used to sing, though you can't remember ever learning it. The patterns brighten. The lid loosens with a sigh, like someone turning over in their sleep.

Inside there is no velvet, no gold. There is a staircase, impossibly descending, lit by a light like late afternoon. You climb down into the box, and the stairs open into a kitchen you've never seen but somehow know—a kettle just beginning to whistle, a chair pulled out as though someone was expecting you. On the table sits a small brass key.

You pick it up, and you understand, the way you understand things only in dreams, that it is the key to the box you are currently standing inside of.

You look up. The lid is still open above you, far away now, like a skylight. You could climb out. But the kettle is whistling louder, and footsteps are coming down the hall, and you have the strangest feeling that if you could only stay long enough to see who it is—

—you wake up.

The shape of the key is still warm in your closed hand. You lie there for a long time, afraid to open your fingers and find it empty.
