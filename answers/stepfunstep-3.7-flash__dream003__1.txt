Woven between the doodles are tinier, fainter lines: the ridged edge of the pressed four-leaf clover you tucked behind your best friend’s ear the day before she moved across the country, the exact way the late afternoon sun slanted through the kitchen window to hit the linoleum the day you hid under the table during a thunderstorm, too scared to come out until your mom brought you a bowl of applesauce.

You fumble for a key, but your fingers brush only warm, shifting wood that feels like the old oak desk in your childhood bedroom one second, the dented metal of the lunchbox you lost on the bus in 4th grade the next. There’s no keyhole at first, not until you press your thumb to the tiny doodle of the tabby cat you used to have, and a faint indent melts into the surface of the box—shaped exactly like the locket you cried for three days over when you misplaced it at 12, the one with the crumpled photo of your parents’ wedding inside.

You don’t need a key. You press your thumb into the locket-shaped hollow, and the box lets out a soft, sighing click, like it’s been waiting for you to remember this shape for years. When the lid lifts, no creak sounds, just the smell of dandelion fluff and the vanilla candle your grandma used to burn on her porch.

Inside, there’s no gold, no jewels, no secret note scrawled on parchment. Just a single, perfect dandelion puff, intact, its seeds glowing faintly like they’re holding starlight. You lean in, and it doesn’t scatter—instead, it hums, soft, against your cheek, and you hear all the wishes you ever whispered and forgot you made: the one you blew out on your 8th birthday candle for a fluffy golden retriever, the one you muttered into your pillow at 16 when you thought your heart was broken forever, the one you made last month when you saw a double rainbow after the storm, that your grandma would be warm and comfortable in her new nursing home.

A single seed drifts out of the box, and when it lands on your palm, it blooms into a tiny, perfect scene: your 8-year-old self, laughing as the puppy you got for your birthday chases a butterfly through the yard, your old hoodie slung over its back. Another seed floats up, blooms into you and your best friend, 16, sitting on the curb outside the convenience store, sharing a melting ice cream cone, crying and laughing so hard your sides hurt. A third seed drifts to the floor, blooms into your grandma, sitting in her porch rocker, holding the patchwork quilt she sewed you for graduation, waving like she can see you.

When you reach to touch the scene of your grandma, the box dissolves under your fingertips, turning into a cloud of dandelion fluff that drifts up through the ceiling. You realize then that the patterns on the box were never engraved—they were made of all the little, forgotten good things you thought you’d lost, waiting for you to stop looking for a key that didn't exist, and just open your hands instead.

The light shifts, and for a second you swear you can smell your grandma’s lavender lotion, and the sound of your old dog’s collar jingles faintly from somewhere down the hall. You know if you look for the box now, it’ll be gone, but you don’t mind. The dandelion seed in your palm is still warm, and when you wake up, you’ll find it tucked in the pocket of the flannel shirt you wore to visit your grandma last week, even though you swear you didn’t put it there.
