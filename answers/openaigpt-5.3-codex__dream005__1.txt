…each group seems to be arranged by a different rule you can’t quite understand.

At one table, everyone is laughing at a joke no one says out loud. Their mouths move a beat late, like dubbed film. At another, people are introducing themselves with names that sound almost familiar—your third-grade teacher’s name, your old password, the title of a song you forgot existed. You nod as if this all makes sense.

You spot your friend across the room near a staircase wrapped in fairy lights. You raise your hand. They smile, then drift behind a column, and when you reach the staircase it leads not up, but into a grocery aisle full of fog. Someone hands you a paper cup of something warm and says, “You’re late for the toast,” though no one has glasses and no one is facing the same direction.

You keep moving.

The house keeps changing shape: ballroom to backyard to school cafeteria to train station platform. Music leaks from everywhere at once—string quartet, club bass, a lullaby your mother used to hum. You ask strangers, “Have you seen—” but before you say your friend’s name, they answer, “Just missed them.”

Then suddenly you find a quiet hallway with one door half-open. Inside is a tiny room with only a coat rack and a mirror. In the mirror, the party is behind you, bright and crowded and impossible, and your friend is right there in the reflection, standing close enough to touch.

You turn around.

No one.

When you look back at the mirror, your friend mouths something slowly, clearly, like they need you to get it right this time:

“Wake up before you forget.”

And you do—heart pounding, hand still half-raised as if you’re waving through glass.
