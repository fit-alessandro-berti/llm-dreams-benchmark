

You watch as the words tumble out between you—sharp, jagged, echoing off invisible walls that seem to pulse with a soft, phosphorescent glow. The topic flickers in the air like a half‑remembered song: the promise you both made years ago to start a community garden on the vacant lot behind the old bakery, a promise now strained by differing visions of what that garden should become. Your friend’s voice, usually warm and steady, rises in a strained timbre, “You’re forgetting why we started this— to give kids a place to dig their hands into soil, to watch something grow from nothing.”  

Your own reply feels both honest and strangely detached, as if you’re observing yourself from a corner of the room you’ve never entered: “And what about sustainability? If we don’t plan for water capture, native plants, and a compost loop, we’ll just be planting hope that wilts before the first frost.”  

The space around you begins to shift. The linoleum floor under your feet ripples like a pond struck by a gentle breeze, and the walls melt into cascades of translucent vines that bloom with flowers you’ve never seen—petals that shift color with every breath you take, from deep indigo to blazing amber. The argument, instead of fading, becomes a soundtrack to a slow, graceful dance: each heated sentence is a step, each pause a turn, and the surrounding flora sways in time, leaves whispering counter‑points in a language you almost understand.

Your friend’s eyes, usually bright with mischief, now hold a reflective sheen, like the surface of a quiet lake at dawn. You notice, for a moment, that their iris is flecked with tiny, glowing motes—like fireflies trapped in glass. As you speak, those motes drift outward, forming constellations that map the very points of contention: water lines, sunlight angles, seed varieties. The constellations pulse, then re‑arrange themselves, suggesting a compromise you hadn’t considered— a terraced design that captures rainwater on the upper levels while letting sun‑loving herbs thrive below.

A sudden, gentle wind sweeps through the dreamscape, carrying the scent of damp earth and freshly baked bread—a nostalgic reminder of the bakery that once stood where the garden will be. The wind lifts a loose page from your pocket—a sketch you’d made months ago, half‑finished, of the garden’s layout. As it flutters, the lines on the page glow, ink turning into luminous threads that stitch themselves into the air, forming a three‑dimensional model hovering between you and your friend. You can reach out, tilt a terrace, adjust a trellis, and watch the model respond in real time, vines curling, blossoms opening, tiny fruit forming.

In this shared, lucid space, the argument softens—not because you concede, but because you both see the same vision materializing, shaped by the very passion that fueled your disagreement. Your friend’s hand finds yours, fingers intertwining, and you feel a warm current flow between your palms, like the sap rising in a young tree. The dream’s scenery begins to fade at the edges, the vines retracting into delicate mist, the constellations dimming to a faint afterglow.

You awaken with the echo of that shared breath still lingering in your chest, a faint taste of soil on your tongue, and the vague, reassuring sense that the garden—both the one you argue about and the one growing inside your partnership—will find a way to take root, no matter how tangled the arguments become. The dream leaves you with a quiet certainty: sometimes, the most fertile ground is the one we till together, even when the furrows seem to run in opposite directions.
