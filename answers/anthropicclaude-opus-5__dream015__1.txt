...start a library.

It doesn't make sense, and in the dream I know it doesn't make sense, which is the way of dreams — you carry the knowledge of your own absurdity like a coin in your pocket and it doesn't stop you.

The world is the color of a photograph left in a window. Everything has been bleached toward the same tired amber: the overturned buses, the ferns coming up through the asphalt, the sky that never quite finishes being morning. Somewhere behind me there was a city and something happened to it, though the dream declines to tell me what. I only know that resources are scarce. This is a fact I possess the way you possess your own name.

So: a library.

I find the first book under a car seat. It's swollen with old water, the pages fused into a single geological object, and I can't read a word of it, but I brush it off and set it on a shelf. The shelf is a piece of drywall balanced on two milk crates in what used to be a gas station. This is the library. I am the librarian. I have made a sign, and the sign says LIBRARY, and looking at it fills me with a pride so enormous and ridiculous that I almost wake up.

People come.

That's the part the dream insists on. They come out of the ferns, out of the long grass, out of the culverts. A woman with a rifle held like a walking stick. A boy wearing three coats. An old man leading a dog that is somehow also my dog from childhood, and I decide not to think about that too hard. They come, and they look at the drywall shelf with its one ruined book, and none of them laugh.

The woman with the rifle reaches into her jacket and takes out a paperback with no cover. She sets it on the shelf. She says, *I know most of it by heart anyway.*

The boy has a comic book folded into eighths, worn soft as cloth. The old man has nothing, so he sits down on an overturned bucket and begins to tell the story of a man who went to sea, and the dog puts its head on my foot, and I understand that this counts. That he is shelving himself.

More arrive. Someone brings a manual for a washing machine that no longer exists anywhere on earth, and we shelve it with great ceremony. Someone brings a diary. Someone brings a child's drawing of a house with smoke coming out of the chimney, and this is the most valuable acquisition in the collection, and we all know it.

The shelves multiply the way things multiply in dreams — I turn around and there are more, I turn again and they're going up the walls, out the broken windows, into the parking lot, rows and rows of them standing in the wet grass with the amber light coming through. Ferns growing between the volumes. Rain, at some point, and everyone rushing out with tarps and coats and bare hands to cover the books, laughing, shouting, soaked through — and this is the happiest I have been in any dream I can remember.

Later, I'm sitting on the curb. Night, which the dream has finally allowed. Someone has made a fire in a hubcap. The boy in three coats is reading aloud to the old man, stumbling on the long words, and the old man is correcting him gently and getting it wrong himself, and neither of them minds.

I think: *this was the useless thing.* Of all the things to build. Not a wall, not a well, not a wall around a well.

And the woman with the rifle sits down next to me and says, as if she's been reading over my shoulder, *No. This is the well.*

I wake up before I can answer her, but I take the sentence with me, the way you sometimes get to. It sits in my chest all morning like a coin in a pocket. Like a book on a shelf made of drywall and milk crates, in the amber light, at the end of the world, open.
