You wake up in a completely different era, with no modern technology around. As you explore your new surroundings, you realize you need to find out not just where you are, but when you are.

The air smells of woodsmoke and rain. Horses clatter over uneven stones, and people in long coats hurry past you carrying baskets, candles, and bundles of cloth. No one looks at a screen. No engines hum in the distance. The world feels slower, heavier, and strangely alive.

You reach into your pocket for your phone, but your hand closes around something else: a small brass key, warm as if it has been held tightly for hours. Etched along its side are words you somehow understand:

**“The door opens when you remember why you came.”**

Before you can wonder what that means, a bell rings from a tower above the rooftops. Everyone in the street freezes. Windows slam shut. A man grabs your arm and whispers, “You shouldn’t be out when the clock calls.”

“Why?” you ask.

But he has already disappeared into the crowd.

The bell rings again.

At the end of the street, where there had only been a brick wall moments before, a narrow door appears. It is painted the same color as the sky just before sunrise. Your brass key begins to glow.

You step toward it, heart pounding, while the dream folds around you like a page being turned. Behind the door, you hear a familiar sound: your own alarm clock, faint and far away.

But when you unlock it and push it open, you don’t find your bedroom.

You find another version of yourself waiting on the other side.

And they say, “Good. You made it. Now we can fix what happens next.”
