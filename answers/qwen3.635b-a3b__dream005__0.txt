They speak, but their voices unravel into the scent of rain on dry soil and the click of watch gears. When they gesture, origami birds peel from their fingertips and drift into your coat. You open one to find a coordinate you’ve never written down; another holds a word that tastes like copper and forgiveness.

The floor softens beneath your shoes, turning to polished water that reflects not your face, but yours at different ages. You keep walking, calling a name that folds backward in the air. A doorway materializes where a wall stood. Inside, a string quartet plays a melody that ties itself to your wrists in threads of warm amber light. You pull, but the tension doesn't feel like restraint—it feels like being held. You let your hands fall still.

Through the crowd, you glimpse them, just past a marble column that breathes. Your pulse quickens, but when you step forward, the column's rhythm syncs with your own and the figure dissolves into a flock of pale moths that scatter through the chandelier. You're suddenly standing on a balcony overlooking a sea of glass tiles. Each tile shows a conversation you've had, half-remembered, half-dreamed. One tiles up and shows an empty chair across from yours.

A server glides by with a tray of silver bells. When you touch one, it doesn't ring—it inhales. The sound it pulls out is your own footsteps, walking in circles, until they loop into a single, steady pace. Around you, the guests begin to fold inward like book pages, the music slows to the hum of a refrigerator in an empty room, the lanterns brighten until they're the color of dawn. You feel the weight of the paper birds in your pockets grow lighter, until they're just air.

You open your eyes. The room is quiet. The sheets are warm. There is no search, no gathering, no missing name. But in the space behind your ribs, where dream-logic leaves its mark, you know exactly where to begin looking when you close them again.
