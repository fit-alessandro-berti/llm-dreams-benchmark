...begin to speak, but the words come out as moths.

They flutter from your mouth—pale, powdery wings beating against the spotlight's heat. The audience doesn't flinch. A woman in the front row catches one between two fingers, examines its dusted wings, and places it carefully in a glass jar she produces from her purse. The jar is already full. They glow faintly, like trapped stars.

You try again. *Thank you for coming tonight.*

This time the words are small stones. They hit the stage floor with sharp *ticks*, rolling toward the orchestra pit. A man in the third row—wearing your father's shoes, you notice suddenly—begins collecting them in his coat pockets. His shoulders grow heavier with each sentence.

The microphone cord has become a vine. It pulses against your fingertips, alive, climbing your wrist. You wonder briefly if you should pull away, but the thought dissolves before you can hold it.

*I want to tell you about—*

Feathers now. White, impossible softness spilling from your throat. The audience opens umbrellas. Indoor umbrellas. Black, uniform, blooming like dark flowers. None of them are wet.

Behind you, the backdrop shifts. Was it a curtain? Now it's a window. Through it: your childhood kitchen at 3 AM. The light is on. Someone sits at the table with their back to you. You know who. You always know who.

The dream-you realizes you've stopped speaking. The audience waits. Patient. Eternal. The vine tightens its grip.

The person at the kitchen table turns.

You wake with your hand pressed to your throat, the phantom taste of moth-wing dust on your tongue.
