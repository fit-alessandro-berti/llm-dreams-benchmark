You are participating in a competition where the grand prize is your dream job. As the competition progresses, you face various challenges and...

...you realize none of the challenges are quite what they said on the flyer.

The competition isn't in a building. It's in a building that *used to be* a building. You're in a vast office made of late-afternoon sunlight, where the cubicles stretch on forever and the carpet is soft, warm grass. There are hundreds of other contestants, but you can't quite focus on their faces. The judges are sitting high above you on office chairs that float like balloons.

A voice comes over an intercom that sounds exactly like your old high school principal trying to whisper:

**CHALLENGE ONE: The Commute.**

You have to get to your desk. Simple. Except your desk is on the other side of a shallow, endless ocean made of crumpled resumes. All of yours. Old versions. *Objective: To obtain any job.* *Fluent in Microsoft Paint.* You have to wade through them. The paper gets stuck to your ankles. Everyone else is swimming gracefully. You are knee-deep, laughing, because the tide just turned up a resume you wrote when you were 8: *Dream job: Professional dinosaur petter / astronaut.*

You make it to the desk. On it is a single, steaming mug of coffee that has your name spelled right for the first time ever.

**CHALLENGE TWO: The Group Project.**

You are placed on a team with three people: a very serious 6-year-old you, a very tired 80-year-old you, and your childhood dog. You have to build a ladder to the ceiling using only office supplies that keep changing when you aren't looking. Staplers turn into turtles. Paperclips turn into silver earrings. 

Little-you builds fearlessly, stacking everything crooked and tall. Old-you just sits, smiles, and says, "We don't need to reach the ceiling, kiddo." Your dog knocks the whole thing over and you all crack up so hard the floating judges wobble.

You fail the challenge. You get full points anyway.

**CHALLENGE THREE: The Final Interview.**

They call your name. The room goes silent. You walk into a door that wasn't there a second ago and inside is not a boardroom.

It's a small, perfectly lit workshop. Or a library. Or a bakery. Or a studio. It's shifting, trying to be whatever your dream job is at this exact second. The air smells like potential - like fresh paint and old books and coffee and electricity.

The interviewer is you. Just you, wearing the clothes you feel most like yourself in, sitting cross-legged on the desk.

The other you asks, "So, why do you want this job?"

And you try to give the polished answer. The one about passion and skills and five-year plans. But in the dream, your mouth won't say that. Instead what comes out is:

"Because I want to do something that makes the day feel less like waiting."

Other-you nods like that's the best answer they've ever heard. They slide a nameplate across the desk to you.

It doesn't say CEO or Director or Founder.

It just says your name. And underneath: *Professional Dreamer.*

"Congratulations," they say. "You got it. You've had it the whole time."

Just then, the office starts to dissolve at the edges. The grass carpet grows wild. The floating chairs pop like bubbles and rain down as sticky notes that all say "WAKE UP SOON." The coffee mug in your hand is suddenly warm again.

You realize the grand prize was never a job they could give you. It was permission to build the workshop yourself.

And somewhere, very far away, an alarm is ringing - but it sounds a lot like birds.
