You are feeling lost and alone in a bustling city. Suddenly, a group of kind strangers offers to help you find your way, and you freeze for half a second, throat tight with surprise—you’d asked three people before this, all hurrying past with headphones clamped tight, ignoring you as their shoes clicked over the wet cobblestones. The first one, a teen with a backpack plastered neon dinosaur stickers, kneels to scoop up the crumpled neighborhood map you’d dropped when you panicked, brushing a fleck of street grime off the folded corner. “Don’t sweat it,” they say, grinning, “I got hopelessly lost here last spring trying to find the hidden bookshop behind the noodle cart.”

The rest of the group falls in around you: an older woman in a flowy linen sundress tucks a warm red bean taiyaki into your free hand, the pastry still steaming through its crinkly paper wrapper, and a barista in a faded coffee apron pulls out a crumpled printout of the subway map, pointing to the stop you’d been muttering to yourself about trying to locate. “We’re headed that way too,” she says, “let’s walk—it’s too pretty to rush through the side streets.”

The walk floats by in soft, hazy bursts: the tang of grilled corn from a street cart makes you pause, and the older woman buys you an ear, butter melting sweet on your tongue as they point out hidden alleyway murals, the fluffy calico cat that naps every afternoon on the fire escape of the laundromat, and the best late-night boba spot in the neighborhood where they give free extra toppings if you mention the stray cat. You almost trip on a curb again, and a stranger’s gentle elbow catches you, their palm warm through your thin jacket.

When you reach the subway entrance, the teen presses a tiny folded paper crane into your palm—when you unfold it, it’s a hand-drawn map of all the easiest, most welcoming spots in the area, scrawled with little sticky-note-style notes like “stop here for free lemon water!” and “ask the pickle vendor for extra dill.”

“You don’t have to go through this alone,” the barista says, squeezing your shoulder before the group turns to wave and head toward their own destination. You stand there for a minute, clutching the crane and the crumpled taiyaki wrapper, the hum of the city still loud but now feeling soft, like a lullaby wrapped around you. When you buy your train ticket, the attendant winks and says “First time lost here? This one’s on me,” and suddenly the whole city feels less overwhelming, less sharp around the edges.

You take your seat on the train, and as it pulls away from the platform, you look out the window at the glowing neon signs and twinkling apartment windows, and for the first time all day, the tight, lonely knot in your chest loosens. When the train lurches suddenly, you blink, and for a half-second you’re not sure if you’re still dreaming—but the paper crane is still tucked in your jacket pocket, and a faint sweet smell of red bean lingers on your hands, and you know you’re not alone anymore.
