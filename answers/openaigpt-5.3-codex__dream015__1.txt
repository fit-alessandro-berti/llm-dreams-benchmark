…build something instead of just surviving.

At first it’s small: a fire in the shell of an old bus station, a tarp tied between rusted pillars, rainwater caught in cracked satellite dishes. You trade a bent wrench for seeds. You trade stories for trust.

People begin to arrive one by one—an ex-teacher with a cart full of books wrapped in plastic, a mechanic missing two fingers, a child who won’t speak but draws maps in the dust. Together, you turn ruins into routine.

You string bottle-glass along the perimeter to chime when the wind shifts. You paint symbols on walls to mark safe wells, unsafe roads, and where medicinal plants still grow through concrete. Nights are still dangerous, but no longer silent. There is laughter now—brief, surprised, real.

One evening, while scavenging the skeleton of a library, you find a metal box in a collapsed office. Inside: packets of heirloom seeds, dry and intact, and a note faded to near-white:

**“If you find these, plant for someone you will never meet.”**

So you do.

Seasons pass in dream-time—fast and impossible. Vines climb over freeway signs. Solar lanterns blink between buildings like grounded stars. Children who once flinched at every sound now race through rows of corn growing in what used to be a parking lot.

Then, on a cold dawn, you climb to the roof and look out. Smoke rises in the distance—not black plumes of fire, but thin, steady columns from cooking stoves. Other settlements. Other people choosing the same stubborn thing.

You wake just as the sun breaks over broken towers, and for the first time in the dream, the world doesn’t look like an ending.

It looks like chapter two.
