...stop using words. I open my mouth to keep shouting, but instead of sound, a silver thread unspools from my throat—thin, trembling, impossibly long. My friend freezes mid-sentence, their anger crackling like static around their shoulders.

*This*, I think, though I’m not sure if I’m thinking it or if the thread is singing it. *I’ll show you.*

I reach up and hook my fingers behind my ribs. There’s no pain, only a giving sensation, like pulling back a curtain. My chest opens like a cabinet, and inside there’s not blood but weather. A whole storm system swirling amber and violet, and at the center, a kitchen table from the house we shared seven years ago. The argument we’re having in the waking dream—whatever it was about, the words have already turned to ash—was never really about now. It was about that table. The coffee ring. The unspoken thing between us that we left there like a houseplant we both forgot to water until it petrified.

My friend steps closer, and I realize they’ve opened too. Their chest is a lantern, glowing sepia, and inside it I see my own face from a perspective I’ve never seen before—how small I looked when I thought I was being brave, how loud my silence was.

We don’t touch. We just stand there in the middle of whatever room we started in—though the walls are already dissolving into fog, the way walls do when the dream decides the architecture has served its purpose—and let our chests speak to each other in frequencies below language. The table inside me wobbles, then steadies. The petrified plant puts out one impossible green shoot.

Then the thread between us knots. The knot becomes a door. On the other side, I can hear an alarm clock, or maybe it’s just the sound of the ocean remembering a shell.

I wake up with my hands still curved, still holding the shape of my own ribs, and for three full seconds, I know exactly what I need to say.
