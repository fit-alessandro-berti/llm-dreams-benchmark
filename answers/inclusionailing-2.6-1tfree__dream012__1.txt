The sky has turned the color of a bruised plum, and the sea is stitching itself into a frenzy of gray whip-lashes. Somewhere below, the bulkheads groan like old lungs.  

I do not shout. The noise is already absolute, and fear, when amplified, deafens. Instead I set my feet against the brass ring, feel the ship’s slow, heavy roll, and let the wheel teach me what the maps don’t. The helm is speaking: *left a hand, ease when she shudders, give her the green shoulder of the swell.*  

A cry comes from the fo’c’sle—a hawser has parted, flailing like a steel snake. Without turning I say, “Marek—triple sheet, quick as mercy.” My voice does not waver because I have rehearsed this weather in my sleep.  

Then lightning unsutures the horizon, and for one breath I see—not water, but a pale geometry beneath us, a lattice of currents glowing like veins in stone. The ship tilts, eager, almost grateful, as if she has been waiting for me to notice that the storm is not attacking us; it is asking a question.  

I answer by turning into the trough, not away. The bow knives down, green walls rising on either side like cathedral doors. Somewhere in the roar I hear the crew pick up the rhythm I am carving: lines cinched, shoulders braced, breath held and released. They do not need a hero; they need a needle, and I have become the eye.  

The ship climbs, shivering, and crests the ridge. For a suspended heartbeat we hang above the world, sky and sea inverted, the wind pulling taut like a violin string—and then we slide into the valley, fast and clean, the hull singing a note I have never heard before, bright and silver beneath the thunder.  

I do not know whether the storm will break or swallow us. But in this glide, between one breath and the next, the sea and I have agreed on a direction. We will not flee; we will translate. And as the crew’s hands find the same pulse in the timbers that I feel in the wheel, the dream steadies, tipping toward dawn, still wild, but navigable.
