...find the rhythm of this world before it swallows you whole.

The air tastes different—thick with woodsmoke and animal fat and something floral beneath it, crushed herbs maybe, or wild roses. No exhaust. No ozone. No hum of electricity in the walls.

Light cuts through shutters in dust-motes. A rooster screams somewhere. Your fingers, brushing the bedside, find rough-spun linen and a wooden frame strung with rope, not springs. The mattress is straw. You can feel each stalk through the fabric.

*Right. Not a hotel. Not a film set.*

Your phone—your *life*—is gone. No pocket rectangle glowing with maps and memories and the collected knowledge of the species. Just a leather pouch at your belt containing: a knife with a horn handle, three coins of unfamiliar mint, a whetstone, and a folded scrap of vellum you can't read.

The writing looks like chicken scratches. Cyrillic? Runic? Something older.

Outside the window: a mud street. Timber-framed buildings leaning toward each other like gossips. People in wool and leather moving with purpose—no headphones, no hurry, no *elsewhere* to be. A woman carries water in wooden pails, yoke across her shoulders. A man sharpens a scythe on a stone, *shhh-k, shhh-k*, the sound marking time.

You don't know the year. The century. The *continent*.

But you know this: the rules have changed. Survival isn't a metaphor anymore.

You need to **find someone who can read that vellum**. Or **learn the language of this place fast**. Or **earn a meal before sundown**—because whatever era this is, the sun still sets, and the dark still comes, and you have no flashlight.

The rooster crows again. Closer this time.

*Decision time, dreamer.*

**What do you do?**
