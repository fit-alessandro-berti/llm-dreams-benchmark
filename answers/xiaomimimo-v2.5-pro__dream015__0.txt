You find yourself in a post-apocalyptic world, with resources scarce and dangers everywhere. Despite the hardships, you decide to **seek out others, not to scavenge or fight, but to gather stories.**

You begin to walk, not towards the distant, smoldering ruins where most survivors huddle, but along the quiet, overgrown roads and into the silent towns. Your pack holds little—some water, a few dried apples, a knife—but your hands hold a worn, empty notebook and a charcoal pencil.

In the skeleton of a library, you find a family. They don't want your food. They want to tell you about the book they saved: a children's picture book about a rabbit. The father reads it aloud every night for his daughter, and they both weep and laugh. You write down the title, and the feeling of the pages in his hands.

In a collapsed subway station, a former engineer shows you his life's work: a intricate, gravity-powered light system made of bottles and mirrors. It casts dancing waterfalls of light on the damp walls. "It does nothing useful," he says, "but it remembers the sun." You sketch the design and his tired, proud smile.

By a river, you meet an old woman washing a single, fragile piece of blue china—a teacup. She tells you it was from her grandmother's set, and how they would drink tea on Sunday afternoons. The memory is so vivid you can almost smell the bergamot and rain. You note the color of the glaze.

You do not promise safety or restoration. You simply listen, record, and bear witness. You collect the fragments of humanity not for currency or power, but as the only seeds for a future that might still feel.

You wake, the dream fading, but the weight of the notebook and the echoes of voices remain. The world is broken, but you have found your purpose: to be a keeper of the small, persistent fires that darkness cannot extinguish.
