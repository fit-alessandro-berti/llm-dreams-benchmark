I trace one of the swirling lines with my index finger, and it chimes—a sound like a drop of water hitting a crystal goblet. Suddenly, I just *know*, with the unquestioned certainty that only exists in dreams, that the box doesn't require a metal key. It requires a memory.

I close my eyes and think of the smell of rain on hot summer asphalt. 

The box gives a deep, resonant click. The lid doesn't just open; it unfolds outward like the petals of a metallic lotus. Inside, there is no physical object. Instead, there is a miniature, swirling storm cloud, no larger than an apple. Tiny, silent lightning flashes illuminate the mist from within. 

I reach out to touch the cloud, but as my finger makes contact, the perspective violently shifts. 

I am no longer standing in the room. I am falling *upward* into the storm. The box is gone, and I am tumbling through a sky made of dark, churning velvet. I don't feel afraid; I feel entirely weightless. The lightning flashes around me, and with each flash, the clouds briefly take the shape of giant, sleeping faces. 

"You're late," one of the cloud-faces murmurs, its voice vibrating through my chest rather than my ears. 

Before I can answer, the velvet sky shatters like a mirror. The shards fall away to reveal that I am standing ankle-deep in a perfectly still, infinite expanse of shallow water. The sky above is a brilliant, bruised twilight. In my hand, I am holding the locked box again, but it is now made of woven grass and humming softly. 

A shadow glides beneath the water at my feet. It’s a koi fish, but it’s the size of a submarine, and its scales are made of the same flowing, liquid brass as the box. It breaks the surface, opening its massive mouth. Resting on its tongue is a single, rusted iron key.

I reach for it, my fingers brushing the cold metal. 

The moment I grasp the key, the water turns to mist, the mist turns to blinding morning light, and the humming fades into the sound of my actual alarm clock. 

I open my waking eyes, staring at my bedroom ceiling. The dream dissolves rapidly, slipping through my mind like water through a sieve. But as I reach over to turn off the alarm, my thumb is still tingling, and resting on my nightstand, just for a fraction of a second, I swear I see the faint, golden shimmer of liquid brass.
