From painting, you turn to sculpture, and mould figures of men and women, and animals, and fantastic shapes, which are immediately dissolved. Next, you become an architect, and build houses, and temples, and cities, that last no longer than the last. After that, you set yourself to dig mines, and you burrow into the bowels of the earth, and bring treasures to light; but the fruits of your labour perish almost as soon as enjoyed. From mining, you pass to farming, and sow seeds, and reap harvests, but the corn ripens not to bread, and the bread feeds not men, but birds of the air. At length, you become a fisher, and cast a net into the sea, and draw it up full; but the fish are not fit for food, and, instead of being eaten, they are cast back again. Wearied with these vain occupations, you resolve to remain quiet; and, sitting down on the shore, you watch the waves. But the waves themselves cease not from agitation, and so you fall asleep.
