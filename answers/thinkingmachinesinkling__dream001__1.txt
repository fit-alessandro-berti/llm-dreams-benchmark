You start to run, but the pavement softens like wet paper beneath your feet, and every alley you turn reveals the same towering clock—its hands spinning backward, its face weeping rust. You flag down a stranger, an old woman with eyes like shattered mirrors, who tells you the address is *“the place you left behind”* before she dissolves into a flock of pigeons that murmur in your mother tongue.

The buildings lean closer, whispering directions you almost understand. You check your pockets and find only a key made of ice, melting slowly, and a map drawn in breath on fogged glass. The city rearranges itself with every step: bridges become staircases, staircases become mouths.

As the hour strikes—not with a bell, but with the sound of a door closing deep inside your chest—you realize there is no building. You are already sitting in an empty plaza that smells like rain and forgotten names, across from a figure who has your face but calmer eyes. The meeting was never about arriving. The important thing you needed to say has been waiting on the tip of your tongue since before you fell asleep, and now, in the dream, you finally remember the words.
