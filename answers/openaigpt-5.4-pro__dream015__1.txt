…plant a garden.

At first it feels absurd. The city around you is all broken glass, leaning towers, and rusted skeletons of cars half-swallowed by sand. The wind carries ash instead of birdsong. People barter in whispers. Nights belong to things with glowing eyes.

But still, you find a cracked courtyard between two collapsed buildings, a place where sunlight spills through for a few hours each day. With your bare hands, you clear rubble. You pry open cans to save the rainwater. You trade a knife for a pouch of seeds from an old woman who smiles as if she remembers a different century.

Every morning, you come back.

Others laugh at first. Then they watch. Then, one by one, they help. A child brings a bucket. A stranger builds a fence from bent rebar. Someone hangs little mirrors to scare away scavenger birds. Someone else tells stories while you work, so the silence doesn’t swallow the place whole.

And then, one day, there is green.

Tiny, fragile, impossible green.

The sight of it changes everyone. The settlement stops feeling like a place where people are merely waiting to die. It becomes a place that might continue. Tomatoes swell like small suns. Beans climb a broken traffic light. Flowers bloom in a bathtub full of soil, and their colors are so vivid they look unreal, even in the dream.

Soon the garden becomes more than food. It becomes a meeting place, a promise, a rebellion against the ruined world. Travelers arrive and stay. Children who had only known dust learn the names of herbs. At night, lanterns glow among the leaves, and the cracked concrete smells of basil and wet earth.

Then, as dreams do, the world shifts.

You look up and realize the stars above are not stars at all, but seeds—millions of them—drifting across the sky. The whole sky is planting itself. Vines creep up the shattered skyscrapers, wrapping them in green. Roots split the highways. Trees rise through train stations. The old world is not ending anymore. It is being quietly, stubbornly replaced.

And standing in the middle of it, dirt under your nails, you understand:

in the end, survival was never just about finding shelter from the dark.

It was about deciding to grow something anyway.
