

You find yourself in a post-apocalyptic world, with resources scarce and dangers everywhere. Despite the hardships, you decide to **plant a garden in the cracked concrete of an abandoned highway overpass**.  

Not for food—though you tuck in hardy potatoes and drought-resistant beans where the rubble allows soil to gather. Not just for medicine—though you crush yarrow for fever and steep mint from a stubborn clump near a leaking pipe.  

You plant for the *color*.  

Scavenged seeds from a buried seedbank (marigolds, cosmos, a single packet of forget-me-nots) go into soup-can planters carved from overturned cars. You rig a drip system from salvaged IV bags and hollowed bamboo, catching the rare morning dew. When the wind howls through the skeletal skyscrapers, you hum off-key tunes to the seedlings—songs your grandmother sang, half-remembered, that feel like threading light through the dark.  

Others watch. Some scoff: *"Beauty won’t stop the rotters."* Others bring chipped mugs of boiled water to sit silently beside you as you work. A child with ash-smudged cheeks presses a dandelion into your palm one day, whispering, *"It’s yellow like the sun used to be."*  

That night, as you eat stew thickened with your first tiny harvest, you trace the crack in your hands where blisters split the skin. The garden isn’t safe. Raiders come. Storms flood the lower beds. But for those moments—when your fingers sink into cool earth, when a bee (miraculously, *miraculously*) drifts toward a marigold bloom—you remember: **this world didn’t just take. It also left room to tend.**  

And tending, you realize, is how you whisper back to the silence: *I am still here. We are still here.*  

*(You wake with soil under your nails and the ghost of marigolds on your tongue.)*
