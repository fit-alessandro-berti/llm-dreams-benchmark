 speak, and your voice emerges not as sound but as something visible—threads of silver light that weave themselves into shapes above the crowd. Each syllable becomes a bird, a key, a small burning star. You look down at your notes and find they've turned into water, running through your fingers and evaporating before they hit the stage floor. 

The audience isn't staring at you anymore; they're breathing in the light, their eyes closed, their chests rising in perfect unison with your heartbeat. You realize, with the calm certainty that only arrives in dreams, that you were never meant to give a speech at all. You were meant to remember something the crowd has forgotten, and the words are merely the path your body takes to reach it.

The stage begins to grow. The walls expand outward until the theater is a valley, then a plain, then something vaster than geography. The people are no longer sitting in seats but standing in tall grass, looking up not at a podium but at the night sky—which is where you now find yourself, suspended and effortless. You continue speaking, or perhaps the speaking continues you, and the light-words drift down like snow, settling on their shoulders, sinking into their skin. No one applauds. Instead, they begin to glow, softly, one by one, until the darkness is filled with a constellation of waking. 

You feel a gentle tug at your ankle. Someone is calling your name from behind the curtain, but the curtain is also a river, also your childhood bedroom door. You know that when you turn around, the speech will end, the valley will fold back into a stage, and the silver threads will vanish. So you linger a moment longer, floating in the dark, watching the crowd become stars, understanding exactly what you needed to say even as the dream begins to let you go.
