...the air hums with overlapping conversations and the scent of rain on pavement, though no storm has touched the glass ceiling of this vast, palatial space. Marble floors reflect strings of warm, golden light, and I catch glimpses of strangers laughing in circles, their faces shifting like leaves in a breeze.

I keep searching for Maya—she’d promised she’d be here, holding a crimson scarf so I could spot her. But instead, I stumble into a circle of historians arguing passionately about forgotten treaties, their hands sketching maps in the air. One of them turns to me, eyes bright: “Don’t you think revolutions always begin in whispers?” Before I can answer, the group dissolves like smoke.

I drift toward a quieter corner, where artists sit cross-legged on cushions, weaving glowing threads into tapestries that seem to pulse with memory. A woman with silver-streaked hair hands me a loom. “Everyone adds a line,” she says softly. My fingers move, weaving a pattern I don’t recognize—a lattice of bridges and stars.

Then, laughter pulls me toward a cluster of dancers moving in sync, not to music, but to the rhythm of their own breath. They part to let me through, and for a moment I’m at the center, the room spinning softly around me. I still don’t see Maya—but somehow, the ache of not finding her has softened.

Suddenly, a hand touches my shoulder. I turn, and there she is—not with a crimson scarf, but with two mugs of tea, steam curling like question marks in the air. “You took the long way,” she smiles, as if she’d been watching me wander through all those rooms, all those words and threads and dances. “I was about to leave a trail of breadcrumbs.”

And just like that, the dream steadies. We sit on the staircase, sipping tea that tastes of chamomile and distant rain, and the gathering continues below us—a kaleidoscope of moments, all somehow connected. I don’t know how long we stay. But in the way of dreams, it feels both timeless and fleetingly brief.
