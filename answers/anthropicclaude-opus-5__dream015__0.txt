...start a library.

Not a farm, not a militia, not a still for turning rot into liquor. A library. The decision arrives the way decisions arrive in dreams — already made, already old, as though I'd been carrying it in my coat since before the coat existed.

The building I choose used to be a laundromat. The dryer drums make excellent shelving if you lay them on their sides; each porthole becomes a little chapel with three or four books inside, and when the wind comes through the broken window the pages riffle and the whole wall murmurs at once. I sweep the floor with a broom made of somebody's antenna and a bundle of dry sage. The sweeping takes eleven years. It takes an afternoon. Both are true; the dream doesn't care.

People come, at first, to ask what I'm selling. I tell them: nothing, but you can borrow. They look at me with the specific pity reserved for the harmlessly insane, which is a much better look than the other one, the one that measures your body for its water content.

A woman named Odile brings me a waterlogged paperback she's been using to level a table. It's a manual for repairing outboard motors. There is no water within four hundred miles. I stamp it and shelve it under *Faith*.

A boy brings a photograph. Not a book, I say, and he says, *it has a story on the back*, and turns it over, and it does: three lines in pencil, a name, a date, *she was laughing because I fell in*. I stamp it. Shelf: *Faith*.

The trouble starts, as it always does in these dreams, with men who have decided that the future is a thing you take rather than a thing you leave lying around for others. They arrive in the late gold of an afternoon that has been late gold for what feels like a season. Their leader has a good face ruined by certainty. He asks me what use a library is when nobody can eat it.

And here the dream does the thing dreams do — it hands me the answer before I've thought of it, the way a stagehand slips a prop into your palm from behind the curtain.

*None,* I say. *That's the point. Everything else here has a use, and so everything else here gets fought over. This is the only building in a hundred miles that nobody has a reason to burn. So it's the only place people put things down. Look —* and I show him the shelf where strangers have left what they couldn't carry and couldn't bear to throw away: a wedding ring, a child's shoe, a jar of buttons, a folded map of a city that isn't. *You can take the books. They're just paper. But then this becomes a place with something worth taking, and then it becomes a place worth defending, and then it becomes a fort, and then there is nowhere left in the world to set anything down.*

He's quiet a long time. Long enough for the light to go from gold to the colour of a peach's inside.

Then he takes off his belt — the whole rig, holster and all — and lays it on the shelf next to the jar of buttons, and says, *I'd like to borrow something. I can't read much.*

*That's fine,* I say. *Neither can most of the shelves.*

He picks the outboard motor manual. Of course he does.

And after he's gone, and the wall is murmuring again, I notice that the laundromat has gotten larger while I wasn't looking — that the dryer drums go back and back in ranks past where the wall should be, thousands of them, tens of thousands, each porthole lit faintly from within, and I understand that I am not a person in a ruined world at all but something more like a room where things are kept, and that the apocalypse was never the point. The point was that afterward, somebody has to be the place where you can put a thing down and know it will still be there.

I wake up sweeping.
