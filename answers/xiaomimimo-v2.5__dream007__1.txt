We’re standing on the edge of the old, cracked pier—the one we used to fish off of as kids. The air is thick, smelling of lake water and coming rain. You’re insisting that I have to move back home to help with the family business, that it’s my duty. I’m firing back that my art, my life in the city, isn’t just a phase—it’s who I am.

The words are flying, faster and sharper than we’ve ever thrown at each other. The sky darkens, mirroring our mood. I feel a terrible, hot pressure in my chest, a mix of anger and the fear of actually losing you over this.

Then, you say, “If you can’t see that, you’ve never really cared about any of us.”

Something in me just… breaks. Not with another shout, but with a sudden, heavy silence. I look at your face, really look at it. I see the hurt there, not just from this fight, but old hurt. And I realize I’m seeing it because the first drops of rain are starting to fall, splashing on the wooden planks between us.

Without thinking, I reach out and grab your arm. Not to shove, but to pull you with me. “Come on,” I say, my voice raw. “Just… come with me.”

I start running, dragging you along the pier toward the old boathouse. You’re confused, stumbling, but you come. We burst through the creaking door just as the sky opens up. The rain drums deafeningly on the tin roof. We’re both panting, soaked, standing in the dim, dusty space smelling of pine and gasoline.

You look at me, eyes wide. “What was that for?”

I don’t answer with words. I just point to the window. We watch together as the rain turns the lake into a churning, grey mirror. The argument, the heat, the terrible words—it all feels distant out there now, washed out by the storm. In here, it’s just the drumming of the rain and our shared breathing.

“I’m not choosing against you,” I finally say, quiet enough to be heard over the downpour. “I’m choosing *for* me. And it’s the hardest thing I’ve ever done.”

You’re quiet for a long time. Then, you let out a slow breath. “I know,” you whisper. “I’m just scared of losing the *us* we were.”

And in that moment, the dream shifts. We’re not fighting anymore. We’re just two friends, huddled in a boathouse, watching a storm pass, understanding that some things can’t be solved with a winner and a loser. They can only be weathered, together. The dream ends with the rain softening, and a sliver of watery sunlight touching the water.
