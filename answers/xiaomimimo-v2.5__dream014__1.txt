As I follow them, the city’s harsh edges seem to soften. Their laughter is easy, and they point out little things I’d missed—a mural of birds taking flight on a brick wall, a café that smells like cinnamon, a quiet garden tucked between two towers. One of them hands me a warm cup of tea, its steam curling like a gentle question mark in the cool air.

As we walk, the streets begin to feel less like a maze and more like a story I’m slowly remembering. They tell me about their own moments of being lost, and how sometimes, the detour is where the real journey begins.

And then, at a small crossroads under a streetlamp, they smile and say, “You’re almost where you need to be.” I look up and realize the direction I’d been searching for isn’t marked on any map—it’s the feeling of connection itself. The city hums around us, no longer overwhelming, just alive, and I understand: sometimes, being found isn’t about reaching a destination, but about discovering you were never truly alone.
