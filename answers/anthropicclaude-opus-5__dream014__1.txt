...and you follow them, though you never quite see their faces.

The city has that dream-logic to it: the streets rearrange themselves politely behind you, like people making room on a train. I had been standing at an intersection where all four signs said the name of a street I'd never heard of, holding a paper bag with something warm in it, and I couldn't remember whether I was arriving or leaving. The crowds moved through me more than around me. That's how I knew I was lost in the serious way — not misplaced, but unreferenced.

Then one of them touched my elbow. A woman in a yellow raincoat, though it wasn't raining, and she said, "Oh, you're one of ours," which made no sense and yet unlocked something in my chest. Behind her: a man carrying a folding chair, a teenager with headphones around his neck like a collar, an old woman eating grapes from her coat pocket. None of them asked where I was going. That was the mercy of it. They just began walking, and I began walking with them, and the walking itself was the answer.

We went down a street where every shop sold lamps, all of them lit. We went through a courtyard where someone was practicing scales on a trumpet, badly, beautifully. The man with the folding chair unfolded it whenever I slowed down, and I sat, and no one hurried me. The teenager gave me one earbud and I heard a song I'd loved at nineteen and forgotten completely, and I started crying, and the old woman handed me a grape as if that were the standard treatment.

"Where are we going?" I finally asked.

"You'll recognize it," the woman in yellow said.

And then the city thinned, the way fog thins, and we were standing at the top of a flight of stairs that went down into water — clear, shallow, warm, with the reflection of streetlights lying on it like coins. And I did recognize it, though I have never been there. It was the feeling of the back seat of a car at night as a child, when the adults are talking quietly in the front and you don't have to know the route.

"You were never actually lost," the man with the chair said, folding it one last time. "You were just unaccompanied."

I turned to thank them and they had become the crowd again, thousands of strangers moving through the streets on their thousands of errands, each of them carrying a warm paper bag, each of them, I understood suddenly, findable. The trumpet got the scale right. Somewhere far off a door opened and the light coming out of it was the color of the inside of an eyelid.

I stepped down into the water and it was the sound of my own name, spoken by someone who loves me, in a room I haven't woken up in yet.
