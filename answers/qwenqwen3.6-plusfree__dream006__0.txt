...speak, but the words don't come out as sound. Instead, they slip from your lips as silver threads, catching the overhead lights and weaving into the air like luminous spider silk. The microphone in your hand warms, its metal casing softening into polished wood, then sprouting a single green leaf at the base. You don't panic. In that quiet, unspooling logic of dreams, it feels completely right.

The audience doesn't blink. Their faces blur and reform—not into strangers, but into people you haven't thought of in years: your high school English teacher, a childhood friend who moved away, the barista who always remembered your order. Even familiar streetlights and stray dogs seem to have taken seats in the back rows. The stage beneath your shoes begins to tilt, not dangerously, but gently, like a ship settling into calm water. With every thought you shape, the silver threads pulse brighter, drifting into the rafters and blooming into slow-turning constellations.

You try to reach for your prepared notes, but your pockets hold only smooth river stones and a single dried magnolia petal. The dream doesn't want a script. It wants the thing you've been avoiding saying out loud. So you speak instead of quiet doubts, of unmailed apologies, of the strange comfort in watching rain trace paths on a windowpane. And as you do, the crowd exhales in unison—a sound like tall grass bending in a summer wind. Someone in the front row lifts their hand, and a strand of your words loops gently around their wrist like a bracelet. They smile. You realize you aren't performing. You're translating something everyone already knows but forgot how to listen for.

The house lights dim, not to black, but to the exact shade of dawn pressing through closed eyelids. The wooden microphone crumbles into fine ash that smells like petrichor and old paper. You bow, though you never finished a single planned sentence, and the stage floor parts like the pages of a well-read book, releasing a cool, updraft breeze. Just as you step backward into the dark, a single word—*enough*—hangs in the air, glowing softly at the edges, before you feel the strange, weightless sensation of falling upward into morning.
