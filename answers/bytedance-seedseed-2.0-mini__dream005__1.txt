You are at a large social gathering where you only know one person. As you try to find them, you encounter various groups of people and stumble into a huddle of art students arguing over whether a rubber chicken half-buried in melted candle wax counts as "site-specific installation art." I nod along politely, even though I couldn’t define the term if I tried, and slip past before they can press a clipboard full of "edible performance manifestos" into my hands.

Next, a pair of stand-ups do an impromptu set mocking party appetizer nomenclature—“bacon-wrapped dates” might as well be “space dragon mozz sticks” for all anyone can parse, they cackle, and a cluster of nearby guests toss their pretzel bites in agreement. I snort before remembering I don’t recognize a single face in the crowd, and hurry off when one of the comics gestures wildly at me like they want me to join the bit as their "expert on confounding snack names."

I wind up stepping in a puddle of glittery blue punch that sticks my sneakers to the hardwood, then dodge a waiter carrying a tray of champagne flutes each holding a tiny glowing goldfish instead of bubbly prosecco. I steady the tray for them, and one of the fish blows a pearlescent bubble that pops into a tiny speech bubble reading "Look left." When I turn, a string of iridescent mylar balloons bobs over a set of floor-to-ceiling bookshelves that definitely weren’t there two minutes ago.

I push past the shelves, and the room shifts—suddenly I’m in my childhood backyard, strung with fairy lights and covered in the same foldable tables from the main gathering. Lila, the only person I know here, is perched on a lawn chair wearing a crown of dandelions, holding a mug of steaming hot cocoa. She waves and grins: "Took you long enough. I watched you try to debate the rubber chicken guys for ten minutes and decided to let you work through it."

When I go to sit down, I realize the lawn chair is actually the arm of my desk chair from my small apartment, but I don’t even blink at the weird, dream-logic twist. She hands me the mug, and the mini marshmallows on top float into the shape of tiny stars, sticking to the glitter still dusted on my sweater. For a second, I spot the art students and the comedians and the goldfish waiter standing at the edge of the yard, waving, before they fade into the glowing fireflies flitting over the grass. The next thing I know, I’m leaning against my actual bedroom door, still holding an empty, sticky cocoa mug, and the faint sound of a kazoo orchestra playing *Somewhere Over the Rainbow* drifts through my open window.
