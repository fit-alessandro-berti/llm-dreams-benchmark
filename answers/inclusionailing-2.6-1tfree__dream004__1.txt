…and the canvas is not lying flat—it is standing on three spindly legs, humming in a low register that tickles your molars. The colors rushing past in the sky—tangerine tornadoes, indigo rivers that fold like origami—seem to hesitate when they reach its edge, as if waiting for permission.

You reach out and touch the surface. Your fingertip sinks with a soft pop into a thin film of liquid glass. A single breath escapes you, and it blooms across the canvas like smoke pressed into lace. Instantly, the blankness remembers what it was: a map of every room you have ever slept in, rearranged into a forest. The floorboards become branches; the pillows puff into cumulus animals with button eyes that blink in slow Morse.

A brush materializes in your hand. It is made of a rib of something pale and warm—maybe whalebone, maybe a wish—and its bristles are stiff eyelashes. When you drag it across, it does not leave paint. It leaves pockets of time. A streak of ochre drips upward and turns into the afternoon you learned to ride a bicycle without hands; a curl of ultramarine pools and becomes the night you whispered a secret to a stone.

Shapes begin to break their choreography to listen. A hexagon yawns and stretches into a ladder. A spiral rolls over and offers you its center like a soft fruit. You climb the ladder into the ultramarine night, and there, hanging from the ceiling of the sky, is a second canvas, smaller and more impatient. It is whispering your name in a voice that sounds like rustling pages.

You realize you are not painting for yourself. You are painting a door. But whether it opens in or out is a question the dream keeps folding into itself, like a letter tucked into a bottle tossed back into the same river that is already flowing upstream.
