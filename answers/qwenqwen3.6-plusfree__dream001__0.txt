The faster you move, the more the city breathes: balconies tilt like closing eyelids, streetlamps bloom with silver moths, and shop windows reflect a sky full of floating staircases. You duck into an alley where a vendor sells compasses made of hollowed pears. One needle spins, not north, but toward your own chest. "Follow the quiet," the vendor says, pressing it into your palm. 

You break into a sprint, but the pavement softens into moss. A tram arrives without rails, gliding over a river of crushed magnolia petals. You step aboard. The passengers sit perfectly still, reading newspapers printed in their own childhood handwriting. The conductor, wearing a coat stitched with calendar pages, nods. "We stop where the hour is keeping its breath," he says, and pulls a brass lever.

You step out beneath a canopy of suspended clocks. Every face ticks backward except one hanging directly above you. Its hands freeze. A heavy oak door materializes in the air, grain shifting like water, your name carved into the wood in letters you feel you've always known. You turn the handle.

Inside isn't a conference room. It's a sunlit courtyard you haven't visited in years. The table is set for one. On it rests a single envelope sealed with wax the color of dawn. You open it. The paper is blank until you breathe on it, and your own handwriting rises like steam: *You were never late. You were arriving exactly when you were ready to listen.*

The city outside fades into the sound of your own steady breathing. The meeting wasn't a place to go. It was a part of you to return to. And as the dream begins to lighten, you realize you already know the way back.
