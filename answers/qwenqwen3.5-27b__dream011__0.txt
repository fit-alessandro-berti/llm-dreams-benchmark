You pick up your pen, but it feels weightless, like a hollow reed. As you hover the tip over the first question, the text ripples. Instead of asking for a date or a formula, the first line asks: *"Describe the taste of the silence in this room."*

You blink, and the text shifts again. *"Explain why the clock on the wall is counting backward."*

You look up. The clock is indeed ticking backward, but the hands are melting into the face, dripping like wax. Around you, the other students are silent, but you realize they aren't breathing. Their faces are smooth, featureless masks that reflect your own anxious expression back at you.

Panic rises in your chest, the familiar cold sweat of an unprepared student, but then you notice something strange. When you look away from the paper, the questions vanish. When you look back, they return, but they have changed. They are now questions about things you haven't experienced yet. *"What will you do when you forget your own name?"*

The proctor walks down the aisle. It’s you, but older, wearing a suit made of starlight. You don't speak, but you hear a thought in your head: *"Stop trying to answer. Start trying to understand."*

You look down at the paper one last time. The ink stops moving. The words dissolve into a single, clear instruction: *"The exam is over. You have already passed."*

The classroom walls dissolve into a sky of endless blue. The paper turns into a bird and flies out the window. You feel the sensation of falling, not in fear, but in relief, as the dream begins to fade into the morning light.
