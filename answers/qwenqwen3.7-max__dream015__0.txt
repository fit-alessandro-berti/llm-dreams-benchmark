…build a lighthouse in the middle of the Great Dust Basin, a place where water hasn’t existed for a century. 

In the waking world, this would be madness. But here, in the fluid logic of the dream, it feels like the most urgent and necessary thing I have ever done. 

The sky above is the color of a bruised plum, and the air tastes faintly of copper and crushed lavender. I begin to gather my materials. I don't use bricks or mortar; instead, I scavenge the ruins of the old world. I collect shattered panes of stained glass from collapsed cathedrals, rusted hull-plates from abandoned cars, and the long, curved ribs of some unnamed, fossilized leviathan. My hands don't blister. The heavy steel feels as light as balsa wood, bending to my will like warm wax.

The dangers of this wasteland are always watching. Shadows with too many joints skitter at the edge of my vision. Flocks of ash-birds with razor-sharp feathers circle in the bruised sky, waiting for me to show weakness. Occasionally, a pack of Scavenger-Hounds—creatures made of twisted wire and glowing red optics—prowl the perimeter of my construction site. 

But I don't run, and I don't fight. I simply hum a low, resonant tune. In this dream, the hum creates a physical barrier, a shimmering geometric shield of sound that keeps the monsters at bay. They pace and snap their metallic jaws, but they cannot cross the melody.

Days pass, though time here is measured not in hours, but in the shifting colors of the dust storms. Finally, the lighthouse is complete. It is a towering, impossible spire of kaleidoscopic glass and rusted iron, spiraling up into the heavy sky. 

I climb the winding, floating staircase to the lantern room at the top. There is no oil, no bulb, no fire. Instead, I reach into my coat pocket and pull out a small, glass jar. Inside is a captured piece of a dying star I found in the crater of a fallen moon. It pulses with a warm, liquid gold light. 

I place the jar in the center of the grand prism. 

The moment the starlight hits the stained glass, the lighthouse ignites. Beams of brilliant, multifaceted color shoot out across the desolate, cracked earth. The light sweeps over the dunes, painting the wasteland in vibrant hues of sapphire, emerald, and ruby. 

Then, the dream shifts, as dreams do. 

Where the colored light touches the dry, cracked earth, the dust begins to liquefy. It doesn't turn to mud, but to clear, glowing water. The dry basin fills rapidly, the water rising to mirror the stained-glass beams. The Scavenger-Hounds at the base of the tower dissolve into schools of silver fish, darting through the newly formed shallows. The ash-birds in the sky turn into white sails. 

I stand at the top of the lighthouse, looking out over an impossible, glowing sea that stretches to the horizon. Ghostly galleons and sleek, futuristic skiffs begin to emerge from the mist, drawn by the beacon. The wind picks up, no longer smelling of copper, but of salt and impending rain. 

The light from the star in the prism grows brighter, intensifying until it is blinding, washing out the sea, the ships, and the bruised sky. The warmth of the light wraps around me, heavy and comforting, and as the white brilliance consumes everything, I feel myself gently falling upward, ready to wake.
