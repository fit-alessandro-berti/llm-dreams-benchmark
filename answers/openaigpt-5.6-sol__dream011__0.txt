written in a language you almost understand—each symbol familiar until you look directly at it, when it rearranges itself into something else.

Around you, everyone is already writing. Their pencils scratch in perfect unison. You raise your hand, but the proctor has no face, only a clock where their features should be. Its hands spin faster each time you try to speak.

You turn the page. The next question reads:

**“What did you want to become before you learned to be afraid?”**

Suddenly, the classroom walls fall away. Your desk stands alone at the center of a vast train station. Departures flicker across the board—not cities, but lives you might have lived: **DOCTOR**, **MUSICIAN**, **TEACHER**, **UNKNOWN**. Every train is leaving in one minute.

There is no answer sheet now. Only a ticket in your hand, blank except for your name.

Then a voice over the loudspeaker says, gently:

“Your final exam is not to choose the correct future. It is to choose one and wake up.”

You run toward the nearest train just as the alarm begins to ring.
