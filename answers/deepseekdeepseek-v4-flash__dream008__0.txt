The fluorescent lights hummed a low, anxious frequency. The air in the office was cold, too cold, the kind that makes the skin prickle. Your boss, Sarah, stood over you, her smile a thin, tight line that didn't reach her eyes. In her hand was a single sheet of paper, which she laid on your desk with a final, definitive *thwack*.

"Here it is," she said. "Client wants it by 8 AM tomorrow. Full design, copy, and market analysis." She tapped the paper. "Don't let me down."

You looked at the paper. It was mostly blank, except for a single, deeply complex instruction written in the center:

*The solution must be a perfect palindrome, using only the prime numbers found between the seventh and thirteenth hours of yesterday. Also, it needs to be blue.*

Your desk phone rang, a shrill, jarring sound. You picked it up. A voice, calm and disembodied, said, "The clock is ticking. The cat is on the roof." The line went dead.

You looked at the empty whiteboard, then at the blinking cursor on your black computer screen. You typed a single, hopeful letter: "A."

The screen flashed red. **INVALID. PALINDROME CORRUPTED.**

The stress built like a physical pressure behind your eyes. The room began to tilt. The humming of the lights became a roaring. You gripped the edges of your desk, wood splintering under your fingertips, and realized, with a cold, terrifying clarity, that the wood was not wood. It was bone.

And the cursor on your screen began to type on its own, slowly, deliberately, forming the words: *"You already know the answer. You've been dreaming of it for years."*
