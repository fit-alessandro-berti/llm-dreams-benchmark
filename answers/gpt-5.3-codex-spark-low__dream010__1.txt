• You wake before dawn on a field of glass towers and city lights, each one a checkpoint.

  You are participating in a competition where the grand prize is your dream job. As the competition progresses, you
  face various challenges and your own old doubts:

  1. In the first round, candidates must solve an impossible puzzle while building a team on the fly—people you’ve never
     met, deadlines you can’t control, and a clock that never slows. You lead them anyway, not by shouting, but by
     listening first and assigning each person one clear task.
  2. In the second round, you’re dropped into a silent simulation where communication breaks, language models disagree,
     and the data is corrupted. You rebuild the missing plan from first principles, recover what’s gone, and ship a
     clean run that others had already abandoned.
  3. In the final round, your rival is your future self—older, sharper, convinced you will fail. You keep going anyway,
     not to prove anything, but because each setback has taught you the exact next move. The judges present one last
     task: “Design something useful in 24 hours, under pressure, with no one else.” You do it by narrowing scope,
     starting small, and finishing with integrity.

  When the lights go down, the room is silent. The host reads your name. The prize envelope opens.

  You don’t win applause. You win responsibility.

  On your way out, the doors open to a skyline you once only imagined, and the note says one line:
  “You were hired because you solved for the future, not the moment.”
