I try to raise my hand to ask the proctor for help, but when I look up, the proctor is me — or a version of me, older, wearing the clothes I wore on my first day of school as a child. This other-me shakes their head slowly and taps a wristwatch that has no hands.

Panic starts to rise, but then I notice something odd: my pencil has been writing on its own. When I look down at my paper, I see answers in my own handwriting — long, flowing paragraphs about things I didn't know I knew. Memories, really. The smell of my grandmother's kitchen. The name of a boy I used to sit next to in second grade. The precise blue of a sky I saw once from an airplane window.

The exam, I realize, was never about the questions.

The bell rings. I haven't finished, but somehow I know I've passed — or that passing was never the point. As I stand to leave, the walls of the classroom dissolve into a hallway of doors, each one slightly ajar, each one leaking a different kind of light.
