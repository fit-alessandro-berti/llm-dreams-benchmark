You are at a large social gathering where you only know one person. As you try to find them, you encounter various groups of people and — 

**they all already seem to know you.**

The first group is clustered in the kitchen around an island that is way too big for the house. They're laughing, but the sound is wrong. Like someone turned the volume all the way down on their laughter and all the way up on the clinking of their glasses. One of them turns to you, smiling too widely, and says, "We saved you a plate!" The plate is full, but it's all garnish. Parsley and lemon rinds and olives with the pits still in.

You say, "Have you seen —" but you can't remember your friend's name for a second. It sits right on the tip of your tongue. The group just nods like they understand and points deeper into the house.

So you keep going.

The house keeps going, too. It wasn't this big from the outside.

In the living room, a second group is sitting on the floor watching a TV that isn't plugged in. It's just soft static light. They all turn to look at you at the exact same time when you step on the creaky board, which is strange because you were trying so hard not to step on it. A little kid in that group tugs on your sleeve and says, "You're going the wrong way," very helpfully, very seriously.

The hallway is lined with coats. Way too many coats. You have to push through them like a forest, and for a second you are sure you'll get lost in there and no one will find you. When you come out the other side, your phone buzzes in your hand.

No signal. The text from your friend just says "out back!! :)" from three hours ago, even though you just got here.

A third group is by the back door, playing cards at a tiny table. None of the cards have numbers on them. Just faint pictures of places you half-recognize. Your old elementary school. The bus stop near your house. They don't look up at you at all, which somehow feels worse than staring.

You push the sliding door open.

The backyard is huge and it is suddenly dusk, even though it was afternoon in the kitchen. String lights are tangled overhead, not lit. And there, sitting alone on the bottom step of the deck, feet in the grass that is a little too tall, is the one person you know.

They're looking at their own phone, with that little crease they get in their forehead when they're waiting for a text to go through.

You say their name — and this time you remember it perfectly — and they look up.

"Oh my god," they say. "I thought you weren't going to find me."

And all the vague, buzzing anxiety of the house — the too-many coats, the too-big kitchen, the people who knew your name — just drains out at once. You sit down next to them. The grass is cool and damp and completely real.

"I think I'm dreaming," you whisper, and it feels true and not scary at all.

"I know," they say, leaning back. "Stay a little longer anyway?"

And you do.
