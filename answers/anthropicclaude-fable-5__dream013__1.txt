...begin to notice that the puzzles are changing. They're no longer abstract riddles carved into stone — they're becoming personal.

One puzzle asks you to arrange a series of mirrors, but each mirror reflects a different version of yourself: younger, older, the person you almost became. To solve it, you must align them so the light passes through all of you at once.

Deeper still, the walls of the maze grow soft, like the corridors are breathing. You come to a door with no lock — only a question written in handwriting you recognize as your own, though you don't remember writing it: *"What were you looking for before you started searching?"*

You realize, with that strange dream-certainty, that you've been solving the puzzles too quickly. The maze isn't testing whether you can escape. It's slowing you down on purpose, making you *look* at things.

The next chamber contains a table with a single jigsaw puzzle, nearly complete. The missing piece is in your pocket — somehow you've always known it was there. But when you place it, the image doesn't resolve into a picture. It resolves into a sound: a voice from very long ago, saying your name the way only one person ever said it.

The maze walls begin to dissolve like sugar in water, and you understand that the center was never a place. You wake — or you don't — with your hand still curled around something small and puzzle-piece-shaped, already forgetting what the voice said, but not how it felt to hear it.
