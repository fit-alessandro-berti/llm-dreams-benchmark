Suddenly, the wind ceases. Not a fade, but a violent snap into total silence. 

The massive wave looming over the starboard side—a mountain of water that should have crushed the hull—stops mid-crash. It freezes into a towering wall of translucent glass. Inside the frozen wave, you see not fish or debris, but a city of shimmering gold, its spires reaching toward the surface, its inhabitants staring back at you with wide, curious eyes.

You realize the ship isn't fighting the storm; it is being drawn toward the glass. 

With a surge of inexplicable certainty, you don't steer away. You heave the wheel hard to the right, driving the bow directly into the heart of the frozen wave. There is no crash, only the sound of a thousand crystal bells ringing at once. 

The ship glides through the glass, the turbulent sea vanishing instantly. You are no longer sailing on water, but drifting through a celestial ocean of starlight and floating islands, where the wind smells of jasmine and old books. 

Your crew begins to cheer, their voices sounding like music, and as you look down at your hands, you see they are beginning to turn into light. You smile, knowing that you aren't the captain of a ship anymore, but the architect of the horizon. 

And then, the light grows too bright, and the sound of the bells becomes the distant ringing of an alarm clock.
