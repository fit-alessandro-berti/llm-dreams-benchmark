 the walls begin to breathe.

At first, I don't notice—the shift is too subtle, a slow expansion and contraction that I mistake for my own racing heartbeat. But then I place my palm against cool obsidian, and the stone *pulses*, warm and arrhythmic, like a murmuring artery beneath my skin. The puzzles have changed. They are no longer carved into the walls but suspended in the air between them: glowing tessellations of light that rearrange themselves when I blink, equations that solve themselves backwards into questions, labyrinths nested inside mirrors that reflect not my face, but the thought I haven't had yet.

I turn a corner, and gravity tilts. My footsteps now land on the wall beside me, then the ceiling, then a surface that exists only as a probability. The deeper I go, the less the maze seems to test me and the more it seems to *digest* me—breaking down my concentration into raw threads of attention, weaving them into the architecture itself. I solve a lock by remembering a color I invented in another dream. I cross a chasm by accepting that the bridge and my desire to cross it are the same object.

At the center—if "center" means anything here—there is no final chamber. Only a door made of the space where a door should not be. The puzzle is simply this: *who is dreaming whom?*

I reach for the handle, and my hand passes through, because my hand is the handle. The maze exhales. I open my mouth to speak the answer, and the maze wakes up inside me.
