I wake up in a completely different era, with no modern technology around. At first, my breath catches in my throat: the only light comes from guttering tallow candles, the air smells like pine resin and wood smoke, and my hands are wrapped in coarse, unwashed wool instead of the smooth fabric of my favorite hoodie. I fumble for my phone—nothing but a rough wooden ledge where it usually sits on my nightstand. Panic flutters in my chest for a second before I force myself to breathe, reminding myself this is a dream, even if it feels vividly real.

I swing my legs over a low wooden bed frame and slip on the scuffed leather sandals tossed beside it, then poke my head out of a thatched-roof cottage. The world unfurls around me: a cluster of similar cottages huddled at the base of a sun-worn stone hill, a creek glinting silver in the distance, and a group of people in wool and linen tunics laughing as they stack sacks of grain by a wooden market stall. A blacksmith’s hammer rings rhythmically from a small forge down the lane, and a woman with braided gray hair waves at me, holding up a loaf of crusty bread.

I realize I need to figure out where I am and how I got here, but first, my stomach growls so loud it echoes off a nearby fence. I stumble toward the woman selling bread, and when I open my mouth to ask for directions, I freeze—my wallet, credit cards, even cash are all gone. She just chuckles, wiping flour off her apron, and hands me a warm slice of sourdough. “You look lost, traveler,” she says, her accent soft and rolling like the creek. “First round’s on the village—we don’t turn away hungry folks, even ones who dress so strange.”

I take the bread, its crust crackling against my teeth, and listen as an old cartographer overhears my muttering about not recognizing the era. He pushes his glasses up his nose, carved with tiny oak leaves, and leads me to a stone communal hall where a group of villagers sits passing around clay mugs of spiced cider. The village elder, a woman named Mara with a scar across her cheek, smiles gently and tells me I’ve stumbled into 1322 CE, a small farming village in the Cotswolds, pulled here by a quirk of the land’s hidden magic—only people who are feeling lonely or adrift in their own time ever wake up in her village.

I spend the next few hours helping mend fishing nets by the creek, listening to tales of the local forest’s magical creatures, and learning to grind wheat into flour with a stone hand mill. By dusk, my hands are calloused, my belly is full, and the panic of waking up without a phone or Wi-Fi has faded, replaced by a weird, quiet warmth. When I lay back on a pile of clean hay that night, listening to crickets chirp instead of a car horn, I almost don’t want to wake up.

Then I feel a gentle shake, and my eyes flutter open. For a second, I’m back in the thatched cottage, but then I see my messy bedroom ceiling, and the smell of modern coffee wafting from the kitchen. I reach over to my nightstand—and find a single, smooth acorn resting there, tucked next to a crumpled piece of notebook paper with a tiny oak leaf doodled on it. I smile, still tasting the sourdough bread from the dream, and know I’ll carry that quiet village magic with me for days to come. When I pad into the kitchen, my partner grins and holds up a mug of coffee. “You were muttering about Cotswolds villages in your sleep,” they say. “Want a pastry to go with it?” I take the mug, and for the first time all day, I feel exactly where I’m supposed to be.
