You are in the middle of a heated argument with a close friend. The disagreement is about something important to both of you, and you decide to take a breath.

You step back. Literally — you take a few steps away, look out the window, and notice the way the late afternoon light is turning everything golden. For a moment, the beauty of it catches you off guard, and the sharpness in your chest softens just a little.

Your friend is still standing there, arms crossed, jaw tight. But when you turn back, you don't re-engage the argument. Instead, you say something you didn't expect:

"I think I'm more afraid of losing you than I am of being right about this."

And their expression shifts. Not all at once — but something cracks open behind their eyes. They uncross their arms. They sit down.

"I'm sorry," they say. "I wasn't listening to you. I was just... trying to win."

You sit down too. And for a while, neither of you says anything at all. The silence isn't uncomfortable anymore. It's the kind of silence that happens when two people remember that they chose each other — and keep choosing each other — even when it's hard.

The disagreement doesn't disappear. But it becomes something you can carry *together* instead of something that stands *between* you.

Then you wake up — and pick up your phone, thinking maybe you should call them.
