I turn the box over. No hinges. No seams. The patterns begin to shift, rearranging themselves slowly, like letters deciding where to stand in a sentence. A circle forms, then dissolves. A triangle folds inward. I realize I'm not just looking at the box — I'm reading it, and the box is reading me back.

Then I notice: the lock isn't a lock at all. It's an eye. Small, carved, lidless, staring up at me from the center of the lid. It blinks.

Not mechanically. *Tiredly.*

"You've been carrying me a long time," the box says, though it has no mouth. The voice comes from everywhere — the patterns, the warm metal, the air itself. It sounds like my own voice played backward and then translated into something gentler.

My hands are shaking now, but I don't want to put it down. The patterns are forming words I can suddenly read:

*What did you lock away to keep yourself safe?*

The box grows heavier. I feel something pressing against the inside of the lid — not physically, but emotionally. A pressure behind my ribs. A memory I've been circling for weeks, months, years.

I don't open it.

Instead, I sit down on whatever surface exists in this dream-place — a floor, a field, a threshold — and I hold the box in my lap. The eye watches me. I watch it back. Neither of us blinks for a long time.

And slowly, without my doing anything at all, the patterns begin to change again. They soften. The sharp angles round out. The symbols become less like warnings and more like invitations. The warmth in the metal shifts from feverish to steady — the warmth of a hand held in the dark.

The box is still locked.

But it feels different now. Not like something sealed shut. Like something that will open when I'm ready, and not a moment before.

The eye closes. The breathing inside slows to match mine.

And I wake up with the strange, aching certainty that I was given something important — even if I can't name it yet.
