You wake up in a completely different era, with no modern technology around. As you explore your new surroundings, you realize you need to survive—find shelter, food, and water. You look down at your clothes—they’re strange, like garments from a forgotten age. As you stumble through tall oak trees, cushioned by moss and ancient ferns, you begin to suspect you’ve slipped into another time—perhaps a forgotten century before engines roared or wires carried voices. Panic flutters in your chest. How did you get here? Your last memory: a dimlit lab humming with power, a successful teleportation experiment—then a flash of white light and a sensation of weightlessness. Now birdsong fills the air instead of sirens, no buzzing in your pockets, no screen waiting to be checked. The air smells sharp and clean, untainted by smoke, plastic, or fuel. You hear gurgling in the distance and follow it to a stream, crystal clear and untouched by concrete. Your reflection flickers in the water—you, yet Foreign... not just the time, but the way your posture is tense, unfamiliar, like you’re trapped in someone else’s story. You cup the cool water to your lips, drinking deeply, forcing yourself to focus. Drink slowly, don’t gulp—remember basic survival training. “You’re not dreaming,” you whisper, but the line between dream and reality has blurred. You look around carefully. No footprints but yours. No tools—but you’re not helpless. In this era, what you know might be more dangerous than helpful. But knowledge itself is a tool—maybe the edge you need to survive. Plotting your next move, you head along the riverbank, searching for signs of life, listening for anything familiar. Your main priority: shelter before nightfall. Your mind races with plans—perhaps indigenous dwellers live nearby and might welcome a disoriented traveler... or not. Every rustle in the leaves could be salvation or threat. As you travel deeper, memories of geography lessons float up—how rivers lead to villages, settlement logic, and safety fundamentals. Yet it’s strange to realize that in this time, you’re the technological dinosaur, the one stuck in obsolete patterns. Your reality crumbles slightly. You find a shelf of rock near an overhang—space enough for a temporary shelter against wind and rain. Instinct tells you to dig through your clothes for anything useful. In the breast pocket: nothing but a small torch (a relic from your time, out of juice but metal-edged). Tucked discreetly with careful hands: a lighter flint and firesteel. You don’t know how you got here—but the mission is the same, no matter the era: adapt, survive, return. You begin gathering stones and dead branches, remembering old lessons on combustion and airflow. Flick spark to tinder, coax it gently until small flames feed dry moss. Smoke curls upward like ancient warnings, yet it comforts you. As campfire crackles to life, night arrives wrapped in stars. Beside the glow of embers you sit, lulled by animal noises, and wonder: is this evolution reversed, a rebirth into simplicity, or just a glitch in cosmic code, projecting you into a lost world of moss and menace, wondering if there is any way home now that thunder rolls gently in the distance, weaving through the valley, echoing across the trees.
