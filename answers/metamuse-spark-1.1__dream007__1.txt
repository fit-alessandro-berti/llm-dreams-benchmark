You are in the middle of a heated argument with a close friend. The disagreement is about something important to both of you, and you decide to **stop talking.**

Just... stop. Mid-sentence.

Which is strange, because in the dream you can actually *see* your words. The last thing you shouted hangs in the air between you like hot, black smoke letters, slowly dissolving. Your friend's words are there too — sharp, red, jagged.

You're both breathing hard. You're in your childhood kitchen, but also in your current apartment. The fridge is humming too loud. The disagreement is about whether to stay or whether to leave — to sell the house you both built together in the dream, the one with the impossible garden in the back.

And then you decide to do something different.

You decide to walk to the sink.

Your friend is still yelling, but it sounds muffled now, like they're underwater. You turn on the faucet, and instead of water, cool, clear air pours out. You cup your hands and splash it on your face. It smells like rain and library books.

You turn around and look at your friend — really look at them. You notice for the first time in the dream that they're exhausted. Their shoulders are up around their ears. They aren't angry. They're scared. Same as you.

So you decide to sit down on the kitchen floor.

This is absurd dream logic, but your friend sits down too, right there across from you among the jagged red letters.

You reach out and pick up one of your black smoke words. It's not hot anymore. It's soft, like ash. You crush it in your palm and it turns into a small, white bird.

"I don't want to win the argument," you hear yourself say, and your voice is normal again. "I just don't want to lose you."

Your friend picks up one of their own sharp words. It softens in their hand too. "Me neither."

The house creaks. The impossible garden outside the window has grown wild while you were fighting — vines curled around the table legs, sunflowers pushing through the floorboards, filling the kitchen with impossible light.

You both just sit there for a while on the floor of the overgrown kitchen, not fixing anything, just breathing in the same air, watching the birds — all those unsaid things — fly out the open window.

And then you wake up, with your heart still beating fast, but with that quiet, green light still behind your eyes.
