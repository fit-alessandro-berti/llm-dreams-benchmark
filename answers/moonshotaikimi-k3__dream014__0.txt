...follow them through streets that seem to rearrange themselves around your little group. The strangers—there are five of them, or maybe seven, the number keeps shifting—each carry something that glows faintly: a paper lantern, a jar of fireflies, a small bell that rings without moving.

They don't ask where you're going. Somehow, they already know.

"You've been walking in circles," says a woman whose coat looks woven from starlight. "Everyone does, the first time."

The city tilts gently, like a ship finding its balance, and the buildings lean in closer—not threatening, but curious, the way animals sniff the air. The strangers lead you down an alley that wasn't there a moment ago, and the alley becomes a staircase, and the staircase becomes a river of light flowing upward.

"Where does this go?" you ask.

"To where you left your heart," says the smallest stranger, a child with eyes like held breath. "You dropped it when you started worrying about being lost."

You climb the river of light, and with each step you grow lighter, as if the loneliness is being rinsed off you like dust. At the top, there is a door with your name on it—but it's written the way your grandmother used to say it, soft and full of knowing.

You turn to thank the strangers, but they have already become the morning: ordinary golden light now, pouring through a window you somehow recognize.

And you understand, in the way you only understand things in dreams—you were never truly lost. You were just on your way.
