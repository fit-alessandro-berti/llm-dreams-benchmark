 The wind drops.
  For a heartbeat everything pauses. Then the leaves reverse direction, turning their pale undersides toward the sky as
  if they’ve suddenly remembered a secret.

  You glance upward. The sun vanishes behind a bruised wall of clouds, and the temperature falls so quickly your breath
  fogs in little puffs. The birds that had been singing vanish into silence. A low metallic hum rolls through the trunks
  around you—like distant machinery, though there’s no road, no wires, no reason for it.

  Rain begins not as drops, but as thin silver lines, slanting sideways through the dim light. Each one hits the ground
  and does not splash. It vanishes into the soil as though swallowed by thirst. The path behind you seems to dissolve.
  Ahead, you see a faint glow beneath the canopy: a clearing you don’t remember entering.

  You take a step forward. The trees bend inward, making a circle. In the center stands a stone with an old, unfamiliar
  symbol etched into it—wet, warm, and pulsing like a heartbeat.

  As the first true bolt of lightning forks above, it does not strike earth. It splits open the sky in a perfect, bright
  seam, revealing, for an instant, that the clouds are not clouds at all, but a vast, dark ocean overhead, churning—and
  you realize the storm didn’t arrive from the weather, but from beneath the world.
