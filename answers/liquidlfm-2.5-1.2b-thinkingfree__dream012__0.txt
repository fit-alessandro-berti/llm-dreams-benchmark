Somewhere, abandoned, the old log worried twice over losing its course. Your breath catches: *Not just a ship needing saving, but a reckoning.* 

A flicker of memory surges—a younger captain years past, vanishing into the same tempest, clenching a board and whispering, *“Balance isn’t about refusing the storm. It’s learning to dance with it.”* 

Decision time. Cut the engine’s roar to a hiss. Reverse course, not forward, into the twenty-kilometric distance where the northern lights blaze like a promise. The crew falls silent, breath held. You act. 

One crewman grumbles, “Too much delay,” but the engine still ticks faintly, a heartbeat beneath the roar. The compass spins again, yet you know—*trust this*—and press onward. Even if the world beyond the horizon unravels. 

The storm doesn’t wait. Your vessel can’t, either. But if the sea bends to your will, perhaps—for now—we’ll survive. Or maybe we’ll rest. Either way, it’s up to you. The decision settles. 

Thus, you mend the rigging, risk the gap, and hand the helm to cornered sailors, turning a lifeboat course into a dance with destiny. The crew cheers, sharper than before, not just for survival—but for the lesson that in storms, even the cruelest tempests render leaders just vessels, carrying hope backward. 

The clouds shudder. You lock eyes with the horizon, knowing the night will come, or is it now? For now, you steer the ship into silence, its sails tentative yet resolute, the dream demanding answers you refuse to flee. 

...And so it sails onward.
