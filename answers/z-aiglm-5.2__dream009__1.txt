You wake up in a completely different era, with no modern technology around. As you explore your new surroundings, you realize you need to find water, shelter, and food — and fast.

The sun hangs low over a landscape of rolling green hills dotted with stone cottages. Smoke curls from chimneys. The air smells of woodsmoke and livestock. You're wearing rough-spun clothes that itch against your skin, and your feet are bare against the cool earth.

You follow a worn path toward the village. A woman carrying a basket of bread eyes you suspiciously. You try to speak, but the words that come out aren't quite English — something older, guttural. Somehow, you understand her when she speaks back.

"Lost, are ye?"

You nod.

She tilts her head toward the well. "Drink. Then we'll see about the rest."

The water is cold and tastes like iron and earth. When you look down at your reflection, the face staring back isn't quite your own — similar, but subtly different, as if the era has reshaped you.

Days blur together. You learn to tend a fire, knead bread, barter in a market where no money changes hands — only goods and favors. You discover a strange comfort in the simplicity, the directness of survival.

Then one morning, you wake again — in your own bed, your phone buzzing on the nightstand. But your hands still smell faintly of woodsmoke, and there's mud on your bare feet that wasn't there when you went to sleep.
