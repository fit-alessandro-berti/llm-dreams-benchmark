The first group is standing in a circle, introducing themselves by the names of people they've lost. When my turn comes, I say the name of the person I'm looking for, and everyone nods like I've answered correctly.

A woman in a green dress tells me they're "in the room with the clocks," and I thank her, because in the dream this is useful information. But every door I open is the right door leading to the wrong room. In one, people are debating whether the ocean is closer than it used to be. My third-grade teacher is there, younger than me now, and she doesn't remember me, which seems fair.

I catch sight of them at the end of a hallway — or maybe it's their coat on a hook, or just the shape of the idea of them. I call out and my voice comes out as a dog barking, gently, apologetically. Someone hands me a plate with a small paper boat on it. "Eat it before it rains," they say, and I notice the ceiling has become sky, or always was.

A man at the piano explains that the party actually ended hours ago and we are all just the memory of the party. Everyone nods politely and keeps drinking.

Then someone tells me the person I'm looking for left. Someone else insists they're in the kitchen. The kitchen is the kitchen from the house I grew up in, and my mother is there, and she says, "They waited for you for a long time," and I feel the grief that only exists in dreams — enormous and weightless at once.

That's when I realize I can't remember who I was looking for. Their face has gone soft, like a photograph left in the sun. All that remains is the searching.

Everyone turns to look at me again, kindly this time, as if to say: that's all any of us are doing here.

So I sit down. Someone pours me a glass of something dark. Across the room, the woman in green raises her glass to me, and I understand, suddenly, that I knew everyone here all along — I just hadn't found them yet.
