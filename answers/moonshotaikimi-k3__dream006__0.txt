...speak, but the voice that comes out isn't quite yours—it's older, familiar in a way you can't place, and the audience leans in as if they understand it better than you do.

You glance down at your notes and they've become a grocery list in your childhood handwriting: *milk, eggs, call your brother back, don't forget the thing you promised.* You can't remember the thing you promised, but it feels urgent, and somehow also fine.

The podium has grown taller, or you've grown shorter—it's hard to say, and no one seems to mind. The microphone bends toward you like a curious flower. You tell it the thing you've always been afraid to say out loud, though you can't recall now what it was, only that saying it felt like setting down something heavy.

You look up and realize the front row is your entire third-grade class, wearing formal attire. Behind them sits everyone you've ever loved. Behind *them*, the crowd stretches back into a soft fog where faces keep arriving, patient and expectant.

The speech is going wonderfully. You haven't said anything in several minutes, but the applause comes in waves that sound like rain on a roof you once slept under. Someone hands you a glass of water. When you drink it, you suddenly understand what the speech was always about—but the thought is already dissolving, the way thoughts do—

—and now the stage is the kitchen of a house you used to live in, and the audience is seated around the table, waiting kindly. Your mother says, "Go on. Finish."

You open your mouth—

—and the alarm clock is ringing, or the applause is, and you wake with the strange certainty that you almost said it, whatever it was, and that they would have understood.
