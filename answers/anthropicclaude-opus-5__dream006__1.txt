...you begin to speak, and the words come out fine — better than fine, actually, they arrive already polished, as though someone had written them for you years ago and stored them under your tongue. The audience leans in. Somebody in the third row is nodding with such conviction that you want to thank them personally, later, after.

Then you glance down at your notes and find the page is a photograph of a kitchen you have never been in. Morning light on a countertop. A glass of water half-drunk. You turn the page: the same kitchen from a slightly different angle. You keep talking. The mouth continues without you, which is a relief, and also the exact thing you were afraid of.

The room has gotten larger. Not gradually — it simply is larger now, the way rooms in dreams are renovated without permits. The back rows recede into a kind of weather. You realize the audience has been standing this whole time, though you'd have sworn there were seats.

Someone raises a hand. You call on them. They ask, very politely, "But what about the part you skipped?"

And you know instantly which part. You have known the whole time. It sits in your chest like a swallowed stone, and the terrible thing is that you can't remember what it *is*, only its weight and its shape, only that it is the reason you were invited, the reason for all of this, the actual speech, and the thing you have been elaborately talking around for what may have been forty minutes or eleven years.

You open your mouth to begin again, properly this time.

The microphone becomes a sparrow and leaves.

The audience waits, kindly, patient as furniture, and you understand they will wait as long as necessary — that they have nowhere else to be, that they were assembled precisely for this waiting — and you stand in the enormous light with the photograph of the kitchen in your hand, and you think: *I have been there. That's my glass. I set it down that morning and meant to come back for it.*

And then the applause starts, warm and enormous, before you've said a word, and you can't tell whether you've been forgiven or simply excused, and you bow, because that's what the body knows how to do, and the bowing goes on much longer than it should, all the way down, past the floor, into the cool dark under the stage where the water is still waiting, half-drunk, exactly where you left it.
