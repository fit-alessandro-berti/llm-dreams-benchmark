I glance around the exam hall. The other students are writing furiously, their pens moving in confident arcs. But when I look closer, I notice they're all writing with the same hand — their left, even the ones I'm certain are right-handed. Their faces are familiar, but wrong. My old roommate's hair on a stranger's jaw. A professor's reading glasses on the barista from the coffee shop downstairs.

The clock on the wall has no numbers, just twelve hands pointing in different directions, none of them moving, yet I feel time pressing against my ribs like a physical weight.

I look down at my exam again. The symbols have resolved into a question I understand:

*What is the thing you keep forgetting to remember?*

My pen hovers. The answer is right there — I can almost feel it, like a word on the tip of my tongue that tastes like rain and childhood hallways. I start to write. The ink flows blue, then turns to water, soaking into the paper and leaving behind a blank page that slowly fills with a new question:

*Why are you afraid of the door you've already opened?*

The room goes quiet. Every head turns toward me. Their eyes are kind, not threatening — expectant, like an audience waiting for the one performer who hasn't realized the show started twenty minutes ago.

I stand up, knocking my chair backward. It makes no sound when it hits the floor. I walk toward the door at the far end of the hall, and as my hand touches the handle, I notice it's warm. Not metal-warm from sunlight, but skin-warm. Alive.

I open it.

Behind the door is this exact moment — me, telling you about the dream, the words already half-formed before I even knew what I was going to say.

And somewhere, an alarm is beginning to ring.
