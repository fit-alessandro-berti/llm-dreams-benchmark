…return a stolen shadow to the Weaver before the second bell tolls. 

I look down at my hands. I am holding a small, heavy glass jar. Inside, a dark, shifting silhouette taps against the glass like a trapped moth. It’s my shadow, I realize with a sudden, unquestioning dream-logic certainty. I had traded it yesterday—or was it a lifetime ago?—for a cup of water that tasted like starlight. Now, without it, my feet feel dangerously light, as if a strong gust of wind could blow me into the sky.

The air around me smells of woodsmoke, damp earth, and crushed thyme. I am standing in a winding alleyway paved with uneven cobblestones. The buildings tower impossibly high, made of pale, porous stone that seems to expand and contract as if breathing. People brush past me in heavy wool cloaks and leather boots, their faces obscured by wide-brimmed hats and plague-doctor masks, though no one seems afraid. They are just going about their day.

I need to find the Weaver. 

I start to walk, but the geography of the city refuses to stay still. Every time I blink, the street stretches or folds. I turn a corner expecting a market square, but instead, I step directly into a vast, sunlit library where the books are bound in bark and the pages are made of pressed leaves. 

"You're walking too loudly," a voice whispers. 

I turn. Sitting on a stack of giant, leather-bound ledgers is a fox with feathers instead of fur. It tilts its head. "The Weaver is in the space between the ticking of the clock. You have to walk to the rhythm of your own heartbeat, not your feet."

I close my eyes and focus on the thudding in my chest. *Thump-thump. Thump-thump.* I begin to step in time with it. When I open my eyes, the library is gone. 

I am standing in a circular courtyard beneath a sky the color of bruised plums. In the center sits the Weaver. She is not entirely human; her lower half is merged with a massive, ancient loom made of polished bone and dark mahogany. Her face is a smooth, blank mirror. 

She doesn't speak, but I hear her voice vibrating in my teeth. *"You are late. The first bell has already rung."*

A deep, resonant *GONG* echoes through the courtyard, shaking the dust from the pale stones. Panic flares in my chest. I fumble with the brass latch of the glass jar. My fingers feel thick and clumsy. 

*GONG.* The second bell begins to toll. 

I pop the lid. The shadow doesn't just fly out; it pours from the jar like black ink, flowing across the cobblestones and leaping onto the loom. The Weaver’s hands become a blur as she weaves my shadow back into the grand tapestry she is building. 

As the last thread is tied off, I feel a sudden, heavy warmth snap back onto my heels. I am grounded again. I look at the tapestry to see what she has woven. 

It’s a perfect, moving image of my modern bedroom. The digital clock on my nightstand is glowing red. The numbers shift from 6:59 to 7:00. 

The loom begins to spin faster. The rhythmic *clack-clack-clack* of the wooden shuttle accelerates, morphing seamlessly into the harsh, electronic *beep-beep-beep* of my real-world alarm. The bruised-plum sky shatters into fragments of white plaster. The smell of thyme is replaced by the sterile, cool breeze of my air conditioner.

I reach out to grab the edge of the loom, but my hand slaps against my wooden nightstand. 

I open my eyes, gasping. Sunlight is streaming through my blinds. The alarm is blaring. I reach over and silence it, my heart hammering against my ribs. I sit up and rub my face, letting out a long breath, telling myself it was just a dream. 

But as I pull the blankets back to get out of bed, I freeze. 

There, resting on my pillow, is a small, heavy glass jar. And inside, a dark silhouette is tapping against the glass.
