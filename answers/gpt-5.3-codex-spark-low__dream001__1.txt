• You find yourself in a vast, unfamiliar city, and you realize you have an important meeting in an hour but no idea
  where it is. You start to run, but every clock you pass glows backward, the minute hands spinning the wrong way.
  Street signs flicker between alphabets you almost recognize, then blur into each other like wet ink. People move
  quickly past you, all of them speaking in confident voices, all of them knowing where they’re going—except you.

  A woman in a cobalt coat steps out from the throat of an alley and hands you a folded map made of ticket stubs.
  “Second bell,” she says, as if announcing weather. “Take the one with no exits.”

  You look up at the map: a single line of roads that fold in on themselves like a ribbon dropped in water. The city
  hums beneath you, metallic and warm, and the sky above the rooftops pulses with a low amber light like a sun still
  deciding whether to rise.

  You follow the line. Every block you turn into opens into the same square, but each time the skyline changes—sometimes
  old, sometimes new, sometimes impossible. Cafés appear and vanish as you cross thresholds. A man selling newspapers
  whispers your name before you reach him. You don’t remember having one.

  At the edge of the square, a bronze clock tower reads 14:58.

  You panic, because the meeting is in an hour, and this city is draining minutes without spending them. You glance at
  the map. The paper is warm. Your own reflection slides across the surface where you’re not standing.

  The line now leads under a bridge. Beneath it, the river is frozen, not with ice but with pages of your old calendars.
  Every date you’ve forgotten. Birthdays. Appointments. Promises. One page shows this evening, underlined in red.

  On the far side, the crowds split into two processions: one rushing forward with briefcases, one standing still in
  place as if waiting for permission. The moving crowd gives you room without seeing you. The still crowd looks through
  you. You choose the still one and follow its lead toward a narrow glass building whose doors open inward and never
  close.

  Inside, a hall of clocks hangs from the ceiling, all ticking in impossible harmonies. At the back, your nameplate
  gleams on a polished table:

  Conference Room 47-B
  13:00 / 14:00 / 15:00

  You step forward.

  At the long table, someone else is already there, waiting calmly with a cup of steaming tea. It is you—older, younger,
  and somehow less frightened. He—she—you nods and slides a final paper toward you.

  “Late,” you say.

  “Not by the rules of this city,” they reply. “By the rules that brought you here, yes.”

  The room door clicks shut. Through a narrow window you can see the clock tower again, now reading 15:00. The tea steam
  forms the shape of a path, winding up into the ceiling and disappearing. A voice speaks from somewhere above the
  table, gentle and precise:

  “Your meeting is with your next decision. Begin.”

  You answer before you can think: “I came as fast as I could.”

  The older-you smiles, and points to the paper, where the only readable line reads:

  Tell me where you were going.
