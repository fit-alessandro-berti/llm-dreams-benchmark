
The wind rises first—a low, whispering rush through the canopy that turns the leaves silver-side up. The air grows thick and electric, tasting of damp earth and ozone. Above, the once-clear sky bruises purple and green, swirling like ink in water.

Then the rain comes, not in drops but in a sudden, roaring curtain that hammers the forest floor. It drums on your hood, runs in cold rivulets down your neck, and turns the path to slick, dark mud. Lightning fractures the sky—not just white, but violet and sickly yellow—and the thunder doesn’t just sound, it *shakes*, a physical force that vibrates in your chest.

You duck under the broad, gnarled roots of an ancient oak, its bark rough against your back. In the strobe-like flashes, you see the forest transformed: familiar trees now twist into strange, looming shapes, their branches clawing at the tempest. The world is reduced to the roar of the storm, the drumming rain, and the electric tang in the air.

And then, in a blinding instant of lightning, you see it—a figure standing perfectly still just beyond the trees, unaffected by the maelstrom. It’s too far to make out details, but it’s facing you. Waiting.

The thunder that follows doesn’t fade; it seems to gather, rolling closer, as if the storm itself is breathing. The figure doesn’t move. The rain stings your eyes. You realize, with a dream-like certainty, that this storm isn’t just weather. It’s a threshold. And the figure in the downpour is what waits on the other side.
