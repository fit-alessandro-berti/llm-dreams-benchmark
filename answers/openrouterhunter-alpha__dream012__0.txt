You are the captain of a ship in the middle of a turbulent sea. Your crew looks to you for guidance as the storm worsens, and you —

Grip the wheel until your knuckles split white against the wood. The rain isn't falling — it's *attacking*, horizontal and furious, stinging your face like handfuls of thrown gravel. You can taste the salt before it even reaches your lips.

"Port side! Haul in the foresail before it takes the mast with it!"

Your voice doesn't sound like your own. It sounds older, rougher — carved by years of commands given in weather exactly like this. The crew moves. They trust you. You don't know why, but in the logic of dreams, it simply *is*.

Lightning splits the sky, and for one frozen moment you see everything: the black water heaving in great muscular swells, the tattered canvas above you snapping like the wings of something enormous trying to escape, the faces of your crew — young, terrified, but *here*, staying, choosing not to go below deck because you haven't gone below deck.

And then the lightning dies, and the darkness presses back in even heavier than before.

But something has changed. Out on the horizon — impossibly far, impossibly bright — there is a thin line where the black sea meets something *else*. Not land. Not dawn. Something luminous and low, like a city made of light resting on the surface of the water itself.

You don't point at it. You don't announce it. You just feel your grip on the wheel loosen, just slightly, because the storm hasn't won — it was never the enemy. It was the door.

You turn the wheel toward the light, and the ship — against all wind, against all reason — begins to move.
