Despite the hardships, you decide to **plant a garden in the ruins of an old library.**

The air is thick with ash and the scent of decay, but as you push through the shattered glass doors, you find a pocket of stillness. Sunlight filters through the collapsed ceiling, illuminating rows of dust-covered shelves that once held knowledge, now reduced to kindling for desperate fires. But in the center of the main hall, where the roof has caved in just enough to let rain pool, you see it: a single, stubborn green shoot pushing through the cracked marble floor.

It’s a tomato plant, wild and untamed, growing from soil that shouldn’t exist here.

You kneel, your knees cracking against the cold stone, and gently brush away the debris around its base. Your hands are calloused and stained with grime, but they move with a tenderness you haven’t felt in years. You remember your grandmother’s voice, faint like a radio signal from another time: *"Life finds a way, even when we forget how to look."*

Over the next few weeks, you return every day. You scavenge for water in rusted barrels, filtering it through cloth and sand. You protect the sapling from scavengers who mistake your quiet dedication for weakness. They don’t understand why you’d waste precious calories on something that won’t feed you today. But you know what they don’t: hope isn’t measured in calories. It’s measured in roots.

One evening, as the sky bleeds orange with the setting sun, you notice tiny yellow flowers blooming along the vine. And there, nestled among the leaves, is the first fruit—small, red, and impossibly vibrant against the gray world.

You pick it carefully. It’s warm from the sun. As you take a bite, the burst of sweetness floods your mouth, tasting not just of fruit, but of memory, of loss, and of something new beginning. For the first time in years, you don’t feel like a survivor. You feel like a gardener.

And in this broken world, that feels like enough.
