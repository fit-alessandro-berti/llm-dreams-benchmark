...decide to plant a garden.

Not just any garden—something impossible, defiant. In the cracked concrete plaza where the old bookstore once stood, you kneel in the gray ash, your hands raw from digging through the ruins. You’ve salvaged seeds: a few wrinkled beans from a forgotten tin, a handful of wildflower seeds tucked inside a waterlogged book about bees. You’ve repurposed a rusted sink as a planter, lined it with broken ceramic tiles from a collapsed wall, and carefully layered it with sifted earth and compost made from crushed plastic bottles and the ashes of a burned-out library.

The air hums with low danger—the distant creak of collapsing steel, the skitter of mutated rats in the subway grate below. But as your fingers press the tiny seeds into the soil, a strange calm settles over you. You whisper to them, not knowing if they’ll hear: *"Grow. Please."*

Then, slowly, a miracle. A single green shoot pushes through the ashen crust. Not just any green—vibrant, undeniable. It trembles in the wind, reaching for a sky still choked with dust, yet somehow... *alive*.

You realize you're not just growing food. You're growing hope. And as you water it with collected rainwater from a dented tin can, you know this tiny patch of green isn't just a garden—it's a promise. A quiet, stubborn rebellion against the end. One seed. One sprout. One life. And then, from the corner of your eye, you see it: a child, peering from behind a shattered window, her eyes wide not with fear, but wonder. She’s holding a cracked doll. She takes a step forward. The garden has begun.
