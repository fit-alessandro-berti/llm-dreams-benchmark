The wooden stage feels solid yet strangely soft under your feet, like walking on moss. The spotlight is warm on your face, and you can see the first few rows—a sea of faces blurring into a soft-focus pattern of eyes and half-smiles. You open your mouth, the first words of your perfectly rehearsed speech on your tongue—

—but what comes out is not your speech. It's a melody. A soft, humming tune you’ve never heard before, yet your voice knows it by heart. The audience doesn’t gasp or leave. Instead, they lean forward, their expectant looks melting into serene curiosity. 

You feel the papers in your hand, but they’re no longer paper. They’re fluttering, luminous notes, like fireflies, rising from your palms and drifting toward the ceiling. You aren’t reciting; you’re conducting. Your hands move, and the melody swells, weaving through the hall. The crowd begins to glow, not with light, but with shared understanding. No one needs to hear the old words. The new song says everything.

You wake up with the phantom sensation of a final, resonant chord hanging in the air, and a smile you can’t quite explain.
