...you decide to stop talking mid-sentence.

Not because you've given up, or because they've won — but because in the way dreams work, you suddenly notice how loud your own voice sounds.

You're both standing in your childhood kitchen, except it's also your current apartment. The argument is about something important — about whether to leave. To stay and keep the little life you've built together here, or to take that opportunity on the other coast. It matters. You've both been holding it for weeks.

Your friend's face is flushed. Yours is too. The light above the sink is buzzing and flickering with every word.

And then you decide to whisper.

So you do. You drop your voice to almost nothing.

"What are you actually afraid of?" you whisper.

The dream stutters. Because that's not the script. In the dream-logic, your friend was supposed to yell back. But now they have to lean in to hear you.

The kitchen goes quiet. The buzzing fridge stops buzzing. Outside the window, which a second ago was looking out at a brick wall, now there's suddenly a huge, calm ocean.

Your friend opens their mouth, and for a second their voice comes out as two voices — their younger self and their current self at the same time.

"That if you go, we won't know how to find each other anymore."

And as soon as they say it, the argument isn't an argument. The kitchen floor is suddenly made of sand. The table between you is a tide pool. You both crouch down without thinking about it.

You decide to put your hand in the water.

It's warm. When you look up, your friend is doing the same, on the other side of the little pool. Your reflections are in it, but they're warped, rippling.

"We'll leave a light on," you say. Or maybe they say it. In the dream you can't tell who is speaking for who anymore.

Then, like dreams do — the feeling stays, but the details start to wash away. The kitchen, the ocean, the tide pool all dissolve into the soft, dark blue behind your eyelids.

You wake up with your heart still beating a little fast, but not from anger.
