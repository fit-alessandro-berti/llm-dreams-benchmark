You decide to stop mid-sentence, your throat raw from yelling, and not storm away like you almost did three times already. The shared community garden we’d nurtured for five years—our first big project after college, where we’d buried our first failed startup’s business cards under the first apple sapling, where I’d had my breakup breakdown and she’d held me while we weeded dandelions—swirls around us, and in the dream’s hazy, unmoored logic, I can smell the rain we’d waited three weeks for, even though the sky above us is streaked neon pink like a faded highlighter.

Instead of repeating that I thought she was selfish for even considering selling the plot to the urgent care clinic that had made an offer, or firing back that she was right I was scared of losing the only thing that had ever felt fully ours, I reach across the splintered picnic bench where we’d eaten hundreds of takeout pizzas, and lace my fingers through hers. That’s when the dream’s quiet magic kicks in: the cherry tomato vines tangled along the fence start to glow gold, and they unfurl fast, stretching over the overgrown, abandoned lot next to our garden—we’d complained for years that the city would never clean it up—sprouting full-grown sunflowers, raised beds, even a small covered patio in ten seconds flat.

We both freeze, and in the glow, I can see she’s crying like I am, because neither of us had said what we were really scared of: that choosing one “good” thing over the other would mean we lost each other. The argument melts, and we run through the new vines, laughing so hard we stumble, and a group of the neurodivergent neighborhood kids I’d wanted to build the safe outdoor space for are already chasing each other through the sunflowers, while a group of nurses from the clinic set up a free blood pressure table on the new patio.

I’m just about to ask her how that even happened, how we got both things we wanted so bad it made us stupid, when my phone alarm blurs the glowing vines into my bedroom’s bedroom walls, and I wake up reaching for my own phone to text her, even though it’s 3 a.m.—the argument wasn’t a dream, we’d had it the day before, and I’d stormed off before I could say any of the soft things the dream let me live.
