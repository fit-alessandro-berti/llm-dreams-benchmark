You’re the captain of a ship in the middle of a turbulent sea. Your crew looks to you for guidance as the storm worsens, and you…  

feel the deck pitch under your boots like a bucking animal. Rain lashes sideways, salt stings your eyes, and the rigging screams in the wind.  

You take one breath. The crew’s faces are lit by flashes of lightning — young, old, scared, but waiting.  

You don’t shout. You set your feet wide and point to the helm.  

“Strike the topsails!” your voice cuts through the gale. “Ramos, take the wheel. Keep her bow to the waves. We ride *with* them, not against.”  

The first mate hesitates. “Captain, if we turn into it—”  
“We live,” you say. “Tackling it broadside kills us. Trust the hull. Trust me.”  

You grab the shoulder of the deckhand fumbling with a frozen line. Your hands guide his. “Like this. Again. Good.” One by one, they move. Fear doesn’t vanish, but it has a job now.  

A wave like a mountain rears up. For three heartbeats the world is only water and noise. The ship climbs, groans, hangs at the crest—  
—and crests. She slams down, shuddering, but she holds.  

You wipe your face and grin, wild and real. “Bail detail, starboard! Lanterns out! We don’t fight the storm. We *dance* with it until it tires.”  

Hours blur. You lose track of how many times you call orders, haul rope, brace shoulders with the cook and the navigator alike. But the wind shifts. The waves stop breaking *over* the deck.  

Dawn comes dirty and grey, but it comes. The sea is still ugly, but it’s no longer trying to swallow you whole.  

Your crew is soaked, exhausted, alive. Ramos hands you the wheel, eyes steady now.  

“What’s the heading, Captain?”  

You look at the bruised horizon where the clouds are finally tearing open.  

“Home,” you say. “We held together. Now we go home.”  

The dream lingers for a second — the smell of salt, the ache in your arms, the quiet pride of not letting go — and then it softens at the edges, like all dreams do.
