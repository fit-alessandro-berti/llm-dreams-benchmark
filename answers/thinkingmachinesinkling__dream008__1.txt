…and you open the folder, but the pages are made of liquid glass that runs through your fingers and pools around your ankles until the carpet becomes a slow-moving river. Your boss isn’t waiting by your desk anymore—he’s perched on the ceiling, folded like a paper airplane made of old memos, his voice echoing from inside the fluorescent lights.

The deadline isn’t written on paper; it’s a physical object, a heavy brass key that suddenly hangs around your neck, ticking louder with every heartbeat. You try to begin the project, but your keyboard sprouts roots and digs into the floor, growing into a tree that bears fruit shaped like clock faces. When you bite into one to understand the task, the hands spin backward, and the office walls dissolve into the sky of your childhood bedroom—yet your boss is still there, floating, smiling with teeth made of silver staples.

You realize you aren’t actually working. You are weaving the project out of starlight, forgotten passwords, and the sound of rain on a roof that doesn’t exist. The river carries your desk away toward a horizon that looks exactly like the ceiling you used to stare at in meetings. The impossible deadline isn’t approaching; it has already happened, is happening, and will happen again, and you keep building, because in this dream, finishing was never the point—only the endless, floating act of beginning.
