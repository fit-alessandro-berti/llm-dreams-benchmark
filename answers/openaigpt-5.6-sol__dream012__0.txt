You are the captain of a ship in the middle of a turbulent sea. Your crew looks to you for guidance as the storm worsens, and you realize with a strange certainty that the storm is calling your name.

Not shouting it—singing it.

Each wave rises like a dark cathedral, its foaming crest filled with whispering faces. Lightning stitches the sky together in brief, silver seams. The compass spins beneath your hand, faster and faster, until its needle breaks free and points not north, but down.

“Captain?” your first mate asks.

You order the sails lowered, though the wind has already torn them into streaming ribbons. Then you steer directly toward the tallest wave.

The crew cries out, but they obey.

At the last moment, the wave opens like a door.

The ship plunges through.

On the other side, the sea is perfectly still. Stars float beneath the hull, and far below them lies a city of golden towers, upside down in the depths. Bells ring from its streets. You recognize the sound: it is the same melody your mother used to hum when you were a child.

A small boat waits ahead. In it sits an older version of you, dressed in a captain’s coat faded almost white.

“You’re late,” they say.

“For what?”

“To wake up.”

Behind you, the storm closes. Your crew has vanished. Your ship has become a wooden toy beneath your feet, small enough to hold in one hand.

The older captain reaches out.

You take their hand—and wake with salt on your lips, a compass clenched in your fist, its needle pointing toward the bedroom floor.
