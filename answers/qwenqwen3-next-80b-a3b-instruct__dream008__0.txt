You are at your workplace, and suddenly, you are given a project with an impossible deadline. Your boss is waiting for you to start, and you realize — you’re not in your office anymore.

The fluorescent lights flicker, then dissolve into golden candlelight. The cubicles melt into towering bookshelves that stretch into a sky threaded with constellations of floating keyboards. Your boss? He’s no longer wearing a suit. He’s a owl in a velvet coat, perched atop a stack of blinking clocks, tapping his beak impatiently.

“Midnight,” he hoots. “Or the library burns.”

You look down. Your hands are made of parchment. You try to speak, but words float out like origami cranes, each one a half-written email, a forgotten to-do, a dream you never finished.

A whisper echoes: *You’ve been here before.*

And then you remember.

This isn’t your workplace.

This is your subconscious — the place where all your unfinished projects live.

The “deadline” isn’t real. The “boss” is your fear. The project? It’s not a report. It’s your life.

The room shudders. The bookshelves whisper names: *The novel you started in college. The painting you abandoned. The apology you never sent. The trip you postponed “until next year.”*

You reach out — not to type, not to click — but to touch the spine of the oldest book.

It reads: *You.*

Your fingers tremble.

You open it.

Inside, blank pages.

But you are holding a pen.

And for the first time in years… it doesn’t feel like a burden.

It feels like a gift.

The owl watches.

The clocks slow.

And you begin to write.

Not for your boss.

Not for a deadline.

But for you.

The dream doesn’t end.

It rewinds.

And you wake up — not to your alarm.

But to sunlight.

And your notebook.

Open.

On the first page, in your own hand:

> “Today, I begin.”
