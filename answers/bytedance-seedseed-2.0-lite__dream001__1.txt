The street signs above twist and flip like windblown book pages: one second they read “Maple Avenue,” the next they’re scrawled with half-remembered names of elementary school teachers, then they blur into nothing at all. A woman pushing a stroller passes me, but instead of a baby, her stroller stacks high with unopened birthday cards I never sent, and she tsks under her breath like I’m late to a party I forgot I RSVP’d to.

I skid to a stop in front of a subway entrance, where the tile mosaic on the wall forms a face that blinks at me, its mouth shaping the address I’d been scrambling to recall before I even knew I knew it. I fumble for my wallet to buy a ticket, but all that falls out is a crumpled meeting agenda, the only legible line at the top reading: *You. 3PM.* The turnstile clicks open without me touching it, and I race down the stairs, the subway’s rumble shaking the fillings in my teeth.

When the train pulls up, the doors slide open to reveal not a crowded car, but a sunlit forest clearing. A little girl sits on a log holding out a glowing honey-colored lollipop. “You’re actually early,” she says, popping a bubblegum bubble that bursts into a flock of tiny sparrow-shaped confetti. “You thought this meeting was with your boss, or your landlord, right? It’s just with the part of you that’s been hiding from the thing you actually want to do. You were running in circles chasing someone else’s deadline.”

I step onto the train, and the doors close behind me, the clearing melting into a cozy room with one table and two chairs. Across from me sits a version of myself grinning, holding two mugs of steamy chamomile tea. The clock on the wall ticks, and it’s only one minute past the hour I’d panicked over. I wasn’t late at all. I’d just been running the wrong way, chasing someone else’s idea of where I needed to be.
