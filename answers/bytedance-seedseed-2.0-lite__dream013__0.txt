The brass combination locks and logic grids etched into stone doorways blur, their numbers twisting into crayon wax and smudged notebook ink, until the next barrier blocking your path is plastered with every half-finished sketch, failed test, and awkward love note I ever threw away in middle school—things I’d long forgotten I’d even lost. The clink of tumblers vanishes, replaced by a soft instruction breathed through the crack under the wood: Put them in the order you grieved them. That’s the puzzle now, no logical key, only the messy, quiet work of remembering how I first mourned the dragon drawing I’d crumpled because I thought its wings were lopsided, then the algebra test I’d studied for for weeks, then the note I never worked up the courage to pass my first best friend. When I sort them right, the door swings open without a click.

The maze walls shift too, no longer rough stone but stacks of unread work emails, unsent texts to my sister who lives across the country, half-planned road trips I told myself I’d take only when I was “successful enough” to deserve time off. A tabby cat that’s been padding after me this whole time—its fur exactly matching Mochi, my childhood cat who died when I was 16—meows and sits at the end of the corridor, staring past the final “puzzle”: a wall of mirrors that flash every mistake I’ve ever made, every time I let someone down, every flaw I’ve picked apart in myself. I step toward it, ready to parse what I need to fix to pass, but Mochi rubs against my ankle and jumps straight through the mirror like it’s smoke.

I freeze, then follow, and on the other side isn’t another stretch of maze. It’s the fire escape of my current apartment, the sun bleaching pink over the city skyline, a mug of still-warm coffee on the rail. The maze wasn’t a trap to escape, I realize. It was every unnecessary puzzle I’d built for myself, every thing I’d convinced myself I had to solve perfectly before I could let myself be okay. I pick up the coffee, Mochi curls around my feet, and for the first time all night, I don’t fumble for a missing key. I’m already where I needed to be.
