You glance down at your notes, but the pages have turned into water, rippling off the podium and pooling at your feet. The microphone begins to sprout leaves. You keep talking.

A woman in the front row raises her hand and asks a question, but she's speaking in a language you don't recognize, yet somehow you understand completely. You answer her, and your voice echoes as if the auditorium has become a canyon.

The lights flicker, and for a moment everyone in the audience has the face of someone you knew in childhood. Then the lights steady, and they're strangers again.

You realize you're no longer wearing shoes. The floor beneath you is soft — grass, somehow, growing through the stage. The podium is a tree now. You lean against it and keep speaking, though you've lost track of what you're saying. It feels important. It feels like it always has been.

Then you notice — the back row is standing up, one by one, and walking out through a door that wasn't there before. Behind the door is a beach. You can smell salt air.

You want to follow them.

You wake up before you decide.
