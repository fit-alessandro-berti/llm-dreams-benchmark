...written in a language that hasn’t been invented yet, and the ink keeps rearranging itself into questions about subjects you are certain you never studied—like the calculus of forgotten smells, the history of shadows, or the precise weight of a decision you haven’t made.

The paper breathes beneath your hands. The clock on the wall ticks backward, and every answer you try to write dissolves into a small bird and flies out the window, leaving you holding a pen that writes only in more questions. The other students are already finished, but when you look closer, they have no faces—only mirrors reflecting versions of you that made different choices. The proctor whispers that your career depends on solving the final question, which is blank except for the echo of your own heartbeat, growing louder until it becomes the only sound in the room, and you realize the exam was never about what you know, but about whether you can remember how to wake up.
