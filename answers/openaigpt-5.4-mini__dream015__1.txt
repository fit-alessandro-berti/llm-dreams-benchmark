…build a little garden on the roof of a half-collapsed library.

At first it’s only a few salvaged tins, a cracked rain barrel, and seeds traded for batteries and bandages. You spend your mornings clearing rubble from the ledges and your evenings coaxing soil into anything that can hold it. The world below stays broken, loud with distant alarms and the occasional shout of strangers learning whether they are friend or threat.

But up there, above the dust and rust, green begins to return.

A bean vine climbs a bent antenna. Tomatoes swell in jars cut open and wired to the railing. Herbs take root in old book boxes lined with cloth from a torn curtain. People start coming by—not just for food, but to rest, to remember, to talk. One by one, they bring their own small offerings: a spoon, a blanket, a packet of seeds, a story.

And in that fragile patch of life, you realize survival was never only about enduring the end.

It was about making room for a beginning.
