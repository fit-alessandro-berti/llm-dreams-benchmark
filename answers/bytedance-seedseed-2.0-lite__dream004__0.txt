…it’s not propped on an easel or nailed to a wall, but floating chest-high, its edges fraying into wispy cloud that bleeds into the shifting magenta and indigo fog roiling around the surreal landscape. The blankness of it hums, like it’s been waiting for me, even as the spiky glass spires behind it melt into dandelion fluff and re-form into swimming, iridescent fish mid-air. When I lift a hand to touch it, my fingertip comes away warm, streaked with a gold that doesn’t smudge until I press it to the canvas’s surface—where that tiny mark blooms into a lopsided sun, bleeding tangerine light that seeps off the canvas and softens all the jagged, shifting shapes in the landscape into clover and wild strawberries.

My hands move on their own after that, no plan, just the dream’s loose, unthinking logic. I drag a finger down the middle of the canvas and the crack I leave doesn’t split the fabric, but spills starlight that pools at my sneakers, and from that glow steps a figure made of the canvas’s unbleached weave: my grandma, who taught me to paint watercolors in her backyard before she died, her apron smudged with the same gold and lavender I’d been daubing on the canvas. She doesn’t say anything at first, just hands me a brush carved from a peacock feather, and whispers that this blank canvas was the part of me I’d tucked away while I was busy rushing through real life, the empty space I was scared to fill with anything that mattered. The shifting, chaotic world outside the canvas fades as I keep painting—first a fox with paws that leave dandelion puffs in its wake, then a porch swing strung from an apple tree, then a mug of steaming chamomile tea that floats off the canvas and into my hand, warm through the ceramic. For the first time since I started dreaming, nothing shifts, nothing melts or re-forms. Everything stays: the sun glowing soft over the apple tree, grandma settling onto the swing next to me, the fox curling up at our feet. The canvas isn’t blank anymore. It’s home.
