You start to walk faster, your shoes clicking against the uneven pavement that feels both ancient and brand-new. The city stretches endlessly in every direction—towering spires of glass and steel melt into crumbling stone arches, neon kanji flicker beside faded Art Deco murals, and the sky above is a bruised twilight that never quite decides if it’s dawn or dusk. Your phone is dead. Your watch has stopped at 11:47, the second hand twitching like a dying insect.

You stop a woman in a crimson coat who looks vaguely like someone you once knew. “Excuse me, I have a meeting—do you know where—”

She tilts her head, eyes reflecting the streetlights like mirrors. “Everyone has a meeting,” she says softly. “But the address changes when you’re not looking.” She presses a small brass key into your palm and walks away, her coat dissolving into the crowd like smoke.

The key is warm. It hums.

You start to run.

Alleys twist like living things. A busker on the corner plays a violin that sounds exactly like your childhood piano lessons. A street vendor sells steaming cups of tea that smell like your grandmother’s kitchen, but when you sip, it tastes like starlight and regret. Every corner you turn reveals the same distant clock tower, its hands spinning backward.

Forty minutes left. Then thirty. The air grows heavier, thick with the scent of ozone and old books. You press the key into every lock you pass—doors that lead to broom closets, to roaring waterfalls, to empty rooms filled with floating lanterns. None fit.

In a narrow courtyard overgrown with luminous vines, you finally see it: a plain wooden door set into the base of a skyscraper that wasn’t there a moment ago. The key slides in perfectly. The lock clicks like a sigh.

Inside is a vast circular chamber lined with mirrors. Each reflection shows a different version of you—younger, older, laughing, crying, furious, at peace. In the center floats a single chair and a small table holding a single envelope addressed simply: *To the one who almost didn’t make it.*

You sit. The envelope opens itself.

Inside is a single sentence written in your own handwriting:

“You were never late. You were exactly on time for the meeting you needed to have—with yourself.”

The mirrors ripple. The city outside begins to fade, its lights winking out one by one like stars at sunrise. You feel the pull of waking, the familiar tug behind your ribs.

But before the dream dissolves completely, one of the mirrored versions of you—the one with tired eyes and a gentle smile—reaches out and places a hand on your shoulder.

“Next time,” it says, voice echoing from everywhere and nowhere, “just ask the city what it wants to tell you.”

You wake with the brass key still warm in your fist, the weight of it real against your skin for three long heartbeats before it, too, dissolves into morning light.
