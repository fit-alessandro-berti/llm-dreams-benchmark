…survive without anything you once called “normal.”

The air smells of smoke and wet earth. No engines. No wires. No glass towers. Just a village of timber houses huddled beneath a gray sky, and people who stare at your clothes like you’ve stepped out of a myth.

You try your phone out of instinct. Dead. Useless. A black mirror reflecting a face that suddenly looks too soft for this world.

By noon, you’ve learned your first lesson: hunger is louder in the past. Bread isn’t bought with a tap; it’s kneaded, baked, earned. Water isn’t filtered; it’s hauled in aching buckets from a well. Fire doesn’t appear with a click—it has to be coaxed, breathed into life like a secret.

By dusk, you realize you need more than food and shelter.

You need a new name.

The blacksmith calls you “Ash,” because you appear from nowhere and smell faintly of smoke from the hearth you failed to light. The name sticks. Children follow you. Elders watch you. No one trusts a stranger who asks too many questions about the stars, maps, and kings.

Days become weeks. Blisters become calluses. You trade what little you know—how to boil water before drinking, how to stitch cleaner wounds, how to mark planting cycles by moon phases—in exchange for lessons in everything else: how to split wood, mend boots, read weather from clouds, and stay quiet when soldiers ride through.

One night, under a sky so full of stars it hurts, an old woman tells you the truth in a whisper:

“People like you arrive every few generations. Most try to go home. The wise ones decide what home means first.”

Then winter comes early.

The river freezes. Grain spoils. A fever spreads through the village like spilled ink. And suddenly your strange half-knowledge matters. You organize boiling stations, isolate the sick, beg the healers to wash hands between patients. Some listen. Some don’t. Some live anyway.

When spring finally cracks the frost, the village still stands.

So do you.

And on the first warm morning, standing in a field of new green shoots, you understand what the dream has been trying to tell you all along:

You didn’t wake up in another era to escape your life.

You woke up there to remember that every era asks the same question—

When everything familiar is gone, who do you choose to become?
