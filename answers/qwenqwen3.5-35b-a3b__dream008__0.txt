You find that your hands are no longer fingers, but tiny, mechanical pencils, scratching furiously against the air as if the air itself were a digital interface.

The fluorescent lights above don't hum; they breathe, pulsing with a slow, rhythmic glow like a sleeping giant's chest. The office floor stretches out beneath you, soft like moss, yet you can still feel the phantom stiffness of a carpeted cubicle. You look at your boss. Their face is a shifting mosaic of documents, the faces on the paper forms flickering like TV static, but the eyes are always fixed on your watch.

The deadline isn't a time on a clock; it's a physical weight pressing down on your shoulders, an invisible backpack that you can feel the straps digging into. The deadline is heavy, like holding a mountain in a paper bag.

"Start," the boss says, but the word is a color. A deep, bruised purple fills the room.

You look at the file in your hands. It is blank. As you try to open it, the paper dissolves into a flock of white doves. They swirl around the room, carrying messages you can't read, whispering that the work is already done, you just haven't remembered to finish it yet.

The clock on the wall has no numbers, only eyes. It blinks in sequence, and every time it blinks, the room gets smaller. The walls slide inward, the cubicles folding down like an accordion. The impossible deadline is the shrinking distance to the floor.

You try to run, but your legs are tangled in a rope made of telephone cords. The lines go back and forth, connecting to every phone in the building, and every one of them rings. The sound is deafening, a cacophony of notifications that aren't your own.

*Ring. Ring. Ring.*

You reach for a phone, the receiver warm and breathing like skin. You lift it to your ear, and instead of a dial tone, there is a voice you recognize. It is your own voice from this morning, saying, "Get up."

The office dissolves into a cloud of static. The purple light fades into the grey of dawn. You gasp, your chest heaving, and your mechanical pencil fingers solidify back into your own palms as you jerk upright in your bed. The alarm clock is silent, the screen dark, and for a moment, you can't remember if the deadline is for a project, or for your sleep.
