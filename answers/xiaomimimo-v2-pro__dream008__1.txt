Ah, this sounds like the beginning of a classic stress dream—the kind our minds conjure when we're feeling pressure or overwhelmed. Let's see where it goes...

I take a deep breath, the air in the office feeling oddly thick and slow, like I'm moving through water. The impossible deadline isn't a date on a calendar, but a tangible object—a heavy, ticking brass clock fused to the project folder my boss just handed me. Its hands are spinning backward.

My boss, whose face keeps shifting between familiar colleagues and vague, forgotten acquaintances, just smiles a serene, unblinking smile. "We know you can handle it," they say, their voice echoing slightly as if coming from a great distance.

I open the folder. Inside, instead of documents, there's a single, shimmering thread leading off the desk and down the hallway. The project isn't a report or a plan; it's to *follow the thread*. The deadline is when it runs out.

So, I start walking. The hallway stretches and curves in impossible ways. The thread leads me past the break room, where the coffee machine is brewing a storm cloud, and into the server room, where the humming servers whisper snippets of old conversations.

Suddenly, I realize: in a dream, you can't fail a deadline. The logic isn't the same. I turn to my shifting boss, who is now holding the other end of the spool. "This thread," I say, "it's not a task. It's a question, isn't it?"

The boss's smile finally looks genuine. The clock on the folder stops ticking. "And what is the question?" they ask.

The dream starts to dissolve at the edges, the fluorescent lights brightening into morning sun. I wake up with the answer forming, not for the project, but for whatever real-world worry sparked the dream: the solution isn't to race against an impossible time, but to redefine the task itself.
