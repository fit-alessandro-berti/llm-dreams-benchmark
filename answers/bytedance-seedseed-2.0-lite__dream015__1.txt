She’d gotten separated from your group two years prior, swept away in a crowd panicking from one of the Blight Storms that warp memory and turn loose debris into floating, screaming shards, and you’d clung to the dumb, soft hope that if the book was still where you’d hidden it behind the Winnie-the-Pooh shelf, she might have left a note for anyone who came looking—any piece of the old world that hadn’t rotted away.

The dream warps soft around the edges as you walk: your shadow splits off halfway into your old childhood beagle, Max, the one who’d died when you were 12, his tail thumping so hard it sends dandelion fluff spinning into gold clouds. You outrun a Blight Storm that hums static and erases road signs mid-glance, the air stinging of burnt plastic and your grandma’s lavender soap, a messy mix of fear and comfort only dreams can stitch together. When you push through the library’s cracked oak doors, ivy climbs the shelves, its leaves printed with snippets of old poetry and picture book dialogue, and fireflies that glow with the faded blue of library checkout tags drift between the stacks.

You scramble to the children’s section, your boots thudding on water-stained carpet, and yank the *Where the Wild Things Are* copy free from its hiding spot. Tucked between the pages is Lila’s neon pink crayon drawing of the two of you dressed as Max and his wolf pack, plus a scrawled note that says she’d waited here three days after the separation, then headed north to the colony by the great lakes—she left the drawing for you, because she knew you’d come looking for your favorite book. Before you can tuck the paper into your pocket, the dream softens into sunlight, Max’s bark fading into the sound of your alarm, but for a second you swear you can still smell the library’s old mildew and Lila’s cherry lip balm, warm and real enough to hold onto.
