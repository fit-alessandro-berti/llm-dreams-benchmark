None of them look quite like anyone you’ve ever met, but they don’t feel strange: the girl at the front of the group wears a raincoat stitched all over with glowing street names that shift if you squint, the old man carrying a wicker basket hums the lullaby your mom sang you through childhood fever nightmares, the teen at the back balances a cardboard box of stray cats that purr so loud they rattle the fillings in your teeth.

You can’t even remember what address you’d been fumbling for, or why you’d felt so small and raw ten minutes earlier, so you let them lead you. They weave you through alleys that turn from cracked asphalt to mossy stone mid-step, past a bodega where the tabby behind the counter slides you a glowing river rock for good luck, past a carousel that only plays lullabies instead of brassy carnival tunes. Eventually they stop at a small clearing tucked between two glass skyscrapers you swear weren’t there a block earlier, and in the middle of it stands the gnarled old oak tree you climbed every weekend after school in your hometown—the one you hadn’t thought of in 12 years, not since your family moved away and you left that small, soft version of yourself behind.

The wicker basket spills open with wool blankets and mugs of hot cocoa that smell like your grandma’s kitchen, the cats curl up in your lap, and the girl with the glowing raincoat says, softly, that everyone gets lost in the city sometimes. Lost chasing jobs, lost chasing approval, lost forgetting the parts of themselves they left along the way, and they bring all those people here, to remember they’re not alone. You reach out to thank them, and that’s when you spot the cuffs of all their coats are stitched with your childhood nickname, the one only people who loved you ever used.

You don’t wake up right away. You sit there for what feels like hours, watching fireflies dance between the skyscraper windows, no ache of loneliness left in your chest, until the sun peeks over the roofline and you drift up, warm and whole, back to your quiet bed.
