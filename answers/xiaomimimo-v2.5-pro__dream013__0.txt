At first, the puzzles are familiar — sliding tiles, pattern sequences, mirrors that reflect light into hidden switches. You solve them with steady focus, and each solved puzzle opens a new corridor, deeper and darker than the last.

But as you descend further, the nature of the puzzles begins to change.

One room contains a door with no handle, only a single sentence carved into the stone: *"The answer is not what you think, but what you've forgotten."* You close your eyes, and suddenly a memory surfaces — a childhood drawing, a song your mother hummed, the scent of rain on hot pavement. When you open your eyes, the door is already open.

Another corridor is completely silent. No echoes. You realize the puzzle isn't something to *solve* — it's something to *endure*. You must sit in the silence until you stop fearing it. Only then does the floor beneath you lower like an elevator.

Deeper still, you encounter a room where every wall is a mirror, and in each reflection, a different version of yourself stares back — one younger, one older, one who made different choices. The puzzle is to choose which reflection to follow. You reach toward the one that looks the most uncertain, the most human. That mirror shatters, revealing a path forward.

Finally, you reach the center of the maze. There is no grand treasure, no final door. Just a small, quiet room with a single chair and a window that looks out onto an endless sky. A placard on the wall reads:

*"You were never solving the maze. The maze was solving you."*

You sit down. And for the first time in the dream, you feel completely at peace. The walls dissolve. Light pours in.

You wake up — but the feeling stays.
