You see a group by the window, but they aren't talking to each other. They are all facing the glass, watching a sky that isn't outside—it is a projection of a forest you visited as a child, complete with the sound of wind through pines that smells like rain. You try to ask them for directions, but your voice comes out as a chime, a clear, high note that makes the people in the group turn and smile without opening their mouths.

You move deeper into the crowd. The floor feels soft, like walking on thick moss or memory foam. You spot a flash of a familiar blue jacket near a staircase that spirals upward into darkness. Your heart lifts. You push through a cluster of people who are holding hands in a circle, their faces blurred like smudged charcoal sketches.

You reach the bottom of the stairs and call out the name. The person in the blue jacket turns. It is your friend, but their eyes are glowing softly, like embers. They don't speak. Instead, they hold out a hand, and in their palm rests a small, ticking clock.

"You're late," they say, but the words are already spoken, echoing from the past. "Or you're early. It doesn't matter anymore."

The room begins to tilt. The ceiling drops away, revealing not more hall, but a vast, starry ocean. Your friend steps forward, not onto the floor, but into the air, walking as if gravity has simply forgotten you. You reach for them, but your hand passes through their shoulder like mist.

The blue jacket dissolves into a flock of moths that flutter upward toward the stars. You are left standing alone on the tilting floor, and the hum of the crowd rises to a crescendo, becoming the sound of a rushing river. You close your eyes, not to sleep, but because the light is becoming too bright, too white, too real.

You feel the weight of a blanket on your legs. The smell of pine and rain fades, replaced by the scent of coffee. You open your eyes, and the ceiling is just a ceiling.
