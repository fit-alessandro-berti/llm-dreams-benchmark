You are the captain of a ship in the middle of a turbulent sea. Your crew looks to you for guidance as the storm worsens, and you —

— can't remember how to steer.

The wheel is in your hands, slick with rain and salt, spinning wildly on its own. The compass in front of you isn't pointing north. The needle is spinning counterclockwise, too fast to follow, and every time lightning cracks the sky, the light catches it and for a second it looks perfectly still.

Your crew is shouting, but their voices are muffled, like you're hearing them through water. One of them, a boy you don't remember hiring, puts his hand on your arm. His hand is warm. Too warm.

And then you realize something that makes the terror drain right out of your chest.

This isn't your ship.

This is a ship you built as a kid out of blankets and kitchen chairs. The sails are bedsheets. The turbulent sea is your bedroom carpet, rippling like water when the wind blows. The storm isn't trying to sink you. It's just trying to get your attention.

So you do the most captain-like thing you can think of.

You let go of the wheel.

You tell the crew — your crew — to stop bailing water and to just *hold on to each other* instead. You climb up onto the bow, rain lashing your face, and you open your mouth and start singing. It's off-key and ridiculous and you don't even know the words, but the moment you start, the wind stops fighting you and starts singing with you.

The clouds break. Not into sunlight, but into a deep, starry blue, even though it was just afternoon. The sea below you goes glass-still, so still you can see not your reflection, but a whole other sky beneath the water.

And underneath that, you can see yourself, asleep in bed, breathing slowly. Peaceful.

You understand then. You don't have to weather this storm. You just have to wake up when you're ready.
