*...you feel a sudden, profound detachment, as if you are watching yourself through the hazy glass of a window. The fluorescent lights overhead, which usually hum with that predictable, weary buzz, suddenly begin to flicker, but the flicker is irregular—not electrical, but rhythmic, almost like a heart monitor that is skipping beats.

You stare at the project brief, titled in aggressively Helvetica font, demanding the impossible. You try to formulate a protest, to argue about resource allocation, or the physical limitations of human time, but your throat feels coated in dust, and the words catch and dissipate like soap bubbles.

Your boss—a man whose suit seems suspiciously crisp, in a way that never looks natural—simply smiles. It's not a reassuring smile; it's too wide, too symmetrical, stretching the skin across his cheeks like rubber. "Just get started," he murmurs, and his voice seems to resonate from the very steel beams of the building, vibrating in your teeth.

The panic finally breaches the dam. It isn't the deadline that scares you; it's the sheer *pointlessness* of the demand.

Then, the walls start to breathe.

It begins subtly. The beige paint on the cubicle walls develops a faint, mottled patina, like bruised skin. The hum of the lights doesn't just buzz—it descends in pitch, morphing into a deep, sonorous drone that you feel in the soles of your shoes.

The desk you're sitting at begins to tilt, slowly, agonizingly. It doesn't fall, but rather tilts into a gentle, perpetual incline, as if the Earth's plane has suddenly decided this quadrant of the office is merely symbolic.

You drop your pen. It falls in slow motion, suspended in a syrup of thick, golden light.

The clock on the wall—the one that has always been reliable—doesn't just show the time; the *numbers* are liquid. They bleed downwards, separating and reforming like spilled ink. And as you watch them writhe, the calendar squares flutter open, revealing not months, but pictures: a childhood birthday cake, a deserted beach at dusk, the chipped handle of a favorite mug.

Your boss doesn't move, yet you feel his authority collapsing. The crispness of his suit wrinkles suddenly, not with the creases of fabric, but with the pattern of petrified wood. He becomes less a person and more an *idea* of authority—a gaunt figure made of tailored dread.

You look at the impossible task, and suddenly, you realize the project isn't a physical document. It's a shimmering knot, woven from threads of anxiety and unspoken expectations, floating just above the desk blotter.

And then, the sound of the buzzing finally reaches a frequency that is too high, too piercing, and it seems to vibrate *inside* your skull. The light intensifies, not getting brighter, but becoming *thicker*, like looking out of water.

You scream, but the sound that emerges is not yours. It is the collective, desperate sigh of every unfulfilled deadline, every forgotten dream, every project that was deemed too risky, too complicated, too *impossible*.

The office shudders one final time, the buzzing crests into a deafening, perfect chord, and everything—the boss, the desk, the blinking, bleeding numbers—snaps, not back to normal, but off the controls entirely.

You are no longer in the office. You are simply floating, suspended in a vast, endless space of lukewarm, dusty twilight. The project, the deadline, the office, they are all dissolving into the golden syrup, leaving you only with the weight of the realization: *the effort was never about the task; it was about the performance of panic.*"
