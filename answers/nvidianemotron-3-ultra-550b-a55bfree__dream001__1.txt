...start to walk, but the streets rearrange themselves behind you. The avenue you came down is gone, replaced by a canal filled with floating staircases. A man in a three-piece suit rows past in a bathtub, checking a pocket watch that melts like warm wax over his fingers.

"Third floor, room 400," he says, though you never asked. "Don't be late for the unmaking."

You check your phone for the address. The screen shows a map of your childhood home's circulatory system. Your thumbprints swirl across the display, rearranging into directions written in a language you spoke fluently at age four but have never learned since.

The buildings lean in. Their windows are eyes. Their doors are mouths. A skyscraper yawns, and a flock of briefcases migrates south from its throat.

You're wearing one shoe. The other foot is bare, and the pavement feels like your grandmother's kitchen table — warm, scarred, smelling of lemon polish and cigarettes. When did you lose the shoe? You've always only had one. You were born with one foot shod, the other kissing earth.

A street sign reads: *YOU WERE SUPPOSED TO BRING THE BLUE FILE.*

You don't have a blue file. You have a sandwich wrapped in wax paper. When you unwrap it, there's a key inside, cut from a single piece of bone.

The meeting is in an hour. The meeting was yesterday. The meeting is the dream you're having right now, and the dreamer is waking up.

You start to run.
