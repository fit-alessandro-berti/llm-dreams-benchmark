But the floor beneath you doesn't feel like wood or carpet anymore. It feels like the surface of a still lake. As you take that step, a ripple spreads out from your heel, distorting the room. The walls breathe, expanding and contracting like the lungs of a giant.

Your friend stops shouting. Their face blurs at the edges, like a watercolor painting left in the rain. You realize you aren't holding the object of your argument in your hands; you're holding a lantern, and the light inside is flickering, casting long, dancing shadows that look like memories you've forgotten.

"I can't hear you," your friend says, but their mouth isn't moving. The sound comes from the lantern instead.

You decide to let go of the lantern. It doesn't fall; it floats upward, turning into a flock of paper birds that scatter toward the ceiling, which has now become a starry night sky. The anger drains out of your chest, replaced by a sudden, heavy calm. You look at your friend, and they are smiling, but they look younger, like they do in the old photographs you keep in your wallet.

"You were right," they say, though you don't remember what you were arguing about anymore.

Then, the floor of the lake turns into grass, and you are standing on a hill overlooking a city made of glass, humming with a low, electric vibration. The argument is gone, but the feeling of it lingers, like the taste of salt on your lips after a storm. You take a breath, and the air tastes like rain, and you know, without knowing why, that you are about to fly.
