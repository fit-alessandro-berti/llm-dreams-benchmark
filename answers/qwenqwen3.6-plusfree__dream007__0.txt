...and you decide to stop speaking. Not out of defeat, but because the air between you has grown too thick to carry more syllables. As the last frustrated word leaves your lips, it doesn't echo. It crystallizes. Then fractures. Soon, every sentence you've flung back and forth is pooling on the floor like colored glass, catching the light of a sun that wasn't in the sky a moment ago.

Your friend steps back, breathless, and reaches down to pick up one of the shards. As their fingers touch it, it melts into a small paper boat. Then another melts. Then yours do too. Within seconds, the floor is a quiet river of tiny vessels, drifting toward a doorway that's slowly becoming a shoreline. 

You look at each other, and the anger hasn't vanished so much as it's been translated. You suddenly remember why this matters: not because one of you has to win, but because both of you are trying to protect the same fragile thing. The dream doesn't care about logic; it cares about weight. And the weight lifts.

Without a word, you both step into the current. The water is warm. It carries you forward, past half-remembered conversations, past versions of yourselves that argued in kitchens, in cars, in parking lots after midnight. The shoreline ahead is just a place to sit. You do. The sky folds itself down like a blanket. Your friend leans in, not to speak, but to rest their shoulder against yours. 

And just like that, the dream decides it's finished with its lesson. It fades not with a jolt, but with a slow exhale, leaving behind the quiet conviction that some disagreements aren't walls. They're just doors you haven't learned to open together yet.
