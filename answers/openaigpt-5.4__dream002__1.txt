…the trees begin to lean toward one another as if whispering a warning above your head.

At first it is only the wind, cool and strange against your skin, carrying the smell of rain and something older—wet stone, moss, and distant smoke. The sky darkens too quickly, clouds folding over the sun like heavy curtains. The path beneath your feet, soft with pine needles a moment ago, becomes silver with restless shadows.

Then the first drop falls.

It lands on your hand, impossibly cold, and where it touches your skin it glimmers like liquid moonlight. Another follows. Then hundreds. The rain does not fall straight down, but in slow, drifting lines, as if time itself has begun to tilt.

You keep walking.

Ahead, between the black trunks of the trees, a warm golden light appears. It flickers gently through the storm—too steady to be lightning, too inviting to ignore. As thunder rolls across the forest, deep and ancient, you step off the path and follow the light into a clearing you are certain was not there before.

In the center stands a small cottage with round windows glowing amber. Wildflowers bend in the rain around its stone foundation, though none of them seem crushed by the wind. Smoke rises from the chimney in a thin, calm ribbon, untouched by the storm. You approach the wooden door, and just before you knock, it opens on its own.

Inside, everything is warm.

A fire crackles in a wide hearth. Bundles of drying herbs hang from the beams. A kettle sings softly over the flames. In a rocking chair by the window sits an old woman with silver hair braided down to her waist. She looks up at you as if she has been expecting you for years.

“You took your time,” she says, smiling.

You want to ask who she is, how she knows you, why the storm feels as though it followed you personally across the sky—but instead you notice the room is filled with things you somehow recognize: a toy from childhood on the mantel, a book you lost long ago on the table, a photograph of a place you have never been but miss with all your heart.

The woman gestures for you to sit.

As you lower yourself into the chair across from her, the thunder outside softens. The rain against the glass begins to sound less like a storm and more like fingers tapping out a message. The old woman pours tea into a blue cup and places it in your hands.

“Listen,” she says.

So you do.

Beneath the rain, beneath the crackle of the fire, beneath your own breathing, you hear the forest speaking. Not in words exactly, but in feelings so clear they become meaning: you are lost, yes, but not abandoned. You are changing, yes, but not broken. The storm did not come to destroy the path. It came to wash away the one that was never yours.

The old woman nods, as if hearing it too.

When you look back toward the window, the glass no longer reflects the cottage. It reflects a shoreline at dawn: a calm sea, pale pink sky, and someone standing at the water’s edge waiting for you. Their face is blurred by light, but you know, with dream-certainty, that if you step through that window you will remember something important—something you left behind before this life, or perhaps before this very day.

The woman leans forward.

“When you wake,” she says, “follow what feels gentle, not what feels loud.”

Then the room trembles softly. The fire lifts in a swirl of golden sparks. The walls of the cottage begin to dissolve into mist. The storm outside turns suddenly silent. You rise, carrying the blue cup, and walk toward the shining window.

As your fingers touch the glass, it melts into cool morning air.

And you step through.
