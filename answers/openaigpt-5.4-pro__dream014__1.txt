They guide you through winding alleys, across a bridge made of shimmering glass, and into a quiet square where the stars hang impossibly low above the rooftops. In the center of the square stands a door, freestanding and painted deep blue. “This is where you were trying to go,” one of them says gently.

When you reach for the handle, you hesitate. “I don’t even know what’s on the other side,” you whisper.

“Neither did we,” another stranger says with a laugh full of warmth. “That’s why we walk each other there.”

You open the door and step through—not into a room, but into a vast meadow under a dawn-colored sky. The city is gone. The loneliness is gone too, as if it had only ever been a shadow cast by forgetting. In the distance, you see a path unfolding just as your feet touch it, and for the first time in a long while, you know exactly what to do:

You keep walking, not because you are lost, but because you are finally on your way.
