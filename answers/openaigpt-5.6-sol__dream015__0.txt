plant a garden.

The idea seems absurd at first. The soil is gray, the rain tastes faintly of metal, and every distant sound could be a collapsing building—or something hunting. Still, in the courtyard of an abandoned library, you pry cracked tiles from the earth and dig with the blade of a broken fan.

In the basement, behind shelves swollen with damp, you discover a small wooden box labeled **SEEDS FOR SPRING**.

You plant them all.

Each day, you carry water from a leaking pipe three streets away. You build fences from shopping carts and wind chimes from bent spoons, hoping the noise will warn you of intruders. Travelers begin to stop. Some laugh at your garden; others quietly help. An old mechanic repairs the pump. A child paints suns on the courtyard walls. A stranger leaves a sack of clean soil without giving their name.

Then, one morning, a green shoot rises from the ash.

By sunset, there are hundreds.

They grow impossibly fast, curling around ruined pillars and spilling through shattered windows. Vines climb the library walls, their leaves glowing softly beneath the moon. Flowers open like small lanterns, and wherever their pollen drifts, the poisoned ground turns dark and rich.

But the garden is not merely growing.

It is remembering.

In the rustle of leaves, you hear birds that vanished years ago. In the roots beneath your feet, you feel rivers searching for their old paths. And from deep below the courtyard comes a slow, enormous heartbeat.

Everyone looks to you.

The earth shudders. The library doors swing open by themselves, revealing a staircase that was never there, descending into golden light.

You take a lantern-flower in one hand and the child’s hand in the other.

Then you lead your little village down, toward whatever the world has been dreaming beneath the ruins.
