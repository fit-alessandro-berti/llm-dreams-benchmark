...and you hesitate, because the city is full of sharp lights and sharper intentions. But their eyes hold a stillness, an old and quiet kindness that doesn't fit with the neon and the noise.

One of them, a woman with silver threading through her dark hair, steps forward. She doesn’t speak your language, and yet you understand. She places a small, smooth stone in your palm. It’s cool, river-worn, and utterly ordinary. She closes your fingers around it.

The group doesn’t lead you, exactly. They simply begin to walk, and you find yourself following. They move not like a search party, but like a tide, slow and inevitable. The city’s frantic rhythm starts to blur. The honking horns become a distant, hollow drone. The flashing signs bleed their colors into the wet pavement, a language you can no longer read. You feel no closer to finding a street name you recognize.

Instead, you are led down a set of stairs you’d never noticed before, tucked between a glowing bodega and a silent bank. The stairs descend into a space that isn’t a subway station or a parking garage. It’s an old, vaulted arcade, tiled in faded emerald and gold, perfectly quiet and smelling of petrichor, though no rain has fallen here for decades. Light filters down from a glass ceiling somewhere high above, dirty and diffused, like light through deep water.

The strangers have stopped. They turn to you, each one smiling that same, unnerving smile of shared knowledge. The woman with the stone points not to an exit, but to a single, heavy wooden door in the middle of the tiled wall. It has no handle, only a small, bowl-shaped indentation.

You look down at the stone in your hand. You walk toward the door, and you fit the stone into the bowl. It’s a perfect match.

The door doesn’t swing open. It simply vanishes, like mist rising from a river. Beyond it isn’t the street, or the city, or the life you think you’ve lost.

It’s a room you remember. Not from your waking life, but from the dream you had as a child—the one with the window seat that overlooked a field of silver grass under two moons, and the scent of your grandmother’s rosemary bread, a grandmother who never existed. The detail is staggering. The dust motes dancing in the double moonlight.

And there, on the small wooden table by the window, is a compass. Not a modern one, but an antique of brass and crystal, its needle spinning slowly, not for magnetic north, but searching for something else. The strangers are gone. The city is gone. You are more lost than ever, in a cosmos that has forgotten the map.

You pick up the compass. Its needle shudders and settles, pointing directly at the door behind you, which is now just a solid wall of smooth, warm plaster.

The feeling of being lost hasn't left you. It has simply become holy. You were never meant to find your way back to a place. You were meant to find your way in. And you realize, staring at the impossible room, that the kindness of strangers was never about pointing you to a subway line. It was about handing you a stone and letting you discover, entirely on your own, that you could build a door.
