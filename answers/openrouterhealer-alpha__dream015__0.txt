The sky is a permanent bruise — violet and ash-grey, as if the atmosphere itself forgot how to heal. You move through the skeletal remains of a city, your boots crunching over shattered glass and rusted metal. The wind carries whispers of distant sirens, or maybe they're animals. You've learned not to trust your ears out here.

But today is different. Today, you found something.

A seed.

Just one, resting in the cracked palm of a concrete statue outside what used to be a library. No name on the statue anymore — just weathered stone fingers curled gently upward, as if it had been waiting decades for someone to notice.

You tuck it into the small leather pouch at your hip — the one your mother gave you, back when "mother" still meant something safe. You don't know what it will grow into. Doesn't matter. What matters is the decision you just made:

You're going to plant it.

Not here, where the soil tastes like battery acid. You've heard rumors — whispers traded between scavengers over scavenged canned goods — of a place west of the ruins where the rivers still run clean. Where green things push through the earth like forgotten promises. Others call it a myth. A trap. A fever dream born of dehydration and desperation.

But you've always been stubborn about impossible things.

As you turn west, a figure steps from behind a collapsed overpass support. You freeze. They raise an empty hand — palm out, fingers spread. No weapon.

"The seed," they say. Not a question.

Your hand drifts to your pouch. "How did you know?"

They smile — cracked lips, sun-scarred face, eyes that have seen too much and somehow still hold light. "Because I've been carrying one too. For three years."

They reach into their own jacket and pull out a small, identical pouch.

"Was starting to think I was the last fool still believing."

The two of you stand there — strangers in a broken world, each holding a tiny piece of evidence that something might still grow.

The wind shifts. West feels warmer.

You start walking together.
