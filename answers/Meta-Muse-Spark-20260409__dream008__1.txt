Then something shifts. The office lights flicker, and the deadline written on the whiteboard starts to bleed, the ink running down like rain. It drips onto the floor and forms a narrow river that cuts straight through the carpet, past your desk, and out the door. 

Your boss stops tapping. They look at the river, then back at you, and say, "Well, are you going to follow it or just stand there?" 

So you do. You step into the river. It's only ankle deep, but it tugs you forward. The office walls peel back like paper. Cubicles become trees. The fluorescent lights turn into a sky full of slow comets. 

The project files in your hands morph too. They are not reports anymore. They are paper boats. Each one has a task scribbled on it. You realize the deadline was never about finishing. It was about launching them. 

You kneel and set the first boat into the current. It takes off, fast, and one of the comets dips down to light its path. Your boss is beside you now, also ankle deep, sleeves rolled up. They hand you another boat. "You've got this," they say, but their voice sounds a lot like your own. 

The river widens. The impossible deadline clock is still ticking, but it is mounted on a turtle now, paddling calmly downstream. You laugh, because of course it is.
