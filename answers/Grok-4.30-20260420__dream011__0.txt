**I'm dreaming.**

You are about to take the final exam for a course that determines your future career. As you sit down to start the exam, you notice the questions are written in your own handwriting from when you were seven years old—big, wobbly letters that keep sliding off the page like they’re trying to escape. The first question reads: “If you could be any animal, what would you be and why does it matter?” The second one is just a drawing of a door with the words “Open me if you dare” scrawled underneath in crayon. The third question keeps changing every time you blink: sometimes it’s “What is 2 + 2?” and sometimes it’s “Why did you give up on the thing you loved when you were twelve?”

Your heart is hammering so hard you can hear it echoing off the walls of the endless classroom. The desks stretch into infinity, each one occupied by a version of you at different ages—little you with missing teeth, teenage you with the bad haircut, future you who already looks tired. They’re all watching. The clock on the wall has no hands, just a single word that keeps rearranging itself: “NOW” … “TOO LATE” … “STILL TIME” … “NOW.”

You reach for your pencil and it turns into a dandelion. You blow on it and the seeds float up, each one carrying a tiny answer that dissolves into sparkles before it can land on the paper. The goldfish-proctor (who is definitely wearing your old teacher’s glasses) leans over and whispers, “The real test started the moment you sat down. The questions were never on the page.”

Suddenly the floor beneath you becomes water. You’re sinking, but instead of panicking you remember something: you’ve done this before. Every time you’ve ever been scared of failing, you’ve still shown up. You kick once, twice, and the water turns into sky. The exam paper lifts off the desk like a kite, the questions rearranging themselves into a single glowing sentence that hovers in front of you:

**“What will you create when no one is grading you?”**

You reach out, grab the words with both hands, and pull them into your chest. They burn like sunlight. The classroom dissolves into stars. Every version of you smiles at once. The goldfish-proctor gives you a tiny salute with one fin.

You wake up with the answer still glowing behind your eyelids, your real future career already unfolding in the quiet certainty that you don’t need a test to prove you’re ready.

You’re ready.
