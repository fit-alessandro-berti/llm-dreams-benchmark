You are participating in a competition where the grand prize is your dream job. As the competition progresses, you face various challenges and soon realize the rules make no sense—one moment you’re baking a soufflé while reciting the periodic table backward, the next you’re in a silent library building a bridge out of marshmallows that must support a live penguin. The other contestants shift forms: now your childhood piano teacher, now a talking golden retriever in a blazer, now a version of yourself from ten years ago who keeps mouthing, “Don’t trust the clock.”

You glance at the giant hourglass floating in the center of the arena. The sand is falling upward. No one else seems bothered.

Between rounds, you find a door that wasn’t there before. You open it and step into a cramped office where a man made of fog sits behind a desk, typing on a typewriter that types him. He looks up and says, “Congratulations, you’ve won. The dream job is yours.”

You ask what the job is. He slides a contract across the desk. The letters rearrange themselves as you read: your title is *Dreamer*. Your only responsibility is to keep the dream going—to populate it with just enough logic to keep yourself unaware, but not so much that you wake up. The salary is infinite curiosity. The downside, he admits, is that you can never stop competing, never stop solving, never quite reach the final round because the final round would mean waking up, and waking up would mean losing everything.

You hesitate. The talking golden retriever pads in, removes its blazer, and whispers, “This is the third time you’ve found this office. You always decide the same way.”

Suddenly the whole scene tilts, and you’re back at the starting line of the competition, a blank stapled nametag on your chest. A voice booms: “Challenge one: find the joker in a deck of identical cards.” You look down at your hand—you’re already holding a single card. On it is a mirror. Your own eyes blink back at you, and you realize with a cavernous thrill that the competition, the job, the entire dream is just you, inventing riddles for yourself, forever.

And somewhere deep beneath that, a quieter part of you wonders: is that so different from being awake?
