...written in a language you've never seen before — except somehow you recognize your own handwriting in them. You wrote these questions. You're sure of it. But when? And why can't you read them?

You glance around the room. Everyone else is scribbling furiously, their pencils making a sound like rain on a tin roof. The student next to you has already filled seventeen pages. You look down and realize you don't have a pencil. You search your pockets and find only a spoon, a ticket stub from a movie you don't remember seeing, and a small key.

The proctor walks down the aisle. As she approaches, you realize she's your third-grade teacher, Mrs. Halloway — except she's also somehow your dentist, and possibly your neighbor's dog wearing glasses. "Time is half over," she announces, though you're certain you only just sat down. The clock on the wall agrees with her, then disagrees, its hands spinning lazily backward.

You try to read the first question again. This time the letters rearrange themselves into something almost legible: *Describe the thing you forgot to do. Show your work.*

A cold dread settles over you. You *did* forget something. Something enormous. You never attended this class, did you? Not once, all semester. You don't even remember enrolling. And yet your whole future depends on this single sheet of paper that keeps growing — it's the size of a tablecloth now, draping over the edges of your desk and pooling onto the floor.

You pick up the spoon and begin to write with it anyway. Strangely, it works. Silver looping letters flow out, and you realize you're writing answers to questions you haven't read yet, and the answers feel *true* — truer than anything you've ever known while awake. You're writing about the key in your pocket. About the door it opens. About the thing you forgot, which wasn't a class at all, but a person, or a promise, or a song.

The bell rings. It sounds like your alarm clock.

"Pencils down," says Mrs. Halloway, and the exam dissolves like sugar in water, taking the answers with it — all that perfect, urgent knowledge slipping away even as you grasp for it.

You wake with your hand still moving, writing on the bedsheet with a finger, trying to finish the last sentence. For one moment, you almost remember what it said.

Then it's gone. But the small key — you could swear you still feel its weight in your hand.
