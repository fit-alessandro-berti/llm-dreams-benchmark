...the landscape shifts like ink in water. First, you must cross a bridge woven from half-finished promises. Each stepping stone hums with a different voice: *You're not ready. Who gave you permission? What if it slips away?* You don't argue with them. You simply place your hand on each one and whisper back, *I know. Thank you for warning me. I'll go anyway.* The bridge solidifies into warm glass beneath your feet.

Next, you enter an atrium where time hangs in glass jars, each labeled with a year you haven't lived yet. A scale sits in the center, calibrated not in weight, but in attention. You're told to place your current longing on one side, but doing so makes the other side plummet. Instead, you set down your need for a title, your fear of irrelevance, and your habit of rehearsing your success. The scale balances. The jars begin to glow, and inside each, a small version of you is already working, laughing, stumbling, learning.

Finally, you stand before a door that breathes. Behind it, the judges are not people, but echoes of every choice that made you who you are. They ask in a chorus that feels like your own heartbeat: *When the novelty fades, what will you tend to?* You don't recite a vision or quote a manifesto. You simply open your palms and show them the way you make space for others, the way you return to the work even when it's dull, the way you let failure be a teacher instead of a verdict.

The door dissolves into morning light. The grand prize isn't a contract or a key. It's a desk by a window that faces a street you've never walked but somehow miss. It's a schedule written in curiosity instead of quotas. It's the quiet understanding that the dream job was never a place you'd arrive at, but a rhythm you'd learn to keep.

The dream thins at the edges. You wake not with a trophy, but with a steady pulse. And somewhere between sleeping and standing, you realize you've already begun.
