**The questions are written in a shimmering, impossible script that shifts and rearranges itself every time you blink.**  

They’re not even questions. They’re riddles about the exact moment you first felt truly lost in life, mixed with impossible math problems where the numbers are your childhood phone number, your first kiss’s birthday, and the exact weight of the regret you carry for never calling your grandmother back.  

The exam hall is your old high school auditorium, but the ceiling is the night sky of a planet you’ve never visited, and every seat is filled with every version of yourself you could have become. One of them is a famous architect. Another is a washed-up musician who still plays your favorite song on a guitar made of broken promises. The proctor is your first-grade teacher, except she has your mother’s eyes and is slowly turning into a flock of paper airplanes.  

You pick up the pencil. It’s warm, like it’s been waiting for you. The first “question” unfolds in front of you like origami:  

*“If the road not taken led here anyway, what color was the sky the day you gave up on becoming a writer?”*  

Your hand moves before your brain catches up. You write: *The exact shade of the hoodie I wore when I decided “practical” was safer than “possible.”*  

The paper bursts into butterflies.  

The second question is just a mirror. You stare into it and see yourself at age 87, smiling like you finally figured it out. She winks.  

You’re crying now, but the tears are tiny galaxies. The entire room starts humming the song that used to play in your headphones when you were seventeen and believed the future was something you could wrestle into submission.  

Time speeds up. Or maybe it slows down. The exam ends when you realize the only answer that ever mattered was the one you’ve been too scared to write your whole life:  

**“I’m not late. I’m exactly on time for the version of me that’s still becoming.”**  

The bell rings.  

You wake up in your real bed, heart hammering, cheeks wet, the dream already dissolving like sugar in rain.  

But somewhere in the back of your mind, a single glowing sentence remains, written in that impossible shifting script:  

*The course that determines your future career isn’t the one on the syllabus.*  

*It’s the one you’ve been teaching yourself since the day you were born.*  

And the final exam?  

It’s every ordinary Tuesday you choose to keep going anyway.
