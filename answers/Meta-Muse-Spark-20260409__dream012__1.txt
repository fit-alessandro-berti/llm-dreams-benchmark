You grip the wheel as another wave crashes over the bow, salt spray stinging your eyes. The *Meridian* groans like a living thing, timbers protesting under the weight of the black water.  

Your crew looks to you for guidance as the storm worsens, and you realize there's no chart for this.  

The first mate, Reyes, shouts something you can't hear over the wind, but her face says it all: *Tell us what to do.* The younger deckhands are frozen at the rigging, knuckles white on the ropes. Even the storm-hardened cook has come up from the galley, bracing himself in the doorway.  

So you do the only thing a captain can do in a sea that wants to swallow the sky. You breathe in, taste the lightning, and start giving orders that sound steadier than you feel.  

**What you do next:**  
- **You point to Reyes**: "Get the mainsail reefed down to the third point. If that canvas catches one more gust, we'll pitchpole." Your voice cuts through the gale because it has to.  
- **You grab Jonah, the greenest sailor, by the shoulder**: "You're with me at the helm. Feel how she fights? Don't wrestle the wheel. Guide her into the trough, then let the wave lift us." His eyes are wide, but he nods.  
- **You roar to the rest**: "Bail teams! Two port, two starboard! The pumps stay running or we become a submarine! Nobody goes over unless the sea asks politely!" A couple of them actually grin at that. Fear breaks different in different people.  

The ship crests again and for half a second you see it through a gap in the clouds: a weird, greenish glow on the horizon, too steady to be lightning. Not a lighthouse. Not land. Something else.  

You make a choice. You turn the wheel two points toward it.  

Reyes catches your eye. She doesn't question it. She just repeats the heading and the crew adjusts. Because storms end, but only if you decide which direction is *out*.  

The *Meridian* takes the next wave bow-first, shudders, and keeps climbing.  

What do you see when you get closer to that glow?
