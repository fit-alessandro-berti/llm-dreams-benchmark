You begin to speak, your voice steady at first—until it starts to echo back to you from the crowd, not in their ears, but in the stage itself. The wooden planks beneath your feet ripple like water, and suddenly, the audience is no longer faces but a sea of mirrors, each one reflecting a different version of yourself: a child, an elder, a stranger with your own eyes. You raise your hands to gesture, but they dissolve into leaves, which drift down and bloom into words mid-air: *"Speak, but listen first."*  

The lights dim, and the stage stretches into a vast library of floating books, their pages fluttering with stories you’ve never written. You realize you’re not just giving a speech—you’re weaving one, each sentence a thread stitching together the silence of a thousand unheard truths. The crowd leans in, not as observers, but as co-authors, their whispers blending into your narrative. You smile, and the room erupts into a constellation of light, each star a name you’ve never heard but always knew.  

When you wake, your notebook is filled with a speech you don’t remember writing—its last line, *"To speak is to plant a forest in the minds of others,"* glowing faintly on the final page.
