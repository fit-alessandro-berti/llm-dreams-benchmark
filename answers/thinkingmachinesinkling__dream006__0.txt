…begin to open your mouth, but instead of sound, a single silver thread unspools from your lips, glinting in the stage lights. You reach up to grab it, and the crowd inhales in unison—not with shock, but with anticipation, as if they’ve been waiting for this exact thread.

You pull gently, and the thread doesn’t end; it pulls you forward, off the stage, into the air. The audience doesn’t move, yet they grow smaller, then larger, then dissolve into thousands of floating lanterns that bob patiently in a darkness that smells like rain and old paper.

You realize you aren’t speaking at all. You are remembering a song that was sung to you before you were born. Your hands become the words—gesturing shapes that hang in the air like glass birds. The lanterns (the crowd) begin to hum back at you, not in judgment, but in recognition. The stage beneath you is now the deck of a ship made of piano keys, sailing through clouds that are actually the pages of every book you ever loved.

Then, with that peculiar certainty dreams allow, you understand: you were never afraid of forgetting your lines. You were afraid of remembering them too well. And now that you finally have, the speech is over, the dream is ending, and you wake with the strange feeling that part of the audience is still listening—quietly, kindly—somewhere just behind your waking eyes.
