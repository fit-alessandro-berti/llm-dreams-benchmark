
**Dream Continuation:**

You wake up in a completely different era, with no modern technology around. As you explore your new surroundings, you realize you need to **learn how to survive using only what the land and its people provide.**  

The air smells of woodsmoke and earth. You’re lying in a haystack behind a small, crooked cottage, its walls made of wattle and daub. A woman in a coarse wool dress glances at you from across the village square, clutching a basket of turnips. Her eyes narrow—not quite fear, but suspicion. *She’s never seen clothes like mine*, you think, staring at your synthetic jacket.  

You stumble to your feet, disoriented. A rooster crows in the distance. The year must be sometime between 1200 and 1400, based on the architecture and the rudimentary tools scattered around. A blacksmith’s hammer rings nearby.  

**Your immediate needs become clear**: shelter, food, and acceptance. But your modern knowledge is a double-edged sword. The woman’s husband, a burly man with soot-stained hands, approaches. He speaks in a dialect you can barely parse, but gestures toward your clothes. “*What sorcery is this?*” he growls, eyeing your zipper.  

You need to act fast. You spot a patch of wild herbs—nettles, which you remember are edible when cooked. You grab some, hoping to trade. The blacksmith’s wife crosses her arms. “*If you’re hungry, work for it*,” she says in broken but understandable English.  

Over the next hour, you manage to communicate that you’re a traveler, a scholar from distant lands. They’re skeptical but intrigued when you use your knowledge of basic metallurgy (from a high school chemistry class) to suggest a better method for tempering steel. The blacksmith, skeptical but desperate to save his faltering business, lets you try.  

Days pass. You earn your keep by inventing small improvements—a pulley system for lifting water, a more efficient plow design. The villagers start calling you “*the outsider who speaks to iron*.” But you’re restless. You overhear whispers of a sorcerer in the nearby forest, a hermit who “*talks to the stars*.”  

One night, you sneak away. The “sorcerer” is an older man with a telescope made of mirrors and lenses—a relic from a later century? He tells you he, too, was “**falling through time**,” decades ago. “*You’re not the first,*” he says, nodding at the constellations. “*To go forward, you must first go deeper.*”  

He hands you a map carved into bark. “*Beneath the roots of the oldest tree, there’s a door. It leads home—but are you ready to leave?*”  

The next morning, the village is in chaos. Raiders are coming. Your modern tactics—feigned retreats, coordinated strikes—turn the tide. The villagers hail you as a hero, but the sorcerer’s words haunt you. Do you stay and build a life here, or chase the mystery of the door?  

**The dream ends with you standing at the edge of the forest, the map clutched in your fist, as the first arrow whizzes past your head—**
