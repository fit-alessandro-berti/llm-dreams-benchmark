You are at a large social gathering where you only know one person. As you try to find them, you encounter various groups of people and realize that each group seems to be celebrating a different version of your life.

One table is filled with childhood friends you have never actually met. They wave you over and speak fondly about summers you don’t remember. In another room, strangers are wearing clothes you used to own, and they insist they have been waiting for you to return. A woman carrying a tray of small glass birds tells you that your friend was just here, but left through a door “that only appears when you stop looking for it.”

You continue through the house. The hallways grow longer, the music becomes softer, and every clock shows a different time. You ask people where the exit is, but they point in opposite directions. Finally, you see your friend standing at the far end of a crowded ballroom.

You call their name, but no sound comes out.

They smile as if they can hear you anyway and begin walking toward you. Between you, the crowd parts—not because anyone moves, but because everyone suddenly becomes still. As your friend reaches you, they place something in your hand: a small key, warm as if it has been held for a long time.

“What does it open?” you ask.

Your voice works now, but your friend is already fading into the music.

“Whatever you were trying not to remember,” they say.

You look down at the key. It is shaped exactly like the front door of your childhood home. When you look up again, the gathering is gone. You are standing alone in front of that door, with morning light shining through the windows—and someone inside is turning the lock.
