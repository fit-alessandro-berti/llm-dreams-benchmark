The air is thick and humid, smelling of wet earth and blooming jasmine—a scent so potent it feels almost edible. You push yourself up from the mossy stone you were sleeping on, your muscles stiff and unfamiliar. The clothes you are wearing are rough-spun linen, dyed a faded indigo, tied at the waist with a simple leather cord. No pockets. No phone. No watch. Just the weight of the fabric and the sudden, sharp realization that your throat is parched.

You stand, brushing off dirt that looks suspiciously like ancient pollen. Around you, towering trees with silver-barked trunks stretch toward a sky that seems too blue, too clear. There are no power lines cutting through the canopy, no distant hum of traffic, no glow of city lights on the horizon. Instead, there is only the sound of wind rustling through leaves and the distant, melodic call of a bird you’ve never heard before.

A path winds ahead, paved not with asphalt, but with smooth, worn stones that seem to have been placed by hands long turned to dust. As you step onto it, you notice something odd: the shadows cast by the trees are moving slightly faster than they should, as if time itself is flowing differently here.

In the distance, nestled in a valley below, you see smoke rising from chimneys made of clay and straw. A village? Or perhaps a monastery? Your stomach rumbles, reminding you that survival here will require more than just curiosity. You need to learn the rules of this place quickly. Who lives there? What year is it? And why does the air taste faintly of ozone, like the moment before a storm?

You take a deep breath, adjust your tunic, and begin to walk down the stone path, leaving the safety of the unknown forest behind for the promise—and peril—of human connection.
