The ship groans beneath my boots, timbers straining against a black, heaving sea. Rain lashes sideways, and lightning splits the sky just long enough for me to see the fear on my crew's faces—Jonas gripping the mast like it's the only solid thing left in the world, Mira fighting the wheel that wants to spin free.

"Captain! We're taking water in the hold!" someone shouts, barely audible over the wind.

I grab the rigging to keep my footing and look at them—really look. In dreams, I've learned, the storm is rarely just a storm.

"Listen to me!" I call out, and my voice somehow carries, steady in a way I don't quite feel. "This ship has crossed worse water than this. So have you. Mira, hold that wheel—don't fight the waves, ride them at an angle, forty-five degrees to the swell! Jonas, get below with Tomas and start bailing, two-hour shifts, no heroics, no drowning yourselves for the cargo!"

They move. That's the strange grace of dreams—conviction becomes current, and suddenly everyone has a task, a rhythm, something to hold besides fear.

I take the wheel myself when Mira's arms give out, and I feel the ship listen to me, the way dream-things do, half-real and half-following the shape of my intention. The waves are still enormous, black glass mountains lit silver by the moon breaking through cloud-seams, but we are still afloat, still moving forward through them rather than being swallowed.

Then, ahead—impossibly—a break in the storm. A circle of calm water, unnaturally still, moonlight pouring through it like a spotlight.

"Steer for the light!" I shout, and the crew, soaked and exhausted, look up and see it too, and something like hope moves through them like electricity.

We break through the wall of rain into that circle of calm, and the silence afterward is so complete I can hear my own heartbeat, and the creak of the ship settling, and someone—Jonas, I think—laughing in relief, disbelieving.
