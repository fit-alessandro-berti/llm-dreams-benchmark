You start to walk, then hurry, then run—but every street sign is written in a language that rearranges itself when you look directly at it.

The city is impossibly tall. Glass towers lean over narrow streets like curious strangers. Trams glide past without tracks. People move around you with purpose, all dressed for meetings of their own, carrying briefcases, flowers, clocks, bowls of soup, tiny sleeping dogs. You stop one woman and ask where you’re supposed to be.

She smiles kindly and says, “You’re already late for the beginning.”

Before you can ask what that means, she steps into a revolving door that spins once and becomes a fountain.

You check your pockets. No phone. No wallet. Only a folded invitation, damp at the edges. When you open it, the words are blurred except for one line:

**Room 19, above the rain.**

You look up. The sky is full of buildings hanging upside down, their windows glowing gold. Rain falls upward from the street toward them.

A red bus pulls up beside you with no driver. Its doors sigh open. On the front, instead of a destination, it says:

**IMPORTANT MEETING**

So you get on.

Inside, the bus is much larger than outside. Rows of seats stretch into a dim distance. Passengers whisper over papers, but when you glance at them, you realize every face is familiar: an old teacher, a childhood friend, someone you once loved, yourself at twelve years old, yourself much older.

The younger you pats the seat beside them.

“You forgot the map again,” they say.

“I never had a map,” you reply.

“That’s why you keep coming here.”

The bus lurches forward, not down the street but upward, climbing the rising rain. The city falls away beneath you. Windows flash past: kitchens, offices, empty bedrooms, oceans, a forest at night. In one window, you see yourself asleep.

At last the bus stops at a narrow platform suspended in the clouds. A brass plaque reads:

**ROOM 19**

There is no room, only a door standing alone.

You knock.

A voice from the other side says, “Come in. We’ve been waiting.”

You open the door and step into a conference room filled with sunlight. At the long table sit all the people you were afraid to disappoint. They look up from their papers, but no one seems angry.

At the head of the table is an empty chair with your name on it.

You sit down.

Someone slides a document toward you. At the top it says:

**THE THING YOU WERE SUPPOSED TO REMEMBER**

Your hands tremble as you turn the page.

It is blank.

Everyone waits.

Then, slowly, you understand: the meeting was not to receive instructions. It was to give them.

So you pick up the pen.

Outside the windows, the upside-down city begins to turn right-side up. The rain pauses in midair. Every clock in the room stops ticking.

You write one sentence.

You wake before you can read it.
