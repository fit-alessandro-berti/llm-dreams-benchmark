Another, a young man with paint-stained fingers, pulls out a crumpled map covered in doodles and coffee rings, insisting that the real way home isn't on any map. A child tugs at your sleeve, pointing toward a narrow alley you hadn't noticed before, where the bricks seem to hum with a faint, melodic vibration.

You follow them through streets that twist in ways that shouldn't be possible, past shop windows displaying your own childhood memories, past a café where the smell of your grandmother's cooking drifts out unbidden. The city noise softens to a distant murmur, replaced by footsteps and quiet laughter.

When you finally stop in a small courtyard you've never seen, a door appears that feels impossibly familiar—though you can't remember why. The strangers step back, smiling, and you realize with a start that their faces have become *your* face, each one a different version of yourself from a life you haven't lived yet.

The door opens onto a room full of light, and a voice—yours, but older, wiser—whispers:

*"You were never really lost. You were just on your way."*

You wake with tears on your cheeks and a strange, unshakeable peace in your heart, as though some part of you has finally come home.
