But then something shifts.

I look out at the crowd and realize I recognize no one — and they don't seem to be waiting for facts or figures. Their expressions aren't impatient. They look *curious*, the way a child watches a caterpillar decide which direction to crawl.

So I set the notes down.

"I had something prepared," I say, and the microphone catches it in that strange, slightly-too-loud way dreams amplify every sound. "But I think what I actually want to know is — why are *you* all here?"

And the audience begins to answer. Not in words exactly, but in light — warm amber light that rises from their seats like fireflies lifting off a meadow at dusk. The room fills with it. The stage becomes unnecessary because I'm suddenly at the same level as everyone else, standing in a field of quiet voices that sound like rain on old windows.

I wake up with the sensation that I was about to learn something important — the kind of dream-knowledge that dissolves the moment you try to hold it. But the feeling remains. Something about speaking being less about performing and more about *listening*.
