...and you open your mouth to explain that the timeline is unreasonable, that the brief is half a page of contradictions, but the words come out as a language you’ve never spoken yet somehow understand perfectly.

Your boss nods slowly, though you’re sure you didn’t actually say anything coherent. Their eyes are now the color of highlighter yellow, and the fluorescent lights above have begun humming a melody you recognize from a song that doesn’t exist yet. The project folder on your desk is breathing—just slightly, its manila edges rising and falling like the chest of something asleep.

You sit down. The chair is made of fog and held together by your own concentration.

The deadline, your boss says, or perhaps only mouths, is *yesterday*. You look at the clock and see that the hands are running backward, which should be comforting but somehow means you have even less time. When you open the folder, the pages are blank, then full, then blank again, flickering like a dying bulb. Each time the text appears, it is written in your own handwriting but from an angle, as if you were leaning over your own shoulder to forge it.

Somewhere down the hall, a printer begins weeping toner.

You start typing. The keyboard has too many keys and not enough letters, but your fingers know what to do. The report writes itself, then unwrites itself, then writes something better—something that is not a report at all but a map. The map shows your workplace, but the hallways keep folding into each other, and your cubicle is located inside the break room, which is located inside the boss’s office, which is located inside your own chest.

Your boss is still standing there. They are waiting. They are always waiting. They hand you a coffee you did not ask for, and the cup is empty, and the empty cup is somehow full of the sound of rain.

“You’re doing fine,” they say, though their voice is now coming from the potted plant behind you.

You keep working. The deadline is still yesterday, but you are getting closer to it. Outside the window, the sun sets seventeen times in rapid succession, each sunset a slightly different shade of apology.

And then, just as the final page slides out of the breathing folder, perfectly formatted and impossibly complete, you realize with a jolt that you are not wearing pants.

The boss smiles. In dreams, they always smile right before you wake up.
