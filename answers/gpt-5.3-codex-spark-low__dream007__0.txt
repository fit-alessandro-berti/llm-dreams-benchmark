• You decide to stop, take a breath, and step away from the argument—toward the old fountain in the square where you
  used to meet as kids. The voices from your friend trail off behind you, still sharp, still sure they’re right. You
  kneel by the cold stone rim and trace your fingers along chipped initials in the weathered marble. You remember
  another summer, another version of this same conversation, and realize this fight is not about the thing at the center
  of it at all; it’s about fear, and both of you are defending different versions of the same hurt.

  You turn back with a steadier voice.

  “I can’t keep doing this by trying to win,” you say. “Tell me one thing you’re really afraid of.”

  For a moment your friend looks stunned, like they expected another counterargument. Then they hesitate, and something
  cracks open. The anger drains into something raw and human. You tell them your fear too. No more clever speeches. No
  more old scores. Just the two of you, exhausted, trying to protect each other and failing at it.

  The square grows quiet. The streetlight flickers on. In the distance, rain starts to fall—soft at first, then steady.
  You stay there until the storm passes, and by the time it clears, you both know the argument hasn’t ended, but the
  distance between you has.
