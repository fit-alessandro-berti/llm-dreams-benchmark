...despite the hardships, you decide to rebuild the sky. 

The air is thick with the scent of ozone and calcified dust, and the horizon is a jagged line of shattered skyscrapers, like broken teeth biting into a bruised, violet twilight. Water is scarce, and the shadows here have a habit of stretching toward you when you aren't looking. But survival isn't enough anymore. You need to see the stars again.

In your hand, you hold a cracked glass sphere, no larger than a pocket watch. Inside it, a tiny, bioluminescent nebula swirls—a dormant fragment of old-world technology, or perhaps just a piece of the cosmos you managed to scoop up before the light went out.

You begin to climb the skeletal remains of the Great Transmitter, a rusted iron tower that looms over the ruins. The wind howls through the hollow girders, singing in a language that sounds like radio static and old lullabies. With every step upward, gravity seems to lose its grip. The heavy, ash-coated boots you wear feel lighter, until you are practically floating, pulling yourself up by cold metal rungs.

Below you, the ruined world stretches out, vast and silent. But as you climb higher, you see them: tiny, flickering campfires in the dark. Other survivors. They are huddled in the wreckage, hiding from the cold and the things that hunt in the dark. 

You reach the summit, where the wind is so fierce it feels like a physical hand pushing against your chest. The air is freezing, but your hands are warm. 

You place the glass sphere into the rusted copper housing of the ancient satellite dish. It fits perfectly, with a soft, metallic *click*. 

You whisper a password you didn’t know you remembered. 

The sphere shatters. 

Instead of falling, the liquid starlight inside pours upward, defying gravity. It shoots into the dark atmosphere like a reverse lightning bolt, branching out in brilliant veins of sapphire and gold. The bruised violet sky begins to fracture and peel away, replaced by a deep, velvet blackness dotted with a billion sparkling, swirling constellations. 

Down in the ruins, the campfires go quiet. You can feel, even from this height, thousands of faces turning upward, washed in the gentle, ancient blue light. The world is still broken, and tomorrow the hunger and the dangers will return. But tonight, the dark is no longer empty.

As a warm, impossible rain begins to fall from the newborn stars, the tower beneath you starts to dissolve into mist, and you feel yourself drifting upward, lighter than air, finally ready to wake up.
