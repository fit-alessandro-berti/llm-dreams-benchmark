• Despite the hardships, you decide to keep moving.

  You strip the city of its names and follow the rusted train tracks north, where the maps still show a broken line of
  green between three dead rivers. Every few hours you trade, bargain, or beg for water, and every night you count your
  supplies like prayers. A broken radio crackles with old weather alerts and static, but once in a while a voice leaks
  through—someone else is alive, someone else is searching too.

  You pass what was once a school and now is a fortress of scavengers; you don’t enter. You mark their fire with ash on
  a cracked sign and keep walking.

  By dusk, the wind shifts and brings the smell of ozone and smoke. In the distance, under a collapsed billboard, you
  spot a child’s jacket snagged on a chain-link fence—too clean to be long abandoned. You climb and pull it down. In the
  pocket is a folded map with a single word circled in red marker: Wellspring.

  No one knows if it’s real anymore. Could be a trap, could be a lie, could be the last place with working pumps and
  clean water.

  You sleep on top of an overturned bus, jacket over your face, and dream of flowing water for the first time in years.

  At dawn, you choose the unmarked road and start toward the mark.
