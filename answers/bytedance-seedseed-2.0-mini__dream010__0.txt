You are participating in a competition where the grand prize is your dream job. As the competition progresses, you face various challenges and soon find yourself standing in the final round, the only contestant who didn’t lean on pre-planned portfolio pieces: first, you’d aced a live story-editing sprint where you reworked a flat fairy tale to center a disabled protagonist who used a wheelchair, then you’d navigated a chaotic team-building puzzle where you rallied a group of frazzled strangers to build a functioning miniature library out of popsicle sticks and hot glue.

The final challenge is unveiled with a dramatic sweep of the host’s arms: you must create a full, self-contained 8-page picture book spread in 90 minutes, using only the odds and ends scattered across the table before you: torn vintage book pages, crushed crayons, sticker sheets of misprinted forest creatures, a roll of wrinkled tracing paper, and a single half-empty bottle of metallic gold paint. Worse, the story must be rooted in a memory that made you fall in love with storytelling in the first place—something you’ve never shared with anyone outside your own head.

Panic pricks at your throat for a split second. You scan the table: a crumpled seed packet peeks out under a stack of old poetry clippings, its faded ink reading “Tomato Tiny Tim” — a relic from your late grandmother’s garden, who taught you to draw stories on scrap paper when you were six, back when you’d sit cross-legged on her porch and turn her seed packets into tales of talking vegetables. You grab the seed packet first, carefully peeling it free and gluing it to the top of a sheet of tracing paper. Next, you tear out a page from a discarded kids’ book about fireflies, and use a crushed crimson crayon to draw a little girl with braids tied dandelion fluff, kneeling in a patch of tomato plants.

Halfway through your 90 minutes, the overhead stage lights flicker and die, leaving only a single handheld spotlight that a flustered stagehand rushes over to hand you. You hold up each page one by one as you finish them: the girl finding a tomato that glows with tiny handwritten words, the tomato turning into a tiny boat that carries her through a field of sunflowers that hum lullabies, the girl bringing the glowing words back to her grandma’s empty garden chair. When you set down your last crayon, the timer dings, and the audience erupts in quiet, reverent claps.

When the judges step forward, your hands are shaking so bad you can barely keep yourself upright. Ms. Marisol Cruz, your favorite childhood author and the head judge, picks up your final spread—the girl tucking a handful of glowing tomato seeds into the soil, promising to tell their stories every year. “You didn’t just draw a picture book,” she says, her voice thick with emotion. “You told us exactly why you deserve this job.”

As she hands you the official contract for your dream role: staff writer and illustrator at Pebble & Page, the publishing house that put out your favorite childhood books, the stage shifts beneath your feet. Instead of the competition’s sterile event space, you’re suddenly standing on your grandmother’s old porch, the scent of tomato plants thick in the air. She’s there, leaning against the railing, holding a jar of fireflies that glow with the same handwritten words from your illustrations. She smiles and tosses a handful of tomato seeds into the air, and they sprout into tiny, glowing plants before your eyes.

You reach out to grab her hand, and just as your fingers brush hers, a sharp buzz cuts through the dream—your phone alarm, blaring for your morning shift at the local coffee shop. You jolt awake, staring at your bedroom ceiling, the faint outline of a glowing tomato seed still stuck to the edge of your notebook where you’d doodled in the middle of the night last week.
