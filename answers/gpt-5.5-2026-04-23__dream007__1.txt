…step outside before either of you says something that can’t be taken back.

The door closes behind you with a soft click, though you don’t remember opening it. Suddenly you’re not in the room anymore but standing in the middle of an empty train station at dusk. The argument is still ringing in your ears, but now it sounds distant, like it’s being spoken underwater.

Your friend is there too, on the opposite platform.

Neither of you says anything at first.

A train rushes between you, silver and impossibly long, each window flashing with tiny scenes from your friendship: laughing so hard you couldn’t breathe, sitting together in silence after bad news, sharing food, sharing secrets, sharing the kinds of fears neither of you would admit to anyone else.

When the train finally passes, your friend is closer.

They look tired, not angry.

You try to explain yourself, but when you open your mouth, a handful of small paper birds comes out instead. They flutter between you, each one carrying a sentence you meant to say but couldn’t: *I was scared.* *I thought you didn’t hear me.* *I didn’t want to lose this.* *I’m sorry I made it about winning.*

Your friend catches one gently.

Then they open their hand, and from their palm rises a paper bird of their own.

It lands on your shoulder.

You unfold it.

It says: *Me too.*

The station lights flicker on, one by one, warm and golden. Somewhere, a voice announces a train arriving for a destination you can’t quite understand, but it sounds like forgiveness.

You and your friend sit together on the platform edge, feet dangling over tracks that have become a quiet river. The argument doesn’t disappear, but it changes shape. It becomes something you can hold together instead of throw at each other.

After a while, your friend nudges your shoulder.

“Do we still have to be right?” they ask.

You look down at your reflection in the river tracks. For a moment, your face is yours; then it is theirs; then it is both.

“No,” you say. “I think we just have to listen.”

The train arrives without making a sound.

And when the doors open, you both step inside.
