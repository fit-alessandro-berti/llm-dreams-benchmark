You are at your workplace, and suddenly, you are given a project with an impossible deadline. Your boss is waiting for you to start, and you notice the stack of papers in their outstretched hands isn’t standard office stationery at all—instead of crisp white printer paper, each sheet is thin, crinkled dried lavender, edged with faded purple petals that smell like summer afternoons in your grandma’s garden. When you reach to take them, your shadow detaches itself from your ankles and slithers over to curl around your boss’s wrist like a sleepy house cat; they don’t so much as glance at it, their voice a low, steady hum that matches the distant whir of the breakroom fridge. “Due at closing,” they say, tapping the top sheet twice with a knuckle that glows faintly green. “No exceptions.”

Your desk, which moments ago was piled with half-finished spreadsheets and a half-empty cold coffee mug, has shifted overnight: every surface is dotted with folded paper cranes, each bearing a tiny handwritten date scrawled on its wing—all days before today, all fluttering softly in a warm breeze that shouldn’t be leaking out of the sealed office AC vents. When you grab for the pen tucked behind your ear, it zips away into a flash of glowing green light, revealing itself to be a firefly that leaves a trail of stardust on your fingertips.

The wall clock above your boss’s head spins backward so fast its hands blur into a silver ring, but the tiny closing-time stamp on the lavender project sheet counts down in bold, charcoal smudges: 3 hours, 12 minutes, 47 seconds. You reach for your work laptop, but the screen flickers to life showing not your email inbox, but a live feed of your childhood backyard—there you are, 10 years old, stacking pine needles and lavender sprigs into a lopsided fort, grinning like you know a secret you’ve forgotten.

Your coworker Marnie, who’s been out with a broken ankle all week, rounds the cubicle wall, her head replaced by a plump echeveria succulent with a daisy tucked into its rosette like a hair clip. She hands you a tiny metal trowel instead of a stapler, her voice soft and chipmunk-soft: “You can’t hurry fort-building. You’ll have to dig up the hours somewhere else.”

Before you can ask what she means, the office fire alarm blares—not its usual shrill beep, but the opening verse of your grandma’s lullaby, the one you haven’t heard since you were a kid. A swarm of fireflies pours out of the overhead fluorescent lights, and the stack of lavender project sheets unfurls like a butterfly, revealing the “project” wasn’t a work assignment at all: it was a list of all the small, easy, joyful things you’ve been pushing off for months: write a letter to your grandma, spend an afternoon building forts with your niece, learn to fold paper cranes properly.

The closing-time stamp vanishes from the lavender sheet, and your boss walks over, their sharp, glowing deer eyes softening, and presses a perfectly folded paper crane into your palm—stamped with your actual signature, even though you never signed it. “Closing time was when you stopped trying to rush it all,” they say, and then they melt into a pile of the dried lavender sprigs, drifting out through the cracked window to join the fireflies.

Your desk snaps back to normal in an instant: the cranes are gone, the laptop screen is back to your spreadsheets, and the coffee mug is still half-full of lukewarm brew. Your phone buzzes with a text from your sister: “Wanna build forts this weekend? I dug up some lavender sprigs from grandma’s garden.” When you glance at the wall clock, it’s exactly 5 PM—closing time, just like the impossible deadline said. For a second, your shadow curls around your wrist, warm and familiar, before slipping back into place against your ankle.
