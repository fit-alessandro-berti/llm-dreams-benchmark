You find yourself in a post-apocalyptic world, with resources scarce and dangers everywhere. Despite the hardships, you decide to seek out other survivors, not just for safety, but to rebuild something meaningful from the ashes. 

The sky is a permanent bruised twilight, filtered through layers of dust. You move through the skeletal remains of a city, your steps careful on cracked concrete. In a collapsed bookstore, you find not just pages to burn, but a child's drawing—a sun, a house, a family—pinned under a heavy shard of glass. It feels like a message.

Following a faded sign promising "Community Garden," you discover a walled courtyard miraculously intact. Inside, green things struggle upward in neat rows. A figure looks up from their work—weathered, wary, but alive. They don't raise a weapon. They simply ask, "Do you know how to fix a water pump?"

In this dream, your purpose crystallizes: not merely to survive, but to tend, to repair, to connect. The danger is real, but so is the stubborn, quiet resilience taking root in this broken place.
