...symbols carved into its surface — not carved, exactly. They move. Slowly, the way frost moves across a window if you look away and back again. I understand, in the effortless way you understand things while dreaming, that the patterns are a language, and that I used to be fluent in it.

The box is warm on one side and cold on the other, and when I turn it over, the warmth follows my hand.

There is no keyhole. I check five or six times, and each time the surface is smooth, but I keep expecting one, the way you keep expecting a step at the bottom of a staircase. Eventually I find a seam so fine that it might only be the memory of a seam.

I try the obvious things. I shake it, and hear nothing — a very specific nothing, a listening nothing. I ask it to open, politely, then less politely. I look for a hinge and find a small brass hinge on the inside of my own wrist, which seems important but which I decide not to examine.

Then someone is standing beside me. In the dream she is my grandmother and also a librarian I have never met and also, faintly, the box itself. She says, *You've been holding it wrong.*

So I stop holding it. I set it on the ground — there is a ground now, tiled, the tiles slightly wet — and I sit down across from it, and I wait.

And this is the part where the dream becomes very quiet.

The lid does not open. Instead the box becomes gradually transparent, the way ice does when it thins, and inside there is: a room. My childhood bedroom, with the lamp on, seen from very high up, as though through the wrong end of a telescope. And someone small is in the bed, awake, waiting for someone to come and say it's alright.

I try to reach in. My hand goes through the lid like water through water, but the room is farther away than the box is deep, and my arm is not long enough, and I know it never will be. The distance isn't a distance you cross with an arm.

*It isn't locked,* the grandmother-librarian says. *It's just far.*

And I say — and in the dream this feels like the cleverest thing anyone has ever said — *Then I'll wait here until it comes closer.*

She sits down too. The tiles are cold. The box glows faintly, like a house with one window lit, and we watch it the way you'd watch weather.

I wake up with my hands cupped in front of me, holding nothing, very carefully.
