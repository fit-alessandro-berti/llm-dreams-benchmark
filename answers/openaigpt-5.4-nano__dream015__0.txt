Despite the hardships, you decide to **keep moving toward other people instead of away from them**.

You’ve learned the rules the hard way—don’t trust voices in the dark, don’t drink from cups that have been touched by strangers, and don’t camp where the wind carries smoke in circles like it’s signaling. But tonight the wind carries something else: not smoke, not rot, not the metallic tang of old fires. It carries the faint, almost impossible sound of **laughter**—thin and distant, like someone has decided the world is still allowed to be kind.

So you follow it.

At first it’s just a rhythm you can’t place: a laugh that breaks, a cough that tries to hide it, then silence. Then you see the source—an overpass lit by scavenged panels of solar glass, trembling in the night like a constellation someone swept down to earth. The area beneath it is crowded but not chaotic. People are moving with purpose, the way you do when everyone has agreed—without ever saying it out loud—that stealing won’t be the default.

A woman sits at a table hammered from a truck bed. Her hands are stained with soot, but her smile is clean. In front of her is a stack of paper—real paper, not torn plastic—edges curled like it survived a hundred storms. She looks up as if she expected you.

“You’re late,” she says, and it doesn’t sound like a threat.

You should ask how she knows you’re coming. You should explain why you’re here. Instead your mouth does what your body has been training to do: it tells the truth, simple and plain.

“I heard laughter,” you say. “And I wanted to see if it was real.”

For a moment her face softens, almost imperceptibly, the way a lock gives when you finally match the key.

“It is,” she replies. “But it’s expensive. Comes with rules.”

She gestures to a sign someone painted in thick, careful strokes:

**TRADE WHAT YOU CAN. SHARE WHAT YOU KNOW.**

Underneath, another line in smaller letters:

**NO ONE EATS ALONE.**

A man near the cooking pot—what passes for a pot, anyway, a salvaged boiler with a cracked lid—nods at you. He has a scar that looks like a river on his cheek and eyes that look tired in the way only survival makes possible.

“You can stay,” he says. “If you help. We’re low on—” he checks a list scratched into a piece of metal—“filters, antibiotics, and nails. Also batteries. And someone who can listen when the bad sounds start.”

You realize you’ve been hearing bad sounds all evening, faint at first: a distant clicking like something counting steps. A low vibration in the ground that makes your teeth want to clench.

You could pretend you didn’t notice. That would be safer, the sensible thing. But you came because you promised yourself you’d stop walking alone in your own life.

So you crouch near the edge of the lit area and you listen.

The sound comes again: clicking, closer now. Not random. Not hungry, either. It’s patient. Like a metronome that knows exactly how long it can wait.

The woman at the table moves beside you, quiet as dust.

“See?” she says. “They’re out there.”

“They?” you ask.

“Not people,” she answers. “We used to call them raiders. Then we realized they don’t raid. They just… find.”

Your skin prickles. The world beyond the light feels thinner, like paper someone is tearing carefully along a seam.

“What are they looking for?” you whisper.

She doesn’t answer right away. She taps the list of supplies with a knuckle, once, twice, as if it’s the only rhythm that keeps her from being pulled under.

“Us,” she finally says. “Or whatever we’ve been trying to replace in ourselves. Sometimes it’s food. Sometimes it’s a voice. Sometimes it’s hope.”

You swallow. Hope feels like a dangerous thing to keep carrying. Like water in a cracked canteen.

But then a child—barefoot, hair braided with wire, face smudged with soot and joy—runs past holding something small and bright. A jar, no bigger than your hand. Inside it, a piece of metal is balanced on a filament, humming softly as if it’s alive.

“What’s that?” you ask.

“Wind,” the child announces, as if wind is something you can bottle and trade.

You lean closer. The humming vibrates through your bones. It’s not loud, but it makes the darkness feel less certain.

You understand. The laughter isn’t because things are good. It’s because they’re building a future with whatever’s left—filters from old breath masks, antibiotics from scraped moss, light from broken things.

And you realize you can contribute without pretending you’re a hero. You can do something small that keeps everyone moving forward.

You reach into your pack and pull out the one thing you’ve been saving because it seemed too precious to use: a pouch of **fine sand and a strange coil of copper**. You found it weeks ago near the ruins of a power station. You didn’t know what it was for, only that it might become useful if you learned the right way to see it.

You lay it on the table like an offering.

“I can make a better filter,” you say. “Not perfect. But it’ll buy you time.”

The woman’s smile widens, and for the first time you see the exhaustion behind her expression—hope isn’t effortless. It’s labor.

“Then make it,” she says. “Before the clicking gets bored.”

You work as the night tightens.

You scrape corrosion from the coil until it shines dully, thread the copper into a makeshift housing, pack the sand where it needs to go, and check the seal twice because you’ve learned seals are the difference between breathing and dying. Your hands move with a focus that feels like prayer. Around you, others don’t just watch—they contribute. Someone hands you cloth for wiping. Someone brings a thermometer made from a broken watch face. Someone laughs quietly when you curse because your hands tremble once and you swear it won’t happen again.

The clicking grows louder. The vibration in the ground becomes a heartbeat you can feel.

Then it stops.

Not because whatever’s out there leaves—dreams don’t work like that. It stops because it’s listening too.

When you finish, the filter hums like the small jar humming in the child’s hands. Clear air. Not perfect, but enough. Enough to breathe without tasting the world’s rot.

The woman takes it from you carefully, like it’s a fragile animal.

“You did it,” she says.

You exhale and realize you didn’t know you’d been holding your breath.

But the night isn’t over.

The lights flicker—just once—then steadied again as the wind jar’s filament burns brighter. Outside the circle of light, shadow gathers where it shouldn’t. You feel the presence, not as a body but as attention.

A voice whispers from nowhere, thin and wrong, in a language you don’t recognize but somehow understand anyway.

**FOUND.**

The woman places a hand on your shoulder. Her grip is warm, human. Real.

“We don’t run,” she says.

You look at her, startled.

“We can,” you argue. “We could hide. Hide is safer.”

“Hide is temporary,” she counters. “We’re not built to be temporary.”

Then she turns and signals to the group. People move, not in panic but in order. Tools are gathered. Doors are reinforced. Someone sets up a false light down the road to lure the attention away. Someone else arranges supplies in patterns—geometrics you’d normally dismiss as decoration until you remember that in a world like this, decoration is often survival.

And you realize what you decided when you stepped out of your loneliness: you decided **to become part of the reason laughter survives**.

So you help.

You stand at the edge of the light with a repaired filter in your hands and a coil of copper still warm from your work. When the clicking starts again, closer now, you don’t flinch away into fear. You face it with the steadiness you’ve earned.

You shout—not a threat, not a command, but a call for everyone to hear you.

“Together!” you say. “Now!”

The group responds instantly. The lights flare. The false signal burns. The clicking veers, distracted—then hunts again, uncertain. Something about your unity changes the math.

In the dream, that’s what matters: not defeating the darkness forever, but refusing to let it define you.

And as the night stretches and the laughter returns, thicker this time, you finally understand the completion of your decision:

You decide to **build a life where people don’t have to disappear to stay alive**.

You wake with your hands smelling faintly of copper and sand, as if the world inside the dream has left you a residue of courage you can carry into morning.
