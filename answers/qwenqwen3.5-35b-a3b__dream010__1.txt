You are participating in a competition where the grand prize is your dream job. As the competition progresses, you face various challenges and find that the landscape around you shifts with your breathing.

The first challenge is a bridge made of glass, spanning a canyon that doesn't have a bottom. As you step forward, your reflection in the glass beneath your feet begins to walk backward. You learn quickly: do not look down. Focus on the horizon. Once you cross, the bridge dissolves into water, and you are floating, not falling.

The second challenge awaits you in a room filled with ticking clocks. Every minute lost is a year of your life subtracted. You don't have to speed up or stop the time. You simply have to realize you can't measure your worth in hours. When you stop looking at the faces on the dials and close your eyes, the ticking stops. The room opens into a garden where flowers bloom into brief, glowing documents with your name on them. You pick the ones that feel warm.

Finally, you arrive at the summit. There is no podium, no judges. There is only a mirror. Inside the reflection, the interviewer is sitting there, but it's you. It looks tired, hopeful, and exactly where it needs to be.

"I have to ask you one question," the reflection says. Its voice is not your own, but it sounds like the sound of your own heart. "Is this job what you want, or is this what you think you deserve?"

You pause. In the dream, you feel the weight of the title, the benefits, the salary, the prestige. Then you feel the weight of the work, the passion, the struggle, and the art. You realize the competition wasn't a test of skill. It was a test of alignment.

You step toward the mirror. You don't break it. Instead, you reach out and touch your reflection's forehead. The glass warms and shatters into dust, but the dust reforms into a pen and a blank page.

The reflection smiles. "Congratulations," it whispers, and the world begins to dissolve.

You wake up gasping, the silence of your bedroom heavy and real. The dream job hasn't been given to you; the competition was the rehearsal. You sit up, grab your notebook, and turn to the first page. The dream was right. You have the job. You have the pen. Now, you just have to write the first sentence.
