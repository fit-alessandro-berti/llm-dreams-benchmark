You find yourself in a post-apocalyptic world, with resources scarce and dangers everywhere. Despite the hardships, you decide to trek 12 miles across the cracked, kudzu-choked interstate to the only place your little sister Lila—bedridden with a fever that’s clung to her for three weeks—has babbled about nonstop: the old pink candy shop at the edge of the next town, with a spinning neon cherry lollipop sign she swears is a “falling star that stuck to the roof just to be sweet.”

It’s a dream, so the world warps in soft, uncanny ways that only feel normal while you’re inside it: the sun bleeds tangerine watercolor over the horizon, dandelions pushing through the asphalt glow faint blue, and the worst dangers aren’t roving raiders or mutated beasts—they’re static wraiths, shadowy figures that leak fizzing 90s TV static, that hunt anyone carrying warm, sharp memories. They can smell Lila’s crayon drawing of the lollipop tucked in your jacket pocket, can hear the lullaby you hum under your breath to steady your nerves. You hide in the rotted shell of an elementary school bus while one drifts past, watching it sniff a tattered stuffed rabbit left on the asphalt, lose interest in its faded memories, and melt back into the treeline.

When you finally reach the town, the candy shop isn’t the crumbled ruin you expected. It’s whole, its screen door squeaking when you push it open, the bell above the frame jingling exactly how you remembered it did when you were 10 and snuck in with allowance for sour straws. Behind the counter, your grandma—who died ten years before the apocalypse even began—wipes a smudge off a jar of lemon drops, like she’s been waiting for you. “Knew you’d make it,” she says, nodding toward the beaded curtain strung across the back room. “Lila’s been impatient.”

You yank the beads aside, and there’s your sister, barefoot, chasing a monarch butterfly across a sun-dappled wooden floor, her fever gone, her cheeks pink and sticky with cotton candy. She skids to a halt when she sees you, holding up a giant cherry lollipop that glows the exact same pink-red as the spinning sign outside. “Told you the star would wait for us,” she says, running to throw her arms around your waist.

As you hug her, the edges of the candy shop start to blur, like a polaroid left out in the rain. You catch a glimpse of your own couch in the real world, your laptop propped on the armrest still looping a forgotten 90s candy commercial that seeped into your nap, the sound of your actual phone pinging to wake you up. You realize the post-apocalyptic wasteland was just your mind weaving all the small, sad, soft things you’d been carrying lately—your grandma’s death, the time you missed with your sister when she’d had the flu last month, the way you’d forgotten to stop chasing small, sweet things just to survive the grind of everyday life. For a second, you hold onto the dream a little longer, the lollipop’s glow warming your palms, before you blink awake, already reaching for your keys to go pick up her favorite candy on your way over to visit.
