You decide to **stop shouting, turn your back on the room, and walk into the wall behind you.**

You expect to hit drywall. Instead, your palm meets the cool, vibrating surface of a piano key. You press it, and the wall dissolves into a cascade of silver musical notes that spiral upward, pulling you with them. You aren't walking; you are falling, but the air is thick like syrup, slowing your descent.

As you drift downward, the argument echoes in your head, but the words change. Instead of anger, the friend’s voice comes through calm and melodic, singing the lines of the disagreement as if they are a lullaby. You look back to find them standing on a cloud formation below the floorboards of the house. They aren't shouting anymore. They are holding out a hand, offering you a key that looks like a frozen tear.

"Does it matter?" they ask, their face shifting between different people you love, then different people you know.

The house around you begins to crumble not into rubble, but into origami folds. The walls become paper airplanes, the furniture dissolves into ink blots. The conflict about the future, about the job, about who was right—it all feels suddenly fragile, like a sketch on a piece of tissue paper.

You reach for the frozen tear key. As your fingers touch it, it melts into a cold droplet of water that drips down your wrist. You realize, with a jolt of clarity that feels like a shiver, that the argument was never about the thing itself, but about the silence between you.

The sensation of falling vanishes instantly.

You are not in the house. You are sitting upright in your own bed, the morning light gray and unfamiliar. The air is silent. The memory of your friend’s face is fading, but the feeling of the frozen tear on your wrist remains, real and cold. You look at the clock; the time is wrong. It's not the morning you expected. The dream is over, but the silence in the room feels heavy, as if you are waiting for someone to knock on the bedroom door that you are suddenly afraid to open.
