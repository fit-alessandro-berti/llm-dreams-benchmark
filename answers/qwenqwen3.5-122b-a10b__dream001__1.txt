You start to run, but the cobblestones ripple like dark water beneath your shoes, slowing your pace without dampening your urgency. The streetlamps overhead flicker in a rhythm that matches your heartbeat, casting long, stretching shadows that seem to detach and point the way forward.

You don't need a map. You realize, with a sudden calmness, that the city knows where you need to go. The buildings breathe; their windows blink like eyes. You turn a corner and the street stretches out, not linearly, but spiraling upward like a staircase that defies gravity. You climb, but you are not tired. The air grows sweeter, smelling of old books and rain on hot asphalt.

An hour passes in the time it takes to take a single step.

You reach a door floating in the center of a vast, open plaza. It is made of polished wood, glowing with a soft, amber light. There is no handle, only a keyhole shaped like a question mark. You reach into your pocket and pull out a key you didn't know you were carrying—it is warm, heavy, and made of glass.

You insert it. The door opens not into a room, but into a mirror. Standing on the other side is not a stranger, but a version of yourself wearing a suit made of starlight. They smile, look at their watch, and say, "You're right on time. We've been waiting for you to wake up."

The city dissolves into light, and you feel the pull of the sheets, the weight of your own body, and the lingering echo of a clock ticking in the dark.
