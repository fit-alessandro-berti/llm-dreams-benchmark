You find yourself in a post-apocalyptic world, with resources scarce and dangers everywhere. Despite the hardships, you decide to plant a garden.

Not just any garden—a quiet, defiant act of hope. You sift through the ash-choked earth with calloused hands, digging past rusted car frames and the skeletons of streetlights, until you find soil that still breathes. You carry seeds in a tin can you found in the pocket of a dead librarian’s coat—sunflower, tomato, basil, and something labeled only “Moon Petal,” its origin unknown.

Every night, you water them with precious drops boiled clean in a dented kettle. You sing to them—not lullabies, but fragments of poems you once memorized in school. The wind howls through broken skyscrapers, and shadow-stalkers prowl the ruins, but here, beneath the fractured dome of a collapsed greenhouse, life pushes upward.

One morning, the first sunflower breaks through. Its golden face turns to the pale, washed-out sun.

And then—you hear it.

A whisper.

Not from the wind.

From the seedling.

*“Thank you.”*

You freeze. The voice is soft, warm—like a child’s breath against your cheek. You reach out. The petals shiver… and press gently against your finger.

Around you, the garden hums.

One by one, the other plants awaken—not as flowers, but as witnesses. The tomato vines curl like gentle arms. The basil exhales spice-scented sighs. Even theMoon Petal blooms with luminescent silver, casting a glow that pushes back the creeping dusk.

You realize: the world didn’t die.

It was waiting.

To be remembered.

To be loved.

So you kneel.

And you plant another row.

And the garden sings back.
