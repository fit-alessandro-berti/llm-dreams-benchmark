...and you begin to speak, but the words do not leave your throat.

Instead, small, glowing motes of dust drift upward from your lips, swirling into constellations above your head. The microphone, suddenly cold to the touch, transforms in your hand from polished steel into smooth, warm oak, growing upward like a tree branch that extends into the rafters.

The crowd leans in, but as they focus on you, their faces lose definition. They are not individuals; they are reflections. Hundreds of mirrors standing in the audience, each capturing a different version of you—some young, some old, some wearing clothes you have never owned. Their expressions are calm, devoid of judgment.

The stage lights flicker and die, not plunging the room into darkness, but replacing the artificial glow with the cool, natural light of a moonlit forest. The velvet curtains dissolve into ivy. You realize you are not on a stage at all, but standing in the center of a clearing. The crowd does not clap; the trees rustle in unison, a sound like applause.

You try to ask where you are, but your voice is heavy with sleep. The realization hits you slowly, gently: you are not dreaming. You are waking up inside the dream. The forest fades into the white ceiling of your bedroom, the sound of the trees turns to the hum of a refrigerator, and you blink open your eyes, wondering which one of them is real.
