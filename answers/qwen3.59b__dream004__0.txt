It isn't blank; rather, it is waiting. The shifting colors of the world around us are chaotic and frantic, swirling in loops of neon green and fractured glass, but the moment your palm meets the fabric, the chaos stops. The air becomes solid. A scent of turpentine and rain floods your memory, anchoring you to a place where you might have stood decades ago.

The brush appears in the air without being held by anyone, bristles trembling with a pulse. It touches the canvas not to create a new world, but to reveal one lost. As you follow its motion, you realize that whatever shape you draw—the circle of a memory or the jagged line of a regret—manifests on your clothes, on the floor, on the very windows through which the sky is watching.

There are no mistakes here, only corrections. The dream whispers that the canvas was not meant to be painted; it was meant to be filled by the silence you carry inside you, translating the unsaid words into shapes that float and hang in the twilight. As you draw your first line, the colors outside stop bleeding, finally understanding that order is an act of love, while the world begins to wait for the next sentence you decide to write in paint instead of light.
