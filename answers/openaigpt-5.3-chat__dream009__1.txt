…learn how to belong before anyone notices you don’t.

The air smells like wood smoke and wet soil. Somewhere nearby, metal rings against metal in a steady rhythm. You stand in the middle of a narrow dirt road, the ground cool beneath your bare feet, your memory of electricity and screens suddenly feeling like a rumor you once heard.

People pass you with baskets and bundles. No one looks rushed. No one looks at a phone. A horse snorts beside a wooden cart, and for a strange moment you realize: this world runs on muscle, wind, fire, and patience.

You check your pockets out of habit. Empty.

No map. No money. No explanation.

So you start with the oldest survival strategy humans have: you watch.

A woman kneads dough outside a small house. A boy pumps water from a hand well. Two men argue over sacks of grain using words that sound almost familiar, like your language heard through a wall.

Food. Water. Shelter. Skills. Those are the rules here.

You move toward the well first.

The handle is heavy, but the motion is simple. Up. Down. Up. Down. Cold water rises from the earth as if the planet itself is breathing. The boy stares at you, suspicious at first, then curious.

“You’re not from here,” he says.

You almost laugh.

“No,” you reply carefully. “I’m not.”

He studies you, then shrugs the way people did long before the internet made everything strange.

“Can you work?”

You pause. In your old world you knew passwords, shortcuts, search tricks, things that had no weight.

But hands can still learn.

“I can,” you say.

He nods toward the village.

“Then you can stay.”

And as the sun climbs over roofs of wood and clay, you realize something unsettling and beautiful at the same time:

If you’re careful… if you learn quickly…

You might actually live here.

Or worse—

you might start to prefer it.
