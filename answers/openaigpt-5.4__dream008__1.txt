…open the folder expecting a normal brief, but every page is blank except for a single line that keeps rewriting itself in your own handwriting:

**“You should already know how to do this.”**

Your boss is standing beside your desk, smiling too widely, tapping a watch with no hands. Around you, the office is silent in that strange, padded way dreams make silence feel—heavy, listening. You try to ask a question, but when you speak, staplers begin answering from all over the room, clicking in frantic little bursts like insect chatter.

You turn on your computer. The screen is already open to a countdown timer.

**00:09:59**

Then:

**00:09:58**

The numbers drip downward like wet ink.

You tell yourself to stay calm. You can do this. You just need the files. But every icon on the desktop has the same name: **FINAL_v2_LAST_REAL_FINAL_USE_THIS**. You click one. It opens to a video of yourself sitting at the same desk, a few seconds ahead of you, clicking the same file and frowning in exactly the same way.

You minimize it too quickly, heart pounding, and realize the cubicles around you are much taller than they were before. They rise like walls now, stretching upward into a dim ceiling full of flickering fluorescent lights. Somewhere far away, a printer starts running nonstop, spitting out pages that flutter through the air like startled birds.

Your boss leans closer and says, gently, “We’re all depending on you.”

When you look back, everyone in the office has turned to stare.

Not angrily. Expectantly.

Their faces are familiar but slightly wrong—too smooth, too bright, as if drawn from memory by someone who has never actually seen a person. One coworker gives you a thumbs-up, but their thumb bends backward with a soft crack. Another keeps nodding, faster and faster, until their glasses slide off and shatter soundlessly on the carpet.

The timer hits:

**00:05:00**

You finally find a document that seems useful. It’s a project plan, but the bullet points are impossible:

- Finish before you begin  
- Get approval from the person you become later  
- Present the results to the room underneath the room  
- Don’t let them notice the mistake

“What mistake?” you whisper.

And the whole office answers, in your voice:

**“The one behind you.”**

You turn.

At the far end of the aisle, past rows of desks that definitely weren’t there before, someone is sitting in your chair.

Not your desk chair.

Your actual chair from home.

They’re facing away from you, working calmly, as if they belong here more than you do. You start walking toward them, faster and faster, but the carpet lengthens under your feet like a conveyor belt moving the wrong direction. The lights above begin shutting off one by one behind you, plunging the office into strips of darkness.

The figure in your chair stops typing.

Without turning around, they say, “You missed the deadline.”

“I haven’t even started,” you say.

“Yes,” they reply. “That’s why.”

The countdown starts beeping now, impossibly loud. Every monitor in the office flashes red. Your boss is suddenly at the end of the aisle beside the seated figure, though you never saw them move. “It’s time to present,” your boss says.

A conference room door appears in the wall to your left where there was never a door before. It swings open by itself.

Inside is not a conference room.

Inside is your old school classroom.

Every person you’ve ever disappointed is seated at the desks, flipping through printouts of your unfinished project. A projector shines on the board, showing your screen in giant detail: empty slides, error messages, the cursor blinking in a title box that reads:

**WHY DID YOU WAIT SO LONG**

Someone in the front row—maybe a teacher, maybe your boss, maybe your own reflection—looks up and says, “Go ahead. Explain.”

You try to step backward, but the floor tilts. Papers slide past your feet in white waves. The walls of the classroom stretch taller, the windows darken, and outside them you can see the office floating impossibly high above a black, starless sky.

Then your phone buzzes in your pocket.

Relief floods through you. Maybe this will wake you up.

You pull it out.

It’s a calendar notification.

**Meeting started 10 years ago.**

The room begins to applaud.

Slowly at first. Then harder. Louder. Their smiles widen too far. The projector light grows so bright it bleaches everyone’s faces blank. Your boss hands you a remote with no buttons and whispers, “Just advance to the end.”

You press anyway.

The screen changes.

It shows your bed.

In your dark room.

With someone asleep in it.

The applause stops.

Everyone turns to look at you.

And from the bed on the screen, the sleeping figure opens your eyes.
