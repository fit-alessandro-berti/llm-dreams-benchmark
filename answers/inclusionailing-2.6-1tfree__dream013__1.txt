…the walls begin to breathe.  

At first it is subtle—the plaster sighs when you aren’t looking, the stone expands and contracts like a chest after a long run. But soon the corridors have a pulse you can feel through the soles of your boots, a slow, tidal rhythm that seems to count the seconds you have left.  

The puzzles change in response. The ones you solved earlier slide apart and reassemble themselves into new configurations, as if the maze is learning your habits, your shortcuts, your blind spots. Patterns you memorized turn out to be only dialects of a larger grammar you haven’t yet parsed.  

Light doesn’t come from torches or bulbs anymore but from the walls themselves, a phosphorescent thought-glow that flares whenever you concentrate hard enough. You realize, with a thrill like vertigo, that the maze isn’t testing your logic—it’s digesting it. Every correct deduction is absorbed; every mistake is fermented into a new corridor.  

You reach a chamber where the floor is a black mirror and the ceiling is nowhere. In the mirror you see yourself not as you are now but as a child, hunched over a jigsaw puzzle that has no edges, the pieces made of smoke. The child looks up and mouths a question you can hear in your marrow: *What is the shape of the space between solutions?*  

Before you can answer, the corridor behind you seals with a sound like a book closing. Ahead, the air shivers into a doorway made of braided questions. You step through, and for a moment you are weightless, entirely thought without body, solving the maze by becoming it—untangling its knots from the inside—until you realize that the final puzzle is the belief that there is a final puzzle.  

The walls dissolve into birds. They carry the stones away, feather by feather, leaving only a path of footprints that are not yours leading toward a garden where the trees are shaped like open hands, and the fruit they bear is simply, brilliantly, forgetting.
