...float three inches above the podium.

The microphone doesn't exist. Your voice simply *arrives* in the space between every pair of ears—warm, unhurried, impossible to ignore. You don't remember what you came to say. The notes in your hand have turned into a single white feather, shaft glowing faintly with something that might be text, might be light.

The audience isn't breathing. Thousands of chests suspended in the same held pause. Their faces are *wrong*—too symmetrical, or melting at the edges like wax, or showing you versions of yourself at seven, at thirty, at ninety, all sitting in the same seats simultaneously.

Someone in the third row coughs. The sound unspools into a flock of starlings that burst from their mouth, wheeling through the auditorium's impossible ceiling—there is no ceiling, only a bruised twilight sky turning slowly, stars igniting one by one in time with your pulse.

You open your mouth to speak.

What comes out isn't words. It's *memory*. The smell of your grandmother's kitchen. The exact shade of blue on your first bicycle. The weight of your father's hand on your shoulder the night you learned forgiveness has a texture.

The audience begins to *change*. The melting faces solidify into people you've loved, people you've hurt, people you've never met but will. They're all crying. They're all smiling. They're all you.

The feather in your hand grows heavy. Roots push from its base, threading through your fingers, your wrist, anchoring into the stage floor which has become dark soil.

You understand, suddenly, that you aren't giving the speech.

The speech is giving *you*.

And the first real word falls from your lips like a stone into deep water:

"**Listen.**"

The sky fractures.

You wake with dirt under your fingernails.
