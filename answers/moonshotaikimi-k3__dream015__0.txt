...plant a garden.

You don't know where the seeds came from. In the dream, you've simply always had them—a small tin, rusted at the corners, that rattles when you walk. Your grandmother's, maybe, though you can't quite picture her face. The seeds inside are the only things in this gray world that hold color: deep reds, golds, a green so vivid it almost hurts to look at.

You find a patch of earth behind a collapsed overpass, where the concrete has cracked enough to let the soil breathe. The ground is hard as iron, but you work it with your bare hands, and strangely, it gives. In dreams, effort is never quite proportional to result.

Dangers drift at the edges—shapes moving through the dust, the distant pop of something you'd rather not name—but in the way of dreams, they never quite arrive. You understand, with dream-certainty, that the garden must come first.

You plant the seeds in rows you don't measure. You water them from a canteen that never empties.

And because this is a dream, they grow overnight.

Not just seedlings. Sunflowers tall as the broken buildings. Vines climbing the rebar like it was always meant to be a trellis. Tomatoes glowing against the gray like small suns. And then—this is the part that makes your chest ache even in sleep—people come. Drawn by the color. They emerge from doorways and shadows, cautious at first, then laughing, a sound you'd forgotten existed in this world.

Someone you almost recognize hands you a ripe tomato. It's warm, as if it remembers sunlight even though the sun hasn't properly shone in years.

You take a bite, and it tastes like *before*.

At the horizon, the gray begins—almost imperceptibly—to soften...
