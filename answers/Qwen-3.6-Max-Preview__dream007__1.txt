The words die in your throat, not because you’ve run out of arguments, but because the air around you suddenly turns thick and viscous, like warm honey. You look down at your hands. They are beginning to dissolve into thousands of tiny, glowing paper cranes. Each crane carries a fragment of what you were just saying—angry red ones for the accusations, soft blue ones for the hurt, jagged black ones for the things you didn’t mean to say but did anyway.

Your friend stops mid-gesture. Their eyes widen, not in anger, but in wonder. They reach out, and as their fingers brush against the swarm of paper cranes rising from your palms, their own skin begins to shimmer. Flowers bloom rapidly across their arms—wild roses for passion, ivy for entanglement, dandelions for wishes blown away by the wind.

The room around you melts. The walls of the apartment stretch upward, turning into towering trees with leaves made of old letters and text messages. The floor becomes a river of ink, flowing slowly toward a horizon where the sun is setting in shades of purple and gold.

You realize the argument wasn’t about being right or wrong. It was about trying to hold onto something that wanted to change shape.

One of the paper cranes lands on your friend’s shoulder. It unfolds itself, revealing a single sentence written in your handwriting: *"I am afraid of losing you."*

Your friend smiles, a sad, gentle expression, and plucks a rose from their arm. As they hand it to you, the thorns vanish, leaving only soft petals. You take it, and as your fingers close around the stem, the dream shifts again. You are no longer arguing. You are sitting side by side on the bank of the ink-river, watching the cranes fly into the sky, carrying the weight of the disagreement up into the stars, where it dissolves into light.

And then, you wake up.
