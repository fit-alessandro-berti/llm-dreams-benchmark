
You look down at the document in your hands, but the words keep shifting and rearranging themselves. The deadline, once a specific date on the calendar, now glows like a dying neon sign: **"NOW."** Your boss’s face, usually familiar, seems to blur at the edges, their voice echoing from multiple directions at once.

“Just start,” they say, but their mouth doesn’t move in sync with the words.

You turn to your computer, but the keyboard is made of cold, smooth stone. The screen displays not a spreadsheet or presentation, but a single, blinking cursor in a vast, white void. You try to type, but your fingers feel heavy, uncoordinated. Each keystroke produces not letters, but the sound of a distant bell.

Around you, the office begins to warp. The fluorescent lights flicker, casting long, grasping shadows from the potted plants. Your coworkers’ desks are empty, but you hear the low murmur of a hundred conversations, none of which you can quite make out.

You feel a surge of panic, then a strange calm. You realize the project isn’t a report or a presentation—it’s a **key**. A large, intricate iron key that has appeared in your hand, warm to the touch. The boss points not to a door, but to a blank wall that now seems to breathe softly.

“The door is always there,” the boss says, their form flickering between your manager and a childhood teacher. “You just have to remember where you put the lock.”

The floor feels less solid. The air smells like old paper and rain. You look at the key, then at the wall. The blinking cursor on the screen seems to pulse in time with your heartbeat.

What do you do with the key?
