The air smells like woodsmoke, wet earth, and animals. No engines. No wires overhead. No distant hum of cities. Just wagon wheels grinding over mud and the slow ringing of a blacksmith’s hammer somewhere beyond the square. Your shoes sink into the street. People pass in wool and linen, carrying baskets, tools, bundles of herbs. They look at you only briefly, but every glance feels dangerous, as if your clothes, your posture, even the way you stare might give you away.

You turn in a slow circle, trying to find something familiar—glass doors, street signs, a glowing screen, anything—but there is only a stone church, a crooked row of market stalls, and a well with a bucket creaking on its rope. It hits you then with dream-logic certainty: you need to survive until morning, and morning is somehow years away.

A bell begins to toll.

Everyone in the square stops.

Not pauses—stops. A woman freezes with apples in her apron. A dog stands mid-step. Even the smoke from the smithy seems to hang in the air, unmoving. Only you are left breathing, left turning, left hearing a second sound under the bell: your phone vibrating in your pocket.

Hands shaking, you pull it out. The screen is cracked, though you don’t remember dropping it. There is no signal, no battery symbol, no time. Just one message, appearing letter by letter:

**DON’T LET THEM WRITE YOUR NAME DOWN.**

When the bell stops, the world starts again all at once. Voices rush back in. The dog barks. The woman drops an apple and doesn’t seem to notice. At the far end of the square, three men in dark coats are setting up a table. One opens a great ledger. Another dips a quill into ink blacker than it should be. A third looks up and meets your eyes from impossibly far away, as if he had been waiting for exactly this moment.

“New arrival,” he calls.

No one else reacts. The villagers keep walking, buying bread, drawing water, muttering over turnips and thread. But you know he is speaking to you.

You back away.

The man smiles and turns a page.

You run.

Through an alleyway barely wide enough for your shoulders. Past stacked barrels, laundry lines, a yard full of geese that erupt screaming into the air. Your breath burns. Your phone vibrates again.

**FIND THE CLOCKMAKER. HE REMEMBERS.**

At the end of the alley, the town narrows into a maze of stone lanes. You spot a sign swinging above a narrow shop: a painted brass moon and a cluster of gears. Inside, the room is dim and full of ticking. Clocks cover every wall—tiny ones in carved wooden frames, tall ones with painted faces, golden pocket watches laid open like metal flowers. The sound is overwhelming, a thousand patient heartbeats out of sync.

Behind the counter stands an old woman with silver spectacles and ink on her fingers. She looks up, and unlike everyone else, she is not surprised to see you.

“You’re late,” she says.

“I don’t even know where—or when—I am.”

“Most people ask where first.” She locks the door behind you and draws the curtain. “That means you still think time is a place you can stand outside of.”

Outside, footsteps pass, then stop.

The old woman takes your phone from your hand as if it were something sacred and terribly fragile. She turns it over, studies the blank black screen, and sighs.

“They’ve begun using the ledger again,” she says. “If they write your name, you stay. Not just here. In every version of here. You become part of the town, and then you forget there was ever another life.”

A heavy knock shakes the door.

“Open,” says a voice from outside. Calm. Certain. The man from the square.

The clockmaker grabs your wrist and pulls you through a curtain into the back room. There, under a sheet, is something impossible: a machine of brass rings, hanging pendulums, and mirrored glass, humming with a low electric note that has no right to exist in this century.

“You can go back,” she whispers, “but only if you remember the one thing dreams always steal first.”

Another knock. Harder.

“What thing?”

She looks at you, and all the clocks in the shop fall silent.

“Your own name.”

You open your mouth—

—and realize you cannot say it.

Panic rises like floodwater. You know your apartment, your street, your coffee mug with the chipped handle. You know passwords, birthdays, fragments of songs, the color of a jacket you haven’t worn in years. But your name, the word that means *you*, is gone, leaving a clean white space in your mind.

The door splinters.

The clockmaker shoves you toward the machine. “Then make a new one,” she says. “Quickly. In dreams, that counts.”

The men are in the shop now, boots on wood, pages rustling, quill scratching.

“State your name,” calls the voice from the front room.

The brass rings begin to spin. The mirrored glass fills not with your reflection but with scenes from your life: a summer sidewalk after rain, a dim classroom window, the face of someone you loved and lost and almost called yesterday.

The voice comes closer.

“State your name.”

And in the strange bright terror of that moment, you understand: this isn’t a dream about the past. It’s a dream about being rewritten.

So you choose.

You speak a name that feels like flint striking steel. Not the one you were given, not the one on records and mail and forms, but the one beneath it—the one made of your stubbornness, your mercy, your unfinished hopes.

The machine flares with white light.

The ledger bursts into flame.

Every clock in the town begins to ring.

You wake up in your own bed, breathing hard, your phone glowing on the nightstand.

No messages.
No cracks.
5:17 a.m.

Outside, a car passes. A refrigerator hums. Somewhere a neighbor’s television murmurs through the wall. You laugh once, shakily, and press a hand to your face.

Then you notice the smear of black ink across your fingers.

And from the kitchen, very softly, you hear a clock you do not own begin to tick.
