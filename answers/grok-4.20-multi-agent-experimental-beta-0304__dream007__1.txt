...and you decide to let the words evaporate mid-sentence. They don't disappear—they crystallize into tiny glass birds that flutter around the room, singing distorted versions of your best arguments. Your friend’s face ripples like water, cycling through every version of them you’ve ever known: laughing at 15, furious at 30, and then something ancient and owl-eyed that whispers, “This was never about the principle. It was about who we became trying to win it.”

The floor softens into a vast chessboard made of clouds. Your feet sink in. Gravity politely excuses itself. You’re both floating upward now, still gesturing angrily but slower, like underwater dancers. The disagreement itself detaches from your mouths and becomes a glowing red orb hovering between you—pulsing, ridiculous, alive. You both reach for it at the same time. Your hands pass through. The orb laughs in your own voice and splits into two smaller orbs that orbit each other like wary moons.

Below, the argument room has become a theater with no seats, filled only with every person you’ve ever disappointed watching silently. Then the theater folds up like paper and is tucked into the pocket of your childhood coat, which you’re suddenly wearing.

Your friend (or the owl wearing your friend’s smile) nods once, as if something important has finally been settled without a single additional word. The stars turn into fireflies. The fireflies turn into the pattern on your bedroom ceiling.

You wake up with your heart racing and the vague, embarrassing sense that you both won—and that the real disagreement was about something you’ll forget by breakfast. But the feeling of reconciliation lingers like the taste of a dream you almost remember.
