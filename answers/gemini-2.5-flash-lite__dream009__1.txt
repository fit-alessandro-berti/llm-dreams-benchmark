You wake up in a completely different era, with no modern technology around. As you explore your new surroundings, you realize you need to **find a way to communicate your origins and intent without betraying yourself, and quickly, before suspicion turns to outright hostility.**

The air is thick with the scent of woodsmoke and something vaguely earthy, a smell entirely alien to the sterilized, perfumed air I'm used to. The cobblestones beneath my bare feet (when did I lose my shoes?) are uneven and grimy. Buildings rise around me, their facades timber-framed and leaning precariously. People, dressed in rough spun tunics and worn leather, move with a deliberate pace, their faces etched with lines of labor.

Panic, sharp and icy, tries to claw its way up my throat. This isn't a historical reenactment; it's too visceral, too real. The absence of even a flickering streetlamp, the lack of any sound beyond the clatter of cartwheels and hushed conversations in a language I dimly recognize as an archaic form of English, sends a fresh wave of dread through me.

My mind races. My phone, my lifeline, my connection to everything I know, is utterly useless. My clothes, a simple t-shirt and jeans, scream "outsider," an alien in this tapestry of browns and greys. I can feel the stares, the sidelong glances. They’re curious, yes, but there’s a wariness there too, a primitive assessment of threat.

I need to blend in. I need to understand. But how? My knowledge of history, usually a source of comfort and intellectual curiosity, now feels like a dangerous burden. Knowing about witch trials, about plagues, about the swiftness with which the unfamiliar can be deemed heretical, is no longer academic.

I see a market stall ahead, piled high with what look like root vegetables and coarse bread. The woman tending it has a weathered face and sharp, assessing eyes. I have no coin, no understanding of currency. I have nothing to offer.

My heart pounds a frantic rhythm against my ribs. The instinct to flee is powerful, but where would I go? I’m a ghost in time, a lost echo. I need to find a way to survive.

**I need to observe, to listen, and to mimic. I need to learn the rhythms of this place, the unspoken rules, the common courtesies. I need to find someone, anyone, who might be willing to offer a hand, a meal, a safe place to sleep, without demanding explanations I cannot give. And I need to do it before my strangeness, my silence, or my very presence, marks me as a danger to be dealt with swiftly and permanently.**

The dream deepens, the weight of this immediate, terrifying necessity settling upon me. I take a shaky breath, tasting the unfamiliar air, and force my eyes forward, towards that market stall, towards the unknown. The journey has just begun.
