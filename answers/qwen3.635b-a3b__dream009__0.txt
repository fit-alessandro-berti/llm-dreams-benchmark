The river stone in your pocket grows warm. You follow its pulse past archways carved with sleeping beasts, up staircases that shift from wood to glass to bare earth with every third step. Somewhere above, a bell tolls, but the sound travels backward, arriving before it falls.

You realize you need to return the hourglass to the temple at the city's heart before the sand turns to dust. Not because tomorrow will vanish, but because the dream has grown tired of holding its breath.

The temple has no doors. It opens when you stop looking for them.

Inside, the floor is a mosaic of broken clocks, all stopped at different hours. At the center, beneath a dome of woven shadow and starlight, sits a figure with your hands, your posture, your quiet exhaustion. They don't speak. They simply lift their palms. Empty glass waits between them, cold and hungry.

You understand now. The compass was never meant to guide you to a place. It was meant to guide you to a purpose. Dream logic doesn't reward searching; it rewards surrender.

You take the glass. You place your palms against it. It fills with sand from your own wrists, from your own ribs, from the quiet hours you've spent waiting for a waking that never truly comes. The glass turns warm. It turns heavy. It turns necessary.

The sand begins to fall upward.

The architecture softens at the edges. The scent of lavender fades into rain. The figure's face blurs into yours, then into the ceiling, then into the space between your blink and your breath.

You don't wake up. You simply remember that you were never sleeping at all. You were learning how to carry a world that no longer needs to be real to be true.

And somewhere, far below or high above, a paper crane folds itself into stillness, waiting for you to unfold it again.
