The first question isn’t a math problem or an essay prompt. It’s a single, glowing image of your own house from ten years ago, with the door slightly ajar. Below it, the instruction reads: *“Walk through.”*

You hesitate, your pen hovering over the blank page. The clock on the wall has no numbers, only hands that spin backward. You realize with a jolt that there are no other students in the room—just you and rows of empty desks that seem to stretch into an infinite, foggy horizon.

As you lean forward, the paper beneath your hand begins to feel warm, like living skin. You dip your pen into the inkwell, but instead of black ink, a stream of silver light flows onto the page. You don’t write an answer; you draw the key to the house. As soon as the last line is complete, the desk dissolves into mist, and you find yourself standing not in a classroom, but in the hallway of that old house, holding a physical key in your hand.

The front door creaks open on its own. From inside, you hear your mother calling your name, not in anger, but in welcome. You realize the exam wasn’t about what you knew, but about where you were willing to go next.
