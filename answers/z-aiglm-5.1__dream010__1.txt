...and each challenge seems designed to test not just your skills, but the very essence of who you are.

The first challenge is a room full of locked doors. Every door has a job title etched into it—some glamorous, some practical, some that don't even exist yet. The other contestants are frantically trying every key, every handle. But you pause. You notice that one door has no title at all, just a small mirror at eye level. When you look into it, you see yourself doing something that makes you smile—not for recognition, not for money, but simply because it feels right. The door opens.

The second challenge is a stage where you must perform your greatest weakness. While others stumble through impressions of their flaws, you simply stand there and say, "I don't know what I'm doing most of the time, and I care too much about things that don't seem to matter." The judges—faceless, luminous beings—lean forward with what feels like genuine interest.

The final challenge is empty space. Just you and a blank page, a bare canvas, an unlit stage—whatever form your work takes. No rules. No timer. No criteria announced.

You begin to create something that isn't perfect, isn't impressive, but is unmistakably *yours*.

When you finish, the space fills with warm light, and you hear a voice that sounds like your own, but wiser:

"You already had it. You just needed to stop proving you deserved it."

You wake up with a strange sense of clarity—and the feeling that something is quietly possible.
