 the rooms seem to multiply as you walk, hallways blooming like wet ink on paper. a group stands around a piano that is playing submerged songs—music that sounds like drowning in honey. they turn to you with smiles too wide, and their teeth chime like wine glasses when they ask if you’ve seen the host. you haven’t. you don’t even know whose house this is, only that you need to find *them*.

you excuse yourself through a curtain of static electricity and into a library where the ceiling is the night sky. a circle of people stare down into a floorboard gap that reveals cloud cover far below. they are discussing your childhood, but in a tense you haven’t learned yet, something between future-perfect and never-was. one of them wears your father’s wristwatch and offers you a business card printed on a leaf. you take it, but when you look down, it’s a moth that dissolves into dust.

the architecture shifts. now you are descending a staircase that spirals into a garden indoors. a group of faceless figures in masquerade attire waltz in place, not moving their feet, rotating like figures on a music box. they smell like rain on hot asphalt. they part for you, and for a moment you glimpse the gray coat—the one you’d know anywhere, the familiar slope of those shoulders—disappearing through a doorway at the far end of the room.

you push through, but the doorway leads to a kitchen that is also the beach from your seventh summer. people here are roasting marshmallows over a stove burner that casts no heat, only long, violet shadows. a woman with television static for hair tells you your person just left, that they were looking for *you*, that they asked her to tell you something important. when she opens her mouth to speak, a telephone rings inside it, and the sound is so loud the walls inhale.

you run now, not frightened exactly, but filled with that urgent dream-certainty that if you don’t find them before the song changes, the party will end and you’ll be the only one left. corridors elongate. the carpet becomes shallow water. you pass a group holding hands in a chain that threads through seven rooms, their laughter arriving before they laugh. you climb a ladder that leads into a crowded attic where everyone is dressed exactly like you. they all turn. they all look confused. they all ask if you are the one they’re looking for.

then, silence.

the music stops.

at the top of the stairs, by a window that shows not the street but a field of sleeping deer, stands the gray coat. you know the posture of their neck, the way they hold their left elbow when nervous. they are staring out at the deer, and you call their name—but your voice makes no sound, or perhaps it is the only sound, the hum of the dream itself.

they turn.

they smile.

they extend a hand, and in it is your shoe, the left one, which you only now realize you lost years ago.

“took you long enough,” they say. “the party’s almost over.”

and you realize, with the slow dawning logic of sleep, that this was never a party at all, but a rehearsal for waking up. the deer outside stand. the guests begin to fold themselves into napkins, into coasters, into the soft blue light of 6 a.m. filtering through your actual bedroom curtains. you reach for their hand—

and the gray coat dissolves into morning.
