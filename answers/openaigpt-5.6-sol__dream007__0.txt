You are in the middle of a heated argument with a close friend. The disagreement is about something important to both of you, and you decide to walk away before either of you says something unforgivable.

You push through the nearest door, expecting the hallway—but step instead onto a vast, moonlit beach. The tide is frozen in place, each wave a wall of dark glass. Behind you, the door stands alone in the sand.

Your friend follows, still angry, but their words emerge as small red birds. They flutter between you, frantic and bright, before settling along the frozen waves. When you try to answer, your own words become blue moths.

Neither of you can speak normally now.

Far out at sea, a lighthouse turns its beam across the sky. Each time the light passes over the beach, the argument disappears, and you see yourselves as children building a sandcastle together. Then the light moves on, and the anger returns.

At last, your friend kneels and writes in the sand: **I was afraid you would leave.**

You kneel beside them and write: **I was afraid you wanted me to.**

The frozen waves collapse all at once—not violently, but with a long, relieved sigh. The birds and moths rise together, swirling upward until they become stars.

Your friend reaches for your hand. As soon as you take it, the solitary door opens again.

On the other side is the room you left, but the argument is gone. Morning sunlight fills the windows, and on the table sit two cups of tea, still warm.

Neither of you remembers who was right.

Only that you came back.
