The air here doesn't smell like oxygen and exhaust; it smells of crushed jasmine, ozone, and something ancient, like sun-warmed stone. There is no hum of electricity, no distant drone of a highway—only the rhythmic, melodic pulsing of the earth itself, a low vibration that I feel in my marrow.

The sky is a bruised shade of violet, even though the sun—a massive, pale orb that looks too large to be real—is high overhead. I am standing on a terrace of white marble that seems to float amidst a sea of clouds. Below me, a city of bronze and silk sprawls across a valley, its towers spiraling upward like frozen smoke.

I look down at my hands. They are stained with a shimmering, iridescent dust, and my clothes are heavy, woven from a fabric that feels like liquid moonlight.

That is when the realization hits me, sudden and heavy as a physical weight in my chest. 

**I realize I need to find the Weaver of Tides before the bells toll.**

It isn't a logical thought, but in this place, it is the only truth. If I don't find the Weaver, the rhythm of the world will break. The clouds will stop drifting, the bronze towers will lose their height, and the very colors of the sky will bleed into a dull, terrifying gray. I can feel the urgency pulling at my navel, a golden thread of purpose tugging me toward the labyrinthine streets below.

I don't know how to walk these streets, and I don't know the language of the wind, but I know that if I don't move now, I will become part of the landscape—just another silent, marble statue, waiting for a sunrise that will never come. 

I begin to run, my feet barely touching the warm stone, chasing the sound of a bell that hasn't started ringing yet, but is already vibrating in my soul.
