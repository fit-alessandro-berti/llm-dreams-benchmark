You are participating in a competition where the grand prize is your dream job. As the competition progresses, you face various challenges and strange trials designed to measure qualities no résumé could show.

First, you are asked to build a bridge out of things you regret. The other contestants use apologies, unfinished projects, old report cards, and messages they never sent. You use a single memory of a door you were too afraid to open. Somehow, it becomes strong enough for everyone to cross.

Next, you enter a maze where every wall is covered in mirrors. In each reflection, you see a different version of yourself: successful, exhausted, wealthy, lonely, celebrated, invisible. A voice announces that only one reflection may leave the maze. You hesitate, then turn away from all of them and walk toward the sound of your own footsteps. The mirrors shatter into birds.

In the final round, you are brought into a quiet office at the top of a building that seems to float above the clouds. Behind a desk sits the judge, though you realize slowly that the judge is also you—older, calmer, wearing clothes you have never owned but somehow recognize.

They slide a contract across the desk.

“This is your dream job,” they say. “It has everything you asked for: purpose, recognition, security, and a view.”

You read the contract. At the bottom, in tiny letters, it says: *You must give up dreaming of anything else.*

The room grows silent. Outside, the clouds shift, revealing all the roads you might still take.

You pick up the pen.

Then you draw a door in the margin of the contract.

The judge smiles.

The office dissolves. The competitors, the building, the trophies, and the clouds become morning light spilling across your room. You wake with your hand curled as if it is holding a pen.

For a few seconds, you cannot remember what your dream job was.

But you remember the door.
