…find a way to live.

At first, it is the silence that startles you—not the absence of cars or screens, but the absence of all the tiny, constant noises that used to prove the world was still working. No hum of electricity. No distant traffic. No buzzing notifications. Only wind, birds, and the creak of something wooden settling in the sun.

You sit up and see a village, or maybe a town on the edge of one: stone walls, narrow streets, smoke rising from chimneys, people in rough-spun clothes moving with purpose. No one looks surprised by you. That is what frightens you most. They glance once, then go on, as if your presence is strange only to you.

Your pockets are empty, though you search them twice out of habit. No phone. No keys. No wallet. No little objects that prove identity. You are left with the most primitive truth of all: you are hungry.

You stand, unsure of the language—though when you speak, it seems to come out understood anyway, shaped by the dream into something familiar enough. A woman at a well notices your confusion and points to a barrel of apples. You bow awkwardly, and she laughs, not unkindly, as if you have performed a local custom badly but earnestly.

As you walk farther in, reality arranges itself around need. You need shelter before nightfall. You need water before thirst becomes panic. You need to learn the name of the ruler, the season, the customs, the dangers. You need to discover whether you are a stranger, a servant, a burden, or a prophet.

The city—or camp, or kingdom—reveals itself in fragments. A blacksmith hammering iron. A boy running bread between doorways. A guard at a gate who watches everyone and trusts no one. Markets full of salt, cloth, onions, rope, honey, and suspicion. Everything has a price, and the price is almost always labor, or information, or silence.

You begin with the simplest thing: asking how to be useful.

And in a world without machines to do the work for you, usefulness becomes the key to belonging. You carry water. You split wood. You learn which herbs are medicine and which are poison. You learn to kneel when others kneel, to eat when offered food, to keep your face calm when you are afraid. You discover that survival here is not a grand adventure but a long obedience to ordinary necessities.

Still, the dream has not finished with you.

At dusk, an old man watches you from beneath a fig tree and says, “You do not know where you are.”

You answer, “No.”

He nods, as if this confirms something he has suspected for years. “Then you are either very lost,” he says, “or very early.”

And the sky above the roofs darkens into a deep, impossible blue, as though the world itself is waiting to see which one you will become.
