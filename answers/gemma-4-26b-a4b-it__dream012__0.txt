The crew doesn't panic anymore. They stand perfectly still on the tilting deck, their shadows stretching out long and impossibly deep, reaching toward the horizon like ink spilled on silk. You realize then that the ship isn't made of wood and iron, but of memories—each creak of the hull is a forgotten childhood summer, each splash of the sea is a name you used to know.

As a gargantuan swell rises to swallow the mast, you don't reach for the wheel to fight it. Instead, you let go. You step off the edge of the deck, not falling into the water, but floating upward into a sky that has become an ocean of its own. The lightning strikes, but it doesn't burn; it feels like a sudden, bright thought. 

And just as the weight of the deep pulls at your feet, the sound of the crashing waves dissolves into the soft, steady rhythm of my own breathing, pulling me slowly, reluctantly, back to the surface of the waking world.
