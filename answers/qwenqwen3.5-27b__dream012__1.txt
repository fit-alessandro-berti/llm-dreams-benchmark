The wind screams, not like a gale, but like a chorus of lost voices calling out names you used to know. The ship groans, timber protesting against the impossible height of the waves, which rise like black mountains around you.

Your crew is frozen, eyes wide, waiting for the command to brace or to flee. But you notice something impossible: the water isn't wet. It shimmers like oil on glass, and the lightning flashes violet, illuminating the sky not with clouds, but with vast, floating islands of stone.

You realize, with the sudden, absolute certainty of a dreamer, that the storm is not an attack, but a threshold. You do not order them to fight the wind. Instead, you point toward the center of the cyclone, where the air is still and silent.

"Drop the sails," you say, your voice carrying over the roar without effort. "We don't sail through the storm. We sail into it."

As they obey, the ship doesn't crash; it glides. The hull lifts from the water, the keel passing through the waves as if they were smoke. The turbulence below dissolves into a calm, silver mist. The crew looks up, their fear replaced by awe, as the sky above opens like a flower. You are no longer in the sea, but floating in a nebula of stars and memories. The ship transforms beneath your feet, turning from wood to light.

You feel a warmth on your face, not from the sun, but from a waking world. The horizon blurs, the stars fade into the ceiling of a bedroom, and the sound of the ocean recedes into the distant hum of a refrigerator. You open your eyes, the taste of salt still lingering on your lips.
