...find the puzzles changing. Not harder—*stranger*.

The seventh chamber asks you to solve a Rubik's Cube that weeps when you turn the wrong face. Its tears smell like your grandmother's kitchen. Cinnamon. Regret. You solve it blindfolded, fingers remembering an algorithm you never learned in waking life.

The twelfth chamber is a library where every book contains the same sentence written in languages that don't exist. You read them anyway. The sentence is about a door you left unlocked in 2012. You wake up checking the lock on your apartment door. It's locked. It's always been locked. But the dream remembers the version where it wasn't.

Deeper.

A puzzle made of light. You arrange prisms to split a single beam into the spectrum of a memory: your father's hands tying your shoelaces. The knot he taught you. The one you forgot. The beam reassembles itself into a key.

You wonder who built this place. The question triggers a trapdoor.

You fall through floors of logic: syllogisms, proofs, the architecture of certainty. Each floor shatters like glass. You land in a room with no puzzles. Just a mirror.

Your reflection doesn't move when you do. It smiles. It holds up a piece of paper: *You were the puzzle. The maze was the solution.*

The walls breathe inward.

You wake with the taste of cinnamon on your fingers, holding a key that isn't there.
