You try to focus, to find the familiar logic of the material, but the ink itself seems to writhe. The first question asks you to define the color of silence, but the accompanying diagram shows a weeping clock spinning backward into a place that smells of ozone and old paper.

As you scratch out an answer, the desk begins to dissolve, not into dust, but into a swirling vortex of deep indigo and unsettling green. You realize the 'exam' is not a test of knowledge; it is a mirrored reflection of your deepest, most unexamined fears about your future.

Suddenly, the room shifts. The walls are no longer concrete; they are made of woven threads of pure anxiety. The clock on the wall melts down into a stream of liquid regret. And the voice—not a teacher, but the echo of your own future self—whispers, "You don't need to find the answer. You only need to accept the question."

You look at the blinding, impossible text, and the fear doesn't vanish. Instead, a strange, cold certainty settles over you. You realize the true exam was never about the career; it was about whether you were ready to surrender to the chaos, or to embrace the beautiful, terrifying impossibility of the unknown.

The pen falls from your numb fingers, hitting the floor with a sound like a breaking bone, and the entire room folds inward, leaving you floating in a silence that is louder than any scream. You are no longer in the room. You are simply the feeling of waiting.
