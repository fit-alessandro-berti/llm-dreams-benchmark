The room flickers like a candle in a draft. You stand at the starting line of a labyrinth made of floating islands, each one a different shade of impossibility. The host—a shadow with a voice like velvet static—announces the first challenge: *The Interview of Mirrors.*

You step onto an island where your reflection steps out of every surface—not just mirrors, but puddles, windows, even the glossy eyes of a ceramic bird. Each reflection asks a question that sounds like your own inner doubt: *“Why do you think you deserve this?”* one whispers. *“What if you fail before you even begin?”* another echoes. You realize you're not answering them—you're *becoming* the answer. The more confidently you speak, the more the reflections smile and fade. Finally, only one remains: a version of you holding a glowing key. It hands it over and says, *“You remembered. This was never about proving anything. It was about listening to your own certainty.”*

The labyrinth dissolves into a field of clocks—all running backward. The second challenge: *The Hour of Reversal.* You must undo a mistake from your past that has been haunting your career journey. You see a younger version of yourself at a desk, about to delete an email that turned down a pivotal opportunity. Your hand reaches through time—it's cold, like plunging into a river—and you type a different reply: *“Yes, but I need help learning.”* The younger you looks up, startled. The clocks stop. A door appears.

Beyond it, a giant jigsaw puzzle made of your own résumé pieces, but each piece is a memory you've locked away: the failed project, the embarrassing presentation, the rejection letter. The final challenge: *The Assembly of Self.* You have to place each piece not where it fits, but where it *belongs*—in the story you tell yourself. One piece—the one labeled “not good enough”—you almost toss away, but the dream whispers: *That’s the keystone.* You slot it into the center. The whole picture glows, showing not a perfect career, but a winding path where every setback became a stepping stone.

The host reappears, now solid, smiling. *“The prize is yours,”* it says, handing you a single sheet of paper. On it, a simple sentence: *“You already have the job. You were always the one hiring yourself.”*

You wake up with the sensation of a key still warm in your palm—and the faint smell of coffee from a kitchen you’ve never seen, but somehow know is yours.
