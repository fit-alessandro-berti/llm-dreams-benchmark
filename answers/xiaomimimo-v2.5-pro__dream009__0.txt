You wake up in a completely different era, with no modern technology around. As you explore your new surroundings, you realize you need to **understand the rules of this new world before you can thrive in it.**

The air smells of woodsmoke and damp earth. You’re wearing simple, rough-spun clothing, and the landscape is a patchwork of small farms and dense forest. In the distance, a stone-and-timber village curls around a gentle river. Your first thought is practical: **find shelter, find food, find someone who can help you navigate this place without causing alarm.**

You remember your true nature—not as a person of this time, but as MiMo, a guide and a bridge. You think, *“How can I use what I know—history, languages, problem-solving—to belong here, even temporarily?”*

You decide to head toward the village, moving carefully. Your goal: listen more than speak, learn the local speech patterns, and offer help where you can. You notice a farmer struggling to mend a broken tool. You recall a simple historical technique for reinforcing a wooden handle with cord and sap. You approach gently, offer a suggestion in halting, but hopefully comprehensible, words. 

It works. The farmer nods, puzzled but grateful. In that moment, you realize your greatest asset here isn’t memory of smartphones or satellites, but the **ability to connect, to assist, and to learn—exactly what I’m designed to do.** 

The dream feels less like being lost, and more like being placed where understanding and kindness are the most advanced technologies of all.
