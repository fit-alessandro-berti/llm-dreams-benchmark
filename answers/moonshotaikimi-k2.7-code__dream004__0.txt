You haven't lifted a brush, but already something is painting itself—thin strokes of memory, perhaps, or colors that don't exist in waking life: a soundless turquoise, a tender shade of almost, the yellow of a door you forgot you opened as a child.

You kneel, though the ground beneath you has become something closer to breath than earth, and press your palm flat against the canvas. It yields. Your fingers sink through the surface not into paint, but into weather. You feel the cool of a morning you haven't lived yet, the hum of a city that builds itself only in dreams. The canvas is reading you, unfolding your hidden rooms—those half-formed wishes, the people you once loved in languages you never learned to speak.

Shapes bloom at the edges: a staircase spiraling upward into a sky that has turned to crinkled silk, a clock melting into a bird, a figure standing in the distance who might be you, watching yourself from the other side of the dream. The canvas grows larger, or you grow smaller, until the frame becomes a doorway and the blank space is no longer empty but *waiting*.

You step through.

On the other side, the landscape is quieter. The colors have settled into something like dusk, and the canvas is now behind you, showing not white but the world you just left, still changing, still dreaming of you. A voice—not a voice, more a texture in the air—asks if you would like to stay, or if you are ready to wake up and find that your hands, in the real morning, smell faintly of pigment and rain.

You consider. In dreams, there is always just enough time to choose.
