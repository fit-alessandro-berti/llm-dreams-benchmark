…stop talking.

Not in the dramatic way people do in movies, with a final cutting line and a slammed door. You just go quiet, as if the argument has used up all the oxygen in the room. Your friend is still speaking, but their words begin to blur at the edges, stretching and bending like sound underwater.

You turn away.

The apartment is no longer your apartment.

The hallway outside has grown impossibly long, carpeted in dark red, lit by chandeliers that sway though there is no wind. Each step you take is muffled, and yet behind you your friend’s voice follows clearly, not loud now, but wounded.

“Is this really what you want?”

You don’t answer. You keep walking.

On the walls hang framed photographs, but when you glance at them, they are all pictures of the two of you: laughing on a bus, sitting on a rooftop, soaked in rain, younger than you remember, older than you’ve ever been. In every photo, one of you is slightly out of focus.

At the end of the hallway there is a door you don’t recognize. Blue paint peeling. Brass knob cold as ice. You open it and step through, expecting stairs, a street, anything ordinary—

—but instead you are standing in the middle of a vast shoreline at night.

The sea is black glass. The moon is far too large, hanging low like a lantern about to fall. Your shoes sink into wet sand. The air smells like salt and old paper.

Your friend is already there.

Not behind you, not chasing you—just standing a little farther down the beach, as if they had always been waiting. Their anger is gone. Or maybe it has become something quieter, something harder to look at.

Between you lies a small wooden boat. It is filled, absurdly, with all the things you never said: letters with blank pages, wrapped gifts that were never opened, cracked teacups, a scarf you lost years ago, a ticket stub, a key with no lock. They shift gently with the tide.

Your friend looks at the boat and then at you.

“If we push it out,” they say, “we don’t get it back.”

You want to ask what “it” means, but in dreams some words are too large to pronounce.

The waves crawl closer. The boat knocks softly against the shore.

You kneel and pick up one of the letters. Though the envelope is sealed, you know what is inside: the apology you were too proud to make. In your friend’s hand appears another letter, theirs perhaps, or yours from some other version of the night.

“I was right,” you say, but your voice sounds small here.

Your friend nods. “Maybe.”

The moonlight trembles on the water like a path.

“And you were cruel,” they say.

This hurts because it is true in the deep, exact way dream-truth often is.

The sand beneath your feet begins to give way, not dangerously, but insistently, as if the beach itself is reminding you that standing still is also a choice. Far out at sea, something enormous turns beneath the surface—something sleeping, something ancient, something made of all the times two people almost stopped loving each other and didn’t.

Your friend steps toward the boat.

For one terrible second, you think they mean to leave in it without you.

Instead, they hold it steady.

The gesture is so simple it nearly breaks your heart.

Wind rushes over the shore, carrying scraps of your argument back to you: names, accusations, old disappointments, the familiar sharpness of being known too well. You see then that every wave washing in is made of memory. Every wave washing out takes a little pride with it.

You step closer.

“I don’t know how to say it right,” you admit.

“That’s never stopped you before,” your friend says, and in spite of everything, the corner of their mouth twitches.

You laugh. It comes out like a sob.

Then the sky begins to crack.

Thin golden lines split the darkness above, spreading like fractures in glass. Through them pours warm daylight, impossible and bright. The sea lifts. The boat rises between you. The photographs from the hallway whirl overhead like startled birds.

Your friend reaches for your hand.

You hesitate only because this is the part dreams are cruel about: the part where you know something is ending.

When you take their hand, the entire beach folds inward like paper.

The ocean becomes a ceiling.

The moon becomes a lamp.

The boat becomes the kitchen table between you.

You are back in the apartment, exactly where you started, except now both of you are sitting down, breathing hard, cups of untouched tea gone cold beside your elbows. Morning light pushes through the blinds in pale stripes.

Neither of you looks victorious.

Neither of you looks ready to leave.

Your friend says your name, softly this time, like setting down something fragile.

And just before you wake, you realize the important thing was never the argument at all—

it was whether the two of you would choose to remain in the room after it.
