As you sit down to start the exam, you notice the questions are printed on paper that smells like the beat-up copy of *The Phantom Tollbooth* you had in third grade, which is weird, because you’re pretty sure this is the Advanced Interstellar Career Aptitude final—the one your guidance counselor said would lock you into your life path for the next 80 years.

The first question is multiple choice, 5 points:
> 1. You encounter a 2010s-era vending machine in an empty office break room at 2 a.m. that has swallowed three dollars’ worth of sour candy without dispensing anything. Which is the correct course of action?
> A) Kick the lower left panel three times while whispering “I know you have more at the bottom”
> B) Leave a single blue M&M behind the coin return, face up
> C) Send it a formal written apology via carrier pigeon, citing the candy’s expiration date
> D) All of the above, but only if the moon is waning

You blink. You swear option C wasn’t there a second ago. You reach for your pencil to circle B, but your pencil is a tube of finger paint that smells like the lemonade your mom used to make for summer barbecues, and when you drag it across the page the ink bleeds through so bad it stains the laminate desk underneath— the same scuffed desk you had in 2nd grade, with that little carved heart and the initials J.S. you wrote next to your crush’s name.

You flip to the next page, and the questions shift the second your eyes land on them:
> 2. (Short Answer, 20 points) List every time you almost said “I love you” to someone, in chronological order. For each entry, note whether the recipient owned a cat, and rate on a scale of 1-10 how likely that cat would have been to let you pet it.

Your throat goes tight. You can’t remember if your 8th grade band crush had a cat or a bearded dragon, and the room is suddenly too hot, the air thick with cotton candy. You look up, and the proctor is your 10th grade chemistry teacher Mr. Henderson, but he’s wearing a giant fuzzy sunflower costume, the petals brushing the front row students’ heads, and he’s holding a megaphone that’s playing the jingle of your childhood dog’s collar.

“Fifteen minutes remaining!” he booms, but the clock on the wall is a Dali melting clock, its hands oozing down the chalkboard like warm honey, the numbers all backwards. The kid sitting next to you is the version of you that took the safe corporate marketing job, the one you always told yourself you’d never be. They’re not even writing—they’re sucking on a cherry lollipop, wearing the ill-fitting suit you bought for your cousin’s wedding, and they glance at you and shrug like they don’t know the answers either.

You flip another page, and the questions change again:
> 3. (Essay, 50 points) What is the name of the first pet you ever loved? Bonus points if you can recall the exact shade of its fur, and the song you sang to it when it was scared of thunderstorms.

Your mind goes blank. Was it Buster the golden retriever that used to steal your socks? Or Muffin the calico your neighbor gave you when you were 5, that ran away after a week? You can picture both of them so clearly, but you can’t remember which one came first, and the room starts to tilt. The kid in the sunflower costume is leaning over your desk now, the velcro on his costume making a ripping sound, and he whispers, “That’s not the right question. Scroll down.”

You flip to the last page, and there’s only one question left, taking up the whole sheet:
> 4. (Pass/Fail, 100 points) Do you remember the way home?

You stare at the page. Suddenly you can’t picture your apartment, can’t remember the route from this elementary school to the coffee shop you go to every morning, can’t even remember your own middle name for a full three seconds. You grab your finger paint pencil to write something, anything, but the tube is empty, and when you look down the page is full of doodles you drew when you were bored in class: sailboats, stick figures of your family, a crude rocket ship you drew when you wanted to be an astronaut.

The fire alarm goes off, but it’s playing the Nokia ringtone you had in high school, and everyone else just keeps scribbling away, heads bent over their papers, like they don’t hear it. You stand up to leave, but your legs feel like vanilla pudding, and you look down and realize you’re only wearing your frayed rocket ship underwear, the ones with the little star patches on the hips. You’re so mortified you want to sink through the linoleum, but Mr. Henderson just waves at you like it’s totally normal.

You wake up heart hammering, your actual exam study guide splayed open on your chest, the clock on your nightstand saying you have 45 minutes left until the real test starts. For a full minute you can’t remember if you took the test or not, or if you still owe an apology to the vending machine that ate your dollar in the campus library last week.
