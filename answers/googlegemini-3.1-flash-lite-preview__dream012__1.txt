I don't look at the sky—the sky is a bruised throat of purple and black, pouring rain that hits like lead shot. Instead, I fix my eyes on the First Mate, whose face is pale, illuminated only by the intermittent, jagged lightning that turns the churning swells into mountains of glass.

"She’s taking on water in the hold!" he bellows, his voice nearly consumed by the gale. "If we don't turn into the swell, we'll be pulled under by the trough!"

I see the fear in their eyes, tethered to me like a lifeline. I am the anchor in their chaos. 

I shift my weight, locking my knees against the violent pitch of the deck. I don't give the order to turn. Instead, I reach for the heavy iron bell that hangs by the helm and strike it once—a resonant, defiant toll that cuts through the whistling wind.

"Lash yourselves to the mast!" I roar, finding a strength in my lungs I didn't know I possessed. "We don't fight the ocean! We yield to it!"

I heave the wheel hard to the starboard side. The ship heels over, the horizon line tilting into a terrifying, vertical drop. For a heartbeat, the world goes silent. The roar of the storm is replaced by an impossible, humming stillness. I look over the railing and see it: not dark water, but a vast, bioluminescent city beneath the waves, its spires glowing in soft amber. 

The ship isn't sinking. It is descending.

As we plunge into the heart of the ocean, the turbulence vanishes. The water outside the glass turns to cool, liquid starlight. The crew stops their praying, their hands falling away from the rigging, their faces bathed in the golden glow of the deep. I realize then that we never belonged to the storm. We were only waiting for the courage to let go of the surface.

I turn the wheel one last time, steering us into the silent, shining heart of the abyss, and as we drift into the calm, I realize I am no longer a captain, but a passenger, waking up as the ship dissolves into soft, morning light against my bedroom wall.
