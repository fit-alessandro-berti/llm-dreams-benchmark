The air is thick with the smell of burning peat and roasted chestnuts. The streets are paved with uneven cobblestones, slick with morning mist. Around you, people move with a slow, deliberate pace, dressed in heavy woolens and rough linens. There are no electric hums, no distant sirens—only the rhythmic clopping of horse hooves and the murmur of a dialect that sounds like a mix of French and something ancient.

You pat your pockets frantically; they are empty. No smartphone. No keys. No wallet. Just a crumpled piece of parchment with a map drawn in fading ink and a small, intricate brass key that feels warm to the touch.

You wander through the bustling marketplace, dodging carts piled high with turnips and live poultry. The architecture is towering and Gothic, with gargoyles watching your every move from high above. You feel like an outsider—an impostor in your modern synthetic fibers.

As the sun begins to dip below the horizon, casting long, unnatural purple shadows across the square, you notice a large iron gate at the edge of town slowly beginning to crank shut. The locals are hurrying toward the inner sanctum of the city, looking over their shoulders nervously at the darkening forest beyond the walls.

That is when the instinct hits you, hard and fast. 

**As you explore your new surroundings, you realize you need to find the specific door that matches the brass key in your pocket before the last rays of sunlight vanish, because whatever lives in the dark outside those walls is already beginning to whisper your name.**
