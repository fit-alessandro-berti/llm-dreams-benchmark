First, the competition floor turns into a vast, endless library where the books are made of liquid light. To pass, you must catch specific memories before they evaporate, cupping them in your hands like glowing butterflies. The challenge isn't about reading, but about *feeling* the weight of the stories. If you hold a memory too tightly, it burns; if you hold it too loosely, it vanishes into the scent of old parchment and ozone.

Suddenly, the library walls dissolve into a sea of violet clouds. You are no longer walking; you are swimming through an atmosphere that feels like warm silk. The challenge shifts: you must navigate a labyrinth of floating mirrors. In each mirror, you see a different version of yourself—one where you stayed in your hometown, one where you became a hermit, one where you never learned to dream. To move forward, you must look into your own eyes in the glass and nod in recognition, accepting every version of yourself without letting them pull you into the reflections.

The gravity begins to flicker. You feel weightless, then heavy as lead. The judges appear, but they are not people; they are colossal, shifting constellations that hum in a frequency that vibrates in your marrow. They don't ask for a resume; they ask for a color. They demand you produce a shade of blue that has never been seen by human eyes. You reach into your own chest, pulling out a thread of pure, unadulterated hope, and weave it into the air.

As you approach the final gate—a doorway made of pure, blinding white sound—you realize the "dream job" isn't a position or a title. The prize isn't a desk or a paycheck. As the gate swings open, you see that the prize is simply the ability to remain awake within the dream, to hold the brush that paints the world as it changes.

Just as you reach for the handle, the stars begin to fade into the gray light of morning, and the roar of the constellations turns into the soft, persistent hum of my alarm clock.
