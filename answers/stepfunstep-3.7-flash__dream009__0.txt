You wake up in a completely different era, with no modern technology around. As you explore your new surroundings, you realize you need to figure out whose life you’ve woken into, before the man shouting your name at the edge of the lane reaches the cottage you just stumbled out of.
The first thing you register is the smell: damp straw, woodsmoke tucked into the chinks of the stone walls, the sharp, yeasty tang of sourdough baking somewhere down the path. There’s no hum of appliances, no distant traffic, just the cluck of hens scratching in the yard, the creak of oak beams settling as you sit up, and the distant ring of a blacksmith’s hammer. You’re lying on a straw mattress stuffed with dried lavender, covered in a rough-spun wool blanket that scratches at your bare arms. The only light comes through leaded glass panes streaked with dirt, gilding the dust motes dancing in the air in thick, honey-colored late afternoon light, and the edges of your vision are soft, blurred like a half-remembered memory.
You push yourself up, your feet sinking into packed dirt floor dotted with corncobs and straw. Your hands brush against homespun linen patched at the elbows, and you pause—these aren’t your hands. They’re calloused, blistered at the fingertips, a thin white scar slicing across your left knuckle you don’t recognize. You pat your hips, and your fingers close around a worn leather pouch stitched shut with coarse thread.
You step outside, damp earth squelching between your bare toes. The village that spreads out before you is a cluster of thatch-roofed cottages, an ox-drawn cart trundling down the dirt path toward a stone windmill at the edge of the woods. A woman in a linen apron churns butter outside the cottage next door, and she nods at you like she knows who you are, calling out something in rapid French you almost, but not quite, understand. The faint echo of your old life—glowing phone screens, the hum of a refrigerator, the beep of a text notification—fades faster now, like a dream you’re already half-forgetting.
You pull open the pouch, and your stomach drops: three tarnished copper coins, a folded scrap of paper with the names of three small village children scrawled in hasty cursive, and a note smudged with flour that reads: *Meet me at the mill by sundown. Don’t let the elder see you.*
That’s when you hear him: a rough, angry voice booming your name from the end of the lane, the village elder, his face flushed, a wooden cudgel slung over his shoulder, striding toward you.
You don’t know what the person whose body you’re wearing did to earn his wrath, but you know the three kids on the list sell eggs at the market every Sunday, and if you don’t get to the mill before the elder does, whatever plan the old you had to help them is going to fail before it even starts. The water wheel of the mill creaks in the distance, the sun dipping lower to gild the tree line gold. The elder is only fifty yards away now, and you can feel the muscle memory of this body kicking in already—your feet know the hidden path through the brambles behind the cottages, the one the elder won’t take, your lungs already remembering how to run without tiring. You need to choose: bolt for the mill now, and hope you can figure out what you’re supposed to do when you get there, or stand your ground and try to talk the elder down, even though you have no idea what lie to tell.
