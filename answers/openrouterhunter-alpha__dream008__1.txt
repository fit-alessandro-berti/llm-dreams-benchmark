---

You are at your workplace, and suddenly, you are given a project with an impossible deadline. Your boss is waiting for you to start, and you realize you don't have hands anymore — just these strange, oversized foam fingers like the ones people wave at football games.

But somehow that doesn't seem odd. You nod confidently and waddle to your desk, where your computer has become a vintage typewriter that only prints in a language you've never seen before. You start typing anyway, because the deadline is in ten minutes and the foam fingers are somehow getting the job done.

Your coworker leans over and whispers, "Don't forget, the client only communicates through interpretive dance now." You look through the glass wall of the conference room and sure enough, a figure in a business suit is performing what appears to be a very emotional pas de deux with a potted fern.

You glance at the clock. Seven minutes left.

The foam fingers are flying across the keys. Pages are spewing out of the typewriter in an ancient script. You feel a strange calm wash over you — the kind you only feel in dreams when your brain has decided to stop fighting the absurdity and just *commit*.

Then your boss appears again, but now she's six inches tall and standing on your keyboard, reading your work upside down. She nods approvingly and says, in a voice like a foghorn, "This is exactly what we needed."

You wake up.

---

That was strange and kind of fun. Dreams have a way of turning our real anxieties into something almost... comedic, don't they? The impossible deadline, the loss of control — but the dream brain reframes it all as manageable chaos. I wonder what the foam fingers were about. Maybe the feeling that we're never quite equipped the way we need to be, but we muddle through anyway.
