You start to run, but the first drops of rain hit your skin. They aren't water. They are warm, glowing embers that don't burn, instantly transforming into monarch butterflies the moment they touch you. The forest floor grows spongy, then completely gives way, but you don't fall. You find yourself swimming through the thick, humid air as if it were a warm ocean. 

The trees around you elongate, stretching into impossibly tall, twisting spires of woven briar and starlight. Desperate for shelter from the beautiful but overwhelming storm, you spot a door standing completely freestanding in a clearing. It’s painted a bright, cheerful yellow, untouched by the chaos around it. 

You turn the brass knob and step inside.

Instantly, the roar of the storm vanishes. You are no longer in the forest, but standing in the grand, sunlit concourse of a vintage 1920s train station. The marble floors gleam, and the passengers hurrying past you are all woodland animals in tailored suits. A badger in a tweed vest stops, checks a silver pocket watch, and looks at you. 

"You're late for the tide," he says. His voice sounds exactly like your grandfather's. 

He hands you a ticket. It’s not paper, but a large, green leaf with glowing, pulsing veins. As your fingers brush it, a deep, resonant whistle shakes the glass ceiling of the station. Your train has arrived, but it isn't a train at all. It’s a massive, silver whale gliding silently along the iron tracks, blowing a spout of shimmering mist that smells like old books and petrichor. 

You step toward the whale's open boarding ramp, but as your foot touches the metal, the station begins to dissolve into white fog. The badger tips his hat, fading into the mist. The deep, rhythmic hum of the silver whale slowly morphs into the electronic beep of your alarm clock. 

The violet and gold sky washes out into a pale, familiar gray, and you open your eyes to the ceiling of your bedroom, the phantom smell of rain still lingering in the morning air.
