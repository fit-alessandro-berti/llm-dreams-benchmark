I nod, heart hammering against my ribs, and the first challenge begins.

**The Maze of Mirrors.**  
I step through a velvet curtain into a hallway where every wall reflects a different version of me—one in a crisp suit giving a TED talk, another hunched over a laptop at 2 a.m., a third shaking hands with someone I can’t quite see. The instructions echo overhead: *“Find the door that shows not who you are, but who you are becoming.”* I walk slowly, ignoring the reflections that flatter me, until I reach a cracked mirror at the end. In it, I’m not polished or perfect. I’m tired, curious, mid-question. The glass swings open.

**The Interview on the Tightrope.**  
Next, I’m standing on a thin silver wire suspended over a city made of open books. Across from me sits the interviewer—sometimes a stern CEO, sometimes my childhood teacher, sometimes a stranger with my own eyes. They ask me impossible questions: *“Name the one thing you failed at that you’re proud of.”* *“What do you do when no one is watching?”* With each answer, the wire trembles, but I learn to stop gripping and start balancing. I speak not to impress, but to be understood. The wire turns into solid ground.

**The Room of Noises.**  
Then comes the hardest trial. A thousand phones ring at once. Notifications scream. Emails flood in, subject lines flashing *URGENT* and *TOO LATE* and *WHY HAVEN’T YOU?* The other contestants scramble, answering everything, crying, drowning. But something tells me to sit down in the center of the chaos and close my eyes. I breathe. The noise doesn’t stop—it never stops in dreams—but I stop fighting it. Slowly, the clamor arranges itself into a single clear voice: *“What do you actually want to build?”*

I answer out loud.

And just like that, I’m standing on a stage. The lights are warm, not blinding. The audience is small—friends, strangers, versions of people I’ve loved and lost. The judge hands me a key, simple and brass. *“You didn’t win,”* they say gently, *“because this was never a prize to be taken. It was a door to walk through.”*

I wake up just as I turn the key, the dream already slipping like sand, but the feeling stays: the job was never the grand prize. The grand prize was finally believing I was ready to try.
