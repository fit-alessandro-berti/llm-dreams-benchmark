You paint without deciding to. The canvas drinks in every stroke, and the colors refuse to stay put. They drip off the edges and pool on the ground, and wherever they pool, new pieces of the world spring up: a forest of amber trees, a river of slow violet light, birds shaped like brushstrokes that scatter whenever you look at them directly.

Then you notice the canvas is painting you back.

A figure is forming in its surface—you, but woven from all the colors you've been spilling. The painted-you lifts a hand. You lift yours. Somewhere in the middle of the gesture, you lose track of who is copying whom.

The whole shifting landscape goes quiet, holding its breath, as if it had been waiting all along for you to understand: you are the canvas and the painter and the paint.

You step forward—or the painting steps toward you—and everything dissolves into a soft, warm white, like the inside of a shell, like the moment just before morning.

And as the white brightens toward waking, you feel it still on your fingertips: the faint, tingling residue of color, as if some small piece of the dream agreed to come back with you.
