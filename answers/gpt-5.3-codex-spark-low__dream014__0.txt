• You accept their offer with a small, uncertain nod.

  They don’t ask for money or your name, just tell you to keep your phone in your pocket and follow. One of them carries
  a bright umbrella against the evening rain, another carries a thermos of sweet tea, and a third maps the subway lines
  in broad strokes on a napkin with perfect calm. As they lead you through the crowd, every street seems less chaotic.
  They point out landmarks you had missed: the old clock tower that marks the district, the bakery that stays open until
  midnight, the florist whose red awning means the nearest safe station, the tiny church where volunteers hand out maps
  and snacks.

  Halfway through, they stop beside a fountain and say, “You’re not lost anymore. You just took the wrong turn in your
  first thought.” Then they laugh softly, give you a nod, and melt into the crowd. You stand there blinking, holding the
  map, suddenly certain you can find the way—because what you really needed was direction, yes, but more than that, a
  reminder that strangers can be landmarks too.
