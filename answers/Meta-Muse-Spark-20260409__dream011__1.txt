You are about to take the final exam for a course that determines your future career. As you sit down to start the exam, you notice the questions are **written in a language you've never seen before**.  

The symbols swirl on the page like ink in water. Panic flickers, but then you realize everyone else is scribbling calmly, like this is normal. The proctor, a tortoise in a tweed vest, clears his throat and says, "Remember, class, the answers must be sung, not written."  

You look down again. The first question rearranges itself into your name, then into a map of a city you've never visited. Question two becomes a recipe for "confidence soufflé". Question three is just a small drawing of a door with your childhood house number on it.  

Your pen starts humming. When you touch it to the paper, it plays a single note that sounds exactly like your mom saying "you've got this" from the kitchen when you were eight.  

Suddenly you understand: this isn't a test of what you memorized. It's a test of whether you can **improvise when the rules change**. You stand up, tap the desk like a drum, and answer question one by whistling the way home.  

The tortoise winks. The classroom walls fold outward into a sky full of paper airplanes, each one carrying a different version of your future. You grab the one that smells like coffee and old books.  

Then you wake up, but the note from your pen is still in your head for the rest of the day.
