**...decide to find the source of the color.**

Not the water, or the clean metal, or the forgotten radio signal. Not the physical source of life, because I know the apocalypse didn't end with a bang, but with a silent, suffocating *fade*.

The world was rendered in shades of rust and ash, a monochromatic palette of decay. The sky was a bruised, perpetual twilight. Everything I sawŚthe skeletal remains of skyscrapers, the dust that tasted like burnt ozone, the ragged edges of my own coatŚwas brown, gray, or sickly ochre.

This was my singular, obsessive mission: I had to find the sourceŚthe place, the object, the organismŚthat still contained the pure, reckless vibrancy of life.

The journey became a pilgrimage into the deepest shades of desolation. I walked through ruins of what might have been libraries, where the paper dust felt like pulverized, dead memory. I navigated canyons built of rusted, overturned infrastructure, where the silence was so heavy it pressed against my chest like solid glass.

The scarcity didn't just mean hunger; it meant a scarcity of *meaning*. I encountered husks of people, not zombies, but ghosts haunting the broken arteries of civilizationŚpeople who moved in their exhaustion, keeping the worst shades of gray close to their skin. They warned me away from certain places, pointing instead to the perpetual horizon, as if the mere fact of wandering was the ultimate danger.

Days bled into nights, and the only guides I had were the faint, recurring internal memory flashesŚa flash of liquid red, like spilled wine; a flash of defiant green, like jungle deep beneath a canopy; a flash of purest, unbearable blue.

I finally found it deep within what was once a botanical garden, now nothing more than overgrown, choked ruins. The greenhouses were shattered domes of twisted iron and pulverized glass. Everything here was dying, except for one corner.

It was shielded by a fallen wall of concrete, a place that gravity and time seemed to have failed to fully engulf. There, in a perpetually damp hollow, was a patch of earth that looked different. Not merely dirt, but *dark* dirt. It smelled overwhelmingly, almost offensively, of wet loam and life.

And within it, pushing up through the cracked, gray pavement, were flowers.

They weren't the simple, hardy weeds of survival. They were riotous. There were petunias of impossible, dripping violet; dahlias the color of melted gold; and, dominating it all, a cluster of small, unbelievably bright scarlet poppies. They defied the gray physics of the world. They were screaming color.

A profound weariness washed over me, the kind that settles in the bone marrow. I collapsed beside the defiant clump of color, recognizing that this small pocket of life was not a reservoir of infinite hope, but merely a miracle of persistence.

I knelt down, the dust giving way beneath my knees, and reached out, not to pluck a flowerŚfor that would destroy the magicŚbut to gather the seeds scattered at the base of the riot of color.

I understood then, in the hushed, aching clarity of the dream, that finding the "source" wasn't finding the magic. It was realizing that the magic was never the untouched, pristine thing. It was always the desperate, continuous effort. It was the defiant little pocket, nurtured by sheer, illogical will.

And so, despite the hardships, I decided to **build.**

I gathered my knees, my trembling hands holding the seedsŚthe seeds of the poppy, the dahlia, the violetŚand I started to scoop away the trash, the detritus, the *decay* from the sacred corner. I wasn't just saving the seeds; I was saving the ground. I was creating a cradle for the next explosion of improbable color, starting with one careful weed, one intentional, desperate act of gardening in a dead world.

I would not allow the world to be merely shades of rust and ash.

I would build a garden.

And as I worked, digging my hands into the black, rich earth, the faintest, sweetest scentŚnot of decay, but of promiseŚrose up and settled over the wreckage. The dream did not end in waking, but in the deep, rhythmic, comforting sound of the trowel hitting the satisfying, hopeful crunch of damp soil.
