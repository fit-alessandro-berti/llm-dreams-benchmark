You turn a corner and see your friend’s back, the familiar curve of their shoulder, but when you tap it, a stranger turns—wearing your friend’s smile but not their eyes. “You’re close,” they say, and gesture toward a set of double doors.

Beyond the doors, the party transforms into a library with chandeliers. Groups are now arranged in reading nooks, sharing stories that physically manifest in the air as faint, swirling ink. You hear your friend’s laugh from behind a towering shelf of encyclopedias, but the aisle stretches longer as you walk, the carpet pattern shifting underfoot.

Finally, you reach a small courtyard open to a starless night sky. Your friend is there, pouring tea from a pot that steams with constellations. “I’ve been waiting,” they say, and you realize you’re both wearing masks you can’t remove. They hand you a cup—it’s empty, but you feel the warmth spread through your hands, and suddenly you understand every conversation you overheard earlier, all at once.
