...a brush that hums softly in my hand, as if it remembers every painting it has ever made.

The canvas isn't propped on an easel—it's floating, tethered to nothing, rippling slightly like the surface of a pond turned vertical. Around me, the world sheds its forms: a tree unspools into ribbons of violet, then reknits itself as a staircase of glass. A bird flies past and becomes, mid-flight, the idea of a bird, then the sound of one, then nothing at all.

I dip the brush into the air itself. It comes away loaded with something heavier than paint—a color I don't have a name for, somewhere between the feeling of remembering a face and the smell of rain on warm stone.

I touch it to the canvas.

And the canvas drinks.

Whatever I paint, the landscape around me begins to mimic. A curved line, and the horizon bends to match. A circle, and a second sun blooms overhead, casting shadows that fall upward. I realize, slowly, that the canvas isn't receiving my painting—the *world* is. The canvas is only a window into what I'm actually doing: editing the place I stand in.

I hesitate. The brush hums louder, patient.

I paint a door.

A door appears in the air before me, plain and wooden, with a brass handle worn smooth by hands I've never seen. On the other side, I can hear something—not a sound exactly, but the suggestion of a room I used to know, or maybe one I was always meant to find.

I reach for the handle—

and wake, with the shape of the brush still warm in my palm, and a color on the back of my eyelids that fades before I can name it.
