…despite the hardships, you decide to **plant a garden**.

At first, it feels absurd.

The soil is gray and brittle, the sky hangs low with the color of old metal, and every distant sound could be wind, or machines, or something hungry moving through the ruins. You have no fence, no clean water, no guarantee that tomorrow will come.

But in the pocket of your torn coat, you find a paper envelope.

Inside are twelve seeds.

No label. No instructions. Just small, dark shapes, like commas from a language the world has forgotten.

You kneel beside the cracked remains of a courtyard between two collapsed apartment towers. Around you, the city leans inward, all broken windows and rusted balconies. Somewhere nearby, a radio coughs out static. Somewhere farther away, a siren begins and then dies mid-note.

You dig with your hands.

The earth resists at first, hard as bone, but beneath the ash there is still dampness. You press the seeds into the ground one by one, covering them gently, as though tucking in children.

For water, you use the last mouthful from your canteen.

By nightfall, you regret it.

Your throat burns. Your stomach twists. The cold slips through your clothes. You curl inside the hollow shell of an old bus, listening to footsteps pass in the dark. They stop near the courtyard. You hold your breath.

A voice whispers, “Someone’s been here.”

Another answers, “Nothing worth taking.”

The footsteps move on.

You sleep.

When you wake, the world has changed.

Not much. Not enough for anyone else to notice.

But in the courtyard, where you planted the seeds, twelve green shoots have broken through the gray soil.

They are impossibly bright.

You stare at them until your eyes sting. Then you laugh, and the sound startles a flock of black birds from the roofline. The laugh becomes a cough, then almost a sob, but you do not let yourself cry. Not yet.

Over the next days, you guard the shoots.

You trade a battery for a dented rain barrel. You drag sheets of glass from an office building and make a crooked little greenhouse. You hang bells from wires so they will ring if anyone comes too close. You begin sleeping less, listening more.

People notice anyway.

A girl with a shaved head appears first, holding a knife too big for her hand. She says she only wants to look. You let her, but keep your distance. She crouches by the plants for a long time.

“My grandmother had tomatoes,” she says.

“These might not be tomatoes.”

“They’re something.”

The next day, she returns with a jar of cloudy water.

Then comes an old man who knows how to mend filters. Then a woman with a broken wrist who remembers which weeds are edible. Then two brothers hauling a sack of compost made from rot and patience. No one says the word community. No one trusts it yet.

But the bells ring less often.

The fire barrels burn a little longer.

Someone paints a sign on a slab of concrete:

**DO NOT STEAL FROM THE GARDEN. ASK.**

Weeks pass, or maybe years do. Dreams are loose with time.

The twelve plants grow tall, then taller. Their leaves are silver underneath, and at night they glow faintly, as if holding moonlight. They do not grow tomatoes, or beans, or anything you recognize.

They grow lanterns.

Small pods swell along the stems, translucent and warm. When they ripen, they open like cupped hands, each holding a soft golden flame that gives no smoke and needs no fuel.

People come from the ruins to see them.

Some kneel. Some laugh. Some accuse you of witchcraft. Some ask what they cost.

You tell them, “Take one seed when it falls. Plant it somewhere dark.”

And they do.

Soon, from the rooftops and train tunnels and abandoned schools, little lights begin appearing across the dead city. Not many at first. Just enough to make the darkness look uncertain.

One evening, you climb the tallest tower still standing. From there, you see the world spread below you: broken highways, flooded streets, skeletal cranes, the old scars of fires and wars.

And everywhere, tiny golden lights.

Not enough to save the world.

But enough to begin.

Behind you, someone asks, “What do we do now?”

You look down at your hands. The dirt has settled permanently beneath your nails. Your canteen is empty. Your coat is still torn. The dangers have not vanished.

But the city is glowing.

So you answer, “Now we plant more.”

And as the dream begins to fade, you realize the apocalypse was never the end of the world.

It was the place where the world waited to see what you would choose to grow.
