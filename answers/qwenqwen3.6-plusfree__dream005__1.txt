One circle of strangers speaks in overlapping harmonies, their words melting into the clink of crystal and a low cello hum that seems to vibrate up through your shoes. You smile politely, but when you turn, the corridor has lengthened into a gallery of arched mirrors, each reflecting a different version of the party: some are dancing underwater, others are seated at tables where the candles burn with cold blue flames.

You catch a familiar silhouette near a balcony draped in ivy. You follow, but the stairs ripple like fabric beneath your feet, turning to warm sand, then to polished glass that shows your own footsteps fading. The music shifts—a jazz trio that wasn't there a moment ago—playing a tune you almost recognize from a childhood summer. A man in a tailored coat hands you a glass of something that smells like rain on hot pavement. You nod thanks, look down, and he's holding a potted fern.

Through a doorway of hanging beads, you finally spot them. Your one familiar face. They're laughing at something, head tilted just so, and relief loosens your ribs. But as you step forward, the crowd parts like a curtain, and you notice the details don't quite hold: their watch runs backward, their shadow points the wrong way, their voice reaches you a half-second after their lips move. You call out. They turn, and for a breath, their features dissolve into a mosaic of every conversation you've ever avoided, every road not taken, every word left unsaid.

The chandeliers dim. The music slows to a single piano key that lingers in the air. You realize the gathering wasn't a place, but a procession, and the person you've been chasing was never meant to be found—only remembered. The floor softens. The walls breathe. Somewhere in the distance, a clock strikes an hour that doesn't exist.

You blink, and the dream lets you go.
