…and the first cluster is gathered around a piano that no one is playing, yet music rises from the keys in pale, visible ribbons. Their faces catch the light at wrong angles—noses slightly too long, eyes reflecting colors that don’t exist in the room. They turn to smile at you in unison, and you smile back, but your mouth feels full of warm, soft feathers.

You excuse yourself, pushing through velvet curtains that become heavy rain for three seconds, then dry again, and step into a library where all the books are shelved spine-inward. People stand in pairs, leaning close, whispering secrets that crawl from their lips as moths. You ask if they’ve seen your friend, and a man with no eyebrows points with an arm that bends backward at the elbow, directing you toward a staircase that descends upward into the ceiling.

You climb—though gravity insists you are dropping—and emerge onto a balcony overlooking the *same* party, but viewed from twenty years ago, or perhaps next week. The music is still that piano piece, slowed until each note is a footstep echoing in a marble hall. And there, across an impossible distance, through the glass floor of the atrium below, you see the back of their head: the familiar slope of shoulder, the jacket you recognize even though in waking life they’ve never owned it.

You call out. Your voice leaves your throat as a silver fish that swims through the air, darting between guests who have begun to waltz without partners, each spinning alone in perfect orbit. The room expands with your desperation. Doorways recede like ocean tides. You push past a group holding empty champagne flutes, toasting a moon that has lodged itself in the corner of the dining room, glowing and pocked and impossibly close.

Then the crowd thins. The music dims to a single heartbeat. You turn a corner formed by two mirrors facing each other, creating an endless corridor of your own reflection, and at the very end—where the reflections should finally meet—stands your friend. But as you approach, you realize it is you, wearing their expression, their posture. They raise a hand. You raise yours. The touch of glass is warm.

Behind you, someone says your name, exactly the way they always do, and you turn—

waking before you see their face, the silver of their voice still trembling in your chest, half-convinced that if you could just fall back asleep, you’d find them in the next room, waiting with two cups of something steaming, saving you a seat by the window.
