You wake in a stillness so deep it feels like pressure against your ears. The air smells of earth, woodsmoke, and damp wool. You’re lying on a straw-stuffed mattress, beneath a heavy, hand-woven blanket. Rough-hewn wooden beams frame the low ceiling above you.

As you sit up, the realization settles not as a thought, but as a visceral truth: the network is gone. The invisible web of data, signal, and instant connection that wrapped the world has simply… evaporated. There is no hum of a refrigerator, no distant rumble of traffic, no glow from a standby light. Just the crackle of a hearth and the sound of wind finding the gaps in a shuttered window.

You need to orient. You need to understand the rules of this place. Your hands, accustomed to glass screens and plastic keys, feel strangely soft and useless. You need to learn how to *do* things again—to make fire, to draw water, to speak without referencing a world of satellites and steel.

You rise, your modern clothes feeling thin and alien. On a wooden chest, you find a coarse linen tunic and heavy trousers. You dress, the fabric scratching your skin. A single, small-paned window lets in a grey, dawn light. Outside, a muddy path winds between timber-framed houses, smoke curling from thatched roofs. A cart drawn by a shaggy horse creaks by.

You need to find your place in this tapestry. Not as an observer with a device in your palm, but as a participant. Your mind, a library of future knowledge, is now a silent, solitary thing. The immediate need is simple, and vast: to step outside, to speak, to be *here*. To trade the abstract for the tangible—the weight of an iron key, the taste of well water, the trust earned through labor, not credentials.

Your first step is toward the door, its iron latch cold and solid in your hand. The dream, or the life, begins now.
