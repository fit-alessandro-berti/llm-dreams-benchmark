You are about to give a speech to a large audience. As you step onto the stage, you see the crowd looking expectantly at you, and you begin to speak. Your voice booms through the speakers, clear and confident, though you don’t remember preparing these words. They flow effortlessly, about unity and potential, and the audience leans in, captivated.  

But as you continue, you notice something odd. The faces in the front row are beginning to blur, their features softening like wet clay. You blink, and when you look again, each face has become a mirror of your own—hundreds of you, staring back with the same expectant expression. A cold ripple passes through you, yet you keep speaking, unable to stop.  

The stage beneath your feet feels less solid, more like a thick, slow-moving liquid. You glance down and see your reflection in the polished surface, but it’s not moving with you. It smiles knowingly and continues the speech on its own, its lips syncing perfectly with your words.  

The audience of yous begins to applaud, but the sound is distorted, slow and deep, like a record played at the wrong speed. The lights brighten until everything is washed in white. You try to shield your eyes, but your arms won’t lift. You are floating now, weightless, your voice the only anchor.  

And then you wake up.
