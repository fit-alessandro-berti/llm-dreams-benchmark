You are walking through a peaceful forest when suddenly the weather changes dramatically. You feel a storm approaching—and not just any storm.

The sky doesn’t darken with clouds. Instead, it *unravels*—like a tapestry being pulled apart by invisible fingers. Stars blink out one by one, replaced by floating lanterns made of woven moonlight, drifting lazily downward. The wind whispers not in gusts, but in voices—familiar voices. Your grandmother’s lullaby. The laugh of a childhood friend you lost. Your own voice, from five years ago, saying, “You’ll figure it out.”

Rain begins to fall, but each drop is a tiny, glowing memory. One lands on your palm and flashes with the scent of your mother’s kitchen, the warmth of a summer afternoon you thought you’d forgotten. Another bursts on your shoulder and floods your mind with the sound of ocean waves you’ve never seen—but somehow remember.

The trees around you begin to breathe. Their bark peels back to reveal faces— serene, ancient, watching. One whispers, *“You carry the storm inside you. It’s not coming. It’s remembering.”*

A path lights up beneath your feet—not with lamps, but with footprints made of star-dust and sighs. They lead upward, not downhill, toward a clearing where the sky has collapsed into a single, enormous eye. It blinks. Not slowly. Deliberately.

And then you realize—you are not walking through the storm.

You are the storm.

The wind lifts your hair. The rain sings your name. The trees lean closer, not in fear—but in reverence.

A voice, neither male nor female, neither young nor old, echoes from everywhere and nowhere:

*“Now you remember why you came here.”*

You close your eyes.

And when you open them—

You are back in your bed, sunlight on your sheets.

The alarm is screaming.

But on your nightstand, half-dried like a tear, lies a single silver leaf that wasn’t there last night.

And it hums—just barely—when you touch it.
