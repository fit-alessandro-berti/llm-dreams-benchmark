You are at a large social gathering where you only know one person. As you try to find them, you encounter various groups of people and…  

…each group is made of entirely different versions of yourself.

Not clones. Not illusions. *Versions*.  

The first group is laughing loudly—three of you, dressed in vintage 90s band t-shirts, holding mismatched cocktails, telling a story about a cross-country road trip that somehow involved a sentient cactus. You recognize their voices—you’ve heard them in quiet moments when you let yourself daydream too hard. They wave you over, but before you can respond, you hear a faint whisper: *“Don’t stay. They’re stuck in the past.”*

You turn away, heart pounding, and bump into a circle of five versions of you in crisp, minimalist businesswear, speaking in calm, measured tones about portfolio diversification and mindfulness retreats. One of them glances at you and nods, almost sadly. *“We sold out,”* she says. *“But we sleep soundly.”* You don’t want to admit you fear becoming one of them.

Then—silence. A lone figure stands near a grand window, gazing out at a sky that doesn’t exist. You recognize her immediately. It’s you. But older. So much older. Dressed in a robe stitched with constellations only you would know. She turns slowly. Her eyes are not tired. They’re *full*.

“You’ve been looking for your name,” she says. “But you already know it.”

Tears prick your eyes. “I’m looking for *her*,” you whisper. “The one person I know.”

The older you smiles gently, reaching into her pocket and pulling out a key made of moonlight.  

“She’s you, too.”  

A wave of warmth rolls through you. The party melts away—not fades, but *unfolds*. The walls dissolve into libraries of forgotten birthdays, the ceiling into the inside of a lullaby, the guests into echoes of your own laughter from childhood, from teenage nights, from sleepless early mornings when you thought no one would ever understand.  

And then—you see her. Standing at the center of the empty room, barefoot, holding a single teacup steaming with the scent of jasmine and old paper.  

The version of you you’ve been running toward all night.

She doesn’t speak. She just opens her arms.

You run.

And when you hug her, you remember:  

You’ve been dreaming this moment for years. And now—you’re awake.  

But you’re still dreaming.  

So you close your eyes again.  

And wake up…  

…in your own bed.  

The scent of jasmine lingers.  

And your teacup—still warm—is on the nightstand beside you.  

You didn’t even make tea.
