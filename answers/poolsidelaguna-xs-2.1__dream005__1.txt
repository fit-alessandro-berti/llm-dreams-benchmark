...each group has an unsettling quality. The first cluster is gathered around a table where everyone wears masks, yet they all smile with the same lopsided grin. One woman’s mask is cracked, revealing a patchwork of eyes beneath it—some human, others glowing like a cat’s, flickering in sync with the hum of the room.  

You step closer, drawn by the sound of their laughter, which echoes slightly off-key, like a record skipping. A man in a bowtie offers you a drink, but when you take it, the glass is filled with fog that swirls into shapes—brief flashes of your childhood home, your mother’s voice, a memory you forgot you had. He grins, and you notice his teeth are all tiny clocks, ticking in reverse.  

You back away, and the group dissolves into mist, leaving only a single red balloon tied to a chair. As you approach it, the balloon floats upward, trailing a string that leads through the crowd like a ribbon. You follow it, weaving between conversations that repeat the same sentence in different languages: *“He’s not here. He never was.”*  

The string eventually stops at a mirror hung in an archway. Your reflection smiles back—but it’s not your face. It’s the one person you know, but older, wearing a crown of wilted roses. When you reach out to touch the glass, the mirror ripples, and you realize the reflection is crying.  

Then the lights flicker, and the entire room shifts into a library. Shelves stretch endlessly, each book labeled with names you’ve forgotten. On a table at the center sits a single volume: *The Person You’re Looking For*. Its pages are blank, except for one sentence, written in your handwriting: *“You were always the one who got lost.”*  

The dream dissolves as the book snaps shut, and you wake up—only to find your phone screen displaying a photo of yourself at a party, taken an hour ago. But in the photo, you’re holding the same red balloon from the dream.
