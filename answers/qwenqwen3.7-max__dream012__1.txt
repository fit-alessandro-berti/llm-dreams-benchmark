…reach for the ship’s wheel, but your fingers pass straight through the polished wood. 

You look down at your hands. They are made of sea-glass, translucent and glowing with a faint, bioluminescent green. The storm is roaring, but the sound isn't wind or thunder—it’s the deep, resonant hum of a thousand cellos playing out of tune. 

"Hard to starboard!" you shout, but your voice comes out as the chime of a silver bell. 

The crew doesn't flinch. In fact, as you look at them, you realize they aren't sailors at all. They are figures folded entirely out of old, yellowed parchment. Their faces are blank, save for swirling inkblots where their eyes should be. They nod in unison, the crinkling of their paper bodies barely audible over the cello-storm, and rush to the rigging, which is made of braided spider-silk and ticking pocket-watch chains.

A massive wave rises off the bow. It isn't water. It is heavy, dark velvet, cresting and folding over itself with the sound of whispering voices. You know, with the absolute, unquestioned certainty of a dream, that if the velvet touches the deck, you will forget your own name.

You grab the ship's wheel again. This time, it solidifies beneath your glass hands, but it’s no longer a wheel—it’s a giant, brass astrolabe, its rings spinning wildly. You don't steer left or right; you steer *then* and *now*. You twist the outer ring backward. 

The ship groans, a sound like a waking whale, and suddenly you are sailing *up*. 

The velvet wave freezes into a towering cliff of dark glass. The ship's bow points toward the sky, and you are climbing the face of the frozen wave. Gravity shifts. The deck becomes a wall, but your boots stick to the planks as if magnetized. The parchment crew runs along the masts like insects, unfurling sails that are actually giant, white moth wings. They beat against the heavy air, pulling the ship higher.

You reach the crest of the glass wave, and the storm abruptly breaks. 

The dark, turbulent clouds shatter like dropped porcelain, raining down into the abyss below as thousands of white teacups and saucers. The sky clears, revealing not a sun, but three swollen, low-hanging moons, pulsing with a soft, bruised-purple light. 

The sea below smooths out into a perfect, endless mirror, reflecting the moons so perfectly that the ship appears to be floating in the center of a hollow sphere of stars. 

The parchment crew stops their work. One by one, they fold themselves into intricate paper cranes and take flight, drifting away on a sudden, warm breeze until they are just white specks against the purple moons. 

You are alone on the deck. The ship is no longer a galleon, you realize, but a giant, floating wooden bedframe. The brass astrolabe slows its spinning, ticking softly in time with your own heartbeat. 

Exhausted, you lie back on the deck, which is now covered in a thick, impossibly soft mattress. You pull the mainsail down over yourself like a heavy quilt. The mirrored sea rocks you gently, and as you close your eyes within the dream, you feel the distinct, pulling sensation of the waking world, waiting for you just on the other side of the tide.
