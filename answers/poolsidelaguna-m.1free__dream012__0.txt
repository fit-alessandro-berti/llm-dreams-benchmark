The sails are tattered, the mast creaks like a dying giant, and the compass spins wildly, mocking your grasp on direction. You shout orders that are swallowed by the wind, but your crew—drenched, shivering, eyes wide with a mixture of terror and trust—scramble to obey.  

You remember your father’s words: *“A captain isn’t the one who commands the storm, but the one who steers through it.”* You loosen the mainsail just enough to keep the ship from capsizing, then grab the wheel with blistered hands. The starboard side dips violently, and for a heartbeat, you’re certain you’ll be swept into the black water, but a crewman—Finn, the youngest deckhand—shouts, “Left flank! We’re taking on water!”  

You pivot, barking for the bilge pumps, and suddenly, the storm shifts. The waves, which had been clawing at the hull like wolves, begin to still. The crew exchanges glances—was it your order, or something else? Ahead, a sliver of moon breaks through the clouds, revealing a path through the rocks you’d thought were certain doom.  

As dawn bleeds into the sky, the crew cheers, but you don’t join them yet. You’re staring at the compass, which now points steadfast north. Your hands stop shaking. In your pocket, your father’s locket—warm against your chest—whispers a secret you’ve yet to decode. The storm wasn’t just weather. It never is.  

The sea, for now, lets you pass.
