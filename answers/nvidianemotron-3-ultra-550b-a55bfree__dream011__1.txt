...written in a language that shifts when you blink. One moment it's English, the next it's Cyrillic, then something that looks like sheet music, then geometric proofs rendered in watercolor.

The proctor—your third-grade teacher, but with your mother's voice—announces: "You have forty minutes. Or forty years. Whichever comes first."

Your desk is floating three inches off the floor. The student beside you is a mannequin wearing your high school letterman jacket. It keeps tapping its pencil. *Tap. Tap. Tap.* The sound echoes like a heartbeat in a cathedral.

Question 1: *Describe the color of Tuesday using only verbs.*

Question 2: *Calculate the square root of your mother's disappointment. Show your work.*

Question 3: *The train leaves Chicago at 3:14 PM traveling at the speed of regret. When does it arrive at forgiveness?*

Your pen writes in ink that smells like rain on hot asphalt. Your hand moves without your permission, drafting answers you don't understand—equations made of feathers, essays composed of breath.

The clock on the wall has no hands. Just a small window where a single eye blinks.

*Wake up,* the eye seems to say. *Wake up. You're late for something that already happened.*

The mannequin turns its plastic head. Its mouth doesn't move, but you hear: **"You studied the wrong material. You always study the wrong material."**

The questions keep rewriting themselves. The paper grows warm, then hot. Smoke curls from the edges, spelling out a single word in cursive so elegant it hurts to read:

**Run.**
