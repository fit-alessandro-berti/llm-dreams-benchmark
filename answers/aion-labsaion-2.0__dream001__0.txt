You start to run, but your legs are heavy, as if moving through syrup. The skyscrapers around you seem to lean in, their windows reflecting a sky that is too purple. You fumble in your pocket and find a crumpled paper—a map, but it’s written in a language of symbols you almost recognize. A clock tower chimes, and each toll makes the streets rearrange slightly. A friendly-looking stranger points you toward a subway, but the entrance keeps shifting between a staircase and a bookstore. You descend anyway, and the train arrives silently. Inside, all the passengers are wearing masks of animals. One, a fox mask, nods at you and hands you a briefcase. You open it—inside is not documents, but a single, glowing key. The train doesn’t stop at stations; it just slows in dark tunnels where whispered voices recite addresses. You realize you’re not looking for a place anymore, but a person. The key grows warm in your hand. The train doors open onto a platform that looks exactly like your childhood bedroom. The meeting is there, at your old desk. And everyone you’ve ever known is waiting, patient, expectant. You wake before you speak.
