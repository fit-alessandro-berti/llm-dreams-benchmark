...you grip the wheel, knuckles white against the dark wood, and you realize the wheel isn't connected to anything. It spins freely, the way things do in dreams—weightless, unmoored, no resistance at all.

The crew is watching. Their faces are familiar but you can't name a single one. Someone you went to school with, maybe. Your third-grade teacher is coiling rope. The storm makes a sound like a held breath.

\ of them says, \ains do—toward the largest wave, the one rising like a green glass wall, and the strange thing is *nobody questions you.* They simply turn the ship toward it, trusting, and you feel the terrible tenderness of being believed.

The wave doesn't crash. It opens. There's a door in it, an ordinary door, the one from your childhood bedroom, and behind it the light is warm and yellow.

You want to tell the crew something important before you go through. You open your mouth.

The words won't come. They're underwater somehow, the syllables drifting away like fish.

So you just nod, and the crew nods back, and—

You're already somewhere else. The ship is gone. The sea is a bedsheet. And there's that feeling you always get at the edge of waking, the one that says: *you were just about to understand something.*

But what was it?

The door is still open.

You step through.
