You are walking through a peaceful forest when suddenly the weather changes dramatically. You feel a storm approaching and the trees begin to whisper in voices that sound almost familiar.

The sky darkens to a deep violet, and the first drops of rain fall upward from the leaves into the clouds. A narrow path appears where there was only moss before, glowing faintly as if lit from beneath. You follow it, though you don’t know why.

Thunder rolls—not above you, but somewhere under the ground.

Ahead, in a clearing, stands a small wooden door with no wall around it. Its brass handle is warm when you touch it. As the storm gathers behind you, the door swings open to reveal a sunlit room filled with the scent of old books and sea air.

Inside, a fox wearing a blue scarf looks up from a table and says, “You’re late. The rain has already remembered your name.”

Before you can answer, lightning flashes, and for one bright instant you see the forest reflected in the fox’s eyes—except in that reflection, you are not a person at all, but a bird made of silver leaves.

The wind lifts you gently from the ground.

And then you begin to fly.
