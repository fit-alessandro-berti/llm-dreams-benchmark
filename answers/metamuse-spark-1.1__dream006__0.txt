and you begin to realize you don't remember what the speech is supposed to be about.

Your mouth opens, but your notes are gone. You look down at your hands and you're not holding notes at all — you're holding a warm loaf of bread. Or a small, sleepy cat. It keeps shifting.

A soft panic fuzzes at the edge of your chest, that classic dream panic, but when you look back up at the crowd, you see they aren't impatient. They all have your face. Or — no — they're all people you've ever known, just mixed up. Your third-grade teacher is sitting next to the barista from last week, both of them wearing the same white headset. The entire front row is empty except for one kid version of you, swinging his legs.

You try to speak and at first no sound comes out. Then you hear your voice, but it's not coming from your mouth. It's coming from the microphone itself, which is melting slightly like wax.

You say, "Thank you for being here."

And the moment you say it, you understand that *you* invited them here. Not to impress them. Just to tell them one true thing you had forgotten until now.

So you put down the bread-cat, and you tell them:

"I thought I had to have something amazing to say to be allowed on the stage. But I think I just had to show up."

The audience exhales all at once — a warm wind that lifts the curtains, lifts the papers that were never there. The back wall of the auditorium falls away and it's not a wall at all, it's the sky at dusk. The rows and rows of chairs start to gently float.

And you are floating too, just an inch above the soft, bread-like stage, and you realize — oh. Right. This is a dream. You can say anything you want.
