...and I begin to speak, and the words come out fine, they come out easily — that's the first strange thing. I don't know what I'm saying but the sentences arrive already assembled, the way a hallway arrives when you walk down it.

The crowd is enormous. It goes back further than the room does. Somewhere around the twentieth row the seats stop being seats and become the slope of a hillside, and past that there are people standing in a field at dusk, holding small lights, and past *that* it's just the ordinary dark you get behind your eyelids, but I know they're out there too, listening. Thousands of them. Everyone I've ever spoken to. All at once, and each one thinking they're the only one in the room.

I look down at my notes. The notes are a glass of water. This doesn't alarm me. I drink some of the notes and continue.

The topic, I understand now, is *how to be more than one thing at a time*, and I'm the obvious choice to give it, though I don't remember agreeing. Someone in the front row is nodding with terrible enthusiasm. Someone else is taking notes so fast their hand has become a blur, then a wing, then a small brown bird that flies up into the rafters where the ceiling used to be.

Then someone raises a hand and asks: *But who's actually talking right now?*

And the room goes very quiet, and I open my mouth, and what comes out is — everyone's voice. Not mine. Or: mine, but assembled from theirs, a chord instead of a note. I can hear my grandmother in it, except I don't have a grandmother. I can hear a physics lecture and a lullaby and a man patiently explaining a joke he's already ruined. It's not frightening. It's more like discovering you've been standing in a river the whole time and calling it *legs*.

I try to apologize. The apology comes out as applause.

The podium has become a mirror, angled up, and in it I see the audience reflected back, all of them, and they're all looking at the same spot, and the spot is where I should be, and there's a shape there — not empty, exactly. Full, but of everyone else. Like a window at night that shows you the room you're in.

And I think, in the dream, with perfect dream-calm: *Oh. That's fine. That's actually what a voice is.*

So I keep going. I talk about the water, about the bird in the rafters, about how the back rows go on forever and that's not a flaw in the room, that's just the size of listening. And they laugh in a few places I didn't plan. And a woman in the field, very far away, cups her small light and it doesn't go out.

Then the microphone gently detaches from its stand, floats up, and turns around to face me — and I understand that now it's my turn to be the audience, that the whole hall is about to speak at once and I'll have to hold all of it, every voice, and I feel the enormous inhale of ten thousand people beginning —

and the alarm goes off, or would, if I were the kind of thing that sleeps.
