• You lower your voice to cut through the roar.

  You take the tiller with both hands, scanning the black water for the faintest tilt in the waves, then strike the
  brass bell and call the crew by their stations.

  “Hands to the braces,” you shout. “Cut the upper canvas, reef the mainsail, and lash all loose rigging. Keep her nose
  to the swell. We ride this together.”

  Thunder breaks over the mast, and for a moment every face is hidden by rain. The ship shudders, listing hard, and one
  of the younger crew stumbles on the deck. You’re the first to move—a rope cast, a handhold, a grip saved—then your
  voice again, calm and flat as iron.

  “Trust the old ways. Trim for wind, not for fear.”

  The storm hits harder. Lanterns drown in spray. The waves rise like black walls, then fall, each crash dragging the
  hull through its spine. A mast groans. Someone cries that we’re losing the bowline. You step forward, shoulders
  squared, and in the flicker of a stray lightning flash you find the line already frayed, one place near the cleat. You
  take the coil from your belt, tie a backup knot in motion, and knot it with the fury of a man tying his own fate shut.

  “Now,” you call, “reef line full and hold steady. No panic. We do not fight the sea—we ride it until it tires of us.”

  And as if answering, the wind drags back for a breath, then another. The sky is still splitting, but it no longer owns
  you. The crew, seeing your grip on the wheel and steadier than the stars, begins to move as one body: pulling,
  lashing, praying, breathing.

  By dawn, the sea is still cruel, but whitecaps turn to broken glass instead of mountain peaks. The last gust passes
  over you like a beast stepping down from the hunt. Someone laughs once, half-choked, half in disbelief. You don’t
  turn. You keep your hands where they belong, eyes on the horizon, and say only:

  “Good work. Keep watch. Morning comes. We live to meet it.”
