You start to ask strangers for help, but every answer feels like it was meant for someone else. People point down streets that curve the wrong way, or they repeat directions using landmarks you can’t see—“past the clock that never moves,” “under the arch that sings,” “toward the fountain made of smoke.” When you look for those things, the corners of the world shift, and the city seems to quietly correct itself as if it’s trying to keep your attention on the feeling of being lost rather than the solution.

You glance at your phone. The time is there—an hour exact—and yet the map refuses to load. Instead of roads, it shows a single blinking dot labeled *YOU*. Beneath it, lines of text scroll too fast to read, then stop and reformat into a sentence that only makes sense once you’ve already missed your first chance to follow it:

*You won’t find the place. You’ll find the feeling.*

You don’t know what that means, but you can’t afford to argue with a dream. So you do the next best thing: you walk faster.

The city welcomes you with windows full of motion. Curtains ripple though there’s no wind. Reflections in storefront glass behave like separate beings—smiling when your face doesn’t, looking away when you look straight at them. You pass a row of doors. Each one has a different name engraved into it, all of them clearly important: *Boardroom,* *Briefing,* *Interviews,* *Clarifications.* Your stomach tightens because you can’t remember which title belongs to your meeting. Your mind, reaching for logic, starts to scramble.

Then you notice something: the doors aren’t in a hallway. They’re arranged like teeth in the mouth of the building, and the building itself seems to breathe. Not dramatically—just enough to make the air around you pulse. Each time the façade exhales, one door gets warmer to your touch, and your palm tingles as if you’ve almost remembered something.

You try the first door. It opens onto a room full of chairs facing the opposite direction. No one sits in them. The walls are covered in blank whiteboards that smell faintly of fresh chalk. There’s a clock on the far wall, and its hands are permanently blurred, as if it can’t decide which direction time should go.

You close that door. Your meeting isn’t there.

You try the next. This room is bright and lined with microphones, but the sound is wrong—an ocean of static that somehow feels like someone waiting for you to speak. At the table, there’s a single folder. You pull it toward you and the folder turns soft in your hands, like paper becoming fabric, and then like fabric becoming something warmer: skin. The name on the tab is your own, written in a handwriting you recognize the way you recognize your voice on an old recording.

You yank your hand back. The room doesn’t change, but the static in the microphones seems to lean closer, as if it’s disappointed you’re afraid.

You back away. Your meeting isn’t here either.

Somewhere in the city, you hear a bell. Not loud—just clear enough to cut through the dream’s distractions. You look up and realize the street itself is different around you now: the buildings have shifted, and the sky has developed a faint grid like graph paper. Lines converge toward a single point far ahead, not like perspective, but like an instruction.

Your phone buzzes again, even though it has been dead for minutes. The screen brightens and shows one new message:

*Go to where you are being looked at.*

You stop. Slowly, you turn your head.

You feel it before you see it—an attention, a weight behind your eyes. Not threatening, not exactly friendly. More like a camera adjusting focus. You look at your reflection in a nearby window and see that it’s slightly delayed, as if the glass is processing you a moment later than you move. Behind your reflection, in the space your eyes can’t quite align with, there’s a door you didn’t notice a second ago.

It’s small. Unremarkable. No title. Just a plain handle and a brass plate that has been scratched until the letters can’t be read—yet when you stare at it long enough, the scratches arrange themselves into words you understand without reading them:

*Now.*

The bell rings again. Your hour is suddenly shorter—not with numbers ticking down, but with everything else speeding up. You can feel the dream tightening like a knot.

You reach for the handle.

When you open the door, you don’t step into a room. You step into a hallway made of mirrors, each one reflecting you in a slightly different state: calmer, panicked, smiling, crying, younger by a decade, older by ten years. In every mirror, you look as though you’re about to answer a question.

In the center of the hallway, there’s a conference table made of shadow. Around it, chairs are arranged like people about to sit—but the chairs are empty, the way a stage is empty before the actors arrive. At the far end, there’s a single bright light like an interrogation lamp, but it doesn’t burn. It just waits.

A voice comes from everywhere and nowhere. It sounds like the version of you that remembers what you forgot.

“Why did you think you needed to know where?”

You swallow. Your dream-throat feels dry, like you haven’t spoken in a while. You open your mouth anyway. “I have an important meeting,” you manage, as if that’s an excuse instead of a truth.

The mirrors around you ripple, and for a moment you see what you’ve been circling this whole time: not a location, not a calendar event, but a decision. The meeting isn’t about the other people in it. It’s about the part of you that keeps postponing the moment when something becomes real.

The voice softens. “Then be on time.”

The light at the end of the hallway flares, and you realize the mirrors aren’t showing choices you could have made in the past. They’re showing the choices you could make right now.

You look at your hands. They’re trembling, but they’re also steadier than you expected. You take a breath that seems to come from deeper than your lungs, as if the city itself is filling you with air.

And then you do something you haven’t done yet in the dream: you stop trying to solve the maze and you start to choose.

You walk toward the bright light, past your own reflections, letting them blur behind you. Each step feels like signing something you can’t read but will someday understand. The closer you get, the less the mirrors matter, until only one reflection remains: you, centered, present, no longer searching for directions.

At the threshold of the light, a final thought flashes—sharp and clear, like a ticket stub you didn’t realize you were holding:

*The meeting was never in the city.*

It was in the moment you decided to arrive.

You step into the light—and the dream changes.
