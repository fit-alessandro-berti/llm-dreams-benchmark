The wind picks up, pressing the leaves flat against their branches like the forest is bracing. The light drains from the sky — not darkening the way daylight fades, but more like the color itself is being pulled away, leaving everything in a pale, silver-gray wash.

I walk faster. My feet move, but the distance doesn't cooperate. The trees ahead stay exactly where they are, no matter how many steps I take.

Then the first drop of rain hits my hand, and it's warm — almost hot — and when I look down, it's not water. It's something luminous, faintly gold, like liquid light pooling in my palm.

I close my fingers around it, and when I open them again, I'm somewhere else.

A cabin. Small, wooden, older than it should be. The storm rages outside now — I can hear it pressing against the walls, rattling something loose in the roof. But inside, there's a fire already burning in a stone hearth, as though someone knew I was coming.

There's a chair facing the fire, and on the chair — a letter.

I don't recognize the handwriting, but I understand every word before I finish reading it. It says:

*"You've been walking a long time. Rest here. The storm will pass. It always does."*

And the strange thing is — I believe it.

I sit down. The fire crackles. The storm hums its low, heavy song outside. And for the first time in what feels like days, I don't feel the need to move forward.
