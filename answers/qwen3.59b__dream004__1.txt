You reach out a brush made of light and dip it into the puddle of silence sitting beside the frame. The liquid inside doesn't drip; it pools like heavy honey.

As you touch the canvas, the shifting chaos outside freezes in a circle around your painting tool. For every stroke you place—a jagged line of cobalt or a soft gradient of burnt sienna—the world snaps into that specific color and form, but only for as long as the paint remains wet.

If you lift the brush too fast, the colors rush back into disarray, turning your creation into something wild and unrecognizable before your eyes blink. You understand now: this canvas is not meant to capture a moment, but to steal it from the flow of time. You are about to decide if the world will be blue today, or if you must protect against the fear that if the red dries on the surface, the red shapes in the landscape beneath your feet will turn into fire and burn you.

You choose to paint a small circle of gold. Immediately, a warm light radiates out, and for the first time since arriving, the colors stop spinning. You take a breath, and the air smells like ozone and wet clay. But then, you look down at your reflection in the frame, and your face is shifting into something else entirely. You paint over it quickly with black to seal the identity, realizing that staying on this canvas means becoming part of the art itself—frozen forever, but safe within a world that can finally breathe without changing.
