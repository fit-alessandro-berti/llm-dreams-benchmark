You are at a large social gathering where you only know one person. As you try to find them, you encounter various groups of people, each behaving as though they have been waiting specifically for you.

A circle of strangers applauds when you enter the kitchen. Someone presses a warm cup into your hands and asks whether you brought the moon. Before you can answer, the walls slide apart, revealing a ballroom where everyone is dancing in complete silence. Their shadows, however, whisper and laugh across the floor.

You call your friend’s name. From somewhere upstairs, they call yours back.

The staircase grows longer as you climb. Every landing opens onto a different version of the gathering: in one, the guests are all children wearing the faces of people you once knew; in another, the house is underwater, yet no one seems to notice. At the final landing, you find a narrow door with light beneath it.

Behind the door is the same party, now empty.

Your friend stands alone by an open window, looking out over a city made entirely of candle flames. You hurry toward them, relieved, but they turn around wearing your face.

“There you are,” they say. “I’ve been looking everywhere.”

Before you can ask what they mean, the guests begin filing silently into the room behind you. One by one, they remove their masks.

Every face is yours.

The candles below go out at once, and in the darkness your friend takes your hand.

“Wake up,” they whisper.

But when you open your eyes, you are still holding it.
