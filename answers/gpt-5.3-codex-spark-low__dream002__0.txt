• And then the sky folds over you like a dark curtain, the light thinning into bruised indigo.
  The birds vanish in a silence so complete it sounds like waiting.
  A wind rises, sharp as glass, bending the tallest pines until their tops shiver.

  You look up as the first drop lands—cold, heavy, impossible to miss.
  It is not rain. It is a sheet of silver beads, each strike ringing like tiny bells against leaf and bark.
  Then thunder speaks, not from above but from under the ground, rolling through roots and stone.

  Ahead, the path splits where it always had: one way home, one deeper into the trees.
  You glance back, but the familiar clearing is gone, as if it never existed.
  Ahead, the forest opens into a wide ring of ancient oaks, their trunks carved with pale symbols that glow with every
  strike of lightning.

  The air smells of cedar, wet soil, and something metallic—like old coins and storm glass.

  You step into the circle and the rain falls straight through, no longer above you but around you, as if the storm is
  moving through the world in layers.
  At the center, a single stag waits, coat black and shining, antlers wrapped in lightning-blue threads.
  Its eyes do not look wild. They look like recognition.

  When it speaks, the sound is quieter than thunder and stronger than wind.

  “Walk faster,” it says, and the words arrive like rainwater in stone.
  “Not away from the storm. Through it.”

  You follow.

  The forest shifts as you run—trunks become gates, roots rise to become bridges, shadows detach from branches and leap
  across ravines as stepping stones.
  Behind you, thunder cracks, and behind that, a voice you almost know but can’t place says your name.

  You don’t look back.

  At the far edge of the storm, where the light returns in a hard white seam, the cloud wall thins.
  You step through it and stand suddenly in a clearing flooded in warm amber dawn.

  The rain is gone.
  The stag is gone.
  In your hand, where your phone used to be, there is a leaf—silver-veined, warm, and trembling as if it remembers the
  storm.

  Then you wake with the scent of rain still in your hair and rain ticking on the roof, but no storm outside—only
  morning.
