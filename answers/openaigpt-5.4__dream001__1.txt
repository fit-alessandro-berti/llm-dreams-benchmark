…walk faster, though the streets seem to lengthen beneath your feet like ribbon being pulled from a spool.

The city is bright in the wrong way. Sunlight glints off glass towers, but the shadows between them are blue and deep, as if it’s nearly evening there. People flow around you in silent streams, all wearing small silver pins shaped like compasses. When you try to stop someone, they smile politely and point in different directions at once.

You reach into your pocket for your phone, certain the address must be there, but instead you pull out a folded paper map. The map is warm, as though someone has just been holding it. It shows the city in impossible detail—every alley, every staircase, every courtyard—but there is no marker for where you are, only a red circle pulsing faintly near the center, labeled: THE MEETING.

You choose a street lined with yellow trees whose leaves chime like tiny bells when the wind moves through them. As you walk, the buildings begin to change. A bank becomes your old school. A bakery becomes the hallway outside your childhood bedroom. A train station opens into a library so enormous its shelves disappear into clouds.

Inside the library-station, a conductor in spectacles stamps your map with a date you don’t recognize and says, “You’re not late yet, but you can’t arrive the usual way.”

You want to ask what that means, but a train is already arriving without sound. Its doors slide open to reveal not seats, but a narrow corridor lit by aquarium-blue light. You step inside. The floor tilts gently, like a ship. Through the windows, instead of tunnels, you see scenes from your life passing by: a birthday cake with candles bending in the draft, a rainstorm on a day you almost said something important, a room where someone is waiting and has been waiting for years.

At the next stop, everyone gets off except you and a small girl holding a red umbrella, though there is no rain. She looks at your map and says, very matter-of-factly, “You keep looking for the building, but the meeting is with the city.”

Before you can answer, she presses the umbrella into your hands and steps backward through the closing doors onto a platform full of birds.

The train dissolves around you.

Now you are standing in a plaza paved with black and white stone, a chessboard stretching in every direction. In the center rises a clock tower with no clock face, only a round opening filled with drifting clouds. The red circle on your map glows hotter. Around the base of the tower, dozens of doors stand freestanding with no walls around them: painted doors, iron doors, revolving doors, garden gates, elevator doors opening onto darkness.

Each has a brass plaque. You begin to read them.

ONE YOU MISSED.  
ONE YOU TOOK.  
ONE YOU FEARED.  
ONE STILL WAITING.  
ONE THAT NEVER EXISTED.

Your pulse thuds in your ears. The meeting must be behind one of them. You lift the red umbrella, though you don’t know why, and its fabric catches a wind that isn’t there. It tugs you—not toward the grandest door or the nearest one, but toward a plain wooden door with chipped blue paint and a plaque that simply reads:

ENTER AS YOU ARE.

Your hand shakes as you turn the knob.

Inside is not a room but a long table set for a meeting: papers neatly stacked, glasses of water trembling slightly, sunlight falling through high windows. At every chair sits a version of you from a different age. You at seven, swinging your feet. You at sixteen, trying to look unafraid. You at twenty-three, exhausted and determined. You as you might be in old age, silver-haired and calm, watching you with unsurprised eyes.

At the head of the table there is one empty chair.

No one speaks until you sit down.

Then all of them look up together, and the oldest version of you slides a folder across the table. On the cover is your name, written in handwriting you almost recognize. You open it.

Inside there is only one sheet of paper.

It says:

WE HAVE BEEN TRYING TO REACH YOU
ABOUT THE LIFE YOU ARE ALREADY LIVING.

The city outside exhales. Somewhere, bells ring from the yellow trees. The walls of the meeting room become transparent, and beyond them you see the vast unfamiliar city again—but now it is less unfamiliar. Streets connect. Windows glow warmly. The silent crowds move with purpose, and when you look closely, each person has your face for just an instant before becoming themselves again.

The youngest you asks, “Will you attend?”

The tired you asks, “Will you stay?”

The oldest you smiles and asks, “Will you wake up knowing this counts?”

You try to answer, but your voice comes out as a gust of wind. The papers lift, swirling upward like white birds. The ceiling opens into sky. The clockless tower begins to ring the hour.

And just before you wake, the entire city turns toward you, waiting.
