…the walls begin to breathe.

At first you think it is exhaustion—the flicker of torchlight playing tricks across stone. But then you notice the mortar between the bricks is shifting, gently, like ribs under skin, and every time you solve a puzzle, the exhale of the maze rearranges the corridor behind you. There is no going back. There is only forward, and the puzzles are no longer merely *in* the maze; they *are* the maze.

You reach a chamber where the floor is a vast chessboard of black and white tiles, each tile inscribed with a single symbol: ↔, ¬, ∃, ⊕. A voice—not from anywhere, but from the idea of somewhere—says: *“Step only on truths.”* You look up and see the ceiling is a mirror, but in it, the symbols are different. You realize the floor states propositions, and the ceiling states their negations or transformations. To cross, you must hold both versions in your mind at once, walking only where the floor and ceiling agree under some consistent interpretation. One wrong step and the tile sinks into darkness, pulling at your ankle like thick sleep.

You solve it. The far wall dissolves.

Deeper now, the air tastes of copper and old paper. The next room is a library whose bookshelves form a spiral that seems to narrow toward a point both infinitely distant and right in front of you. Each book is a puzzle with no cover, only a first page and a last page, and the solution is to infer the entire missing middle. You open one:

*“This sentence is the only true sentence in this book.”*

You smile, despite the ache behind your eyes. Liar’s loop. You close it and choose another. Then another. The spiral tightens. You feel time behaving strangely—some puzzles take a heartbeat, others feel like years, and your memories of the outside world begin to feel like someone else’s notes in the margins.

At the center of the maze, there is no monster. There is only a small table, and on it, a blank card, and a pen that writes in smoke.

A door stands behind the table. It has no handle, only a plaque:

*“The final puzzle is the one you brought with you.”*

You pick up the pen. The smoke shapes itself into questions you have been avoiding: *What are you trying to solve? Why must every path have an answer? What if the maze lets you out the moment you stop trying to defeat it?*

You write, not a solution, but a sentence: *“I don’t know, and that is enough.”*

The smoke stills. The door swings open, not onto another corridor, but onto light—ordinary, pale morning light, the kind that falls across a bed and a half-remembered alarm. The maze exhales one last time, and the walls fold in like pages of a book someone has finally decided to close.

You step through.

And somewhere, in the waking world, your eyes begin to open.
