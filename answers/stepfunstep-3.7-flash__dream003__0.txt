As you examine it closely, you notice intricate patterns and tiny, raised ridges along its edges, not carved so much as pressed into the pale, warm resin of the box—the exact same shade as the seashell wind chime your grandma hung on her back porch when you were small, you realize suddenly, though you haven’t thought of that chime in years.

There’s no keyhole, no seam, no visible latch at all. You turn it over in your hands, and the light shifts across the ridges, and you notice they’re not random: the scalloped edge here matches the frayed hem of the blue dress you wore the day you fell off your bike and scraped your knee raw, and your grandma brought you a cherry popsicle that stained your tongue bright red. The sharp zigzag notch there is the route you walked when you missed the last bus home after your first high school football game, rain soaking through your hoodie, until a stranger in a pickup truck pulled over and offered you a ride without asking any questions.

You press your thumb to the zigzag ridge, half on instinct, half because the air around the box feels soft, like it’s waiting for you to make a move. The box hums, low and warm, the same frequency as the porch light your grandma left on for you when you stayed over as a kid. The lid creaks open, no sound of a lock clicking, just the slow, creaky sigh of old wood even though the box is made of resin.

You expect jewelry, or folded letters, or something heavy and valuable. Instead, the inside is lined with the same ridged pattern, and when you lean in, you can smell saltwater and cherry syrup and the faint, dusty scent of old paperback books. You reach in to touch the thing at the bottom, and your fingers close around cool, smooth glass—no, not glass, it’s the exact weight of the smooth gray rock you found on the beach that day you built a sandcastle with your little cousin, the one you kept on your windowsill for three years until it got lost in a move.

When you pull your hand out, you’re not holding a rock. You’re standing on that beach, the wind tugging at your hoodie strings, your cousin laughing as a wave splashes over his sneakers. You realize then what the box holds: not things, but the small, forgotten seconds you didn’t think to keep, the ones that usually slip through your fingers like sand. You stand there for a minute, watching the sun dip low over the water, and you think about staying, about reliving that afternoon forever, but then you hear the faint hum of the porch light from your grandma’s house in the distance, and you remember you have to go back, you have to wake up.

You close your hand around the memory of the rock, and when you open your eyes, you’re back in your bed, the box gone from your hands, but your thumb is still tingling where you pressed the zigzag ridge, and there’s a faint smudge of cherry red on your tongue, even though you didn’t eat a popsicle before you went to sleep.
