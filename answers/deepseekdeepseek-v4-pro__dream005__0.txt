The groups around you shift like schools of fish: a cluster of elderly women knitting with animated, glowing yarn; a circle of children playing a game whose rules they refuse to explain; a man in a tuxedo who winks and offers you a glass of champagne that turns into a tiny hummingbird the moment you touch it. You ask each group if they've seen your friend, but their answers form a chorus of riddles—"They went that way," says a woman whose voice sounds like wind through an empty house, pointing in three directions at once. The floor tilts slightly, and the room stretches long, like a hallway in a funhouse mirror. Your friend's name becomes a word you can taste—something like cinnamon and regret—but cannot speak. You push through a heavy velvet curtain and find yourself outside, where the party continues under a tangerine sky, the guests now wearing masks of your own face, all turning toward you with eyes that don't blink. "You're late," they say in unison, and you realize they mean you've been searching for yourself all along.
