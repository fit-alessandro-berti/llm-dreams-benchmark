The rooms keep changing as you pass through them. One moment you are in a bright kitchen full of laughter and clinking glasses, the next in a dim hallway lined with paintings whose faces seem to follow you, and then in an open backyard where the music is coming from somewhere you can’t quite see. Each group you approach falls briefly silent, then resumes talking as if you were never there. You smile, excuse yourself, ask if they’ve seen your friend, but the answers are vague and dreamlike:

“Maybe upstairs.”
“Try the room with the blue curtains.”
“You just missed them.”

You begin to feel that the person you know is not simply another guest, but the reason you came here at all. The search grows more urgent. You check your pockets for your phone, but it turns into a folded paper map, and the map is blank except for a single dot that keeps moving whenever you look away.

At the far end of the house, you find a door you don’t remember passing. It’s slightly open, and light spills through the gap. Voices murmur inside—one of them almost familiar. You push the door wider and step into a room that feels both enormous and intimate, like the inside of a memory.

Your one person is there, standing with their back turned.

You call their name, or think you do. They turn slowly, and for a moment their face is unclear, shifting between everyone you have ever loved and everyone you have ever lost. Then they smile, and the whole gathering seems to breathe with relief. The noise from the other rooms softens, the house settles, and you understand that you were never really lost.

You were being guided toward this moment.

And just as you reach out to them, the lights dim, the room dissolves, and you wake with the strange feeling that someone has finally found you.
