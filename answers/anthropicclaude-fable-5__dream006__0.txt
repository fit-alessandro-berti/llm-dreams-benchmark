...speak, but the words come out as paper. Actual paper—small white sheets unfolding from my mouth like origami birds, fluttering out over the audience. They catch them and read them, nodding seriously, as though this is how speeches have always been given.

I reach for my notes at the podium, but the podium is now a piano, and I realize with that strange dream-certainty that the speech was *always* meant to be played, not spoken. My fingers find the keys and somehow I know the melody, though I've never learned it. Each note that rings out becomes a sentence the audience understands.

The crowd begins to hum along, and I notice my third-grade teacher in the front row, sitting next to someone who is both my oldest friend and a complete stranger. This contradiction doesn't bother me at all.

Then I look down and realize I'm not wearing shoes—but instead of panic, the audience erupts into applause. Of course. The bare feet were the most important part of the speech. Everyone knows that.

The auditorium's walls dissolve gently into a field at dusk, the audience now seated in tall grass, fireflies drifting between them like slow applause made visible. Someone asks a question from the back, but their voice arrives as the smell of rain.

I open my mouth to answer, and that's when I begin to float upward, the crowd waving warmly below, growing small, the piano still playing itself, and I think: *I should write this down when I wake*—

—already knowing, the way you always know, that I won't quite remember how it ends.
