...and it is the only still thing in the whole world.

Everything else keeps revising itself. The hills breathe from green into a color I have no word for, something between amber and grief. Shapes lose their nerve halfway through being circles. A tree unfolds into a staircase, apologizes, folds back. But the canvas just stands there on its three thin legs, patient as a held breath, and the white of it is so absolute that looking at it feels like remembering.

I have no brush. I check my hands twice, the way you do in dreams, and both times they are the wrong hands — too many knuckles, or fingers that end in small warm lights. So I do the only thing available: I lean forward and breathe on it.

The breath lands as a smear of blue.

I understand then that I'm supposed to be careful, and immediately I am not. I press my palm to the linen and pull, and out comes a road. I drag my thumb sideways and it becomes evening. I lay my cheek against the canvas and when I lift away there is a house on it, my grandmother's house, though I painted it without meaning to and the windows are in the wrong places, or rather in the places they were in before someone moved them, which is to say the right places, which is to say I am crying a little without any change in my face.

The landscape behind me goes quiet. I don't have to turn to know it's watching. The hills have stopped rehearsing their colors. Everything is waiting to see what I decide.

So I paint a door on the house. It takes one stroke.

And the door opens outward, out of the canvas, into the air, at a scale that makes no sense — I can see the hallway, the runner rug worn pale down the middle, the particular gold of a lamp left on for someone still driving home. Warm air comes out. It smells like rain on a screen door.

I step toward it and the canvas does the thing I was afraid of: it turns. Not physically. It simply reorients, the way a word does when you stare too long, and now I am the white space and the house is looking at me, and someone inside it is leaning forward, breathing onto the surface of me, trying to draw something they half-remember, getting the windows wrong on purpose so I'll recognize them.

*Hold still*, they say. *You keep changing.*

And I try. God, I try to hold one shape. But the colors move through me like weather, and I feel myself becoming a road, an evening, a staircase that apologizes, and the last thing I manage before the brushstroke lands is to make my mouth into the shape of the word

*yes*

— and then the alarm, or the light through the curtain, or the sound of a screen door, far off, closing very gently, so as not to wake anyone.
