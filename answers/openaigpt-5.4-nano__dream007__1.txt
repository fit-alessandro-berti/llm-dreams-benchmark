…you decide to end it the only way you can think of: by walking straight through the thickest part of the argument.

The world tilts, like a picture being turned on its edge. Words—your words and theirs—stop being sounds and turn into drifting shapes: sharp, black birds that peck at the air between you. Each time one of you tries to explain, the birds multiply, and the space fills with them until you can hardly see your friend’s face.

You force yourself forward anyway.

You don’t interrupt anymore. You stop trying to win. You simply say the truth that sits under the anger, the one you’ve been stepping around every time you speak: that this matters so much because it touches something real—something you’ve been afraid to admit you need. The moment you say it, the birds recoil as if the words have weight. The air thickens. Your friend flinches, and for the first time, the argument loses its heat.

Then the dream does something strange: it gives you a doorway.

Between you and your friend there is suddenly a narrow frame of light, like the outline of a door hung in midair. It isn’t bright like a hallway—it’s the soft, gray light you see before morning, when everything feels possible but nothing is decided yet.

Your friend stares at it, confused. You look too, and realize the frame is shaped like the exact moment in the conversation where everything started going wrong.

You step toward it.

Your feet don’t make noise. The birds fall away from you and land on the threshold like ashes. When you pass through the doorway, you don’t enter another room—you enter another version of the same moment, one where you’re still mid-argument, but the temperature is different.

Here, your friend’s face is clearer. Their mouth opens, and the first sentence they try to say comes out as a whisper—not because they’re quiet, but because the dream is asking you to listen. You do. You really do. You hear the sentence underneath the sentence: *I’m scared you’ll leave,* their expression says, *and I’m angry because I don’t know how else to ask you to stay.*

You feel it land in your chest like something fitting into a slot that’s been empty for a long time.

You turn back to your friend—still on the other side of the doorway, still half-lit by the argument-shaped birds that have stopped flying. You don’t apologize for your feelings; you just offer your hand.

“Let’s start over,” you say, and it isn’t a plea. It’s a decision.

The doorway widens. For a moment the two of you stand in the same space, but the argument is nowhere to be found. In its place is a quiet that feels unfamiliar, like waking up in the same house after a storm has blown through and you don’t yet know what’s broken.

Your friend takes your hand.

And then—because this is a dream and dreams never let you keep the same rules forever—the argument returns, but transformed. It becomes a long corridor made of conversation, lined with shelves of old memories. Each memory is a small, glowing box. When either of you reaches for one, the boxes open and show exactly what was meant the first time.

You realize the real fight wasn’t about the surface issue at all. It was about safety, and being heard, and the fear that love can’t survive misunderstanding.

The corridor stretches on, but instead of disappearing, it becomes a path you can walk together—one gentle step at a time—while the rest of the world waits behind you, holding its breath.

At the end of the corridor, there’s no conclusion written on a wall. No final verdict. Only a choice.

You can continue the conversation carefully, or you can wake up.

In the half-second before you decide, your friend squeezes your hand and says, softly, “What if we stop trying to be right… and start trying to understand?”

You wake—mid-breath—with the taste of morning in your mouth, still feeling their hand in yours, still hearing that sentence like it’s echoing from inside your ribs.
