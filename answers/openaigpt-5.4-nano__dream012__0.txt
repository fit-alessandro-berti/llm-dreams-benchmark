—you refuse to panic.

Instead, you step onto the bridge with your boots planted wide, as if you’re holding the whole deck steady with your stance alone. The wind screams through the rigging like a living thing, and the waves strike the hull with a dull, patient violence. The compass quivers, then snaps toward a new direction that you didn’t set. In the teeth of the storm, the world seems to rearrange itself around that needle, as though the sea has a will and it’s listening.

“Captain,” someone yells over the howl, “we’re losing power! The sails—!”

You raise a hand. Your glove is already wet, though you’re sure it wasn’t a moment ago. The droplets on your fingers don’t fall; they hover, shimmering, as if gravity is briefly negotiable.

“Listen,” you say, and somehow your voice cuts through the roar.

Your crew leans in—not toward you, but toward the sound you’ve found inside the storm: a rhythm beneath the crashing, a pulse hidden in the chaos. You realize the wind is repeating something. Not words. A pattern. A slow count. Like a lighthouse blinking in an ocean too wild to see.

“Rowers,” you call, though no one remembers the ship ever carrying oars. “Bring the boatswain’s whistle!”

They stare at you, confused, because they can’t imagine where the whistle could be. But then you’re holding it—metal cold, familiar in your palm—though you don’t remember taking it from anywhere. That’s how dreams work: objects appear when the decision is made.

You blow three short blasts.

For a heartbeat, everything stops. The storm doesn’t quiet—it *hesitates*. The waves freeze mid-impact like paintings that haven’t decided to become water. The lantern glass on the bridge stops vibrating. Even the rope in the rigging seems to hold its breath.

Then the ship lurches, not away from danger, but toward it—straight into the darkest pocket of the sea, the place where the storm looks thickest and most certain.

Your first mate grabs your arm. “Captain! That’s—”

“Where it’s hardest,” you say. “That’s where it’s listening.”

You grip the wheel. The wheel is not wood anymore. It feels like bone, warm and alive, and when you turn it, you can sense the ship responding not to wind or current, but to intention. The compass stops jittering. The needle locks true.

A low sound rolls across the water, deeper than thunder—something enormous shifting beneath the surface. Your crew begins to pray, to bargain, to shout theories. But you keep watching the seam of the storm, the way the black cloud folds around the ship as if the sky itself is trying to close a door.

“Now,” you say softly, almost kindly, “we speak back.”

You blow the whistle again—three short blasts, then one long.

The sea answers.

A thin ribbon of calmer water unfurls in front of the bow, as if you’ve opened a zipper across the horizon. It is not an escape route carved by rescue; it’s a path you have negotiated into existence. Around it, the waves still rage, but they begin to curve away, like stubborn people giving you room without admitting they moved.

Your crew’s faces change. Fear loosens its grip and becomes awe—still afraid, but aligned with you now. They start shouting directions that make sense because you’ve taught them what to look for: the way the water darkens at the edges, the way the lightning seems to pause before it strikes.

“Steady!” you command. “Keep her pointed—no matter what the sea tries to suggest!”

The ship surges forward, faster than it should, as though the calm corridor is pulling it like a current. The wind remains furious, but it’s furious somewhere else. On the bridge, the air grows warmer. Salt still stings your lips, but it tastes different—less like punishment, more like coming home.

A final gust hits the side of the ship like a challenge. Your crew flinches. The horizon flickers. The storm threatens to swallow the corridor you’ve made.

You grip the wheel harder, and the bone-wood steadies under your hands.

In the very center of the storm, you see something impossible: a lantern burning under the surface, bright and patient, casting light that doesn’t travel through air so much as through doubt. The lantern swings as though it’s attached to a hook in the ocean’s memory.

You understand then—half in logic, half in dream—that the sea isn’t trying to destroy you.

It’s trying to make sure you are paying attention.

“Captain,” your first mate says, voice trembling with relief. “What do we do when we reach it?”

You smile, though it feels strange to smile while waves still claw at the hull. “We don’t celebrate yet,” you say. “We navigate.”

And as the calm corridor widens, as the storm peels away like a curtain, you steer not toward safety alone, but toward the lantern’s light—toward the place where the world is waiting to be understood.

Your ship slips from darkness into a thin, silvery morning that should not exist after this long.

Your crew quiets, breath by breath.

Then you wake—still holding the wheel—wondering, with a sudden ache of wonder, whether the turbulence was ever outside you at all, or if you simply learned how to command it.
