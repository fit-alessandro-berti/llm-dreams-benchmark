*"Describe, in detail, the moment you decided to become who you are."*

Your pen hovers. The clock on the wall has no hands, but you can hear it ticking anyway. Around you, other students are writing furiously, though when you glance at their papers, the words are just scribbles—distant birdsong rendered in ink.

You look back at your own page. The question has changed.

*"What would you have done differently, if you knew it would all turn out exactly the same?"*

The fluorescent lights above you hum a low, familiar tune—one your mother used to sing. You realize the classroom is your childhood bedroom. The desk is the small table where you once did homework in crayon. The proctor at the front of the room is your younger self, watching you with an expression that is both expectant and impossibly tired.

You try to write. Nothing comes. The pen leaves no mark.

The younger you sighs and says, "You never could finish the things that mattered, could you?"

You wake up. The dream fades like chalk on a wet sidewalk, but the question lingers, and you cannot remember whether you answered it or whether you ever will.
