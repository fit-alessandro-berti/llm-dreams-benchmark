The crew does not shout. They stand motionless at their posts, their faces turned upward, not in fear, but in recognition. The lightning that forks across the black sky does not flash; it hums, a low vibration that rattles your teeth. You try to steer, but the rudder has dissolved into mist. There is no resistance in the water anymore.

You look down at the deck, and the planks have become translucent glass. Beneath them, the turbulent sea is not water, but a swirling galaxy of stars and nebulas, crashing against the hull with the sound of chimes. The storm is not trying to sink you; it is trying to lift you.

You release the wheel. It turns into a flock of white birds that scatter into the gale.

"Let go," you say, though your voice is not your own. It sounds like the wind passing through a canyon.

The ship groans, a sound of wood stretching into metal, then into light. The horizon line blurs. The distinction between the sky and the sea vanishes. You are no longer standing on a deck; you are floating in the eye of the storm, suspended in a bubble of silence. The crew turns to you, their features softening into the shapes of memories you have long forgotten. They smile, and then they dissolve into the rain.

The rain does not wet you. It feels like falling upward.

The ship tilts, not into the water, but into the clouds. You feel the familiar lurch of a dream ending, the sensation of falling from a great height, but this time, the ground never comes. Instead, you drift through a door made of sea foam, and the salt taste on your lips remains as your eyes flutter open to the morning light.
