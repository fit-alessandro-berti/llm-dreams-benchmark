Despite the hardships, you decide to **follow the sound of running water**.

It seems impossible—water is the first thing to vanish in a world like this—but you heard it last night, threading through the ruins like a silver needle: *tink, tink, tink*. You pack what little you have into a satchel made from an old road map, and set out at dawn.

The city breathes dust. Skyscrapers lean against each other like tired giants sharing secrets. Cars sit rusted in the streets, their windows frosted with ash, and somewhere a wind chime made of broken glass sings in a doorway. You walk beneath a sky the color of bruised plums, where two suns sometimes flicker—one real, one a memory the world keeps forgetting to erase.

By midday you reach the old library.

Its doors are long gone, and inside the books have turned to nests. But on the wall someone has painted, in bright blue paint that couldn’t possibly still be wet, an arrow pointing east. Beneath it, in a child’s careful handwriting:

*The river remembers.*

You follow the arrow. The streets begin to change. Asphalt softens into moss. Cracks in the pavement sprout white flowers that close when you look directly at them. You pass a fountain in a plaza where a statue of a soldier has been overgrown with vines, and the vines have shaped themselves into wings. The soldier is weeping stone tears, and in the basin at his feet, water trembles—clear, living water.

You cup your hands and drink.

It tastes like childhood. Like rain on a tin roof. Like the name of someone you once loved and can’t quite remember.

Further on, the buildings fall away entirely, and you find the river.

It shouldn’t exist, but it does, winding through the wasteland like a vein of light. On its banks, others have gathered—not many, but enough. A woman tends a fire of blue driftwood. A man mends a solar lantern that flickers with captured starlight. Children chase fireflies that spell out words in the dusk: *home, home, home.*

They look up as you approach, but no one raises a weapon. In dreams, sometimes, fear is the first thing to die.

“We’ve been waiting,” the woman says, though you know you only just decided to come. “The water calls back everyone who still believes in green things.”

You sit by the fire. Someone offers you a bowl of soup made from mushrooms that glow faintly in the dark. You eat. You feel the warmth spread through your chest, lighting up rooms inside you that had been locked for a very long time.

And then you notice, on the other side of the fire, a door.

It stands alone in the grass, no wall around it, paint peeling, handle brass and warm. You know this door. You have dreamed it before. Behind it is not another room, but the rest of your life.

The fire pops. The river murmurs its old song.

You stand.

You walk toward the door.

Your hand reaches for the handle—

And the dream, as dreams often do, holds its breath, waiting for you to choose whether to open it.
