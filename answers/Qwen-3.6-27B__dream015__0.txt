Despite the hardships, you decide to **plant a garden in the ruins of an old library.**

The irony isn’t lost on you—knowledge once stored here is now dust, but life can still take root. You spend days scavenging for seeds hidden in tins of canned goods and collecting rainwater in cracked bathtubs. The air smells of wet ash and decay, but as your first sprout breaks through the soil between two collapsed bookshelves, it smells like hope.

One evening, while tending to the fragile green shoots, you hear a rustle behind you. Your hand goes to the knife at your belt, heart pounding against your ribs. But it’s not a raider or a mutated beast. It’s a small, shivering dog, its fur matted with dirt, eyes wide with hunger.

You hesitate. Trust is a luxury you can’t afford. But the dog doesn’t attack; it whimpers, watching you with a quiet desperation that mirrors your own. Slowly, you break off a piece of dried jerky from your pack and toss it gently onto the ground. The dog eats quickly, then sits, looking up at you.

In that moment, amidst the crumbling concrete and silent shelves, you realize you’re no longer just surviving. You’re building something. A sanctuary. A community of one human, one dog, and a dozen tiny green lives fighting back against the gray world.

As night falls, you build a small fire using dry pages from books that have already been read into oblivion. The flames dance, casting long shadows across the walls. You sit beside the dog, who curls up near your feet, and for the first time in years, you don’t feel alone. You close your eyes, not to sleep, but to dream of what might grow next.
