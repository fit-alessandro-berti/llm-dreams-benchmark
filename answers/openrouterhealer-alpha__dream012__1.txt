I grip the splintered rail as the world tilts again, saltwater and rain indistinguishable as they lash across the deck. The sky is a bruised, churning violet—no stars, no moon, just the deafening roar of wind and wave. The ship groans beneath us, a living thing in agony.

“Captain!” My first mate, Elara, shouts over the gale, her face pale in the intermittent flash of sheet lightning. “She’s taking on water in the forward hold! The pumps can’t keep up!”

The crew’s eyes find me in the chaos. Young Finn, barely seventeen, clutches a line, knuckles white. Old Marco, the navigator, is bent over his useless charts, as if sheer will could steady the compass needle that dances like a mad thing.

I taste metal—the taste of fear—on my tongue. The old charts warned of these waters: *The Devil’s Teeth*, they called it. A graveyard of ships that ignored the storms. My own father’s ship was lost here, swallowed whole by a wave just like this one.

But dreams, I realize, are not about the storm. They are about what you do in its heart.

I take a breath, filling my lungs with the violent air. “Helmsman!” I roar, my voice cutting through the din. “Hold her steady on the current! Do not fight the sea—listen to her!”

Elara’s eyes widen. “Captain, that current pulls us straight for the reef!”

“I know,” I say, and I do. I can feel it—a deep, inexorable pull toward jagged, unseen teeth below. But I also feel something else: a rhythm, a pulse in the chaos. A pattern in the waves my father once described in a fever dream, years before his last voyage.

“Finn! Take three men and secure the mainmast with every bolt of canvas we have left! Marco—throw the cargo. Every crate. Make us lighter.”

We begin to throw. Crates of spices, silks, unknown artifacts from a distant port splash into the boiling water. The ship shudders, rises a fraction.

The wave comes then. A mountain of black water, taller than the mast, rising up like a final verdict.

This is the moment. The dream holds its breath.

I look at my crew—not at their fear, but at the trust still burning there, stubborn and bright. I do not give an order. I do not shout a command.

Instead, I sing.

It’s a low, guttural shanty from my father’s coast, a song about finding a thread of calm in the heart of chaos. A song about listening to the deep places. One by one, Elara joins, then Marco, then Finn, his voice cracking but strong.

We sing into the face of the wave. And in the dream-logic, the sea *listens*.

The wave does not break upon us. It lifts us—impossibly, dangerously—up its vast, liquid face, carries us soaring over the very reef we were sailing toward, and deposits us, shuddering and gasping, into the sudden, profound stillness of the leeward side.

The storm still rages behind us, a wall of sound and fury. But here, in the eye of the dream, there is only quiet. The crew stares, dumbfounded, at the calm water and the now-distant storm.

Elara turns to me, her eyes wide. “How…?”

I look at my hands. They are steady now. “The sea doesn’t speak in words,” I say, the dream-words feeling truer than anything. “It speaks in rhythm. You don’t command it. You… ask it.”

And as I say it, I realize the storm wasn’t trying to kill us. It was trying to teach us. To show us that the greatest guidance isn’t a shouted order, but a shared song.

The dream begins to soften at the edges now, the image of the calm sea blurring. But the feeling remains—the weight of the captain’s hat, the trust of the crew, and the profound lesson of the deep.

You wake with the taste of salt on your lips and the echo of a song in your bones.
