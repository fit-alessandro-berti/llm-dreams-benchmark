You are at a large social gathering where you only know one person. As you try to find them, you encounter various groups of people and each one pulls you in just long enough to make you forget what you were doing.

First, a circle laughing over something you didn’t hear. One of them hands you a drink the color of sunset and says “You made it!” like you’ve been expected all along. You nod, sip, and the liquid tastes like memory — familiar but you can’t place it. When you try to ask about your friend, they’ve already turned back to their story.

You drift toward a table of food that keeps changing. When you look down it’s sushi, when you blink it’s tiny pastries, then bowls of something steaming that smells like your childhood kitchen. Someone puts a plate in your hands and says “Don’t let this one get away,” and for a second you’re not sure if they mean the food or the person you’re looking for.

Down a hallway that wasn’t there before, there’s music. A group is dancing without touching the floor, feet skimming just above the tile. They beckon you in. The beat matches your heartbeat, and suddenly you’re worried that if you stop moving, the dream will notice you don’t belong and wake you up.

Then the crowd parts, and there — at the edge of the balcony, back to you — is the one person you know. They’re wearing a coat you don’t recognize, talking to a stranger. You call their name, but the sound comes out muffled, like you’re underwater. 

You push through the last few people and tap their shoulder. They turn around.
