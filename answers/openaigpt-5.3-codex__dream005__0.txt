…every group seems to be speaking a different language, except it isn’t language so much as **textures**—one circle talks in crackling laughter like fire, another in soft velvet whispers, another in glassy chimes that make your teeth hum.

You keep saying your friend’s name, but each time it leaves your mouth, it turns into a paper bird and flutters away into the crowd.

You follow one of the birds through a hallway that wasn’t there before. The party stretches impossibly long now: ballroom, greenhouse, school cafeteria, train station—each room opening into the next like shuffled cards. Everywhere, people smile as if they recognize you, and hand you strange things “for your friend”: a key made of sugar, a map with no labels, a coat check ticket stamped with a moon.

At last you reach a quiet room with a single mirror leaning against the wall. Your friend is standing in front of it, back turned, wearing the coat you lost years ago.

You rush over and touch their shoulder.

They turn around—

and it’s you, a little older, a little calmer, holding two cups of tea.

“Sorry,” they say, like they’ve been waiting all night. “You were hard to find.”

Then, from somewhere far away, someone calls your real name, and the whole party inhales at once, lights dimming like eyelids, and you wake with the faint taste of sugar on your tongue and the weird certainty that you forgot to bring the key.
