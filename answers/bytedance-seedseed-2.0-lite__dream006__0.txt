When you lift it, it’s a mason jar full of fireflies, their gold lights blinking in unison, and you can’t remember why you brought it, but it feels right, like you’d packed it for this day your whole life.

The mic is warm when you lean into it, and you open your mouth to deliver the scripted speech you’d rehearsed for weeks, about belonging and the gaps we leave between each other. But the words that come out aren’t the polished ones you memorized. They’re about the time you lost your favorite stuffed rabbit at a grocery store when you were seven, and cried so hard the cashier gave you a lollipop you never ate, still tucked in a cardboard box under your bed. You mention the way your middle school best friend used to leave sticky notes with bad dinosaur doodles on your locker, and you lost touch after high school but still think of her when you pass a sunflower field. The fireflies in the jar start to hum, soft as a lullaby, and the stage beneath your feet shifts—no longer polished wood, but the splintered dock of the lake your family visited every summer, lake water lapping at the toes of your sneakers.

You glance out at the crowd then, and realize they’re not the strangers you’d expected. Your first childhood dog, the one that passed when you were 12, is curled in the front row, his tail thumping when you meet his eyes. Your 5th grade teacher, who told you your messy little stories were worth sharing, is clapping so hard her glasses slip down her nose. Even the stranger you smiled at in a coffee shop last week, who you’d never see again, is there, grinning like he’s been waiting to hear this too. One firefly slips out of the jar, then another, and soon they’re drifting over the crowd, lighting up every tear and grin, and someone yells from the back, “We missed those small stories!” and the whole crowd cheers so loud the water ripples.

You set the empty jar on the edge of the dock, and you don’t feel nervous anymore. You start to tell them all the tiny, unimportant, vital things you’d buried under the “big important speech” you thought you had to give: the night you snuck out to watch meteors with your cousin, the time you accidentally dyed your hair neon blue as a teen, the way you still sing off-key to the Disney soundtracks you loved as a kid. The crowd drifts closer, some sitting on the dock next to you, passing around a jar of lemonade that appeared out of nowhere, and for a second you think this is the only speech you ever needed to give. Just as you’re about to tell them you used to catch fireflies in that exact same mason jar as a kid, your alarm blares, soft at first then loud, and you reach for your phone, already clinging to the glow of the fireflies still burning behind your eyes.
