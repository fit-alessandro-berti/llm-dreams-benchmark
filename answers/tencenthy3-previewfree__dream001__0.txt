You start to run, your dress shoes slapping hard against the pavement, but the faster you move, the more the city seems to stretch and distort. The buildings loom taller, their windows glowing with a sickly amber light that offers no warmth. You stop to ask a passerby for directions, opening your mouth to speak, but no sound comes out. You try again, but your voice is lost in a vacuum.

Frustrated, you look down at the map you’re clutching—the one you didn't remember picking up—but the ink begins to run like black tears, blurring the street names into illegible smudges. The sky suddenly darkens, not to night, but to the deep, bruised purple of a storm that never quite breaks.

Panic sets in. You round a corner and find yourself in a plaza you recognize, yet none of the shops are the same. A clock tower looms ahead, its hands spinning wildly, ticking backwards and forwards in a frantic dance. You check your watch; it says you have five minutes left, but the clock tower reads midnight.

You burst through a set of heavy glass doors, thinking you’ve found the office building, only to realize you are in a grand, empty ballroom. Hundreds of chairs are arranged in rows, facing a stage draped in heavy velvet curtains. The room is silent, save for the sound of your own ragged breathing.

On the stage, a single figure sits at a grand piano, their back to you. They play a melody that sounds like a memory you can’t quite place—sweet, but haunting. You walk down the aisle, your footsteps echoing like gunshots. As you reach the front, the playing stops abruptly. The figure on the stage slowly turns around, and you freeze, realizing it is a version of yourself, wearing the suit you planned to wear, smiling with teeth that are just a little too white.

"You're late," the figure whispers, though their lips don't move. "But don't worry. The meeting was yesterday."
