You don't remember picking it up. But it's in your hands now, heavier than it should be, lighter than it looks.

A sound rises from inside—not a rattle. A hum. Your grandmother's lullaby, but played backward on a music box winding down.

The patterns shift. The birds become keys. The spirals become keyholes. There are seventeen of them, or maybe three, or maybe the number changes when you stop counting.

Your thumb finds a depression shaped like a teardrop. You press.

The box sighs open.

Inside: not gold, not letters, not jewels.

A single white feather. A child's baby tooth. The smell of rain on hot asphalt. A memory you never had—standing on a pier at dusk, someone's hand in yours, the water burning with reflected sky.

And a note, written in your own handwriting, dated tomorrow:

*"You already know how to close it. You just forgot you were the one who opened it first."*

The lid begins to shut on its own.

You wake with your fist clenched around nothing, the hum still vibrating in your palm.
