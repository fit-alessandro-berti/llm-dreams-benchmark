...and each one is stranger than the last.

The first round is a written exam, but the questions keep rewriting themselves while you read them. Your pencil is soft, like it's made of cheese, and every answer you write dissolves into the paper like a secret being kept.

Round two is the interview. The interviewer sits behind an enormous desk, and every time you blink, they're someone different — your third-grade teacher, an astronaut, your own face wearing glasses you've never owned. They ask only one question, over and over, in different voices: *"But what would you do on a rainy Tuesday?"* Each answer you give makes the room either brighter or dimmer, though no one explains why.

The other competitors begin to vanish. One by one they're called through a door at the far end of the hall, and they don't come back. Someone whispers that the door is a good sign. Someone else whispers that it isn't. Nobody knows who told them.

The final challenge is announced by a loudspeaker that sounds uncannily like your own voice: *"Please proceed to the roof."* The staircase spirals up far higher than the building is tall. At the top, the roof is a field of tall grass beneath a sky with two moons, and in the middle of it sits a single desk with a nameplate that reads **YOUR NAME**.

A person in a grey suit — you understand, somehow, that this is the boss — hands you a small brass key.

"Congratulations," they say. "The job is yours. It begins every night, the moment you fall asleep."

You ask what the job actually is.

They smile. "You've been doing it the whole time."

Then the desk begins to sink gently into the grass, or perhaps you begin to float — it's hard to tell — and the loudspeaker crackles one last time, saying your name in your mother's voice, and that's the moment—

—you wake up, with the overwhelming feeling that you're *late* for something wonderful.
