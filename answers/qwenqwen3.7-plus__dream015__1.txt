...open a teahouse in the center of the irradiated glass desert, serving cups of steaming nostalgia to the wasteland's most fearsome monsters. 

You don't have tea leaves, of course. Instead, you gather handfuls of crushed radio static, dried echoes of old symphonies, and the faint, silver dust left behind by shooting stars. When you pour the boiling water—which you heat not with fire, but by whispering warm secrets to the kettle until it blushes and bubbles—the liquid turns the color of a bruised twilight and smells exactly like your childhood home on a rainy Sunday.

The dangers of this world soon find you. A pack of rust-scarred scavengers, riding motorcycles made of fused spine-bones, surround your fragile, crooked little stall. Their eyes glow a feral, toxic green. In the waking world, this would be the end. But this is a dream, and dream logic bends the rules. 

The leader, a towering figure draped in scavenged hazard tape and wire, removes his spiked helmet. Underneath, his face is not flesh, but a mosaic of cracked, beautiful porcelain. He sits down at a wobbly wrought-iron table, the metal groaning in protest, and asks, in a voice like wind chimes, if you have anything with a little honey.

You pour him a cup. As he drinks, the green glow in his eyes softens to a gentle, dandelion yellow. He leaves a single, perfect gear on the table as payment, which immediately sprouts tiny, iridescent leaves and roots itself into the ash.

Word spreads through the dunes. Mutated coyotes with three eyes curl up beneath your tables, purring like diesel engines. Sand-worms the size of subway cars rise from the cracked earth, not to devour, but to listen to the jazz music that spontaneously bleeds from your ancient, unplugged radio. 

The scarcity of the world begins to unravel. The more tea you pour, the more the obsidian ruins around you transform. Twisted rebar blooms into weeping willows made of spun copper. The toxic fog lifts, revealing a sky not of choking smog, but of swirling, bioluminescent nebulas, close enough to touch. 

You feel a profound sense of peace, a heavy, warm blanket settling over your shoulders. You reach out to touch the porcelain face of the scavenger, who is now smiling, but as your fingers make contact, the world begins to dissolve into pixels of warm, golden light. 

The smell of ozone and cinnamon fades. The sound of the wind chimes stretches out, elongating into the steady, rhythmic beep of an alarm clock. 

You open your eyes. You are back in your bed. But as you stretch, you swear you can still taste a hint of silver dust and honey on your tongue, and tucked beneath your pillow is a single, tiny gear, sprouting iridescent leaves.
