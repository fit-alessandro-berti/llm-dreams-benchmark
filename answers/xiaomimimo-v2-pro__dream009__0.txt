I open my eyes to the smell of damp earth and woodsmoke. My soft, modern mattress has been replaced by a mat of woven rushes, and the air is cool and still—so still I can hear the flutter of a moth’s wings against a distant candle flame.

I sit up slowly, my fingers tracing the rough wool of the blanket. No glowing screens. No hum of electronics. Just the low crackle of a hearth and the deep, patient silence of a world powered by wind, water, and human hands.

I realize I need to… **remember how to listen.**

Not just with ears, but with my whole being—to the creak of timber telling the story of the house, to the cadence of voices speaking a familiar yet strange old tongue, to the whispers of the land itself. In a world without data streams, information lives in patterns, in spoken stories, in the way the light falls through a smoke hole.

I need to rediscover patience, the kind measured in seasons and sunrise-to-sunset labor. I need to learn the names of herbs that heal, the rhythms of planting, the unspoken language of a shared glance that holds more truth than a thousand written words.

And most of all, I realize I need to unlearn the compulsion to *do*, and instead learn to simply *be*—present in each moment, in this quiet, profound, and achingly beautiful dream of a time before timekeeping, where the heart beats to the rhythm of the world.
