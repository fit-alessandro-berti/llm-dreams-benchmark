You open your mouth to speak, but no sound comes out. You clear your throat, tap the microphone—a dull thud echoes through the hall—yet your voice is trapped somewhere deep in your chest. The audience leans forward, their faces blurring at the edges, as if someone is smudging charcoal over their features. A few of them are now wearing masks—not costume masks, but smooth, porcelain ones with empty eye holes.

You look down at your notes, but the words are swimming on the page, rearranging themselves into a language you don’t recognize. The paper feels like silk, then like sand, crumbling between your fingers. A wave of cold panic washes over you, but then you remember: this is a dream. The realization hits with a strange, electric clarity.

You decide to abandon the speech entirely. You drop the crumbled notes and step closer to the edge of the stage. “You’re all part of my dream,” you announce, and this time your voice booms, too loud, reverberating endlessly. The audience doesn’t react. Instead, they begin to stand up in unison, a sea of rustling clothes and blank masks, and they start to climb onto the stage. They move slowly, deliberately, not threatening but inevitable, like rising water.

You back away, but the stage has lengthened behind you, stretching into a dark corridor lined with velvet curtains. You turn and run. The crowd doesn’t chase; they simply watch, their heads turning slowly as you flee. The corridor leads to a wing of the theater you’ve never seen—a dusty room filled with mannequins wearing your clothes from different stages of your life. A child’s sweater, a graduation gown, a work suit.

On a small table in the center of the room sits a single, unopened envelope. You know, without knowing how, that inside is the speech you were meant to give. You reach for it, and as your fingers brush the paper, the entire room dissolves into light.
