…begin to notice that the walls are breathing.

The early puzzles were clean and cold: a sudoku carved into obsidian, a Tower of Hanoi whose disks groan like cellar doors when you move them. But the deeper corridors misbehave. The tumblers of a combination lock rearrange themselves while you stare at them. A riddle is whispered in the kitchen of your childhood home, in a voice you almost recognize—and when you ask to hear it again, the riddle is now about a person asking to hear it again.

The maze is learning you.

Worse: each solution costs something small. After the chess problem—white to mate in one, except the king is your father and refuses to move—you can no longer remember the color of your first front door. After the logic grid, you trade away the smell of rain. You pay anyway. What else is there to do? The walls inhale, and exhale you deeper.

The corridors narrow. The puzzles grow *simpler*, which is the frightening part. Differential equations become long division; long division becomes a child's shape-sorter. You stand there holding the star-shaped block, hands shaking, because you cannot remember which hole it goes in. The simple things are hardest here. You try to count your fingers and arrive at a different number each time.

At the center there is no minotaur. There is a desk. On it: one sheet of paper, a pencil worn to a nub. The paper reads:

*You have solved puzzles your whole life. One remains. What were you looking for when you came in?*

You can't remember. You truly can't. The pencil is somehow already in your hand. The walls hold their breath. Far behind you—impossibly far—the entrance swings open, and someone steps inside, and their footsteps sound exactly like yours.

You bend over the page and write the answer.

And then you are awake, and the answer is gone the way dream-answers go, leaving only its shape behind: something warm, something with a heartbeat, something that had been looking for you, too.
