I reach for a pencil, but as my fingers brush it, the wood dissolves into a handful of dry dandelion seeds that scatter across the desk. There is no panic, though. Instead, a strange, heavy calm washes over me, the kind you only feel in the deep layers of sleep.

I look closer at "Question 1." It doesn't ask for a mathematical formula or a historical date. Instead, it pulses with the specific, nostalgic scent of rain on hot summer asphalt, paired with the faint, muffled sound of a piano playing in another room. The instructions, written in faint, glowing ink, read: *To answer, simply hum the melody back.*

I close my eyes, find the pitch in my throat, and hum. As the note leaves my lips, the constellation shifts, arranging itself into a perfect, glowing checkmark. 

The next question is a floating, porcelain teacup that never spills, no matter how the room tilts. It asks me to describe the exact color of my happiest thought. I dip my index finger into an inkwell that feels like liquid starlight and write a single word on the parchment: *Balance*. 

Suddenly, the proctor begins to walk down the aisles. But it isn't a stern professor. It is a younger version of myself, maybe seven years old, wearing a graduation gown that is comically oversized, the sleeves swallowing my small hands. 

The younger me doesn't check my answers or look at the clock, which I now notice has no numbers, only the phases of the moon. Instead, they lean over my desk, smile a knowing, gap-toothed smile, and whisper, "You don't have to prove anything here. You already passed just by getting this far."

As they say this, the exam paper dissolves into a flock of white origami birds. They lift off the desk, fluttering up toward the ceiling, which is no longer plaster, but a wide, open twilight sky. 

I take a deep breath, feeling the anxiety entirely replaced by a quiet, steady confidence, and let myself wake up.
