Near the kitchen, a circle of strangers is laughing at a joke you missed. One of them turns to you and says, “You’re just in time,” then hands you a glass full of something that looks like water but smells faintly like rain on pavement. Before you can ask what it is, the room shifts, and the kitchen is suddenly a train station.

You move through the crowd, still searching for the one person you know. You catch glimpses of them everywhere: their coat disappearing around a corner, their voice rising from behind a closed door, their reflection in a dark window. Every time you get close, someone stops you.

A group of children asks if you have seen their balloon. A table of old friends insists you sit down because they saved you a place. A woman in a silver mask tells you, “You already found them once. That should be enough.”

You keep walking.

The music grows louder, though no one is dancing. The walls of the house stretch upward into a hotel lobby, then shrink into the hallway of a school you forgot you attended. You pass rooms full of people rehearsing speeches, building towers out of teacups, whispering into seashells. Everyone seems to recognize you, but no one knows your name.

At last, you see the person you were looking for standing alone on a balcony. Relief rushes through you. You step outside, but the balcony is now a small dock extending over a black, moonlit lake.

They smile as if they have been waiting the whole time.

“I’ve been looking for you,” you say.

“I know,” they reply. “But you’re the one who invited everyone.”

Behind you, the gathering has gone silent. You turn and see all the strangers watching from the windows, their faces warm, expectant, familiar. One by one, you realize they are not strangers at all. They are versions of people you almost became, people you forgot, people who once changed you in small ways.

When you turn back, the person you know is already stepping into a boat tied to the dock.

“Are you coming?” they ask.

You look at the house, bright and crowded behind you, then at the lake ahead, still and endless.

You step into the boat.

No one rows, but it begins to move. The lights of the gathering drift farther away until they look like stars. The person beside you takes your hand, and for the first time all night, you remember exactly who they are.

Then the lake becomes the ceiling above your bed.

You wake with the feeling that you have just left a room full of people who are still talking about you.
