A ripple moves across its white surface like wind over water. From somewhere above—though there is no real sky here—drops of color begin to fall: first a slow blue, then a bright gold, then a red so warm it hums. They do not splatter. They hover, waiting.

When you lift your hand, the colors follow.

You draw nothing at first, only a single line, but the line wriggles free from the canvas and becomes a silver road stretching into the distance. You paint a circle, and it opens into a second sun, green and soft, casting shadows that bloom like flowers. You press your palm flat against the blankness, and suddenly memories appear beneath it—not exactly yours, but familiar anyway: a house with doors made of rain, a staircase that climbs into the sea, a face smiling from behind a curtain of stars.

The world around you notices.

The changing shapes pause in their constant transformation and turn toward you, curious. Cubes melt into birds. Birds unfold into lanterns. The ground beneath your feet becomes a patchwork of velvet triangles and glass leaves. Each step you take sends music up through your body, as if the earth is a piano and you are walking across its hidden keys.

The canvas grows larger the more you paint, until it is no longer hanging in the air before you but rising all around you like a horizon. You realize, with the strange certainty only dreams can give, that the landscape itself has been waiting for your touch. So you paint a door.

It appears in the center of the canvas: tall, moon-white, with a knob shaped like an eye.

When you open it, there is not another room, but a quiet version of the same world. The colors here do not rush or flicker. They glow. The shapes do not scramble to become something else. They rest. A river of liquid light moves slowly through a field of floating stones, and above it hangs a sky full of unfinished constellations, as if someone stopped drawing halfway through.

On the far side of the river stands a figure made entirely of brushstrokes. It has no face, yet you know it recognizes you.

It lifts one hand and offers you a paintbrush.

The brush is warm when you take it, almost alive. The figure points not to the sky, not to the river, but to your chest. Looking down, you see that there is a small blank place over your heart, a perfect white square no larger than a window.

You understand then that this is the only part of the dream you have not yet colored.

So you dip the brush into the river and paint the square with everything you can’t name while awake: the blue of longing, the gold of becoming, the red of being unafraid. The white space drinks it in. Light spills through your ribs. The unfinished constellations above you connect themselves all at once.

The brushstroke figure bows.

The world begins to dissolve—not frighteningly, but gently, like chalk in rain. The canvas folds inward. The silver road curls into a ribbon. The green sun blinks out. And just before you wake, you hear a voice, soft and amused, coming from everywhere at once:

“Take some of it with you.”

When morning comes, your hands are empty.

But for a moment, in the corner of your room, the shadows are full of color.
