“Reef the mainsail!” you shout, but your voice comes out as a bell tolling underwater.  
The crew nods anyway—as if they understand the language of storms.

Lightning splits the sky, and for a moment the sea is glass. In that flash, you see it: not waves, but enormous sleeping creatures turning in their dreams beneath your hull. Each swell is a breath. Each crash is a heartbeat.

The compass spins wildly, then points—not north—but inward, straight at your chest.

“Captain,” your first mate says, gripping the rail, “we can’t outrun this.”

You look ahead. Through curtains of rain, a lighthouse appears where no map ever marked land. Its beam sweeps once across the water, and every terrified face on deck softens. Not safe—just certain.

“Then we don’t outrun it,” you say. “We sail through.”

The crew moves as one. Ropes tighten. Sails angle. The ship climbs a black wall of water so steep it feels like ascending a mountain in the night. At the peak, time pauses. Thunder goes silent. You hang between sky and abyss.

Then the ship tips forward and plunges through the wave—

—and the storm is gone.

The sea is smooth as ink. The stars are impossibly close, reflected above and below, until it seems your ship is sailing through space. The crew has vanished. The deck is empty except for a single lantern, still burning at your feet.

Inside its flame, tiny and clear, is a shoreline at dawn… and someone standing there, waiting for you to come home.
