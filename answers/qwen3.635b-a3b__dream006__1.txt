...and you begin to speak, but your voice doesn't come out as words. It spills from you in slow, luminous ribbons of sound that curl like smoke around the edges of the stage. Where the ribbons touch the floor, the wood grain softens, then lifts, revealing water that doesn't reflect the ceiling lights but instead swirls with the quiet colors of forgotten afternoons.

The crowd doesn't shift or cough or lean forward. Instead, they rise as one, their outlines blurring like wet watercolor. Faces you've known dissolve into familiar silhouettes, then into strangers, then into versions of yourself at different ages: the seven-year-old who built cardboard stages in the living room, the sixteen-year-old who rehearsed this exact moment in the mirror, the part of you that still waits in the wings for permission to step into the light.

You try to push the next sentence forward, but the thoughts in your head turn into moths. They brush against your wrists, your collar, your jaw, their wings dusted with fragments of half-remembered conversations, unsent letters, and places you drove through without knowing why. One lands on the microphone stand and opens its wings to reveal a map of a house you've never lived in, its rooms filled with furniture from birthdays and funerals and quiet Tuesdays.

The rafters lower, not with weight, but with atmosphere: the smell of ozone before a storm, the distant hum of a train you once missed, the exact shade of your first apartment's hallway at 3 a.m. You realize the teleprompter behind you has gone dark, but it doesn't matter. The script was never in front of you. It was in the pauses, in the breath you took before stepping onto this floor, in the weight you carried that you finally set down when your shoes touched the wood.

The audience begins to drift upward, not as people, but as letters, numbers, and folded paper notes, rising like ash from a fire that never burned. They don't applaud. They don't leave. They simply rearrange themselves into a pattern you recognize: your own name, written backward, then sideways, then as a question you've finally stopped trying to answer.

You stop speaking. Not because you're done. But because the dream is done with you performing. It wanted you to listen. And as the stage folds inward like a closing book, you understand: you weren't here to give a speech. You were here to finally hear yourself.
