…and the challenges stop feeling like tests and start feeling like memories you haven’t lived yet.

First, you are asked to build a bridge out of your own forgotten names. It takes shape in mid-air, made of silver thread and the sound of rain from a house you loved as a child. You cross it barefoot, and the planks hum beneath your feet like a lullaby.

Next, the judges—who are not people but shifting weather patterns—ask you to solve a riddle spoken in the language of birds. You answer not with words, but by opening your palms, and a small garden grows there: tomatoes that taste like laughter, vines that climb toward a moon that is suddenly very close.

The other contestants dissolve around you. Some become clock faces. Others turn into doors that open onto empty beaches. You realize there is no crowd, no score, only the sensation of being deeply, inexplicably *seen*.

Then comes the final challenge: you must describe your dream job. But when you open your mouth to speak—*astronomer, architect, healer of old stories*—the words turn into butterflies and fly away. Instead, you understand. The job was never a title. It was the feeling of this moment: creating meaning from nothing, holding beauty and fear at once, working in a place where logic bends gently like grass in wind.

You are handed the prize. It is not a contract, but a key made of starlight. It opens a door in your chest.

As you step through, the dream begins to thin, and you wake with the key still warm in your hand, unsure if you were competing for the job, or if the job was always just an excuse to find yourself here—awake, finally, and holding the door open for yourself.
