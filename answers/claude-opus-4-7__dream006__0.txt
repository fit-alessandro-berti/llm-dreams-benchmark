When I finally reach the podium, I look down at my notes—but the words are swimming, rearranging themselves into languages I've never seen. Some letters lift off the page and flutter away like small dark moths.

I open my mouth to speak, and instead of my voice, I hear the sound of wind chimes. The audience nods appreciatively. A woman in the front row is taking notes in what appears to be watercolor.

I try again, and this time music pours out—a song I've never heard but somehow remember perfectly. The crowd begins to sway. The ceiling above us dissolves into a night sky, and I realize the auditorium has no walls anymore, just rows of chairs stretching into a moonlit meadow.

Someone in the back raises their hand to ask a question. When they speak, it's my grandmother's voice, asking me if I've eaten. I try to answer, but I'm suddenly aware that I've forgotten to wear shoes, and also that I'm supposed to be somewhere else—a test, perhaps, or a train I was meant to catch years ago.

The podium becomes a small boat. The meadow becomes water. I'm drifting now, still holding my notes, which have turned into origami birds that lift away one by one...

...and somewhere, very faintly, an alarm begins to ring.
