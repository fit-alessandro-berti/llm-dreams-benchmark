…decide to plant a garden anyway.

Not a grand field, just a crooked spiral of salvaged tires and rusted bedframes on the roof of a library that’s mostly rubble now. You carried the soil in handfuls from the old riverbed, sifting it through a torn shirt to remove glass teeth and bone splinters. In your pocket, a single paper envelope—stained with rain and sweat—holds seeds you didn’t even know still existed: basil, purple carrot, something called “Moon and Stars” watermelon that feels like holding tiny meteors.

Each night you trade stories with the few who stop by: how the tomatoes in aisle seven used to be redder than warning signs, how strawberries once arrived in plastic boats from another hemisphere. They listen half in hunger, half in disbelief, but they leave you bits of foil, a cracked mug for watering, a lens from broken binoculars you can use to focus sunlight when the clouds finally break.

You plant by moonlight because the days belong to the dust storms and the skittering things that have learned to open doors. Your hands blister; your water ration shrinks. Some evenings you wonder if you’ve forgotten what green smells like, if your memory is just another hallucination born of hunger.

Then one morning you wake to a tear in the gray—a line of impossible green so thin it looks like a stitch holding the world together. You realize you’re laughing, a dry sound like seeds rattling in a pod, and suddenly you understand: you aren’t building a farm. You’re building a library of aliveness, a catalogue of tenderness in a language no one remembers how to speak.

When the first true leaf unfurls, you press your forehead to the warm metal of the tire and whisper the old words: *grow, please, grow*.  
You don’t know yet whether the garden will feed you or the crows or the dust. You only know that in a world determined to end, you have decided to begin—one stubborn, ridiculous seed at a time.
