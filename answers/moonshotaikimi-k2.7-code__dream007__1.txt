…and decide to **stop talking and step outside**.

The door slams behind you harder than you meant it to, but instead of the city street you expected, you find yourself on a long wooden dock floating over water that is not quite water—more like liquid starlight, shifting between silver and deep violet. Your friend is already there, standing a few paces ahead, arms crossed, not looking at you.

Neither of you says anything at first. The argument is still alive in your chest, hot and sharp, but out here it feels smaller, like a radio playing in another room.

Then the dream does what dreams do: the dock begins to split down the middle, slowly, gently, pulling you apart. You panic and reach out. Your friend reaches back. Your fingertips brush, and the moment they do, the dock stops moving. The water below calms into a mirror.

In the reflection, you don’t see yourselves as you are now. You see two kids—one with scabbed knees, one holding a half-melted popsicle—sitting side by side on a curb, laughing at something no one else understood.

“We’ve been here before,” your friend says, and you realize they’re right.

The anger doesn’t vanish. It deflates, like a balloon losing air, and what’s left is something quieter: the fear of losing each other beneath the noise of being right.

“I don’t want to win,” you say. “I want us to stay.”

Your friend nods. The dock slides back together. The water turns ordinary again, dark and reflective, and you sit down with your legs dangling over the edge, shoulder to shoulder, talking in the low, careful way people do after a storm has passed.

When you wake, you won’t remember what the fight was about—only the feeling of reaching out, and someone reaching back.
