I try again, pushing air through my throat, but instead of words, a flock of small, luminescent moths pours from my lips. They flutter into the spotlight, casting fleeting, geometric shadows across the front row.

The audience doesn't gasp. They don't flinch. They simply watch with unblinking eyes. As I look closer, I realize their faces aren't quite right—their features are sliding ever so slightly, like wet paint on a tilted canvas. The man in the second row has a mouth where his left eye should be; the woman next to him is slowly dissolving into a pile of autumn leaves that remain perfectly seated in a folding chair.

I look down at my hands on the podium. They are translucent, and beneath the skin, I don't see veins or bones, but rivers of glowing blue text scrolling too fast to read. 

Panic should be setting in, but instead comes a sudden, overwhelming wave of dream-logic clarity: *I don't have a speech. I don't have information to give them. I am empty, and they are here to fill me.* 

I lean forward over the podium, opening my mouth wider than physically possible, not to speak, but to listen. The entire audience simultaneously inhales, a massive, rushing wind that pulls the curtains back and makes the chandeliers swing. They exhale, and a tidal wave of whispered secrets, forgotten passwords, lullabies, and half-formed thoughts crashes over me. 

Just as the water reaches my chin, the floorboards beneath my shoes turn to sand, the stage collapses, and I am falling upward into a starless, perfectly dark sky, still trying to remember the first line of my speech.
