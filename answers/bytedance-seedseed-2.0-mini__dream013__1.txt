The air has grown thick with the scent of damp moss and aged parchment, and every few steps, a faint, rhythmic ticking echoes from somewhere ahead—too quiet to be a floorboard creak, too steady to be the drip of distant seepage.

Just ahead, the corridor opens into a triangular junction: three archways, each carved with a different symbol, the flagstones between them lined with raised, pressure-sensitive tiles that glint faintly when you shift your weight. A faded scrap of parchment, which you’d tucked into your jacket pocket hours earlier after finding it tucked beneath a loose stone, flutters out of your pocket when you turn to inspect the archways. You unfurl it: it’s a crude diagram of four elements, labeled roughly in charcoal: Iron, Water, Fire, Air, with a single note scrawled at the bottom in your own childhood, wobbly handwriting: “Only step where the elements feed each other.”

The left arch is inscribed with a smoldering forge, and its riddle is carved into the weathered lintel: “I am fed with iron, quenched with rain, never hunger, never pain. What am I?” The middle arch’s runes are cracked, glowing a cold, unwelcoming blue, as if warning you away, while the right arch is topped with a carving of a fox clutching a small, acorn-shaped stone in its jaws.

You bend to peer at the pressure tiles, counting eleven in a row, each etched with one of the four symbols. As you lean in, the ticking grows louder, and you spot a tiny, hidden niche above the middle arch: a miniature grandfather clock face, missing its pendulum bob. The fox’s acorn looks exactly like the spare pendulum weight tucked into the niche’s ledge.

When you reach up to pluck the acorn stone free, it’s solid, cool and heavy in your palm, and as you hold it up to the clock face, it clicks into place perfectly. The ticking slows, then steadies into a soft, familiar rhythm, and the blue glow of the middle arch fades entirely. Now you just need to solve the riddle.

You flip back to the scrap of parchment, remembering the small forge you passed an hour earlier, where a blacksmith had left their workbench mid-job: a pile of iron ore beside a half-quenched horseshoe left sitting in a puddle of rainwater that had seeped through the maze’s cracked ceiling. Oh! The riddle’s answer is a smelting forge. You step onto the tile etched with Iron, wait three slow, steady breaths, then Fire, then Water, pausing between each to let the rhythm of the ticking guide you—and the tiles under your feet lock into place with a soft, satisfying clunk.

The left arch swings open with a low, groaning whine, revealing a narrow, winding staircase carved into the bedrock, descending further into the earth. The steady ticking follows you down each creaking step, and as you climb, the air grows colder, and you start to make out the faint glint of polished metal through the gathering darkness ahead. By the time you reach the bottom of the stairs, you’re standing at the doors of a circular chamber, its walls lined with shelves of dusty blacksmith’s tools and locked iron chests, and in the center, perched on a weathered stone pedestal, is a small, dented silver locket—strung with a chain made of the same iron links you’d seen scattered across the earlier forge bench.

You reach out to grab it, and as your fingers close around the cool, worn metal, the ticking stops dead. The torch in your hand goes out with a soft *pfft*, and for a heartbeat, you’re swallowed by total darkness—before you jolt awake, lying tangled on your bedroom floor, the locket tucked tight in your palm, and the faint, familiar tick-tock of your alarm clock drifting in through your open window.
