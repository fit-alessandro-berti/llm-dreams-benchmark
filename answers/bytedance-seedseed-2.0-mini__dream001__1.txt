You start to fumble through the overflowing pockets of your thrifted wool coat, half-expecting to pull up a navigation app on your smartphone—instead, your hand closes around a crumpled vintage matchbook, embossed with the same looping, unrecognizable script blazoned on every street sign and storefront nearby. A distant church bell chimes, and I glance at my wrist: the analog watch I haven’t worn in years ticks onward, marking just 52 minutes until my meeting.

I bolt toward the nearest intersection, where neon signs flicker in hues I can’t name: one advertises a floating flower shop called “Nectar Nook” where the blooms drift like dandelion seeds, another blares “KLEOS: THE LAST SONATA” in bold, blood-red letters. Every street lamp glows with the soft, pulsing light of fireflies, and when I press the crosswalk button, the tiles under my feet light up like bioluminescent algae, carrying me a few steps forward before fading out.

Halfway to the next block, my sneakers peel apart at the soles—not a tear, exactly; the rubber dissolves into a swarm of tiny, iridescent beetles that scurry under my socks, lightening my step until I’m practically gliding across the pavement. I don’t stop to fuss over it, every breath that fogs in the crisp, pine-scented air eating further into my dwindling time.

Up ahead, the twisted glass spire I’d spotted in the smudged corner of the matchbook peeks over the skyline, its sides warping like melted glass caught in a warm breeze. When I skid to its brass doors, the doorman is a tall figure with antlers woven through their curly brown hair, holding a placard that reads “YOUR MEETING AWAITS.” The lobby smells like roasted chestnuts and old ledger paper, but when I step inside, the grand marble hall is completely empty—save for a single worn wooden chair in the center of the room, and a voice booming from hidden ceiling speakers:
“You didn’t bring the notes you scribbled over your breakfast scone.”
