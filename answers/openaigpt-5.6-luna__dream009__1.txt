You wake up in a completely different era, with no modern technology around. As you explore your new surroundings, you realize you need to find shelter before nightfall.

The town is built of mud brick and timber, its narrow streets crowded with people dressed in clothes you recognize only from old paintings. Horses pull wooden carts. A bell rings somewhere beyond the rooftops. When you ask a passerby what year it is, the stranger stares at you as if you have spoken a foreign language.

Then you notice the sky.

A thin silver crack runs from horizon to horizon, dividing the blue like glass. Through it, you can see flashes of your own world: towers, headlights, a blinking airplane. Your world is still there—but impossibly far away.

In the town square, an old woman is waiting beside a dry fountain. She looks directly at you.

“You’re late,” she says.

“Late for what?”

“For waking up.”

She places a small brass key in your hand. It is warm, almost alive, and engraved with tomorrow’s date.

“Find the clockmaker,” she whispers. “He is the only one who remembers your century.”

Before you can ask where to find him, the town bell stops ringing. Every person in the square turns toward the forest.

Something is walking out of the trees.

And with each step it takes, the silver crack in the sky grows wider.
