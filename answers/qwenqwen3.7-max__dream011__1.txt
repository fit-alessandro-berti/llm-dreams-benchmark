One moment, the text looks like complex, multi-variable calculus; the next, it morphs into a handwritten recipe for a cake I helped my grandmother bake when I was seven. I blink hard, rubbing my eyes, and now the questions are just a series of intricate, spiraling fingerprints.

Panic, thick and familiar, begins to rise in my chest. I reach for my calculator, but as my fingers press the keys, they squish inward like overripe grapes, leaving my fingertips stained with purple juice. 

I look up to ask the proctor for a replacement, but the classroom has changed. The fluorescent lights are gone, replaced by a canopy of glowing, bioluminescent ferns hanging from the ceiling. The other students don't seem to notice. They are hunched over their desks, writing furiously, but their pencils make absolutely no sound against the paper. In fact, I realize with a jolt of dream-logic, they don't have faces—just smooth, blank surfaces reflecting the soft green light of the ferns.

The proctor walks down the aisle. It’s my third-grade teacher, but she is seven feet tall and her head is a giant, ticking grandfather clock. 

"You only have ten minutes left," she whispers, her voice sounding like grinding gears. "And time is currently flowing backward."

I look down at my exam. The paper is unspooling, growing longer and longer, rolling off my desk, down the aisle, and out the classroom door into an infinite, foggy hallway. I grab my pen and desperately try to answer the only question that has settled into plain English:

**Question 43: Calculate the exact weight of a forgotten memory, and show your work.**

I stare at the blank space below it. My pen hovers. The ticking of the proctor's clock-face grows deafening. The anxiety peaks, vibrating in my teeth, until suddenly, I look at the ink bleeding into the page and a profound, calming realization washes over me. 

*I don't know how to do this,* I think. *But I don't have to.*

I look back up at the clock-faced teacher. "I'm dreaming," I say aloud. 

The ticking stops. The faceless students freeze. 

The final question on the exam slowly fades, replacing itself with one last sentence: *Then why are you still sitting at the desk?*

I smile, drop the squishy calculator, and stand up. As I do, the exam paper dissolves into a flock of white moths that flutter up toward the glowing ferns. The classroom walls fall away like cardboard scenery, revealing a vast, starlit ocean. I step off the edge of the desk, and instead of falling, I begin to fly.
