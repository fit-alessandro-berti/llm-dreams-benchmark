You are at your workplace, and suddenly, you are given a project with an impossible deadline. Your boss is waiting for you to start, and you sit down at your desk—but the keyboard has no letters on it. The keys are smooth and blank, like river stones. You press them anyway, and somehow words appear on the screen, but they're not the words you meant to type. They're sentences from a letter you wrote years ago, one you never sent.

Your boss is standing behind you now. You can feel them there without turning around. "It's due in an hour," they say, but their voice sounds like your third-grade teacher's. When you finally turn to look, the office has stretched—your desk is at the end of a hallway that wasn't there before, and everyone else is working at desks that float slightly above the floor.

You try to explain that you need more time, but your mouth is full of something—paper, maybe, or sand. The clock on the wall is melting at the edges, and the numbers rearrange themselves every time you look. It's 4:00. Then it's 4:00 again, but somehow later.

You decide to just start working, but the project keeps changing. First it's a report. Then it's a bridge you're supposed to build out of the report. Your hands move quickly, folding paper into beams and cables, and strangely, it's working—the bridge is growing across the office, over the cubicles, toward a window that looks out onto your childhood backyard.

Your boss says, "You're almost there," and for the first time, they sound kind.

You step onto the paper bridge. It holds. The deadline doesn't matter anymore, or maybe it already passed, or maybe the bridge *was* the project all along. You walk toward the window, and the light coming through it gets brighter and brighter, until—

Your alarm goes off. You lie there for a moment, heart still hurrying, half-convinced you have something due.
