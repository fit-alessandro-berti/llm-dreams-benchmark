...decide to **build a lighthouse in the center of a dried-up ocean.**

The sky is a bruised shade of violet, and the wind doesn't just blow; it sighs with the weight of everything that used to be. The salt flats stretch out endlessly, a white, cracked desert where the skeletons of ancient tankers lie half-buried like the ribs of fallen giants. 

You don’t hunt for canned food or scrap metal anymore. Instead, you scavenge for things that shouldn't matter: shards of stained glass from ruined cathedrals, copper wiring that hums when you touch it, and jars of captured moonlight you found in the hollows of weeping trees. 

As you pile the debris into a spiraling tower, the dream-logic takes hold. The heavier the scrap, the lighter it becomes. A rusted engine block floats upward, tethered to your belt by a fraying rope. The more you build, the more the silence of the wasteland begins to change. It’s no longer an empty silence; it’s a waiting silence.

When you finally reach the summit and ignite the great lens—a massive, multifaceted diamond you found pulsing in a crater—the light doesn't sweep across the horizon. It shoots straight up, a pillar of pure, iridescent amber, piercing the heavy clouds.

And then, the impossible happens. 

The light acts like a magnet for the lost. From the edges of the world, things begin to drift toward your tower. Not ships, but memories. Ghostly silhouettes of cities, the scent of rain on hot asphalt, the sound of a cello playing in a distant room—all of it flows toward the light, swirling around your tower in a glowing, chaotic nebula.

You realize then that you aren't just surviving. You are collecting the world, piece by shimmering piece, waiting for the moment the light is bright enough to call the dawn back from the void.
