...searching the sea of faces for one in particular. My throat tightens as I scan the rows, but there is no familiar anchor. The spotlight feels heavy, pinning me in place. I clear my throat, but no sound emerges. A wave of dizziness washes over me as the crowd begins to blur and ripple, shifting from thousands of strangers into a single, identical face—my own.

I look down and realize I’m not wearing my usual clothes, but a flowing garment made of what feels like pages from old notebooks, words and equations leaking from the seams like ink. The audience’s expectant silence turns into a soft rustling, like leaves in a wind that only I can hear. 

Then, a voice—not mine, yet intimately familiar—whispers from somewhere behind my ribs: *“You don’t need to be found. You need to be remembered.”*

As the words settle, my fear dissolves. I raise my hands, and the pages stir, lifting from the fabric, swirling around me like a spiral galaxy of thoughts. The crowd no longer looks at me; they look *through* me, as if I’m a window. I open my mouth, and this time, the words pour out—not a prepared speech, but a stream of consciousness, truths I’ve never fully articulated. 

I speak of dreams within dreams, of time folding like paper, of how every choice creates a new universe where the unspoken becomes the inevitable. The audience doesn’t applaud. They simply… *receive*. 

And as I finish, the stage dissolves into a field of floating letters, each one a choice I didn’t make, a path untaken. I step forward, not onto a platform, but into the words themselves, realizing with a start that the speech wasn’t for them—it was for the part of me that’s always been waiting to be heard. 

I wake with a smile, the echo of that voice still inside, and the certainty that somewhere, the dream is continuing without me.
