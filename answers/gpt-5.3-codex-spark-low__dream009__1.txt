• You wake up in a completely different era, with no modern technology around. As you explore your new surroundings, you
  realize you need to learn how to survive before you even think about getting home.

  You move carefully through a narrow street of mudbrick houses, listening for wheels and voices. The air smells of
  woodsmoke and rain on stone. In place of signal bars and glass towers, there are stars so bright they feel almost rude
  against the dark. You check your pockets out of habit for a phone, then laugh at yourself when your fingers close
  around nothing but a rusted coin and a folded scrap of paper no one here can read.

  By the time the bells begin ringing for dusk, you understand one thing: language is your lifeline. You point, mimic,
  gesture, trade small items for a shared meal. A potter named Lian gives you a bowl of lentils and broken water in
  return for helping lift a stone from his kiln. You do, and he teaches you three words in exchange. You repeat them all
  night, mouth stiff with new sounds, while the embers glow in the courtyard.

  The next morning you wake before sunrise and follow the river path with the fishermen. They work with hooks, woven
  nets, and a patience no modern machine could fake. One child hands you a sharpened reed, and you spend the day
  learning knots, knots that hold nets, knots that hold roofs, knots that hold your panic in. You have no map, no
  compass, no battery-powered light, but you begin to make one with memory: where the sun climbs, where the birds turn,
  where the hills cast their long shadows at noon.

  By the third day you can bargain for bread, ask for direction, and keep dry in a storm. By the fifth, you’re teaching
  one of the blacksmith’s apprentices how to keep a fire hot without wasting charcoal, using an old trick from old
  camping gear that he calls lucky guessing and later swears by. They call you not a fool of the lost age, but the one
  who listens.

  When fever takes the village, you become the person who can organize clean water and shade, who knows to boil, to
  rinse, to isolate, to wait. You have never done this before, but it feels less like knowledge now than instinct.

  Weeks pass. You no longer dream of return every night—only of staying useful. Yet every market day, when the crowds
  thin and the sun drops red behind the hills, you stand on the ridge and scan the horizon as if it might flicker and
  open.

  One evening, an old trader arrives with a polished brass mirror, a thing almost magical in your hands. In it, you can
  see yourself, older somehow. Not in years—just in understanding. You grip the frame and whisper the names of
  everything you still remember: your city, your language, your people, your time.

  The mirror does not answer.

  You smile despite yourself. You can’t call it home yet, but you can survive here. And maybe that is what this dream
  was trying to teach: not how to get back, but how to become possible in a world that never promised to understand you.
