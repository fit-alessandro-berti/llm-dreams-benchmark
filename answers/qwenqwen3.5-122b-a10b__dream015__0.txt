Despite the hardships, you decide to **plant a garden.**

You kneel in the dust of a city that no longer breathes, your knees sinking into the gray ash that covers the ruins of skyscrapers. The wind howls, carrying the scent of rust and old smoke, and in the distance, the shadows of the wanderers shift, hungry and watching. But you do not look up. You do not run.

You reach into your pack and pull out a small, velvet pouch. Inside is not a weapon, not a ration, but a single seed. It feels heavy in your palm, warm against the chill of the dead world.

You dig your fingers into the cracked concrete. It should be impossible. There is no soil, only rubble. But this is a dream, and the rules here are written by your own will. You press the seed into the fissure. You cup your hands and catch the falling rain, though the sky above is clear and starless. The water glows faintly blue as it touches the earth.

For a heartbeat, nothing happens. The shadows in the distance pause. The wind holds its breath.

Then, a green shoot pierces the gray. It does not grow slowly; it spirals upward like a green flame, unfurling leaves that shimmer with a light of their own. A bud forms, tight and perfect, and then bursts into a bloom of impossible color—violet and gold, colors that do not exist in the world you left behind.

The light from the flower pushes back the encroaching dark. The shadows retreat, not out of fear, but out of reverence. The hunger in the air dissolves, replaced by the scent of rain on hot pavement, of blooming jasmine, of a summer morning you haven't seen in years.

You stand up, your hands stained with dirt that feels like life. You look at the flower, and for the first time since the world ended, you feel safe. You realize that the apocalypse was just the background, and the garden is the truth.

You reach out to touch a petal, and the sensation is so vivid, so overwhelmingly real, that your eyelids flutter. The light fades. The sound of the wind is replaced by the hum of your ceiling fan.

You open your eyes to your bedroom ceiling, the morning light streaming in. Your hand is open on your pillow, empty, but for a moment, just a moment, you can still smell the jasmine.
