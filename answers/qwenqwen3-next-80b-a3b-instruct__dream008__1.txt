You are at your workplace, and suddenly, you are given a project with an impossible deadline. Your boss is waiting for you to start, and you—

—realize you’re holding a quill made of starlight, not a pen. The project isn’t on a screen or a clipboard—it’s a living, breathing constellation unfolding on the conference table, each star a task, each orbit a deadline. The boss? She’s not wearing her blazer. She’s wearing a crown of ticking clocks, and her eyes are two full moons.

“We need this completed before the next sunset,” she says, voice echoing like wind through chimes. “But sunset… doesn’t come here.”

You look up. The ceiling is gone. Above you, the sky is a massive, swirling hourglass—with no sand. Just silence.

A coworker whispers: “It’s not about finishing. It’s about remembering you’re already done.”

You pause. You close your eyes.

And then—a giggle. Laughter bubbling from your own chest, unbidden. Because suddenly, you remember: This isn’t real. You’re dreaming.

You drop the quill.

The constellation unravels into butterflies made of invoices and coffee cups. The boss’s clock-crown melts into a dandelion, floating upward. The walls dissolve into old notebook pages fluttering like birds.

And then—your alarm rings.

You jolt awake in bed.

Sunlight spills through the curtains.

On your nightstand: a sticky note.

It says:  
*You didn’t miss the deadline.  
You were never supposed to be awake for it.*

You smile.  
You hit snooze.  
And dream again—this time, of flying on a spreadsheet made of clouds.
