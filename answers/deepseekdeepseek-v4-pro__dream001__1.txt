You start to walk, but the streets rearrange themselves the moment you look away. Every corner you turn reveals a plaza that wasn’t there before, filled with fountains that flow upward and statues that whisper your name. You pull out your phone, but the screen shows only a blinking map of a city you’ve never seen, all the street names written in a language you almost understand—words that feel like childhood, but slip away when you try to read them.

A tram made entirely of glass glides silently toward you, and you board without thinking. Inside, the passengers are all people you’ve lost touch with: your college roommate, your grandmother before she got sick, a barista who once remembered your order. None of them acknowledge you, but you somehow know the meeting is about *them*, about something you forgot to say. The tram accelerates, rising off the rails, and now you’re flying above the city, which has become a labyrinth of clock towers all showing different times. You realize you’ve left your body somewhere, maybe still asleep in your bed, and the hour is passing both too fast and not at all.

You try to shout the address you don’t know, and your voice comes out as a flock of moths, their wings dusted with numbers. They flutter into a pattern that forms a door in the air. You step through, and suddenly you’re in a waiting room. The receptionist is a younger version of yourself, who hands you a key made of ice and says, “It’s on the thirty-seventh floor, but the elevator only goes to feelings.” You take the key, and it melts in your palm, leaving a residue of something like hope—or regret. You begin to climb the stairs, each step a memory you’d buried, and somewhere above you, a voice is calling your name for a meeting that has already begun, that has been waiting for you since the day you were born.
