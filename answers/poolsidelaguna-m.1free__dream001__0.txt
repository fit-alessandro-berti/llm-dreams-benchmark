The buildings are impossibly tall, their facades a kaleidoscope of colors that shift when you’re not looking directly at them. Street signs are written in a language you somehow understand—*Marigold Avenue* heads west, but your feet carry you east, toward the river where the skyline is dotted with spires that pulse like heartbeats.  

A stranger with your grandmother’s laugh hands you a map that disintegrates into paper cranes as you unfold it. “The meeting isn’t a place,” they say, “it’s a *question*.” You frown, but the cranes scatter into the wind, and you realize you’re now holding a compass that spins wildly until you decide to stop chasing the needle and start following the rhythm of your own footsteps.  

The city begins to blur. Faces in the crowd flicker between familiar and strange—your childhood dog wearing a business suit, a parking meter that hums lullabies, a bridge made of tangled ethernet cables. You’re late, but time bends here; each step takes you both forward and backward, past a park where trees grow upside-down into the clouds.  

At last, you arrive at what the compass insists is the destination: a revolving door that leads to a room filled with mirrors. In each reflection, you’re a different version of yourself—young, old, wearing a spacesuit, a crown of fish. A voice (your own?) asks, “What did you forget to ask for?” You realize the meeting was never about a location, but a realization: answers are like this city—they reconfigure themselves until you stop searching and start *listening*.  

Then you wake up, of course. But the compass is still in your hand.
