...and the light goes strange first — that greenish-bronze light that comes before hail, the kind that makes every leaf look cut from tin.

The trees notice before I do. They stop being trees, quietly, the way things change in dreams without announcing it: their trunks are still trunks, but now each one is a column of stacked text, black letters running up the bark like ants, and I understand without reading that they are all versions of the same sentence, and that the sentence is about me, and that I would rather not know it.

The wind arrives from underneath. That's the wrong direction, and I know it's wrong, and knowing doesn't help. The forest floor breathes up through the moss and my coat fills like a sail and I have to hold the ground with my hands, the way you'd hold the sides of a boat.

Then the rain. Except it isn't falling. It's rising — droplets pulling themselves out of the puddles and the wet leaves and going up, thousands of them, a slow reverse downpour, and inside each drop there's a small lit window with somebody in it. I see a woman washing a cup. I see a kid asleep with his mouth open. I see a room I lived in once with the furniture in the wrong places. They all go up past my face and I want to catch one but they're warm and they burst like soap bubbles and leave nothing on my palm but the feeling of having almost remembered something.

The thunder, when it comes, is a voice. Not words. Just the shape of someone about to speak, held forever at the moment before the first syllable — that intake of breath, enormous, filling the whole valley. I keep waiting for the sentence. It never lands. It just breathes in, and breathes in, and the pressure builds behind my eyes.

I start running, and running turns into that dream-running where the ground is a treadmill of pine needles, and I pass the same fallen log four times, and on the fourth pass someone is sitting on it.

It's me, but older, or maybe just more finished. Not hurried by the storm at all. Rain going up all around them and their hair not moving.

"You keep trying to get out from under it," they say. "It isn't weather. It's just the world being large near you."

And I say — this is the part I'll remember when I wake, this exact stupid heartbreaking thing — I say, "But I'm getting wet."

And they laugh, and hold out their hand palm-up, and the drops go through it like they go through a window, and I look and my own hand is doing the same, and has been the whole time, and the storm has never once touched me. I've been running from a thing I'm made of the same stuff as.

The breath in the sky finally lets go.

And it turns out the word was just my name, said kindly, the way somebody says it to wake you up gently — and the trees unspool their letters into starlings, thousands of them lifting all at once with a sound like applause, and the green-bronze light breaks open into ordinary morning, and I'm standing in a clearing that's just a clearing, holding nothing, perfectly dry, and absolutely soaked through.
