You start to walk faster, but the streets keep rearranging themselves. You turn a corner and recognize the coffee shop from your hometown — but it's inside a cathedral. Your phone shows the meeting location, but the map is just a painting of a forest, and the forest is moving.

A woman with the face of your third-grade teacher hands you a key and says, "You're already late." You look at your watch. It's running backward. You run, but the ground turns to sand, then water, then a staircase going down.

At the bottom is a door. Behind it: a conference room. Everyone is seated. They've been waiting. They look like everyone you've ever known. The person at the head of the table — you can't quite see their face — slides a paper toward you.

It says: *"You forgot what this meeting was about."*

You look up. The room is dissolving. The walls become sky. The table becomes a field. You realize you're still holding the key, and it's warm, and it's humming, and—

You wake up, heart pounding, with the strange certainty that you were supposed to remember something. Something important. It fades within seconds, like water through fingers, leaving only the feeling of the key in your palm — a sensation that lingers all morning, unexplained.
