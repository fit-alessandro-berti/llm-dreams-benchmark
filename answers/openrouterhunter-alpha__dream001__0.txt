At first, the streets make a kind of sense — numbered avenues, familiar storefronts, people moving with purpose. But every time you check a street sign, the letters rearrange themselves. "Maple Drive" becomes "Lapel River" becomes something that isn't a word at all, just a feeling, like the shape of a word you once knew.

You pull out your phone to check the meeting location, but the email thread is full of messages in a language that looks like yours but means nothing. The time keeps shrinking: 58 minutes, then 43, then somehow back to 71. Time isn't working.

You stop a stranger to ask for directions. They smile warmly and say, "Oh, it's just past the old library. You know the one — where your grandmother used to take you." And you *do* know it, suddenly, a surge of false memory — the smell of dust and binding glue, a particular window with cracked glass. But when you head that direction, the street slopes upward into a staircase that opens onto a rooftop garden with no exit.

A cat is sitting on the ledge. It looks at you with the patient judgment of someone who has been waiting.

"You're late," it says, without moving its mouth. Not threatening. Just factual.

"I know," you say, and you mean it more deeply than you've ever meant anything — not just late for this meeting, but late for something larger, something you were supposed to arrive at a long time ago.

The cat flicks its tail toward a door you're certain wasn't there a moment ago — heavy wood, slightly ajar, warm light spilling through the crack. From behind it, you hear the low murmur of conversation. Someone laughs. It sounds like recognition.

You reach for the handle.

And then you wake up.
