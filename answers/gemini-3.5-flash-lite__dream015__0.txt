Every survivor I’ve met on the scorched asphalt highways tells me I’m a fool. *“Eat to live,”* they rasp, their lips chapped and black from radiation dust. *“Don't waste calories on hope.”* They hoard their scavenged cans of expired protein paste and sleep with one eye open, guarding piles of scrap metal that won't matter in a decade.

But as I press the tiny seed into the damp soil—soil I sifted through my own fingers for hours to remove the glass shards—I realize survival isn't the point anymore. Merely breathing isn't enough to keep the ghost of the old world from hollowing you out. 

I dip my thumb into my last canteen of clean water, rationing just three drops, and let them sink into the dirt. Then, I lean back against the crumbling concrete wall, close my eyes, and wait for the dark. 

Even in a world that ended, I refuse to live like the dead.
