The air smells of coal smoke and wet wool, and the gas lamps flicker with a rhythm that feels suspiciously like a heartbeat. You pull your coat tighter, the fabric rough against your skin, and begin to walk. The streets are narrow, winding like the veins of a leaf, lined with buildings that lean in as if whispering secrets to one another.

There are no cars, only the clatter of hooves on stone, and the people you pass wear hats and skirts that belong to a century you can't quite place. They look right through you, or perhaps they see something you don't, their eyes unfocused, staring at the middle distance where the sky is a bruised purple.

You keep walking, guided by an instinct that feels older than memory. You need to find the lighthouse because you remember, with sudden, sharp clarity, that if the light doesn't burn tonight, the shadows will stay. And if the shadows stay, you won't be able to wake up.

You turn a corner and there it is. It stands in the middle of a dry, grassy field, surrounded by nothing but wind and silence. The tower is made of stone, cold and impossibly tall, reaching into a sky where the moon is cracked like an eggshell.

You climb the spiral stairs. They seem to stretch forever, the steps widening and narrowing with your mood. When you finally reach the top, the lantern room is dark. There is no glass, no oil, no wick. Just a pedestal in the center.

You look down at your hands. They are glowing faintly, a soft, bioluminescent hum. You realize then that you are not the keeper of the light. You are the light itself.

You place your palms on the pedestal. The stone warms. The darkness in the room recoils, pushed back by the sudden radiance of your own presence. The field below begins to brighten, the grass turning gold, the cracked moon knitting itself back together.

You feel a pull, a gentle tug at the base of your spine. The world dissolves into white static. The smell of coal smoke fades, replaced by the scent of laundry detergent and the hum of a refrigerator.

You open your eyes. The ceiling above you is familiar, painted white, with a crack in the corner that looks nothing like a cracked moon. You are back. But for a moment, just a moment, your hands still glow in the dark before fading away.
