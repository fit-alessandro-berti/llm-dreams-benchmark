...settle it the only way that makes sense: by walking to the lighthouse at the end of the street. Neither of you questions why there's a lighthouse on your street now, or why it's painted the exact shade of blue your grandmother's kitchen used to be.

As you walk, the argument continues, but you notice the words coming out of your mouth aren't quite the ones you intended. You say "betrayal" and it comes out as "Tuesday." Your friend nods gravely, as if Tuesday is the most cutting accusation imaginable.

The lighthouse stairs spiral upward forever, but climbing them takes no effort at all. Halfway up, your friend turns to you, and their face is the same but also somehow your third-grade teacher's face, and they say, "You know I never meant for the ocean to find out."

And suddenly you understand—this was what the argument was about all along. The ocean. Of course. You feel a wave of forgiveness so intense it tastes like cinnamon.

At the top of the lighthouse, instead of a lamp, there's a kitchen table set for two. Your friend sits down and pours tea from a kettle that's also, in some way you can't articulate, the moon. "I'm sorry," they say, and the words come out as actual paper boats that drift across the table toward you.

You pick one up. Unfold it. Inside, written in your own handwriting, is the thing you'd been too afraid to say for years.

You look up to respond, but your friend is gone, and the lighthouse is your childhood bedroom, and somewhere below you can hear the ocean—who apparently did find out—knocking politely at the front door, asking if everything is alright between you two now.

You want to answer. You open your mouth—

—and you wake, with the strange certainty that you should call your friend today, even though you can no longer remember what either of you was upset about. Maybe that was the point.
