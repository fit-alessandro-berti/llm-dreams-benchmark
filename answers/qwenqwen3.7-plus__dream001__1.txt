...run down a cobblestone street that inexplicably curves upward into a spiral. My legs feel heavy, moving through the air as if I’m wading through thick syrup, a classic hallmark of panic in dreams. 

The architecture around me is a chaotic collage. I see gothic spires made of glowing fiber-optic cables, Brutalist concrete blocks floating mid-air like un-tethered balloons, and storefront windows displaying not merchandise, but looping, silent memories of places I’ve never been.

I pull my phone from my pocket to check the map. The screen flickers to life, but instead of a GPS, it shows a live feed of my own sleeping face, eyes twitching beneath closed lids. A notification pops up: *Meeting Time: Five Minutes Ago.*

Panic surges, hot and bright. I stop a passerby—a tall figure wearing a suit tailored from what looks like overlapping autumn leaves. 

"Excuse me," I gasp, "I'm looking for the meeting. The important one?"

The figure turns. Where their face should be, there is only the smooth, reflective surface of a pocket mirror. In the reflection, I don't see myself. I see a vast, empty ocean under a green sky. 

"You are looking in the wrong direction," the figure says, their voice sounding like rustling paper. "The meeting isn't a place. It's an hourglass. And the sand is made of your doubts."

Suddenly, the sky shifts from an opalescent dawn to a bruised, heavy purple. The city begins to fold in on itself like origami. Streets snap upward to become walls; towering buildings collapse into flat, two-dimensional drawings. 

I sprint toward a massive, ornate brass elevator standing entirely alone in the middle of a sudden, silent intersection. There are no cables holding it, only a faint, gravitational hum. The doors slide open with a heavy, mechanical sigh. 

I step inside. The interior isn't an elevator car at all; it’s a perfect replica of my first childhood bedroom, down to the faded glow-in-the-dark stars on the ceiling. But the walls are breathing softly, inhaling and exhaling.

In the center of the room is a single, wooden chair. It is empty. On the seat rests an old rotary telephone, painted bright red. 

It rings. The sound is impossibly loud, vibrating in my teeth. 

I pick up the receiver. I don't say hello. 

A voice answers on the other end. It is my voice, but layered, echoing, as if spoken by a chorus of thousands. 

*"Don't worry,"* the voice says. *"The meeting was canceled. You aren't late. You are the agenda."*

The red phone turns to wet ash in my hand. The walls of the room dissolve into a cascade of glowing, white binary code, and I feel that sudden, weightless jolt in my stomach—the feeling of falling, or perhaps, the feeling of finally waking up.
