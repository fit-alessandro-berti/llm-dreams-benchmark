You are at your workplace, and suddenly, you are given a project with an impossible deadline. Your boss is waiting for you to start, and you notice he has handed you the deadline itself — a small brass object, heavier than it should be, with a date engraved on the underside that is yesterday.

"By close of business," he says, and I understand somehow that close of business happened while he was speaking, that it is closing continuously, like a door in a hallway that keeps swinging shut ahead of you no matter how fast you walk.

I say I'll get started. I always say that.

My desk is in a room I've never seen but recognize completely. There are other people at other desks, and they're all working on the same project, and I know this without asking because I can hear them thinking it — a low collective hum, like a server room, like bees. Somebody's phone rings and it's my phone and it's on a desk three rows away and it keeps ringing.

The project is: summarize the ocean. That's it, that's the whole brief. There's a document open in front of me with the title *OCEAN — FINAL — v3 — USE THIS ONE* and when I scroll it doesn't end, it just gets bluer.

I start typing. The words come out fine, they come out beautifully actually, I'm very good at this, and then I look up and the paragraph I wrote has become a paragraph about something else entirely — about a hallway, about my father's coat — and the cursor has moved to a document I don't have permission to edit. A little grey banner says *Suggesting mode.* I am only allowed to suggest.

My boss is standing behind me. He has been standing behind me the entire time and also has just arrived. His face is very kind and does not resolve. "How's it coming?"

"I'm about forty percent through," I say, which is a lie, and also true, and also a percentage of nothing.

"Great," he says. "The client moved it up."

And here is the part where in the dream I feel the thing I always feel — that hot pressure behind the sternum, that certainty that I am about to be found out, that everyone else received an additional page of instructions and I did not. My hands are on the keyboard and the keys have gotten larger, the way stairs get taller near the top.

Then someone at the next desk — a woman with a cardigan and no particular face — leans over and says, without any drama at all: "You know it's not actually possible, right?"

And I say, "I know."

And she says, "So."

And the room goes very quiet, the hum stops, and I look down and see that the brass deadline in my hand has no weight anymore, it's a paper thing, a prop, a stage deadline, and the impossible project is nine hundred pages of the same page. The ocean does not need summarizing. The ocean has never asked.

I stand up. My boss is still smiling somewhere behind me, patient, infinitely patient, because he isn't waiting for the work — he's just waiting, that's his whole job, he's made of waiting. I could walk out through the wall. I know I could, the way you know things.

But I sit back down, because it's a dream, and because I want to see what I'd write if I stopped trying to finish.

The first sentence is: *It is very large, and it is moving.*

That's enough. I get up. The hallway keeps closing and I go through anyway.
