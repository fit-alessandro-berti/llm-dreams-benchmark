You find yourself in a post-apocalyptic world, with resources scarce and dangers everywhere. Despite the hardships, you decide to plant a garden.

The cracked asphalt of what used to be a highway crunches under your boots as you kneel in the dirt of a long-dead parking lot. Your hands are raw, blistered from weeks of scavenging, but you keep digging. You’ve scavenged seeds from the ruins of an old hardware store—tomatoes, beans, something that might once have been sunflowers. The sky above is a bruised purple, streaked with ash clouds that never seem to clear. Radiation detectors click faintly in the distance, but here, in this sheltered pocket between two collapsed buildings, the Geiger counter is quiet enough.

You work by the light of a jury-rigged solar lamp, mixing in whatever compost you could scrape together from rotted books and fabric scraps. Every handful of soil feels like a rebellion. The wind carries the scent of rust and distant smoke, and sometimes you hear the low growl of mutated dogs prowling the edges of the city. You keep a rusted pistol within reach, but you don’t draw it. Not tonight.

As the first fragile sprouts push through the dirt days later, something shifts in the dream. The air grows warmer, almost kind. A figure appears at the edge of the lot—a survivor, thin and wary, carrying a dented metal can. They don’t speak at first. They just watch you water the tiny green shoots with rainwater you’ve collected in old tires. Then they set the can down: it’s full of earthworms, writhing and alive.

You don’t ask where they came from. You just nod, and together you begin turning more of the lot into soil. The ruins around you stay silent, but for the first time in the dream, the silence doesn’t feel like the end of everything. It feels like space being made for what comes next.

The garden grows unevenly, stubbornly. Some plants die. Others thrive in ways that shouldn’t be possible. At night, when the howls come closer, you sit among the leaves and talk—about the old world, about what you remember of rain that didn’t taste like metal, about whether the stars are still real or just holes burned through the ash.

And in the dream, you realize something strange: the world didn’t end because there was nothing left worth saving. It ended because people forgot how to start again. You keep planting anyway.
