…stop trying to win.

The words are still hot in your mouth when you notice the room has gone strangely quiet. Your friend’s face is flushed, their eyes bright with hurt, and for the first time you realize they are not angry in the way you thought. They are afraid of losing something too.

So you take a step back.

“I need to understand why this matters so much to you,” you say, though your voice shakes.

Your friend looks away, and the walls of the room begin to soften, as if made of paper left out in the rain. The argument is still there between you, but it changes shape. It becomes a table. Then a bridge. Then a narrow path through a dark forest.

Without speaking, the two of you start walking.

At first you keep to opposite sides of the path, each of you carrying your version of the truth like a heavy stone. Branches scrape at your shoulders. Somewhere ahead, water rushes over rocks. You want to explain yourself again, to make them see it clearly, but every time you open your mouth, birds burst from the trees and drown you out.

Finally, your friend stops beside the river.

They hold out their stone.

You hold out yours.

When the stones touch, they crack open. Inside, both are glowing with the same small flame.

Neither of you says sorry right away. Not because you don’t mean it, but because the dream seems to understand that some things need a little silence first.

Then your friend sits down beside the water and pats the ground next to them.

You sit.

The river carries the broken pieces away, and in the reflection you see the two of you not as opponents, but as children building something together in the dirt, arguing only because you both cared where the walls should go.

When you wake, you can’t remember what the disagreement was about.

Only that the friendship was important.

And that, somehow, you had both decided to stay.
