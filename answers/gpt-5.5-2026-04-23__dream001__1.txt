You start to run, though the streets seem to rearrange themselves each time you look away. Glass towers lean over narrow alleys; bridges curve into the sky and end in doorways; trams glide past without rails, full of passengers who are all reading the same newspaper with your name in the headline.

You stop a woman in a red coat and ask where the meeting is.

She smiles as if she has been expecting you. “You’re already late,” she says, and hands you a small brass key.

“To what?” you ask.

But she has become a flock of pigeons, and the pigeons scatter upward, leaving the key warm in your palm.

You notice then that every building has a clock, but no two clocks agree. One says noon. Another says yesterday. A third has no hands at all, only a painted eye that blinks when you stare at it. Your phone is in your pocket, buzzing insistently, but when you pull it out, the screen shows a map of a city shaped like a sleeping face.

A blue dot pulses at the center.

You are the blue dot.

You follow a street called Memory Lane, though the sign changes to Elm Street, then First Avenue, then the name of the road you grew up on. Storefronts pass by: a bakery selling childhood smells, a tailor measuring shadows, a bookshop where the books whisper endings before beginnings. In the window of a café, you see yourself sitting at a table, calmly drinking tea.

You push open the café door.

Inside, it is impossibly quiet. The version of you at the table looks up and checks a watch.

“You made it,” they say.

“Is this the meeting?”

“Almost.”

They slide an envelope across the table. Your name is written on it in handwriting you recognize but cannot place. You open it and find a single blank sheet of paper.

“What am I supposed to do with this?” you ask.

“Remember.”

The café begins to fill with people you have known: old friends, strangers from buses, teachers, relatives, someone you once loved, someone you never met but miss terribly. They all take seats around you, waiting, patient and solemn, as if you are about to give a speech.

You look down at the blank page again.

Words begin to appear.

Not written in ink, but rising through the paper like shapes beneath water:

**The meeting is with the part of yourself you keep postponing.**

Outside, the city exhales. The towers fold inward like paper. The clocks all strike at once, not an hour but a sound like a door opening.

The brass key in your hand turns by itself.

And suddenly you remember: the meeting was never somewhere you had to find.

It was something you had to wake up for.
