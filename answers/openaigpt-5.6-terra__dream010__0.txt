You are participating in a competition where the grand prize is your dream job. As the competition progresses, you face various challenges and impossible tests: answering interview questions while underwater, building a presentation out of falling leaves, navigating an office maze where every door opens onto a childhood memory.

The other contestants are strangely familiar. One has your laugh. Another wears the jacket you lost years ago. A third never speaks, but whenever you panic, they quietly point toward the exit.

In the final round, you are led into a vast auditorium. Onstage is a single desk, a glowing computer, and a nameplate bearing your name. The judges ask you to explain why you deserve the job.

You begin listing your skills, your experience, everything you have prepared. But the words sound hollow in the enormous room.

Then the silent contestant steps forward and places a small mirror on the desk.

In it, you see yourself—not as the version trying to impress everyone, but as the version who used to make things simply because you loved making them.

So you tell the truth.

You say you want the job because you want to wake up curious. Because you want your work to matter to someone. Because you are tired of auditioning for a life you do not actually want.

The judges remove their masks. They are all you: older, younger, exhausted, hopeful.

They applaud.

The auditorium dissolves into morning light. Your alarm is ringing. On your bedside table is a business card with no company name, only a handwritten note:

**“You got the job. Start by becoming the person who wants it.”**
