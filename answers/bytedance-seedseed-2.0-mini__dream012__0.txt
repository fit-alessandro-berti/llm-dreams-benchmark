“Mainsail! Ease it to half mast—don’t let it snap and take the rigging with it!” you shout, nodding at Javi, the oldest deckhand who’s seen a dozen storms worse than this. “Milo, check the starboard bilge! The water’s creeping over the planks—bale it fast before we take on too much.”

For a heartbeat, no one moves, and your throat tightens—until Tom, the quartermaster who’s sailed with you since you were a teen deckhand, slaps Javi on the back and grabs his harness. “Let’s move, then!” he yells, and the crew snaps to, their nervous energy channeled into motion. You’d thought they’d panic, but they follow you because they always have: when you were the kid who climbed the rigging faster than anyone else, when you guided them through a squall last year that left you dead in the water for three hours.

A jagged shard of the foremast breaks free, hurtling toward the port side where Yuri, the 19-year-old who joined last month, is frozen staring at the churning waves. You dive forward, shoving him out of the way, and the wood grazes your shoulder, leaving a burning trail through your oilskin coat. You hit the deck hard, your elbow scraping raw, but you are back on your feet before you even register the pain.

Another rogue wave slams the ship, and it lists so far to starboard you swear you can see the dark bottom of the ocean through the scoured hull planking. Lila, your newest navigator, cries out that she’s got a fix on the hidden cove—two miles west, through the thickest squall. You yank the wheel hard to port, your fingers screaming at the pressure, and the ship groans but turns.

Your kid brother, who snuck on board as a stowaway three days ago before you could send him back home, appears at your side, pressing a crumpled thermos of hot spiced rum into your free hand. “We’re almost there,” he says, his voice cracking, and you nod—you see the way he’s been manning the hatch nonstop since the storm hit, the way he’s stopped hiding behind the crewmates he barely knows.

You take a sip of the rum, and it warms you from the inside out, cutting through the bone-deep cold of the seawater that’s soaked through your boots. Then, through the sheets of rain, you spot it: the faint, pulsing orange glow of the marker buoy marking the entrance to the cove. The squall starts to thin, just a little, and you can see the dark outline of the shoreline ahead, dotted with the soft flicker of lanterns from the coastal village we’ve been delivering life-saving medical supplies to for weeks.

Just as you steer the ship into the calm, sheltered water of the cove, the dream shifts. The storm vanishes entirely; the ocean is glassy, reflecting the pale moonlight, and your crew is cheering, slapping each other on the back. You step away from the wheel, and your shoulder doesn’t hurt anymore, the scrape gone like it was never there. Then, suddenly, you’re no longer on the ship—you’re standing on the dock, holding a crate of penicillin, and a little girl in a tattered dress is handing you a iridescent seashell, grinning so wide her missing front tooth shows. Before you can speak, the world blurs again, and you’re back on the bridge of the ship, the storm roaring back to life, but this time, you’re not scared—not when you look at your crew, all of them standing tall behind you, ready to follow whatever you say next.
