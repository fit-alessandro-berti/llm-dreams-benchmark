…open the project folder, but every file inside is blank except for a single sticky note that says, “You already know how this ends.”

Your boss is standing just behind you now, smiling too widely, tapping a watch with no hands. Around the office, nobody is typing anymore. Everyone has turned to look at you, perfectly still, like mannequins waiting for a cue.

You try to ask a question, but your voice comes out as the soft clacking of a keyboard.

So you start anyway.

The moment your fingers touch the keys, the desk stretches into a long hallway of cubicles, each one containing another version of you working faster, more desperately, under dimmer lights. Every screen shows the same progress bar stuck at 99%.

Your inbox begins filling on its own.

“Just checking in.”
“Any updates?”
“Friendly reminder.”
“Urgent.”
“Why is this late?”

The messages spill out of the monitor as paper, fluttering around your chair like anxious birds. You try to stand, but your office chair has grown roots into the carpet.

Then the deadline arrives.

You hear it before you see it: a deep metallic stamping noise, slow and final, coming from the elevator. Ding. Stamp. Ding. Stamp.

The doors open.

A giant red rubber stamp walks out on thin human legs.

“DEADLINE,” it says in your boss’s voice.

It lurches toward your desk while everyone in the office begins applauding politely.

Panicking, you grab the blank pages and start writing anything—charts, bullet points, half-remembered passwords, a recipe, your childhood address, the word “draft” over and over again. Somehow, the pages fill themselves into a beautiful, finished report bound in leather and glowing faintly blue.

You hand it to your boss.

He opens it.

Every page is a mirror.

He looks down, and his smile finally falters. The office lights flicker. The mannequins blink. The roots around your chair crack apart.

Then someone from the far end of the hallway whispers, “Run before they schedule the follow-up.”

So you do.

You sprint past conference rooms where meetings are happening with no people inside, only voices. You pass the break room, where the coffee machine is hissing your name. You shove open the stairwell door and instead of stairs there is a beach at night, lit by the glow of copier machines washing ashore like waves.

Your laptop is still in your hands. It chimes once.

A calendar notification appears on the screen:

“Project kickoff moved earlier.”

And out over the black water, the sun begins to rise twice.
