
You are about to give a speech to a large audience. As you step onto the stage, you see the crowd looking expectantly at you, and you begin to feel a surge of adrenaline—your heart races, your palms sweat, and your mind goes blank. But then, as you grip the microphone, something shifts. The blinding stage lights soften, and the sea of faces before you blurs into a mosaic of whispers. You blink, and suddenly, every person in the audience is holding a small, glowing key. 

Without realizing it, you’ve begun to speak—not the rehearsed words you’d prepared, but something deeper, as if the keys are unlocking memories you never knew you carried. Your voice grows steadier, weaving stories of forgotten places and people the crowd has never met but somehow *recognizes*. The keys begin to shimmer, and one by one, the audience members raise them, as if in silent applause. 

Then, the microphone transforms into a bird—a phoenix, let’s say—and perches on your shoulder, whispering, *“They’re ready now.”* You step forward, the floor beneath you dissolving into a starlit sky. The crowd follows, floating upward, their keys merging into constellations above. Your speech has become a bridge between worlds, and as you speak, the stars spell out words in a language everyone understands: *“Listen.”* 

The dream fades with the echo of a question you’ll forget upon waking: *“What did you forget to say when you were awake?”*
