They begin to weave on their own, pulling from the air around you: a sigh of indigo, the taste of rain on childhood pavement, the sharp geometry of a memory you couldn't quite name. 

You try to step back, but your shadow stretches forward, pooling onto the canvas like ink. Where it lands, it blooms into watercolor constellations. You realize you don't need a brush. You only need to imagine. You trace a curve in the air, and the curve answers, pulling cerulean from the shifting sky and folding it into a door. You whisper a half-remembered lullaby, and the notes crystallize into amber leaves that drift onto the frame. 

The world around you slows its frantic morphing, drawn toward the canvas by a quiet gravity. Shapes that moments ago were jagged and restless soften into spirals, then faces, then the outline of a room you once knew. The canvas isn't reflecting the dream. It's remembering it. Or perhaps, remembering you.

You sit on the ground, which now feels like crushed velvet and dried moss, and let your hands hover above the weave. There's no urgency here. No beginning or end. Just the gentle pull of creation, backward and forward at once. You know, somewhere beneath the layers of pigment and phantom, that waking is an option. But for now, you let the canvas hum you deeper, painting the quiet spaces between thoughts, until even the dream forgets to end.
