You find yourself in a vast, unfamiliar city, and you realize you have an important meeting in an hour but no idea where it is. You start to run, but the streets rearrange themselves behind you with every turn, so that the bakery you just passed is suddenly a library, the library a dentist's office, the dentist's office a vast empty swimming pool filled with blue light.

You try to ask for directions, but when people open their mouths, letters come out instead of words—little gold letters that float up and rearrange into signs in languages you almost understand but never quite can.

Your phone shows a map, but the city on the screen is not the city around you. The dot that should be "you" pulses in a place that doesn't exist here. The meeting place is labeled in a name that keeps changing: *The Lighthouse. The Garden. The Room With No Windows. The Room With All The Windows.*

You catch a bus, but the bus is also a boat, and it floats through the city streets as if they were canals. The other passengers are entirely calm. One of them, a woman in a gray coat, turns to you and says, "Don't worry. The meeting is the one place that has always been waiting for you. You just have to stop looking for it."

You look down at your hands, and you're holding a key you don't remember picking up. It's warm. It hums.

The bus stops. The doors open. The city outside is now a single building, impossibly tall, and at the top there's a single lit window. You step out, and your feet know the way—up a staircase that wasn't there a moment ago, through a hallway lined with doors that are all slightly open, each one showing a different version of the meeting you're about to have.

You pick a door. You step through.

The room is quiet. Someone is already there, sitting across from you, waiting patiently. They look up and smile, as if they've been expecting you for a very long time.

And suddenly, you remember why you came.
