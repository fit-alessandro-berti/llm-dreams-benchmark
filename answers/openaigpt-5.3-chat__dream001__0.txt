You start to walk, then hurry, then almost run, because every street looks like it should lead somewhere familiar but never does. The buildings are tall and glassy, reflecting a sky that keeps changing colors like a slow mood swing. Your phone is in your hand, but every map app just shows a blinking dot in the middle of a blank grid.

People pass you, lots of them, yet none seem to notice your urgency. You ask someone for directions, and they smile politely and point down a street that curves out of sight. When you follow it, the street quietly turns into a staircase. Halfway down the stairs you realize you're inside a library.

The library is enormous and silent. The shelves are labeled with street names instead of book genres. You pull out a book called *East Meridian Avenue*, open it, and inside is a map—but it’s a map of your childhood neighborhood.

Suddenly your phone buzzes. A calendar notification: “Meeting starting now.”

You look up and the library has turned into a conference room. The long table is already full of people. They’re all looking at you, waiting, as if you were the one who called the meeting.

You take the only empty chair.

No one speaks.

Then someone slides a folder toward you. On the cover is your name, written in handwriting you recognize but can’t place. You open it, and inside is a single page that says:

“Please explain how you got here.”

And that’s when you realize you don’t remember where you were before the city. Or before the stairs. Or even before the meeting.

The people around the table lean forward slightly.

Outside the window, the city rearranges its streets again.
