You drift between clusters of voices, each circle of people briefly opening and closing like tides. Someone laughs too loudly at a joke you didn’t hear. A stranger hands you a drink you don’t remember asking for. Every few steps you think you glimpse the person you know—just a shoulder, a haircut, the color of their jacket—but when you get closer it’s always someone else.

The rooms seem to rearrange themselves as you move. A hallway becomes a balcony, the balcony becomes a kitchen full of humming refrigerators. People speak to you as if they’ve known you for years, yet none of them can tell you where your friend went. One person insists they just saw them upstairs. Another says they already left. A third says, “They’re right behind you.”

You turn around quickly.

For a moment the entire gathering goes quiet, as if everyone is waiting for something. In the center of the room is an empty chair, slowly spinning.

And somehow you realize the strange feeling that’s been following you through the party: the unsettling suspicion that the one person you came here to find might actually be you.
