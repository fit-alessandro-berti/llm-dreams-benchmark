You are about to take the final exam for a course that determines your future career. As you sit down to start the exam, you notice the questions are written in a language you don't recognize—swirling, cursive symbols that seem to shift on the page. You blink, and the first question now reads: "What is the color of regret?" The second: "How many times have you lied to yourself this week?" The third: "Define the exact weight of your father's disappointment." 

Panic rises. You look around, but the other students are already writing furiously, as if the questions make perfect sense. The clock on the wall is melting, dripping numbers onto the floor. You pick up your pencil, but it feels like a live eel, wriggling in your hand. 

The instructor, who you now realize has your grandmother's face, smiles gently and says, "Just answer from the heart, dear." But you have no idea what that means. You try to write something—anything—but your hand won't move. The words on the page dissolve into a pool of ink, and from it rises a small, black butterfly. It flutters toward the ceiling, and as you watch it, you understand that the real exam was never about the answers. It was about noticing the butterfly. 

You wake up with a start, your heart pounding, the taste of ink in your mouth.
