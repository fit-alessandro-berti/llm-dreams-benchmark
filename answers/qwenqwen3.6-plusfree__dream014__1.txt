Alleyways bloom into archways of wisteria, streetlamps stretch upward and become weeping willows with glass leaves, and the crowd thins until it’s just you and your quiet companions moving through a city that feels less like concrete and more like memory made manifest. 

One of them smiles, but their face shifts gently—now it’s your old teacher, now a childhood friend, now your own reflection seen through rain-streaked glass. "You weren't going the wrong way," they say, though no mouth moves. "You were just learning how to read the signs."

The stone in your hand grows warm, and suddenly you're standing before a door you've never seen, yet somehow always known. It's painted the exact shade of the sky on the morning you decided to keep going, even when you didn't know how. You push it open. 

Inside isn't a room. It's a feeling. Weightless. Familiar. Like coming up for air after holding your breath for years.

When you turn to thank your guides, the street is empty again—but the air is still humming, and the city, for the first time, doesn't feel like it's swallowing you. It feels like it's holding space for you to finally arrive.
