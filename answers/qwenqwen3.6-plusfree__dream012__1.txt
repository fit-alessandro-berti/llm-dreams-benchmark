The storm doesn't break—it unravels. Each towering wave peels back into sheets of old parchment, scrawled with names you thought you'd forgotten. Each gale softens into the rustle of turning pages, and the ship stops fighting. It begins to rise, not over the water, but *through* it, as if the ocean were a veil and you were finally parting it. 

You turn to your crew, but their oilskins have melted into the coats of people you've loved, their grips on the rail no longer desperate but steady, almost grateful. One of them touches your shoulder, and you realize you've been holding your breath since the gale began. 

A soundless voice, clear as ice water, speaks not to your ears but to your ribs: *You were never meant to steer the storm. You were meant to outgrow it.*

The horizon folds inward like an old map. The mast becomes a streetlamp. The rigging becomes your own quiet thoughts, finally unspooled. And as the first drop of impossible calm brushes your cheek, the deck dissolves into the familiar weight of your mattress, the roar of the sea fading into the steady rhythm of your own breathing. You wake with salt on your lips and the unshakable feeling that, somewhere in the dark, the ship is still sailing, and this time, it has a lighter load.
