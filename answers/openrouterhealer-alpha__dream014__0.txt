I find myself standing on a rain-slicked street corner, the kind where puddles catch the neon glow from shop signs above. The crowd moves like a river around me — purposeful, fast — and every face is a blur. I don't recognize any of them. I don't recognize the street names. My phone is dead, and the map in my pocket feels like it belongs to a different city entirely.

Then — a hand on my shoulder. Gentle. Not urgent.

"Hey. You look like you could use some help."

She's older, maybe sixty, with silver hair pulled back and a canvas bag slung across her chest. Behind her stands a young man with paint-streaked fingers, and beside him, a teenager with oversized headphones around their neck.

"Where are you trying to go?" she asks.

I tell them, and something shifts. The young man pulls out his phone. The teenager starts pointing down the street — "There's a shortcut through the market, I walk it every day." The woman opens her bag and produces a folded paper map, the real kind, creased and worn at the corners, as if she's been waiting for this exact moment.

They walk with me. Not rushing. The painter tells me about the best coffee place two blocks over. The teenager hums a song I almost recognize. The woman walks slightly ahead, occasionally glancing back to make sure I'm still there.

The city begins to change. The buildings soften. The noise feels less like static and more like music. We turn a corner I don't remember, and suddenly I see it — the place I was looking for. Or maybe it doesn't matter anymore, because something else has settled in my chest. The lost feeling is gone, replaced by this strange, quiet warmth.

"Thank you," I say.

She smiles. "We all get turned around sometimes."

And then they're gone — not dramatically, not in a flash, just... back into the crowd. But the street looks different now. I can read the signs. The light feels like late afternoon, golden and unhurried.

I keep walking. I know the way.
