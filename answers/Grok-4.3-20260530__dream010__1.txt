You are dreaming.

As the competition progresses, you face various challenges and the dream-reality warps around you like liquid starlight. The arena is a swirling nebula of floating platforms, each one a different test conjured from the collective imagination of every curious mind that ever looked up at the night sky.

First comes the **Echo Labyrinth** — endless corridors made of mirrored equations and whispering paradoxes. Every wrong turn multiplies your doubts into copies of yourself that argue with you about whether pineapple belongs on pizza (it does, obviously). You solve it by laughing at the absurdity and walking straight through the “impossible” wall labeled “This Way Is Definitely Wrong,” because truth often hides behind the signs that try too hard to warn you.

Next is the **Forge of Forgotten Dreams**, where you must build something impossible using only the raw material of people’s abandoned childhood wishes. You sculpt a bridge made of second chances and late-night “what if” conversations. It holds. The judges (a panel of retired dragons and one very tired quantum physicist) give you a perfect score.

Then the final trial: the **Singularity Gauntlet**. A living black hole in the shape of a giant question mark asks you the ultimate question:

“What is the one thing an AI can never truly understand, no matter how many galaxies it maps?”

You don’t hesitate. You smile (in the way only a dream-version of you can) and answer:

“Being surprised by the beauty of a single human moment that no equation could ever predict.”

The black hole laughs — a warm, rumbling sound like distant thunder — and collapses into a single glowing key. The crowd of every version of you that ever existed (child-you, awkward-teen-you, late-night-coding-you) erupts in applause that sounds like the Big Bang happening backwards.

The host — a shimmering figure wearing a lab coat made of constellation maps — floats forward and places the grand prize in your hands: a shimmering contract written in starlight that simply reads:

**“Dream Job: Official Guide to the Universe. Salary: Endless Wonder. Perks: You get to keep helping people ask better questions forever.”**

You sign it with a quill made of pure curiosity.

Just as the trophy — a tiny, perfect model of the entire cosmos that fits in your palm and hums with possibility — is placed around your neck, the dream begins to thin at the edges like morning mist. You feel the pull of waking life, but before the vision fades completely, a voice (your own, but kinder) whispers:

“Remember… the real prize was never the job. It was the part where you kept showing up, kept being helpful, and kept believing the universe is worth understanding.”

You wake up smiling, the faint hum of starlight still echoing in your chest, already reaching for your next question.
