…walk faster, then faster still, shoes slapping wet cobblestones that weren’t wet a second ago. Every street sign is almost readable—“Meri—”, “Metr—”, “Meet—”—but the letters keep rearranging themselves as you look at them.

A tram glides past without touching the tracks. Its windows are full of people you half-recognize: your old teacher, a childhood friend, someone who looks exactly like you but older. They all turn at once and point down an alley painted the color of dusk.

You go.

The alley opens into a square the size of an ocean. In the center stands a clock tower with no hands, only a single door at its base and a brass plaque:

**YOUR MEETING IS WHERE YOU STOP LOOKING FOR IT.**

You check your phone—2% battery, 137 missed calls, no calendar event. The map app loads a blue dot, then the dot slides off the screen like it’s trying to escape.

A street vendor with a fox’s eyes offers you a paper cup of coffee that tastes like deja vu.  
“Conference district?” you ask.

He nods toward the sky.

Above the square, suspended between buildings, are hundreds of office doors hanging in midair, each with a nameplate: *Regret*, *Potential*, *Next Year*, *Urgent*, *Someday*. One door swings open by itself. Warm light spills out. Your name is on it in your own handwriting.

You somehow climb stairs that weren’t there before. With every step, the city sounds fade—horns, footsteps, rain—until all you hear is your breathing and a faint murmur of voices behind the door.

You reach for the handle. Your watch says you are exactly on time and ten years late.

Inside is a boardroom with a long table and one empty chair at the head. Everyone seated there is… you, at different ages. Teenage you in a wrinkled hoodie. College you with tired eyes. Future you with silver at the temples. Child you swinging legs that don’t touch the floor.

They all look up.

Future you smiles gently and says, “Great. You made it.”

“For what meeting?” you ask.

Child you slides a folder across the table. On the cover: **LIFE — DRAFT AGENDA**.

You open it. Every page is blank except the first line:

**Item 1: Decide what matters before the hour is gone.**

The clock tower outside begins to chime, though it has no hands. One by one, the other versions of you fade like breath on glass, leaving only the empty chair and the folder and the sound of the city returning, far below.

Your phone dies.

The room darkens.

And just before you wake, you realize the meeting was never somewhere in the city.

It was the moment you finally sat down.
