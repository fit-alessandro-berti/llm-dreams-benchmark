You are in a maze filled with complex puzzles. Each puzzle requires intense concentration and logical thinking to solve, and as you move deeper into the maze, you...

realize the puzzles are getting *quieter*.

At first, they were loud and mechanical — walls covered in shifting numbers, brass dials that clicked, doors that would only open if you arranged colored tiles in the perfect sequence. You solved them with effort, forehead furrowed, the way you solve things when you're awake.

But deeper in, the architecture changes.

The stone becomes warm, like skin. The corridors are taller and narrower, and the light doesn't come from anywhere. The puzzles stop being about numbers.

One room is just a childhood kitchen table. On it is a glass of water, filled exactly to the brim. The puzzle is: carry it to the other side without spilling a single drop. You understand you must walk impossibly slowly, breathe with it. When you make it, the water whispers and the far wall dissolves.

The next room has three doors, and behind each one you can hear someone you love laughing. The inscription above says: *You can only open one. How do you keep all three?*
You sit down for a long time. Then you realize you don't have to open any of them. You just sit and listen to all three at once, and all three doors open on their own.

As you move deeper, you stop solving with logic. You solve with remembering. With feeling. The maze isn't testing your intelligence — it's unwinding it.

At the very center, there is no puzzle at all. There is a small, round room with moss on the floor and open sky above it, even though you are miles underground. In the middle is a desk, and at the desk is you — sleeping peacefully, dreaming this maze.

You understand, suddenly and completely, that you built this maze to protect this sleeping self. Every puzzle was a lock you put on to keep the world out while you rested.

You gently shake your own shoulder.

And you wake up.
