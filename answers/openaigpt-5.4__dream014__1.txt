…and you follow them through streets that seem to rearrange themselves every time you blink.

The city is loud, but around them there is a strange pocket of calm, as if their kindness creates its own weather. One of them hands you a paper cup of something warm that tastes like childhood. Another points upward, and you notice for the first time that the traffic lights are glowing like lanterns, softly breathing red, gold, green.

They don’t ask where you came from. They ask where you’re trying to go.

You open your mouth to answer, but instead of a place, you say, “I want to feel like myself again.”

No one seems surprised.

They lead you down an alley you could swear wasn’t there before. At the end is a tiny shop with a blue door and windows full of clocks, all stopped at different times. Inside, an old woman smiles as if she has been expecting you. She reaches beneath the counter and brings out a map, but it isn’t a map of the city. It’s a map of your life: favorite songs marked as train stations, forgotten hopes drawn as parks, old griefs shown as rivers, and the people you loved glowing like constellations over everything.

“Ah,” she says gently, tapping a place near the center. “You are here.”

The spot is not a street or building. It is a small, flickering light.

The strangers gather around you, and for a moment you understand that being lost is not the same as being broken. It only means you are still moving.

The shop door opens to a rooftop garden high above the city. The skyline stretches out like an unfinished sentence. Wind moves through the flowers, and every petal seems to whisper a memory you thought you’d misplaced. Somewhere below, the city continues rushing and roaring, but up here it sounds almost like music.

One of the strangers points to the horizon. In the distance, between glass towers and drifting clouds, you see it: a path made of light, impossible and clear, leading exactly where you need to go.

You turn to thank them, but they are already fading into the dawn, smiling as if this was always their role—to appear just long enough to remind you that the way forward exists.

And then you take the first step, and the city lights rise around you like stars, and for the first time in a long while, you are no longer afraid of being lost, because you understand that sometimes the dream is not about finding the way home.

Sometimes it is about discovering that home has been walking toward you all along.
