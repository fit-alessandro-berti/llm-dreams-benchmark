I decide to stop talking.

Not out of surrender, but out of sudden, vertiginous recognition: my own voice has begun to sound like a stranger’s—metallic, rehearsed, scraping against the inside of my skull. The room tilts slightly, as if the floor has become a slow elevator. 

I look at my friend and see, for a breathless moment, that they are not my friend at all. They are a reflection of the part of me that believes love must be earned through winning. The realization is so visceral it tastes like copper. 

Instead of throwing another carefully curated sentence into the gap between us, I lift the heavy, invisible weight of my chest and push it aside. I say, quietly, “I’m afraid,” and then, because the truth is slippery, “I’m afraid I’m going to lose you, and I’m trying to armor myself with being right.”

My friend blinks. The armor—ours—makes a sound like ice cracking in spring. Their shoulders drop a fraction of an inch. They don’t answer, and in the silence the room expands. The disagreement is still there, a dark shape in the corner, but it is no longer the only shape in the room. 

Outside the window, the sky begins to bruise into twilight. I reach out, not to clinch or claim, but to rest my open palm on the table between us, an unarmed country. The dream softens at the edges, and I wake with the taste of rain on my tongue.
