...and you grip the wheel tighter, feeling the wood bite into your palms. The ship lurches — a violent, groaning thing — and for a moment the deck tilts so steeply that the lantern swings nearly horizontal, throwing shadows like wild animals across the cabin walls.

But you don't let go.

You've learned something about storms. They want you to fight them. They want you to wrestle the wheel and scream orders and believe that your will alone can hold back the sea. That's the trap. That's how you snap a mast or lose a man overboard — by pretending you're the ocean's equal.

So instead, you do something strange. You stop steering *against* the waves and start steering *with* them. You feel the swell beneath the hull like a living thing, a creature with its own rhythm, and you move with it. The crew stares at you as if you've lost your mind — you're steering *toward* the worst of it.

But here's what they don't see yet: there's a pattern in the chaos. The waves aren't random. They're marching in sets of seven, with a brief, breathless calm between each set. And in that calm — if you time it right — there's a gap between the cliffs of water. A passage.

You count. One. Two. Three. The ship rises. Four. Five. It falls into the trough. Six. Seven—

"NOW!" you roar, spinning the wheel hard to starboard.

The ship groans and *slides* sideways through an impossible gap between two walls of black water, and suddenly — impossibly — the sea opens up. The wind dies to a whisper. The rain thins to mist. And there, emerging from the fog like something from a half-remembered story, is a shoreline you've never seen before. Pale sand. Strange trees with silver bark. And a single light burning on the hill, steady and warm, as if it had been waiting for you all along.

The first mate steps beside you, soaked, shivering, staring.

"Captain... where are we?"

You release the wheel. Your hands are trembling. You realize you have no idea where you are — no chart, no map, no memory of this place.

But the light on the hill feels like an answer to a question you forgot you'd asked.

And you know — the way you know things in dreams, without reason, without proof — that you need to go toward it.
