You start to ask a woman at a bus stop for directions, but when she turns around, she's your third-grade teacher. "You should have left yesterday," she says, and somehow this is both helpful and devastating.

You check your phone. The map shows the city as a spiral, your destination pulsing at the center like a heartbeat. The address changes every time you look at it: 400 Deferred Lane. 17 Almost Avenue. 1 Late Plaza. The battery is at 3%, and you understand this measures something other than charge.

You walk. Every street runs downhill, which seems lucky until you realize downhill is also always *away*. The buildings lean over you, curious. Somewhere a clock strikes thirteen, and pigeons scatter in slow motion, each one wearing a small expression of concern.

Your shoes are gone. You don't remember losing them, but the pavement is warm and soft as bread, so it hardly matters. What matters is the meeting. You still can't recall what it's about — only that everyone you've ever disappointed will be there, checking their watches, and that it is the most important meeting of your life.

A taxi pulls up. The driver is your father, or a large dog, or your father as a large dog; the dream declines to decide. "I know a shortcut," he says, "through your childhood." You climb in. The back seat is the kitchen of the house you grew up in. It is raining inside, gently, and someone has set out two cups of tea, still steaming, though no one has lived there for years.

The taxi drives down the hallway of your old school, past lockers hanging open and humming. You check the time. The hour has not passed. The hour will never pass. And it comes to you, with the quiet authority of dream-knowledge: the meeting is not somewhere you go. It is something that has been waiting for you to stop running.

The taxi stops. A door, with your name on it, spelled almost right. You push it open.

The conference room is an ocean, calm to the horizon. Around a long table of driftwood sit all the people you were afraid to face. They look up as you enter — late, barefoot, empty-handed — and they are not angry. They are relieved.

"There you are," they say. "We've been saving your seat."

You sit. The water is warm. The meeting begins, and it is about something wonderful — but the words are already dissolving, like sugar, like salt, like the gray city behind you, like everything that was never really chasing you at all.
