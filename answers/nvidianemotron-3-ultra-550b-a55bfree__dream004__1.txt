and the moment your fingers brush its surface, the canvas drinks the light from the air around it. The blankness ripples—first a hesitation, then a bloom: ink spreading in water, but the ink is made of *memory*.

A childhood kitchen table. The smell of rain on hot asphalt. The weight of a hand you haven't held in fourteen years.

Each brushstroke you didn't make appears anyway, painted by something behind your ribs. The landscape outside the canvas shifts to match—trees reorganizing into the grain of that table, the sky bruising the exact purple of twilight through your grandmother's lace curtains.

A figure steps from the painting. Not you. *Her*. The version who stayed. The one who said no to the train ticket, yes to the marriage, who buried three phones in a drawer and never looked at the messages piling up like dead leaves.

She holds out a palette knife. The metal catches light that doesn't exist.

"Every color you refused to name," she says, and her voice is the sound of paper tearing. "They're still wet."

The ground beneath you softens. The horizon line dissolves into wet paint.

You realize you're still holding the brush.
