You feel a storm approaching and the trees on either side begin to lean inward toward you like embracing giants, their branches stretching out as if to catch the sudden wind. The air turns from cool and clear to a heavy, suffocating density, smelling of ozone and ancient wood. You stop walking to listen, but the sounds do not come in waves; they arrive in sharp, rhythmic flashes—a low hum turning into a thunderclap that feels less like an earthquake than a waking breath. When it breaks, you don't wake up immediately, but the silence is so profound it rushes over you like water filling a bucket until you cannot breathe, only to realize there is no ground beneath your feet—no roots, no soil. You are standing on an edge of glass floating in nothingness within the dream itself, and you know that when the sky opens its mouth, this will be all you can see for another day.

You feel a storm approaching and the silence returns but feels different now. It is not empty, but filled with the memory of voices that no one can hear anymore. Your reflection in the water changes from your own face to something entirely blank and new, then back again as if nothing happens while you were gone. The forest recedes into a blur of light when the wind stops, and in its place stands a path made of floating moss and stones glowing with soft heat rather than fire.

And there is a silence that feels like weight against your chest, heavy enough to make you feel grounded in the dream but light as air if you were to fly away from it all at once.
