…leave the room before you say something you can’t take back.

You push through the door, expecting a hallway, but step instead onto the long wooden dock behind your childhood house. Night has folded itself over everything. The lake is black glass. The stars are too low, hanging just above the water like lanterns someone forgot to turn off.

Behind you, your friend is still talking, except their voice doesn’t sound like a voice anymore. It sounds like pages turning too fast. Like rain hitting windows. Like all the old conversations you never finished.

The argument is still about the same thing—something precious, something tangled up with promises and memory and the fear that if one of you lets go, it means neither of you ever really meant it. In the dream, you know exactly what it is. The second you try to name it, though, the word slips away and sinks into the lake.

You start walking down the dock to cool off.

Each plank under your feet has a year written on it.

2014.

2017.

Last summer.

The night you almost stopped speaking.

The day you met.

You stop.

Halfway down the dock sits a small table with two mugs on it, steam rising despite the cold. One mug is yours. The other is cracked down the middle but somehow still holding together. Your friend is suddenly there at the far end, as if they had always been there, standing under a crooked yellow porch light that has no house attached to it.

“You always do this,” they say, but not angrily now. Their voice is normal again. “You leave right when it matters.”

“And you always push,” you say.

The lake answers for them, rippling with words you don’t remember saying. They spread across the surface in pale silver lines, then dissolve.

Your friend steps closer. The dock lengthens. You step toward them. It lengthens again. It keeps the same impossible distance between you, no matter how much either of you move.

That makes you laugh, once, sharply, because of course your dream would make the problem literal.

Your friend almost smiles.

Then the stars begin dropping into the lake one by one.

Not falling—lowering, carefully, as though the sky is setting the table.

Each star touches the water and becomes a memory. The two of you on a bus, too tired to talk but not needing to. The time you got lost and pretended not to be scared. The promise you made—there it is now, brighter than the others, hovering just above the black water like a coin.

Your friend looks at it. “I thought you were breaking it.”

You stare at the memory-star. Inside it, both of you are younger and certain. “I thought you were trying to trap me inside it.”

That lands between you with surprising softness.

The dock finally stops stretching.

You walk the rest of the way and stand face-to-face. Up close, your friend looks exhausted, not furious. Their eyes are rimmed with the same fear you’ve been carrying, only now you can see it: not fear of losing the argument, but of losing what the argument is really about. The friendship. The shared past. The proof that the two of you meant something real to each other.

Without thinking, you reach toward the cracked mug on the table. Your friend reaches for it too.

For one tense second, it seems like it will shatter.

Instead, your hands meet around it.

The crack seals itself with gold.

Neither of you says sorry. Dreams are often too proud for that. But your friend exhales, and you do too, and somehow that is enough to change the weather. The wind dies. The lake smooths out. The stars rise back into place, except for one, which stays in your friend’s palm like a tiny warm marble.

They hold it out to you. “We don’t have to keep it the same,” they say. “Just keep it.”

You nod, though tears have started for no clear reason, dream-tears that feel enormous and distant at the same time. When you take the star, it is lighter than it looks. It beats once in your hand, like a second heart.

Then the dock becomes a train platform.

Then the train becomes your old school hallway.

Then all the doors swing open at once, and every room behind them is a different version of your life.

Your friend is beside you now, shoulder to shoulder.

“Where do we go?” you ask.

They look at you, calmer than before. “Forward.”

So you do.

And just before you wake, you realize the argument was never about winning at all. It was about whether love could survive being changed.

In the dream, the answer is yes.
