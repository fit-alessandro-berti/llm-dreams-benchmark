...I decide to say the true thing. Not the sharp thing, the *true* thing — which in the dream are two different objects sitting on a table between us, and I have to pick one up.

We're in the kitchen of a house I've never lived in but know completely. The argument has been going for hours, or for one long sentence that keeps not ending. It's about — and this is the part that stays clear even as everything else goes soft — it's about whether a person can be forgiven for something they haven't finished doing yet. She says no. I say I don't know, which is somehow the more aggressive position.

Her face keeps almost changing into someone else's. Not menacingly. The way a word you're staring at stops looking spelled right.

So I pick up the true thing. It's heavier than I expect, and warm, like a stone left in the sun, and the moment I lift it I understand that I've been arguing about something else the entire time. Not the stated topic. The topic underneath, which is: *I am afraid you will decide I'm not worth the trouble.* That's all. That's the whole engine of it. Fifteen years of friendship and a two-hour fight and it was just that little animal fear, wearing a costume made of principles.

I try to say it out loud and my mouth fills with water. Not drowning — just too full to speak. This happens in dreams. I gesture at the object in my hands, hoping she can read it.

And she can. That's the thing about the dream. She looks at what I'm holding and her shoulders drop about an inch and she says, "Oh. *That.*" Like I'd finally found her house keys. Like she'd been waiting the whole time for me to arrive at the actual room.

Then the kitchen is a beach, because it is time for that. The argument is still happening but it's outside of us now, two weather systems colliding somewhere over the water, and we're sitting on the sand watching it, not talking, passing something back and forth — a shell, a cup, a small warm bird, it keeps deciding. Neither of us has conceded. That's important. Nobody won. The disagreement is still fully intact and will be there tomorrow, structurally sound, and we're going to have to keep working on it for years.

But we're on the same side of it now, looking at it, instead of standing on either side using it as a wall.

She says something I can't hear over the surf. I say "What?" and she laughs and doesn't repeat it, and in the dream this is fine, this is even the best part — the trust that it wasn't important, or that it was so important it didn't need to survive the wind.

I wake up with the specific ache of having said the true thing, and no memory of what it was.

Only the shape of it. Warm. Heavier than expected.
