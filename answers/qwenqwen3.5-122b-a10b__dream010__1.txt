One challenge requires you to build a bridge across a chasm, but the bricks are made of your own memories, and they keep turning into birds and flying away before you can lay them. You try to catch them, but your hands pass through them like smoke. Another task asks you to sing a song you've never heard, yet you know every note perfectly, and your voice harmonizes with the hum of the ceiling lights.

The judges, who started as stern figures in suits, gradually lose their features until they are smooth, featureless mannequins. But when you look into their eyes, you see your own reflection staring back, looking tired but hopeful.

Finally, you reach the final round. The prize, your dream job, is sitting on a velvet cushion. It isn't a contract or a key; it is a door. A simple, wooden door standing alone in the middle of the room. You approach it, and as you reach for the handle, you realize you don't need to open it to know what's inside. You already know you are capable. The door doesn't open; instead, it dissolves into light.

The light swells, filling the room, and you feel a sensation of falling upward. The competition fades away, replaced by the feeling of sheets against your skin and the sound of a morning alarm. You wake up, the memory of the dream lingering like a warm mist, and for the first time in a long time, the anxiety of the real world feels a little less heavy.
