You are about to give a speech to a large audience. As you step onto the stage, you see the crowd looking expectantly at you, and you begin to speak—but no sound comes out.

A ripple moves through the audience, not of confusion, but recognition. One by one, they raise small lanterns from their laps, each glowing with a different color. The hall darkens until the crowd becomes a field of stars, and the stage beneath your feet turns into the deck of a ship.

You look down and realize your notes have become a map. The lines on the paper shift and curl like rivers, leading toward a bright mark at the edge of the page. The audience waits quietly, their lanterns swaying like constellations over a black sea.

Then, from somewhere above, a voice whispers, “You don’t have to remember the speech. You only have to remember why you came.”

You take a breath. This time, when you open your mouth, music pours out—not words, but something warmer and older. The crowd smiles as if they understand perfectly. The ship begins to move, sailing through the theater doors and out into a sky full of waves.

And just before you wake, you see the map in your hands draw one final line, leading straight back to your heart.
