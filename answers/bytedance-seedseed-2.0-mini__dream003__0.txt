I pat my jacket pockets, the floor beneath me, even the crooks of the box’s corners for a key, but there’s nothing but fine cedar dust and a stray cobweb stuck to my cuff. The box thrums, soft and steady, like a hive of honeybees quieted down just for me, and I spot a tiny, almost invisible notch cut into the lid where two of the swirling patterns meet—no keyhole, just a gap that fits exactly the curve of the locket I’d unclasped in my fidgeting, forgotten around my neck.

I lift the pendant over the notch and slip it into place. There’s a sharp, sweet click, like popping the top of a warm soda can, and the entire box floods with honeyed gold light. The patterns stop shifting, glowing bright enough to cast faint shadows on my palms, and the lid creaks open on its own, no hands needed.

Inside isn’t glinting jewels or stacks of gold like my silly, panic-fueled daydreams had imagined. Instead, there are just three things: a folded scrap of notebook paper scrawled in my own messy handwriting, a tiny clay sparrow that fits perfectly in the cup of my palm, and a single dried bluebell pressed flat against the lid’s underside. I pick up the note, and my voice catches when I read it: “The key was never something you found. It was something you carried.”

Just as the words settle in my head, the hum fades, the light dims to a soft glow, and the world blurs at the edges—one second I’m kneeling on an attic floor dusted with old photo albums, the next I’m sitting cross-legged on my apartment’s linoleum, the mysterious box gone entirely. Tucked in my palm is still the tiny clay sparrow, and a dried bluebell is stuck behind my ear, even though I didn’t bring either into the room. My hand drifts to my neck, and my locket is still clasped tight around my throat, like it never left.

Then I blink hard, and the dream slips away a little more—but when I glance down at my nightstand, the clay sparrow is sitting right next to my half-empty coffee mug, and a single fresh bluebell is tucked between the pages of my well-worn book. I reach for it, and it crumbles gently to fine cedar dust between my fingers, just like the patterns on the box would have, back in the dream.
