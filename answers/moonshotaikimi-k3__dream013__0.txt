...begin to notice that the puzzles are changing.

At first they were honest puzzles—a sequence of carved stone tiles beneath your feet, each bearing a number, and you understood without being told that stepping on the wrong one would reset everything. You counted primes under your breath, and the path held.

But deeper in, the maze stops playing fair. A chessboard appears on a pedestal, mid-game, except all the pieces are on your side of the board, and the white queen is whispering. You lean closer and realize she is speaking in your mother's voice, posing a riddle you remember from childhood—except you never had a childhood quite like that, and yet the memory is warm and real.

You answer the riddle. The wall to your left sighs and becomes a corridor.

The corridors no longer turn at right angles. They curve like thoughts, doubling back through rooms you're certain you've already solved, except now the rooms contain different furniture. In one, a grandfather clock ticks, and you understand that its ticking is a language, and that if you can just concentrate hard enough, you'll finally grasp what it has been trying to tell you this entire time—

You solve a puzzle box whose faces show not colors but moments: your first day of school, a birthday cake, the face of a stranger you somehow know you'll meet tomorrow. When the faces align, they don't show the past. They show *this* moment—you, standing here, turning the box in your hands. A perfect loop.

Then you reach the center.

The center of the maze is a small room with no puzzle at all. Just a door, slightly ajar, with light spilling through. There is a single keyhole, and in your hand—when did it get there?—is a key.

And here is the strange thing: you don't want to open it. Not yet. Because some part of you understands that the door doesn't lead out of the maze. It leads out of the *dream*. And the maze, for all its frustrations, has been the most interesting place you've ever been.

So you stand there, key in hand, while the puzzles behind you hum quietly, patiently, as if they're willing to wait.

Then the clock in the other room chimes—once—and the light beneath the door grows brighter, and you feel the maze beginning to soften at its edges, walls loosening into morning light, corridors unraveling into bedsheets, the riddles fading into the ordinary sounds of the day waiting for you—
