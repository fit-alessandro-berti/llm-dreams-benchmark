You fumble for your phone, only to find it’s not your phone at all—its screen glows with bioluminescent fireflies instead of app icons, and when you tap the one that blinks “MEETING,” it just drifts up and bobs north, as if leading you.

You chase it past storefronts that warp the longer you stare: a laundromat where the dryers spin not clothes, but faded postcards you’d written and never sent; a bakery where the baker slides you a lemon scone through the window, and when you bite into it, a smudged address in sugar frosting dissolves on your tongue, just enough to make out “rooftop, west of the old clock tower.”

You flag down a cab, but the driver is your 16-year-old best friend, the one you lost touch with after college, who grins and says “Took you long enough to remember this” before the car lurches up a staircase that grows out of the subway entrance, scaling the side of a skyscraper. The clock tower chimes 59 minutes after you started running—you’re 60 seconds late, but when the cab drops you on the rooftop, there’s no stern client, no urgent work deadline. Spread across a blanket are all the people you’d been meaning to call, all the versions of yourself you’d abandoned to keep up with work: the kid who wanted to paint, the friend who missed three of your old group’s birthdays, the part of you that forgot what it felt like to not be late for something.

The firefly from your phone lands on your shoulder, and you realize the “important meeting” was your own mind forcing you to show up for the parts of your life you’d left lost in that unfamiliar city. You sit down, and no one mentions you were late. The skyline glows pink, and for the first time in as long as you can remember, you don’t feel like you’re rushing anywhere at all.
