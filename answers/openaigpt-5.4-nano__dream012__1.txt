You are the captain of a ship in the middle of a turbulent sea. Your crew looks to you for guidance as the storm worsens, and you

grip the cold brass of the wheel until your knuckles ache, as if the metal could anchor you to the only solid thing left: purpose. The wind claws at the sails like it wants to tear the ship into stories it can tell itself. Lightning stitches white seams across the black sky, and each flash reveals a new shape in the waves—teeth of foam, slanted towers of water rising to threaten the bow, then collapsing as though the ocean is trying to remember what it was before it learned fear.

“Captain!” someone shouts, voice flattened by rain. “The compass—”

“I see it,” you say, though you’re not sure you do. The compass needle jitters wildly, spinning between directions that feel wrong, like choices in a dream you can’t stop making. Around you, the crew moves in practiced panic: ropes pulled too hard, lanterns swinging, boots scraping for grip. You can’t give them certainty, not the kind that comes from maps and instruments. All you have is the moment, and the feeling that the storm is listening.

You call for silence. Not loudly—commandingly—but with a steadiness that cuts through the chaos. You step from the wheel to the center of the deck and plant your feet where the ship creaks the most, as if the boards themselves might confess which way the water wants to go. Your breath comes out in bursts; every exhale is swallowed by the wind.

Then—between thunderclaps—you notice something odd.

The ship is no longer simply being battered. It’s being *handled*. The waves strike at angles that repeat, patterns that almost resemble a rhythm. The foam flares in pulses, like a distant lighthouse but without light—only motion, only timing. For a heartbeat, the storm seems to pause, not in mercy, but in attention, as though it’s waiting for you to understand the message hidden in its violence.

You close your eyes.

In the dark behind your eyelids, you see a different deck: a calm one, long ago or never, lit by sun and hung with flags that should not exist. You see yourself there, younger, laughing, guiding the same ship while the sea behaves like a friend. The image flickers like a lantern in wind.

“Captain!” your first mate cries again. “We’re losing her—”

“I know,” you say, and the word lands strangely, not as denial but as agreement. The storm can’t be reasoned with. It can only be *answered*.

You turn to the crew. The rain makes everyone look the same—blurred, determined, terrified. You point to the far horizon, where the darkness is slightly less dark. It might be an opening. It might be another hallucination. Dreams are generous with doors.

“By the time you finish that rope,” you tell them, “we steer toward that seam.”

“But the compass—”

“Forget the compass.” Your voice steadies, surprising even you. “The storm is giving us directions in its own language. We just have to speak back.”

You order the sails adjusted—not toward what the needle says, but toward the pattern you’ve begun to feel in the ship’s rocking. You tell them to slack a line, then tighten it on the next surge, as if timing your breaths to a heartbeat you can’t see. The crew hesitates for a fraction of a second—enough for doubt to become a wave—then moves, because the captain has spoken like a person who believes in survival.

Lightning strikes again. In the brief white glare, the horizon fractures.

Not with clouds or distance—*with structure*. A jagged outline rises out of the sea: ribs of rock, slick as black glass, forming the edge of something enormous. A breakwater. A wall. Or the spine of some sleeping animal. The waves slam against it and rebound, making the same pulse you noticed before. The storm isn’t attacking you at random; it’s trying to keep you from finding the shelter line it already carved.

Your hands move without thinking, guiding the wheel in small corrections. Not dramatic turns—gentle nudges, like steering a dream back toward the moment it wants you to remember. Each time you adjust, the ship responds as if recognizing your touch.

The deck shudders. A gust tries to steal the ship sideways. For an instant the mast groans like a throat about to scream. Your crew clings to lines and each other, eyes wide, mouths opening around prayers that don’t have time to finish.

You hold the wheel through it.

And in that long, bracing struggle, another truth rises in your chest: you aren’t just steering a ship. You’re steering *attention*. In your hands is the decision that says: *We do not panic. We do not bargain. We do not pretend the storm isn’t real—yet we refuse to be led by it.*

So you speak again, this time not to the crew but to the sea.

“Not like this,” you say, and you don’t know if you mean the storm or your own fear. “Not tonight.”

As if hearing you, the turbulence tightens—then loosens. The wind’s teeth close and, abruptly, the pressure on the hull changes. The waves become less wild, more deliberate. They still hit, but now they hit from angles that build momentum rather than break it.

The seam you pointed to becomes a doorway of weather.

Rain turns to mist. The lightning thins. The thunder moves farther away, retreating like an angry thought that has been interrupted. The ship slides—still pitching, still alive—into a pocket where the waves are lower, where the deck begins to speak with steadier creaks instead of frantic cries.

Your first mate stares at you, soaked through, as though you’ve performed a trick with impossible timing.

“How did you—” she begins.

You swallow, because the answer is too strange to fit in a human sentence. You don’t say you felt the storm’s language in the ship’s rhythm. You don’t say you remembered a deck that might have never existed. You don’t say you think the horizon was always waiting for you to decide to trust it.

Instead, you just nod toward the calmer water beyond the rough edge.

“Look,” you say.

In the thinning mist, another ship appears—not on the sea, but in the air, as if it’s made of the same substance as the dream. Its sails are furled, its lights warm and steady. A signal flag hangs at its mast: the same symbol you’ve seen in your vision. The one that stitched itself to your memory during lightning.

The crew follows your gaze. For the first time, their terror loosens into something like awe.

A voice carries across the water, not through wind or sound but through certainty.

*Captain. You answered.*

You feel your own name rise in the back of your throat—then dissolve, replaced by the sensation that you are being acknowledged by something larger than weather.

You take your hands off the wheel, only enough to rest them briefly against the deck, grounding yourself. Then you lift your head and breathe, slow and deep, as though the storm had been trying to steal your breath and you finally refused to let it.

“Signal them,” you say.

“Captain?” your mate asks, cautious.

“Yes,” you reply, smiling in the way you only can in dreams—without knowing what will come next, but knowing you’ll meet it. “Full sail. We’re going home through the place the storm was guarding.”

And as the ship gathers itself and moves forward—guided not by compass, not by fear, but by the strange, remembered rhythm of survival—the sea behind you settles into something almost gentle, as if it, too, has learned the lesson: the storm was never just to destroy you.

It was to test whether you could steer your life by meaning, even when every instrument screamed confusion.
