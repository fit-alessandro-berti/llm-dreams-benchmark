
You are about to give a speech to a large audience. As you step onto the stage, you see the crowd looking expectantly at you, and you begin to **speak—but your voice comes out in a strange, unfamiliar language. The microphone screeches with feedback, and the words on your note cards blur and rearrange themselves into nonsense symbols.**

Panic rises, but then you notice something: the audience isn't frowning or walking out. They are leaning forward, utterly captivated. A woman in the front row nods slowly, as if she understands every word. A man takes out a notebook and begins writing furiously.

You realize, with a dreamlike certainty, that you aren't speaking *to* them—you are speaking *for* them. The words, though alien to you, are giving voice to thoughts they've never been able to articulate. The fear melts into a strange, quiet power.

You finish, not with a bow, but with a silent question in your eyes. The entire crowd rises—not in applause, but in a wave of silent, knowing smiles. You wake up with the echo of that unspoken understanding still humming in your chest, wondering what part of yourself you just gave a voice to.
