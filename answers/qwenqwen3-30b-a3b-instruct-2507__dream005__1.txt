…feel the weight of invisible eyes. The air hums with laughter and low conversation, but it drowns out the sound of my own breath. The person I know – a quiet woman named Elara, with eyes like storm clouds and a laugh like wind chimes – was here just moments ago, standing near a towering, living ivy wall that seems to pulse with a soft, green light. Now, she’s vanished.

I push through a cluster of people dressed in shimmering, shifting fabrics that seem to absorb the light. Their faces are blurred, like watercolor washed by rain. One extends a hand adorned with mechanical fingers that tap a rhythm on their palm – *tap-tap-tap-tap* – but I don’t hear it. It’s just a silent, frantic motion. I step back, the fabric of my own coat feeling suddenly too tight, too real.

Further on, near a fountain that spills liquid silver into a pool reflecting not the ceiling, but swirling galaxies, I see a group gathered around a man arguing passionately with a transparent, floating hourglass. He gestures wildly, and the glass shatters in slow motion, the sand dissolving into tiny, glowing moths that flutter upwards. The crowd is silent, watching, but their expressions are blank, their mouths open in silent screams. I try to speak, to ask if they’ve seen Elara, but my voice is gone, replaced by the faint chime of wind in distant trees.

I turn, desperate, and stumble into a room filled with mirrors. But these mirrors don’t show me. They show *us*. Elara is there, but not as I remember her. Here, she’s a towering figure made of woven light, standing on a balcony overlooking a city of glass spires beneath a double moon. Another mirror shows her, small and lost, huddled in a library with books that whisper secrets in languages I don’t know, her hand reaching out towards a door that isn’t there. And in a third, I see myself – not here, but *there*, standing at a different gathering, utterly alone, staring back at me from the glass with an expression of profound, aching recognition.

A cold hand touches my shoulder. I whirl, heart hammering. It’s Elara. But her face is faded, like an old photograph left in the sun. She doesn't look at me, her eyes fixed on a point beyond my shoulder, her lips moving silently. The chime of wind chimes sounds again, faintly, *familiarly*. I try to speak her name, but the word won't form. The room of mirrors begins to ripple, the reflections dissolving like smoke.

The sound of the breeze fades. The last thing I see before the dream collapses is Elara’s hand, thin and translucent, reaching *through* the mirror towards me, her fingers brushing the cold glass where my own hand is pressed. And I understand, with a sudden, terrifying clarity: *She wasn't lost. She was always here, in the reflection. I was the one who never found her.* Then, the sound of distant, invisible applause begins, building slowly, patiently, as the dream dissolves into the soft velvet dark.
