You are at a large social gathering where you only know one person. As you try to find them, you encounter various groups of people and they all seem to be whispering about the same thing: the floor is made of old photographs that shift under your feet like wet leaves. 

The first cluster you push through is a circle of figures in mismatched formal wear, each one holding a single red thread tied to their wrist. They’re passing around a bowl of what looks like water but tastes like static when they insist you sip. One of them—someone who has your mother’s hands but a stranger’s face—leans in and says, “You’re late. The music hasn’t started yet because no one can remember how it goes.” You keep walking.

Next is a long table of people playing an endless game of cards where the suits keep changing. Every time someone wins a hand, a small door opens somewhere in the wall and a different version of the person you’re looking for steps out for a second before vanishing again. You try to ask directions, but they only answer in questions: “Have you checked under the laughter?” “Did you bring the right kind of silence?” One of them slides you a card that simply reads “Not yet.” You pocket it without looking and move on.

Deeper in, the crowd thins into a smaller group standing perfectly still, facing inward like a huddle of conspirators. Their backs are to you, but when you circle around, you see they have no faces at all—just smooth, warm skin where eyes and mouths should be. They’re humming a single low note that makes your teeth ache. One of them reaches out and gently places a hand over your own heart, as if checking whether it’s still beating in time with theirs. For a moment you feel calm. Then the note changes pitch and you remember you were looking for someone.

You finally spot them on the far side of the room, standing alone under a chandelier made of antlers. They look exactly as you remember, except their shadow is cast in the wrong direction and keeps trying to step away from them. When you call out, they turn and smile with someone else’s teeth. “I’ve been waiting,” they say. “But you brought the wrong thread.”

Before you can answer, the entire room tilts. The floor of photographs slides away like a tablecloth being pulled, and everyone begins to fall upward into a sky that is also a ceiling. You reach for the person you know, but your hand closes on nothing but the red thread that was never yours to begin with. The last thing you hear before the dream folds is the sound of polite applause from people who were never there. 

Then the chandelier goes out.
