...and you almost don't believe them at first.

The city is too loud to be kind. Neon signs are buzzing and dripping color onto the wet pavement, cars honking in a language you don't understand, and every street seems to tilt slightly uphill no matter which way you turn. Everyone is moving so fast. You are standing still in the middle of it all with your hands empty, not even sure what address you're trying to get to anymore.

Then one of them touches your shoulder - gently.

It's a woman in a yellow raincoat, even though it isn't raining. Next to her is an old man carrying groceries in paper bags that never seem to get wet, and a teenager with headphones around his neck playing music just loud enough for you to hear.

"You look lost," the woman says, not like an accusation, but like she's recognizing an old friend.

"We were all headed this way anyway," the teenager says.

You try to explain that you don't know where you're going, that you don't have a map, that you shouldn't be bothering them. But the old man just smiles and says, "That's alright. No one really knows. We just walk together until it feels right."

So you walk.

And as you walk with them, something strange happens to the city. The buildings stop looming. The crowd doesn't feel like it's pushing past you anymore - it feels like it's flowing *with* you. The streetlights flicker in time to the teenager's music. The woman in the yellow coat tells you a story about getting lost in this exact same intersection when she was little, and how she found the best bakery in the world because of it. You laugh, and your voice doesn't get swallowed by the noise.

You turn a corner you are sure wasn't there before - a quiet street lined with small, warm-lit windows. Plants growing out of fire escapes. A cat asleep on a stoop. 

You stop. 

You don't know the name of this street. You've never been here. But your chest loosens. The frantic buzzing in your head finally goes quiet.

"This is it," you say, surprised by how certain you sound.

The three of them nod, like they knew. The woman hands you something - it's small and warm - your own house key, even though you don't remember losing it.

"We'll be around if you need us again," she says.

You wake up before you open the door. But the feeling stays - that warmth in your palm, and the quiet certainty that you were never as alone as you felt.
