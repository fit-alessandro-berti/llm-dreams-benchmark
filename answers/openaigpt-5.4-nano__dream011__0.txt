…written on a sheet of paper that feels warm, as if it has been held in someone else’s hands moments ago.

You flip the page and the first question is simple—too simple:

**1. What do you want?**

You stare at the words and realize you aren’t actually holding a pen. Your hand is empty, hovering above the paper like a puppet about to be given strings. Around you, the classroom is silent in that thick, underwater way dreams sometimes get—sounds exist, but only as distant impressions, like you’re hearing them from another room.

You blink. The paper changes.

The question now reads:

**1. Who do you become when you don’t get what you want?**

Your throat tightens. The exam clock above the board ticks, but the ticking is…wrong. It doesn’t measure time. It measures decisions. Each tick feels like a doorway closing somewhere behind you.

You swallow and turn the page anyway.

**2. Describe the moment you stopped believing.**

You open your mouth to answer and the air won’t let the words out. Instead, the paper begins to fill itself—not with handwriting, but with images that appear in the space where answers should be. A kitchen. A phone screen. A laugh that sounded kind until it landed like a verdict. A hallway where everyone else knew which way to go.

The classroom light flickers. In the flicker, you see the faces of people you’ve tried to impress. Not their real faces—something simplified, like masks left out too long in the sun.

When the light steadies again, a new question is waiting.

**3. Choose one:**
A) Safety  
B) Speed  
C) Truth  
D) Love  

You try to read the options, but each time you focus, a different choice feels heavier, as if it has weight in your hands already. When you glance away, the weights shift.

You hear someone sit down behind you.

No one is there.

The chair in the space behind your desk creaks as if it’s being used by an invisible student who’s been taking the test for years.

A voice—calm, neutral, familiar in the way your own thoughts are familiar—says from nowhere:

“Pick carefully. Careers are built from patterns.”

Your mind scrambles for the trick, the catch, the way dreams often ask you to fail on purpose. But the exam doesn’t feel like a trap. It feels like a mirror with a grading rubric.

You turn to the next page.

The question reads:

**4. Explain how you treat your fear.**

You want to write: *I manage it. I use it. I push through.*

But the pen still isn’t there.

So instead, you do something stranger. You lift your palm above the paper and let your fear float up from your chest like steam.

In the dream, thoughts are physical. Your fear rises in a shape—gray at first, then sharply detailed, like a photograph developing. It becomes a small animal with too many eyes. It looks at you with a hunger that isn’t for food. It’s hungry for certainty.

You realize the exam isn’t asking what you say about fear. It’s asking what you *feed*.

You lower your hand. The creature hovers, waiting for your instruction.

Behind you, the unseen chair creaks again.

You feel the weight of your choices pressing on your skin. You feel the way you’ve tried to be “good” at life—good enough, impressive enough, safe enough—until being good turned into being numb.

The paper waits.

The next line appears, slowly, as if writing itself requires permission:

**5. What are you avoiding?**

You can’t answer with a sentence. The truth doesn’t fit in the space between words. It needs time. It needs a place to land.

So you do the only thing you can think of: you stand up.

The room tilts.

Your desk stretches longer, taller, becomes a set of stairs leading toward the chalkboard. The questions slide across the paper like fish, escaping your grasp. For a moment you see every question at once, overlapping:

*What do you want?  
Who do you become when you don’t get what you want?  
Describe the moment you stopped believing.  
Choose one: Safety / Speed / Truth / Love  
Explain how you treat your fear.  
What are you avoiding?*

They shimmer, and in their overlap you notice something new—a sentence hidden between them, like an answer beneath an answer:

**“Tell the truth even if it changes the grade.”**

Your legs move before you decide. You climb the stairs of your own avoidance, step by step, and reach the chalkboard.

There’s no writing on it—until you touch it.

The chalk appears in your hand. It feels heavy and real, like it has been waiting.

You write one word:

**“Me.”**

The moment the letter curves into shape, the classroom exhales. The overhead lights stop flickering. The ticking clock changes sound; it begins to behave like a clock again—measuring time instead of choices.

Behind you, the invisible student stands.

You don’t turn around, because you’re suddenly afraid you’ll see a version of yourself that already took this exam and chose wrong.

But the voice—calm, neutral—doesn’t sound disappointed.

It sounds…finished.

“Final answer accepted,” it says.

The paper in your hands flutters open by itself and you see the last question appear at the bottom of the page, smaller than all the others, almost shy:

**6. After you pass, what will you do with the future?**

You grip the chalk. Your mouth finally works.

You start to say, *I’ll study. I’ll work. I’ll—*

But in the dream, you can feel the sentence trying to be safe again.

So you erase it.

You write a new response instead, on the exam paper itself, in the blank space where a career plan should go:

**“I will live it.”**

The classroom dissolves into bright, soft daylight.

Not the kind of daylight that reveals everything clearly—more like a curtain pulled back, letting you see the shape of the room you’ve been in the whole time.

You blink.

You’re still holding the paper, but it’s not an exam anymore.

It’s a list of instructions written on something you recognize: the cover of a journal you’ve owned for years.

On the first page, in ink that looks like it came from your own pen at last, there’s only one line:

**Write the next chapter like it’s already yours.**

And then you wake—feeling the strange, quiet certainty that you’re not being tested on who you become in some distant future.

You’re being tested on whether you’re willing to start now.
