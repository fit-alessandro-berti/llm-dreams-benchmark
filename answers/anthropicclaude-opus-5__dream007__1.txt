...I decide to stop talking.

Not out of spite. It's just that mid-sentence I notice my words are coming out as small folded paper birds, and hers are too, and the room is filling up with them, and none of them are landing. They circle near the ceiling like moths around a light that isn't there. I have made a very good point — I know I have — and it's up there somewhere, wing-tip to wing-tip with hers, indistinguishable.

So I close my mouth and the silence has weight, like water.

She's still going. Her mouth moves and paper keeps coming and I realize with the flat certainty you only get in dreams that we have been having this same argument for eleven years, in different kitchens. The kitchen changes. The tile goes from white to green to that awful terracotta. Once we were on a train. But it's the same argument, and the terrible thing — the thing that makes my chest hurt in the dream, actually hurt — is that I can no longer remember what it's about. Only that it matters. Only that if I lose it, something specific and irreplaceable goes away.

I try to catch one of the birds to read it. It comes apart in my hands like wet ash.

"You're not listening," she says, and it's the first thing either of us has said that stays solid, that lands on the table between us with a sound.

"I know," I say. "I've been busy being right."

And she laughs — one syllable, surprised out of her — and just like that the ceiling clears. The birds go out the window in a single sheet, the way starlings turn. The kitchen is her grandmother's kitchen now, the one with the humming refrigerator and the calendar from 1994.

We sit down. Neither of us apologizes, because in the dream apologizing would be a way of ending the conversation, and we don't want it to end. We want to keep having it. That's what I understand, sitting there: the argument was never the obstacle. It was the shape our attention took. Eleven years of one person insisting the other be more real.

She puts the kettle on. I notice she has been crying and also that she hasn't, both, the way dreams allow.

"Say it again," she says. "Slower."

And I open my mouth, and I don't know what comes out, but she's leaning forward with her chin in her hand, and the kettle is starting to tick as it heats, and I think: *oh — this. This is what I wanted. Not to win. Just this leaning-forward.*

Then the floor is sand, and we're somewhere else, and it doesn't matter, because she's still listening.
