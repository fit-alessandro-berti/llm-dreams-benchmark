The patterns begin to shimmer and breathe, like living veins of silver and starlight etched across obsidian wood. They aren't just designs—they're *moving*, slowly rearranging themselves into symbols you almost remember from a life you haven't lived yet. A spiral. A keyhole shaped like an eye. A tiny constellation that matches the freckles on your own left hand.

You run your fingers across the surface and feel it *warm* to your touch, purring like a cat made of midnight. No key appears. Instead, the box tilts its lid slightly (though it has no hinges you can see) and whispers in your own voice, but softer, older:

*"The lock was never on the outside, dreamer."*

You suddenly understand. You press your eye to the moving keyhole and the box unfolds—not opens, *unfolds*—like a paper crane made of infinite dimensions. Inside is not an object but a memory you forgot you lost: the moment you were six years old, standing in your grandmother's garden as she pressed a small smooth stone into your palm and said, "Everything important is already inside you."

The stone is there now, warm and glowing. When you pick it up, the box folds back into itself, shrinks to the size of a walnut, and slips gently into your pocket, where it begins to hum a lullaby you haven't heard since you were born.

You smile in the dream, because you finally remember: you *are* the key. You always were.

And somewhere far above, the real you sleeping in bed turns over and laughs softly in your sleep.
