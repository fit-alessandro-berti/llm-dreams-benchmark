…as the competition progresses, you face various challenges and each one feels less like a test from the judges and more like a test from your own mind.

---

The first challenge is simple on paper:
**“Present yourself.”**

You stand on a stage under hot white lights. The audience is a dark blur, except every now and then you glimpse familiar faces—teachers, old coworkers, people who doubted you, people who believed in you. The host, all teeth and perfect posture, hands you a microphone that turns warm in your palm, like it’s alive.

A giant clock appears above you, its hands spinning wildly before freezing at **03:00**.

“Three minutes,” the host says. “Explain who you are and why you deserve your dream job.”

You open your mouth and nothing comes out at first—not because you don’t know, but because suddenly your voice is a tangled pile of every introduction you’ve ever given.

Hi, I’m—
So, I’m really passionate about—
I know I don’t have as much experience as—

The silence stretches. You can feel the audience’s attention slipping away like sand through fingers. Then, in the front row, someone leans forward. You can’t see their face clearly, but you know, *somehow*, that it’s a future version of you.

They tilt their head, like they’re curious. Not disappointed. Not judging. Just…waiting.

The knot loosens a bit.

You take a breath that seems to go all the way down to your toes.

“My name is…” you say, and this time your voice comes out strong, steady. You don’t list every achievement or every bullet point from your resume. Instead, you talk about the nights you stayed up too late because you were obsessed with a project. The times you failed and still went back for another try. You talk about the thing that has always come naturally to you—the thing your dream job is built around—even when you tried to convince yourself it wasn’t “practical.”

The clock freezes at **00:01** as you finish.

The audience doesn’t clap. Instead, they nod in unison, like a field of flowers catching the same breeze. The host smiles.

“Challenge one: complete.”

---

The second challenge is a maze.

You find yourself in a hallway lined with doors, each one marked with a different job title. Some are versions of your dream job, twisted slightly:

* “Senior Almost-There Specialist”
* “Assistant to Someone Doing What You Want to Do”
* “Freelance But Constantly Anxious About Money”

Other doors have titles that belong to lives you *could* have chosen:

* “Safe but Miserable”
* “Pays Well, Feels Wrong”
* “What Your Parents Wanted”

The rules appear in glowing letters on the ceiling:

> CHOOSE ONE DOOR.
> YOU CANNOT GO BACK.

You walk up and down the hallway, hand hovering over doorknobs that pulse faintly like heartbeats. Every time you get close to one, the wood goes transparent and you glimpse the life inside: early mornings, commutes, colleagues’ faces, inboxes, anniversaries, deadlines, birthdays, regret, satisfaction. It’s all mixed together.

Behind one door, you see yourself sitting at a desk that looks expensive and very far away from any window. Behind another, you see yourself in a tiny place, worn-out coffee mug, but your eyes are bright and alive.

You pace. You calculate. You worry.

Then you notice something you didn’t see before: at the very end of the hallway, almost hidden in shadow, there’s a door with no title at all. The wood is scratched and unfinished. The handle is simple and worn, like many hands have held it before.

You walk toward it, and as you do, the other doors’ titles blur and rearrange themselves into questions:

“Are you choosing from fear?”
“Are you choosing for applause?”
“Are you choosing what you actually want?”

You reach the blank door. When you touch the handle, words burn themselves into the wood:

> “THE JOB YOU GROW INTO”

Your chest tightens. It doesn’t promise perfection. It doesn’t even promise certainty. It just promises that it will stretch you, slowly, into the version of yourself you keep imagining when you’re staring at the ceiling at 3 a.m.

You step through.

---

The third challenge is teamwork.

You’re dropped into a huge open office that morphs like a video game level—walls sliding, desks rearranging, whiteboards filling and clearing themselves. Around you are the other contestants, each wearing name tags with titles like:

* “The One with More Experience”
* “The One Who Never Hesitates”
* “The One Who’s Already Successful”

The host’s voice echoes overhead.

“Deliver a project together. Only one of you gets the job.”

Everyone freezes. The air tightens. You can feel the sharp edges of competition: comparisons, jealousy, fear of being overshadowed.

A digital board lights up with tasks. More than one person could ever do alone. Deadlines pulse in red.

You know this trick. Make them compete so hard they forget collaboration.

One contestant immediately starts barking orders. Another quietly retreats to a corner, trying to complete everything themselves.

You hover in the middle, heart pounding. Part of you wants to prove you can carry the whole project. Another part wants to vanish so no one can see you fail.

Then you notice the project itself is…alive. When someone hoards tasks, the project shrinks and pixelates. When two contestants argue, it fractures into glitchy fragments. But when people talk, share ideas, and build on each other, the project grows vivid and detailed, textures sharpening, colors deepening.

You test it: you walk up to the quiet one.

“Hey,” you say, “you’re good at this part, right?” You point to a column of tasks that match their strengths. “Take these. I’ll cover this other piece that keeps getting dropped.”

Their shoulders relax. The project pulses, a soft glow.

You talk to the loud one.

“Your energy could really help us keep track of milestones,” you say. “Can you keep us on schedule?”

They blink, a little disarmed, then nod. “Yeah. Yeah, I can do that.”

Slowly, the group shifts. People stop trying to be the hero and start trying to make the whole thing work. You’re not doing everything. You’re connecting things. Translating. Making sure no one disappears.

By the time the deadline hits, the project stands in the middle of the room as a fully formed, shimmering construct—part blueprint, part sculpture, part story.

The host reappears, clapping once. The office dissolves around you, leaving just you, the others, and the floating project.

“Interesting,” the host says. “Only one of you gets the job. But look—”

They gesture, and the project splits into pieces, each piece flying to one contestant, reshaping itself to match their style, their skills.

“You were never actually competing,” the host says. “You were auditioning for the kind of teammate you’re going to be.”

They turn to you.

“And you—” There’s something like pride in their eyes. “You’re not just good at your dream job. You’re good at creating the conditions where everyone can do theirs.”

Something warm settles in your chest.

---

The final challenge is the strangest.

You’re led into a small, quiet room. No lights. No cameras. No audience. Just a chair, a desk, and a mirror on the wall.

The host’s voice is softer now.

“For the last challenge,” they say, “there are no tasks. No puzzles. No tricks.”

The mirror shimmers. Your reflection looks back at you—not the polished version you imagine in interviews, not the one who has everything figured out. Just you: tired in some places, hopeful in others.

“Your dream job,” the host says, “is on the other side of this mirror. The only question is: do you believe you’re allowed to have it?”

You almost laugh. It sounds ridiculous. Of course you’re allowed. People get their dream jobs all the time.

But as you look at your reflection, another voice whispers underneath:

*You’re not experienced enough. You’re too late. You’re too scattered. You’re not special. Other people deserve it more.*

The mirror flickers, showing images of all the times you hesitated, held back, told yourself “maybe next year,” “maybe when I’m better,” “maybe after the next course, the next certificate.”

Your reflection looks like it’s waiting for your verdict.

You sit with it for a moment. The fear. The doubt. The long list of reasons why not.

Then you remember the first challenge—your voice finally telling the truth.
You remember the second—choosing growth over guarantees.
You remember the third—seeing that your value isn’t just in what you do alone, but in what you help others do.

You swallow.

“I’m not perfect,” you tell the reflection. “I’m not done. I’m still learning. And I still deserve the chance to do work that lights me up.”

Your reflection nods once. The mirror warms, like sunlight through glass.

Cracks spiderweb across its surface—clean, bright lines. The glass doesn’t shatter outward. It peels away like a thin layer of ice, revealing not some fantastical office or golden stage, but something surprisingly ordinary:

You. At a desk, or in a studio, or on a set, or in a lab, or in a workshop—wherever your dream job lives. You’re in the middle of a day. Not the first day. Not the last. Just a day. You’re focused. You’re challenged. You’re…happy. Not giddy, just quietly fulfilled.

In the reflection, Future You looks up from their work and sees you standing there. They smile.

“Don’t worry,” they say. “You get here. Just keep going.”

The room fades. The host’s voice comes from everywhere and nowhere at once.

“Congratulations,” they say. “You’ve won.”

---

You wake up.

Your heart is pounding, but not from fear—from momentum. The details start to blur—the perfect teeth of the host, the titles on the doors, the exact lines of the maze. But the *feeling* stays.

You remember:

* The moment you spoke honestly about who you are.
* The decision to choose the door that would grow you, not just protect you.
* The way the project blossomed when you stopped trying to outshine and started trying to connect.
* The calm in your chest when you finally said, *I’m allowed to want this.*

The morning light slips across your room. It looks a little different now—not because the world has changed, but because you’ve seen a version of yourself who already has what you’re reaching for.

The competition is over. The dream fades.

But the real challenge—the slow, messy, beautiful work of building that life—
that part?

That starts now.
