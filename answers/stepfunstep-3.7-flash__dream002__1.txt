You are walking through a peaceful forest when suddenly the weather changes dramatically. You feel a storm approaching and the first thing you notice is the silence. The chickadees that were trilling from the hemlock boughs, the rustle of squirrels skittering through leaf litter, the soft gurgle of the creek you’ve been following for ten minutes—all of it cuts out at once, like someone yanked the cord on a wind-up music box.

The dappled gold of the afternoon sun filtering through the oak canopy bleeds out, replaced by a soft, milky indigo that feels like it’s humming against your skin. The air, which smelled like pine resin and wild blackberry a second ago, shifts to something sharper, sweeter: like burnt cinnamon and the metallic tang of the old copper coin you found in your grandma’s cookie jar when you were seven, the one you kept in your pocket for weeks.

A gust of wind first brushes the back of your neck, and it’s not cold. It’s warm, thick, like someone breathing against you. The first drop hits your cheek, and it doesn’t feel like water—it’s sticky, syrupy, and when you brush it away with your thumb, it glows faintly silver, the same color as the locket you lost when you were 12, the one with the tiny pressed violet inside.

The thunder doesn’t rumble. It thrums, a low, steady vibration that starts in the soles of your hiking boots and climbs up your shins, your ribs, the base of your skull. You glance up, and the oak leaves above you aren’t shaking. They’re unspooling, each frond turning into a strand of pale, glowing yarn that drifts down to coil around your wrists, your ankles, gentle as a cat curling into your lap.

You don’t feel scared. You feel… remembered. Like the storm isn’t here to wash the forest away, but to hand you something you forgot you lost.

When the first heavy roll of that thrumming thunder passes, you realize the path ahead is gone. In its place is a narrow, glowing trail of those silver raindrops, leading straight into the thickest part of the woods, where the hemlocks grow so tall their tops are lost in that milky indigo sky. And if you listen close, past the hum of the storm, you can hear your mother calling your name, the way she used to when you wandered too far as a kid, her voice warm and patient, like the wind against your neck.

You take a step forward, and the yarn coiled around your ankle tugs gently, just once, like it’s inviting you along.
