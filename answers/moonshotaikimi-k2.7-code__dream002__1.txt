The wind arrives before the rain—a low, rushing sound that moves through the treetops like a wave. Leaves detach and spiral around you, not falling, but *dancing*, suspended for a heartbeat longer than gravity should allow. You pull your coat tighter, though you’re not cold. In fact, your skin feels strangely warm, electric, as though the storm is charging you rather than the sky.

A path appears ahead that you’re certain wasn’t there before. It glows faintly, slick with sudden rain, winding between two ancient oaks whose branches have grown together like clasped hands. Lightning forks silently in the distance, and for a moment, the whole forest is etched in silver and shadow.

You know, with the calm certainty of dreams, that you have two choices: turn back toward the meadow where the sun still lingers, or follow the path into the heart of the storm. The thunder rolls—not overhead, but *underfoot*, as if the earth itself is calling your name.

You take a step forward. The rain begins to fall, but when it touches your skin, it doesn’t wet you. It hums.

And the forest opens its eyes.
