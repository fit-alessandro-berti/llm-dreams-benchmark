...and I accept, though I can't say what I've accepted, because none of us has spoken aloud. That's how it works here. The offer arrived the way weather arrives.

There are four of them, or six — the number keeps settling differently, like birds on a wire. One wears a coat the color of a subway map. One is carrying a paper bag of something warm; the smell is bread, then oranges, then the particular smell of my grandmother's hallway, and I decide not to ask.

"Where were you trying to get to?" the tallest one asks, and I open my mouth and realize I've forgotten. This should frighten me. Instead it's a relief, like setting down a suitcase I hadn't noticed I was carrying up a very long stair.

"That's all right," she says. "We'll walk until you remember. That's usually how."

So we walk. The city is enormous and indifferent above us — glass towers going up forever, taxis in a river of red light — but down at street level the crowd parts for us in a way it hadn't parted for me alone. I understand, in the wordless way of dreams, that this is the whole difference. The city hasn't changed at all. I have simply stopped being one.

We pass a laundromat where all the machines are spinning the same blue shirt. We pass a man selling keys off a velvet board; my companions greet him by name, and he holds up a small brass one and says, "Not yet." We cross a street that becomes a hallway that becomes a street again. Nobody comments. The one in the subway-map coat takes my elbow at the exact moment I would have stumbled.

At some point I notice I'm telling them things. Not the tidy version — the real one. The thing I did in the spring that I've never said out loud. The thing I'm afraid is permanently true about me. I hear my own voice going on and on, and I keep waiting for the moment they exchange a glance, the small flinch that means *ah, so that's what you are*. It doesn't come. The one with the paper bag just nods and hands me an orange, and it's already peeled.

"Everyone thinks the city is the hard part," he says. "The city's fine. The city is only rooms and directions."

Then we turn a corner and there's my street — my actual street, the one from whichever life I'm currently mistaking for the only one — and the light in my window is on, which is strange, because I live alone, and stranger still because I'm suddenly certain that *I* am the one who left it on, for myself, years ago, in case of exactly this.

I turn to thank them and of course they're already becoming other people: a woman checking her phone, two teenagers laughing, a man walking a dog that looks at me a beat too long. Dream-logic protects itself. But the taste of orange is still in my mouth, and the elbow they held is still warm, and I think: *I will not remember their faces, but I will remember being findable.*

I climb the stairs. The brass key is in my pocket, and it fits.
