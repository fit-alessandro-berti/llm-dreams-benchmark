You are at a large social gathering where you only know one person. As you try to find them, you encounter various groups of people and their conversations seem to stretch, warping into surreal echoes the moment you pass them.

A group of people standing by a mahogany bar are discussing the weather, but every time one of them gestures toward the window, the sky outside shifts—from noon-day gold to a bruised, violet twilight, and then to a starless charcoal. They don’t seem to notice; they are too busy peeling labels off their glasses with surgical precision, the sound of the paper tearing like dry leaves underfoot.

You pull your coat tighter, feeling like a misplaced puzzle piece in an intricate, grand design. You turn a corner into a room draped in heavy velvet curtains that smell faintly of ozone and old books. Here, the floor is an endless expanse of polished marble, and you find a group of people sitting on floor cushions, playing a game of chess with pieces that move on their own. They aren’t talking, but you hear a chorus of whispers coming from the ceiling, reciting the contents of every letter you’ve ever forgotten to mail.

You spot a familiar figure in the distance—the person you’re looking for. They are standing near a grand, spiraling staircase, silhouetted against a tall, arched window. As you begin to weave through the crowd, the partygoers suddenly synchronize their movements. They stop their chatter, turn in unison, and watch you with polite, blank eyes. The music—a slow, wandering cello piece—abruptly shifts into the rhythmic ticking of a giant, invisible clock.

You quicken your pace, but the floor beneath you begins to flow like a slow-moving river, pushing you backward even as you run forward. The person you know turns toward you, their silhouette sharpening. You reach out, your hand stretching toward their shoulder, but just as your fingers are inches away, they start to dissolve into a flurry of white moths.

The moths ascend toward the high ceiling, and as they catch the light, the room begins to flicker—like a film strip caught in a projector. The mahogany bar, the velvet curtains, and the polished floor begin to fade into a soft, steady hum. 

You realize, with a sudden, sinking clarity, that the party was never ending; it was simply waiting for you to wake up. And just as the ceiling dissolves into the morning light filtering through your own bedroom curtains, you hear one final, clear whisper from the person you were chasing: *"Don't worry, we'll finish this game tomorrow."*
