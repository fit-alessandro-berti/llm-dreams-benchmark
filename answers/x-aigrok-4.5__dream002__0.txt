**You are walking through a peaceful forest when suddenly the weather changes dramatically. You feel a storm approaching and the air grows thick and electric.**

The gentle breeze that had rustled the leaves turns sharp and cold, whipping through the canopy like an unseen hand. Dark clouds roll in with unnatural speed, blotting out the filtered sunlight until the woods plunge into a bruised twilight. Thunder rumbles not from above but from deep within the earth, vibrating up through the soles of your feet.

Rain begins in fat, warm drops that smell of ozone and crushed pine needles. Within moments it becomes a solid curtain. The path ahead dissolves into mud and rushing rivulets. Trees lean and creak; their branches thrash like arms raised in warning or welcome. Lightning strobes the forest white, freezing every leaf mid-motion, then leaving afterimages of silver veins burned across your vision.

You should turn back. Instead your feet keep moving forward, drawn by a low sound beneath the wind—a voice? A song?—coming from deeper in. The storm funnels you toward a clearing you somehow know was not there a moment ago. In its center stands a single massive oak, older and darker than the rest. Its bark is carved with spirals that spin slowly when the lightning flashes.

As you step into the open the rain stops mid-air, suspended droplets hanging like glass beads. The wind falls silent. Only the distant thunder continues, now matching the thudding of your own heartbeat. From among the roots of the great tree something uncoils: not quite animal, not quite shadow—long limbs of smoke and starlight that reach toward you with infinite patience.

In the dream-logic that suddenly feels absolute, you understand the choice is not whether to run but whether to take the offered hand or to wake. The suspended rain vibrates, waiting. The oak’s spirals tighten. The storm, paused at its crest, waits for you to decide what kind of weather is coming next.

And then—
(the dream blurs at the edges, softens, loosens its hold…)

You stir, still half in that charged forest air, the ghost of thunder fading as ordinary morning light filters through ordinary curtains. Outside your real window the day is calm and clear. But for a long moment you can still smell rain on pine, and the faint metallic taste of unfinished weather lingers on your tongue.
