I'm lying on a straw pallet in a low-ceilinged room. The walls are daub and timber, and morning light slants through a gap where a shutter doesn't quite close. My clothes are rough wool, itchy against my skin, and my feet are bare. Somewhere outside, a rooster is making its case to the world, and a woman is singing in a language I almost recognize—something old, with the shape of Latin but the music of something older still.

I sit up, and the dream-logic of it all settles on me like a cloak. I know, without knowing how, that I need to find the miller's daughter before sundown. I don't know why. I don't know who she is. But the knowledge is as certain as gravity.

I step outside into a village of maybe twenty houses, packed earth roads, chickens scattering at my feet. A man leading an ox nods at me as though he knows me, and I nod back, playing along. The hills beyond are impossibly green, and there are no wires, no planes overhead, no distant hum of engines—just wind in the wheat and the creak of a cart somewhere out of sight.

I start walking toward the sound of water, because mills need water, and because in dreams you follow the logic you're given. My feet know the path even though my mind doesn't. And as I walk, I begin to wonder if the person I was when I fell asleep is still there at all, or if I've always been here, and the other life was the dream...
