...the rules keep changing. The first challenge was a written exam, but when you looked down at the paper, the questions were written in a language you somehow understood yesterday but can't read today. The proctor — who has your third-grade teacher's face but your manager's voice — keeps saying, "Time's almost up," even though the clock on the wall is melting like warm taffy, its numbers dripping onto the floor.

You pass anyway. You're not sure how.

The second challenge takes place in an elevator that only goes sideways. The other contestants are all people you vaguely recognize — a barista, a cousin, someone you once sat next to on a plane. They're all suspiciously good at everything. One of them whispers, "The judges are watching how you walk," and suddenly you become aware of your legs in a way that makes walking nearly impossible.

By the third challenge, you realize you can't remember what the dream job actually *is*. You knew it at the start — it felt urgent, perfect, the thing you'd wanted your whole life — but now when you try to picture it, you only see a desk floating in fog. You ask another contestant, and she laughs and says, "Same as everyone's. Obviously." This clarifies nothing.

The final round is a presentation. You step onto the stage and realize your slides are blank — no, wait, they're not blank, they're photographs of your childhood bedroom. The audience leans forward expectantly. You open your mouth and begin to speak, and to your astonishment, the words come out confident and true, even though you have no idea what you're saying. The crowd is nodding. Someone is crying. The judges hold up scorecards that just say "YES."

You've won. The head judge approaches with an envelope containing your job offer. Your hands tremble as you open it.

Inside, the paper reads: *"You already had it. Wake up and go do it."*

The alarm sounds — except it's your phone, except it's a bird, except it's just morning light — and you surface from sleep with the strange, buoyant feeling that you were trying to tell yourself something, and almost heard it.
