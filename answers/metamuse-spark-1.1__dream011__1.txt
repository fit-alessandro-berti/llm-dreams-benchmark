...written in someone else's handwriting. Your handwriting.

You sit down to start the exam, you notice the questions are written in your handwriting. Messy, rushed, the way you write notes at 2 a.m.

The classroom is wrong. It's too big. The desks go back for what looks like miles, and everyone else is already writing furiously. The clock above the blackboard has no numbers, just the words *TOO LATE* where the 12 should be. The professor at the front is you, but older, wearing a suit you don't own yet, watching you with a tired, fond expression.

You look down at the exam paper. It says at the top: FINAL EXAM - Introduction to Becoming Who You Are [Required for Graduation]

There are only three questions.

**1. List every name you have ever been called when someone loved you. Spelling counts.**

You open your mouth but no sound comes out. The letters on the page squirm a little when you stare at them. A faint smell of your childhood house - laundry detergent and old library books - comes off the paper.

**2. You have 5 minutes to go back and tell 10-year-old you one thing that will actually help. What do you say? You cannot say "it will be okay."**

Your pen is incredibly heavy. It's made of glass and filled with dark blue ink that swirls like an ocean. Outside the window of the classroom, it's not the campus anymore. It's the ocean from inside the pen, all rolling and endless.

**3. Draw the shape of your future career. You may not use straight lines.**

You look around in a panic. The other students have turned in their exams. Their papers are blank, but they are all smiling, relieved. One of them, a girl you have never met but instantly recognize as your best friend, leans over and whispers, "You don't have to answer them. You're supposed to *remember* them."

And then you get it. This isn't a test you can pass or fail. It's an inventory.

You start to write, and as you do, the words don't stay on the page. The first name you write - the nickname your mom called you when you had a fever - lifts off the paper as a small warm light and floats up to the ceiling. The advice to your younger self dissolves into the glass pen and makes the ocean calm.

For question three, you stop trying to draw. You just press your palm, sweaty and shaking, onto the paper.

It leaves a perfect, imperfect print. No straight lines at all.

The older-you professor at the front smiles for real this time, stands up, and rings a small bell. Not to end the exam.

To wake you up.
