You wake up in a completely different era, with no modern technology around. As you explore your new surroundings, you realize you need to master the art of looking ordinary—fast.

The air is thick with woodsmoke, damp earth, livestock, and the sharp tang of roasting meat. Thatched roofs and timber-framed houses lean over muddy streets. A blacksmith’s hammer rings somewhere nearby; church bells toll in the distance. People in rough-spun tunics and cloaks move past you, their eyes lingering a second too long on your strange fabric, your soft shoes, the unnatural cleanliness of your skin.

You need food, water, and shelter before nightfall. More urgently, you need to learn enough of the local tongue and customs to stop being the most interesting thing in the village. One wrong word, one mention of machines or electricity or the future, and the stares will harden into accusations of witchcraft, heresy, or espionage. History has never been kind to people who appear out of nowhere knowing too much.

Yet as the sun sinks and the stars emerge sharper and denser than any city sky you have ever seen, a strange exhilaration cuts through the fear. Here, in a world without screens or engines, your knowledge of hygiene, crop rotation, basic medicine, and leverage is almost magical—if you can stay alive long enough to use it carefully. The dream deepens. A group of riders on horseback clatters into the square. Someone in a dark cloak watches you from the shadows and gives the smallest nod, as if they already know you do not belong.

You take a breath of woodsmoke and step forward into the only life you have.
