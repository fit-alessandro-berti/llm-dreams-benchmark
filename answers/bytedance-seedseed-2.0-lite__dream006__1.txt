A familiar dream panic spikes in my chest—the one where your throat locks up and you can’t remember your own name, let alone the speech you spent weeks writing—and when I force my mouth open to stammer an apology, a flood of warm golden fireflies spills out instead of words, bobbing over the first few rows of the crowd.

That’s when I notice the audience isn’t the room full of strangers I’d feared. Half the faces are people I haven’t thought of in years: my third grade art teacher who hung my lopsided clay pot above her desk, the teen lifeguard who taught me to do a backflip off the community pool diving board, my childhood golden retriever who trots down an aisle a few rows back, tail thumping so hard the plastic seats rattle. Some people wave sparklers that don’t burn, like they’re at a small-town Fourth of July show; a little girl at the front has a goldfish that swims in midair above her pigtails, looping through the stream of fireflies.

The fireflies aren’t just floating, I realize. They’re stitching words into the black curtain behind me, darting and looping to spell out all the things I never learned how to say out loud: I’m sorry I didn’t call. I’m proud of you for trying even when it was hard. I still miss the treehouse we built. You can rest now. The crowd starts to clap, and their claps are soft as raindrops bouncing off the stage, each one sprouting a white daisy under my feet before they drift off to root in the aisles.

I step away from the mic, ready to go hug my grandma who’s waving from the back row, when the stage under my shoes softens into warm beach sand. The curtain melts into the ocean, the fireflies turning into seagulls that cry as they circle overhead, and I can taste salt on my tongue as the tide licks at my bare ankles—this dream is shifting, leading me somewhere new, and for the first time all night, I don’t feel scared to follow.
