The wheel bucks like a wild thing in my hands, slick with sea-spray and trembling with the heartbeat of the ocean. Every crest is a falling ceiling; every trough, a black lung gasping for air. When I look up, the sky is tearing itself into ribbons—gray and bruised and phosphorescent.

My crew’s faces are lit by livid flashes, each one a question without words: *Now?*  
I meet their eyes and give them only verbs—no speeches above the roar.

“Drop the lower yards!”  
Lines scream through scorings as canvas whips into traitorous wings.  
“Bail the starboard hold—keep her light where she thinks she’s drowning!”  
Barrels and buckets become a single lung.  

I taste copper and lightning. Somewhere below, the ship is remembering she is wood and iron and faith. I let the helm spin free for one terrifying heartbeat, feel her shoulder into the wind, then take her back with a hard, loving twist—*not against the sea, but with its teeth*.

We are not escaping the storm; we are being handed from one pocket of fury to another like secrets. A rogue crest, black as a closing door, rises to starboard.  

“Hold fast!”  

It hits us like a continent falling. The world goes horizontal, green and roaring, and for a breath that stretches into years we are weightless pilgrims skimming the lid of chaos. When we slam back down, the hull groans a shiver of timbers that hum in my own chest. Somewhere, a spar cracks like a bone. The ship does not apologize; she simply bends, teaches me how.  

And in that teaching, the storm begins to rearrange itself—not defeated, but convinced that we are not prey.  

The clouds slowly unclench. Rain softens to a hush. In the settling quiet, my crew’s shoulders drop; knives of tension slip from their jaws. They look at me again, and this time their eyes are clear water.  

I let the wheel find its gentler rhythm, steering us not toward a harbor we cannot see, but into the belief that we can carry the dark without being swallowed by it.  

The sea settles into its ancient habit of breathing, and for now, that is enough.
