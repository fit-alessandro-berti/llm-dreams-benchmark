…find a way to survive without the one thing you’re used to taking for granted: certainty.

You step outside and the air feels sharper—cold with woodsmoke and something metallic in the wind. The sky is the same sky, but the world under it isn’t. Horses replace cars. Firelight replaces streetlamps. People move like they’ve always moved this way, quick and practical, as if every minute has a weight.

You turn your pockets inside out. Your keys are useless. Your phone is gone. Your wallet, your watch—gone. The only thing you still have is what you dreamed with: a vague confidence that you can figure things out, and a memory full of dates and slogans that are suddenly worthless.

Your stomach tightens. Survival isn’t a plan; it’s a series of problems you solve faster than they can solve you.

First problem: **food and water.**  
You follow the smell of bread to a row of stalls in a market square. There’s grain in sacks, apples with bruised skins, thick stew ladled into bowls. You reach for a coin out of habit—then stop, realizing you have none. The vendor’s eyes narrow as you hover like a lost animal.

So you do the only thing you can do without language: you offer work.

You wait until someone drops a sack, and you lift it before they can. The sack is heavier than it looks. Your muscles protest. A man grunts—approval or impatience, you can’t tell—and points you toward a cart. The work is simple: push, lift, stack. Your hands learn what your mind can’t translate.

In exchange, you’re given something rough but real: a heel of bread, a cup of water from a clay jug. It tastes like survival. It tastes like being noticed.

Second problem: **shelter.**  
Night comes early, swallowed by the buildings. The market closes with a clatter of shutters. You ask—carefully, with gestures—for somewhere to sleep. You’re met with skepticism until you show your willingness to help again. A woman whose eyes look tired but kind watches you wrestle a bundle of linens. She nods once, almost reluctantly, and gestures you toward a back room.

“You can stay till dawn,” she seems to say without saying.

Third problem: **information.**  
In the dim light, you listen. People talk in familiar shapes—prices, weather, rumors—but the words are unfamiliar, and the cadence is different. You catch fragments: names of places, dates that aren’t yours, threats that are immediate rather than abstract. War is mentioned the way weather is mentioned: always present, never fully discussed.

You learn quickly that not knowing things makes you dangerous. Not because people are cruel—though some are—but because confusion invites trouble. You need to know the rules of this era, the invisible laws everyone else carries in their bones.

So you do something desperate and ingenious: you trade what you have. Not money. Not technology. **Time and attention.**

You sit near the doorway where travelers pass and you watch. You study faces, clothing, the way people step aside. You learn which words make people stop and which make them flinch. You memorize the patterns of authority: who speaks first, who decides, who corrects.

And slowly, you begin to piece together a map—not on paper, but in your head.

You realize there’s a chance to avoid being a target: become useful to someone powerful enough to protect you, but not so visible that you become a spectacle.

You decide to find employment.

But where?

You follow the sound of hammers and the smell of hot metal. A blacksmith’s shop sits like a heartbeat in the afternoon. The man there looks at you the way a craftsman looks at raw material—measuring if you can be shaped. You can’t explain your origin story, so you don’t try. You ask to help.

He gives you tasks that don’t require trust beyond your hands: carrying coal, pumping the bellows, sweeping away slag. You work until your arms feel like fire. He grunts again and again, not unkindly.

Then, one day, while you’re moving a heavy bar of iron, you overhear a conversation about **a courier who disappeared** and a **message that must reach somewhere before the next moon**.

It’s a simple opening. You have no documents. No identity. But you have something else: you know—vaguely—what “next moon” means in real time, and you can hustle.

You speak carefully, using gestures and whatever scraps of language you’ve picked up. The blacksmith’s assistant translates just enough to let the job make sense. The deal is barely a deal: you run errands, you deliver the message, you are paid in food and a place to sleep.

It’s not freedom. But it’s motion.

As you sprint through streets that don’t have names you recognize, the message burns in your satchel like a secret. You don’t know the exact content. You don’t need to. You know only that whoever receives it expects it soon.

The countryside changes around you—dirt roads, hedgerows, distant towers. You pass villages where people stare a bit too long, not because they’re cruel, but because your clothes don’t match their calendar.

By the time you reach the next town, your lungs feel borrowed. You find the official building by the way people arrange themselves outside it—some in line, some in fear, all in awareness.

You hand over the satchel.

For a moment, nothing happens. No one thanks you. No one smiles. The official reads the message and grows still.

Then the official looks at you—really looks—and you feel something in the air change, like a door closing somewhere you didn’t notice.

“Who sent you?” they ask.

And that’s when you realize the dream has been quietly steering you toward the real reason you’re here:

Your existence isn’t just unusual. It’s **useful** in the way a missing piece is useful to a lock.

Your answer can’t be made of gestures anymore. It has to be made of truth, or it has to be made of the kind of lie that survives questioning.

You take a breath. You consider what you know about survival.

And you choose.

You will complete the message—but you will also try to find out who wrote it… and why it reached you at all.

Before you can decide, guards step forward—not hostile, not friendly, just inevitable. They ask your name in a language you don’t fully know, and the dream lingers at the edge of understanding, waiting for you to respond.

So you have to speak.

You have to improvise an identity.

Or the era will invent one for you.
