You are participating in a competition where the grand prize is your dream job. As the competition progresses, you face various challenges and meet strange, brilliant competitors who seem to know pieces of your past.

The first challenge takes place in a library with no ceiling. Books drift above you like birds, opening and closing their pages in the air. A judge with silver glasses tells everyone to find “the one idea you abandoned.” Around you, people scramble through shelves, ladders, and glowing staircases. You wander until a small, dusty notebook falls at your feet. Inside are drawings you made as a child: impossible cities, inventions that ran on moonlight, stories about people who could talk to storms.

When you touch the pages, the room dissolves.

You are suddenly standing on a stage in front of thousands of faceless spectators. The second challenge is to present your greatest failure. One by one, contestants step forward with polished speeches. When it is your turn, your voice catches. Instead of explaining, you admit that you are afraid you are not as capable as everyone thinks. The crowd goes silent. Then, somewhere in the darkness, a single person begins to clap. Others join. The stage lights warm, and the judge smiles as if this was the answer they wanted all along.

The final challenge begins at dawn.

You are led to a glass elevator that rises through clouds. At the top is an office overlooking every place you have ever lived. Your name is already on the door. Inside, the desk is empty except for a contract and a pen. The job title reads: **Keeper of What Comes Next**.

Before you can sign, the other finalists appear behind you. They are not angry. They look relieved. You realize each of them was competing for a different dream, yet the room has shaped itself for all of you. One sees a laboratory. Another sees a concert hall. Another sees a quiet garden.

The silver-glasses judge asks, “Do you accept?”

You look at the contract again. The fine print is changing, rewriting itself with every heartbeat. It says the dream job is not a place you arrive at, but a promise you keep making: to stay curious, to be brave enough to begin again, and to build something only you could build.

You sign your name.

The office door opens, not into a hallway, but into a bright unfinished world. Tools float in the air. Roads sketch themselves across the sky. Somewhere, your childhood notebook opens to a blank page.

You step forward.

And just before you wake, you hear the judge whisper:

“Now the real competition begins.”
