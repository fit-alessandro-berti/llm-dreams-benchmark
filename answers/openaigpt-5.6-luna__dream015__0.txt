Despite the hardships, you decide to plant a garden.

You find a cracked tin can, fill it with the last handful of soil you can gather, and bury three seeds you’ve been carrying for years. Every morning, you walk beyond the ruins to collect a cup of water. Every evening, you shield the can from the ash-filled wind.

For weeks, nothing happens.

Then, one dawn, a green shoot breaks through the soil.

You kneel beside it, hardly daring to breathe. Around you, the dead city is silent—its towers hollow, its streets swallowed by weeds, its old machines rusting beneath the red sky. But beneath your hands, something is alive.

As the plant grows, so does the garden. Strangers begin appearing at the edge of the settlement, drawn by the sight of green leaves. Some bring seeds. Others bring clean water, tools, or stories of places where the earth is healing.

One night, while you stand beneath a sky crowded with unfamiliar stars, you realize the world has not ended.

It has only been waiting for someone to begin again.
