When I open my mouth, instead of speech, a thin ribbon of silver light emerges and floats gently upward, twisting into a shape that hangs in the air above the audience. The crowd’s expectation melts into soft, collective wonder.

I realize I don’t need a prepared speech. The light is speaking for me—each loop and curl contains a memory we all share, and I watch as the faces below soften with recognition. The stern man in the third row sees his childhood dog bounding through a field of tall grass. A woman near the back tastes her grandmother’s cinnamon, clear as a December morning. The whole auditorium breathes together, a single, slow inhale.

Then the stage beneath my feet becomes the deck of a small boat, and the walls fold away into an endless, moonlit sea. The chairs bob gently on the waves, each audience member now a fellow traveler in a tiny vessel of their own, holding a small candle. The ribbon of light weaves between us all, a silver thread connecting every flame. No one speaks, but the stillness says everything: *We are here. We are not alone. We remember.*

Just as I understand this, the dream begins its gentle unraveling. The sea grows transparent, the candles dim to stars, and I feel the familiar pull of waking. But the silver thread stays behind my eyes for just a moment longer, a quiet promise that the connection remains, even when the dreaming stops.
