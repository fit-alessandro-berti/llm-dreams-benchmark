When you lift a hand to touch its surface, you half-expect your fingers to pass right through, but it gives like warm, sun-softened clay, and suddenly tiny, flickering things begin to bloom unbidden, no brush needed.

First the chipped sky-blue crayon you lost under your elementary school slide when you were seven, the one you sobbed over for an hour because you’d been saving it to draw a picture of your grandma. Then the laugh of your first best friend, who moved across the country in 5th grade, that you haven’t heard in nearly 20 years, rendered as a string of glowing tangerine loops that skip and dance across the canvas’s surface. All the small, buried things you’d forgotten you’d lost spill out next: the half-written poem you never sent to the girl you loved in high school, the road trip you and your college roommate planned but never took when graduation pulled you apart, the tiny tabby kitten you almost adopted but couldn’t afford the vet bills for, the quiet “I’m sorry” you never got to say to your dad before he died. They bloom as soft, shifting shapes, the canvas stretching wider and wider to hold every last one, until its edges swallow the entire surreal landscape around you.

You can’t tell where the canvas ends and you begin, and in that lurch of dream-disorientation, you realize you were the blank canvas all along. The world’s ever-morphing colors and shapes were never just landscape—they were all the parts of you you’d tucked away, too scared to let yourself carry them, too busy growing up to stop and name them. Just as the thought settles soft in your chest, a tiny pair of scissors forged from stardust floats down to nick your palm. You bleed a color no one has ever seen before: something that tastes like summer peaches and smells like old book pages, bright as lemon and warm as red wine, that spreads out from your hand to paint the entire world, turning it from something fleeting and shifting into something solid, yours, yours to keep adding to as long as you dream.
