 look down at the brief, but the paper is made of static, hissing between your fingers. The deadline—**Friday at 9 AM**—glows red, then crawls off the page and scuttles under your desk like a wounded insect. Your boss doesn't blink; her eyes are two wall clocks ticking backward. "I'll need the full synthesis," she says, but her voice comes from the ceiling fan, which is now spinning in a language you almost understand.

You open your laptop, but the keys have turned to teeth, chattering out a rhythm too fast to type. The screen displays a spreadsheet where every cell is a tiny room, and inside each room, a smaller version of you is also typing, also panicking, also failing. The deadline grows back from under the desk—a thorny vine that wraps around your ankle, pulling you through carpet that has liquefied into quicksand made of unread emails.

Your boss follows, gliding without moving her legs. She's holding a calendar that burns but never turns to ash. "Prioritize," she whispers, except now she has the face of your third-grade teacher, your childhood dentist, a pigeon you saw once on a bridge. You try to speak, to explain that the project has turned into a whale and is currently beached across the breakroom table, but your tongue is a rolled-up Gantt chart.

You push through the conference room doors, hoping to find more time, but the room is infinite, filled with thousands of photocopiers all spitting out pages that read **ERROR** in your mother's handwriting. The deadline is now a door at the end of a corridor that gets longer with every step. You run. Your shoes are made of yesterday.

Just as you reach for the handle, the doorknob becomes your phone, buzzing. The whale sighs. The spreadsheets sing. And somewhere between the ringing and the falling, you realize the project was never due at all—it was only the sound of your own heart, finally learning to beat backwards into sleep.
