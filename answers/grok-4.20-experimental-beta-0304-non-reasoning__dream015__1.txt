...decide to build something beautiful anyway.

You start small. With a rusted crowbar and a piece of rebar you pry from the skeleton of an old overpass, you begin clearing a patch of soil behind a half-collapsed supermarket. The ground is poisoned with old chemicals and ash, but you remember something your grandmother once told you: life is stubborn. So you dig, mix in charcoal from old fires, and plant the three precious packets of seeds you found in the glovebox of an abandoned mail truck—tomatoes, sunflowers, and something labeled only as “mystery greens.”

At night you sleep in shifts on the roof of a armored school bus you’ve welded together with scrap. You call it The Ark. Your only companions are a one-eyed German Shepherd named Lucky and a quiet young woman named Mara who speaks mostly in gestures and writes poetry on the walls with charcoal. She draws constellations that no longer exist in the smog-choked sky.

Weeks blur. The first green shoots appear like a miracle. One morning you wake to find that the sunflowers have opened—bright, defiant yellow faces staring at a sun that still rises even if the world forgot how to be gentle. You laugh until you cry. Lucky tilts his head like you’ve finally lost it.

Then the raiders come.

They roll in on spiked motorcycles and a jury-rigged tank made from a cement truck. You have maybe six bullets and a spear you made from a stop sign. Mara stands beside you on the roof holding a Molotov cocktail she made from fermented potato peels and old perfume. Her hands are shaking, but her eyes are steady.

You look at the sunflowers. You look at the tiny tomatoes just beginning to blush red. You look at Mara and Lucky and the fragile green life you coaxed out of the corpse of the old world.

And you decide, again, right there on the edge of violence:

You’re not going to let this die.

You step to the edge of the roof, raise your hands, and shout across the broken parking lot:

“We have food. Real food. Enough to share if you’re willing to work instead of take. First one who puts their weapon down gets the first tomato of the season.”

The raiders laugh at first. Then one of them—a tall woman with half her face covered in burn scars—takes off her helmet. She stares at the sunflowers like she’s seeing a ghost.

She lowers her rifle.

The dream shifts, softens at the edges like all dreams do. You feel the weight of the world lift just a little.

You wake up smiling, with dirt still under your fingernails and the faint scent of sun-warmed tomatoes in the air.
