You feel a storm approaching and the air suddenly tastes like ozone and old memories. The birds do not fly away; they simply turn into gray smoke and dissolve into the canopy. 

The sky cracks open, not with the roar of thunder, but with a silence so loud it vibrates in your teeth. Rain begins to fall, but it does not wet the ground—it floats, hovering inches above the moss like suspended stars. The colors of the forest bleed outward: the green of the leaves turns to sapphire, the brown of the soil to gold, and the white light of the sun to a soft, glowing violet.

You try to seek shelter under the gnarled oak, but as you step closer, the trunk turns into flowing water, rippling without a sound. Panic rises in your chest, a sharp, hot spike, but it is swallowed instantly by a feeling of profound calm. You realize the storm isn't here to drown you; it is here to wash something away.

The wind picks up, tugging gently at your clothes, and you feel your feet lift off the ground. You are not falling; you are drifting. The trees bend toward you, their branches forming a cathedral around your silhouette. Lightning flashes, but instead of light, it releases ribbons of color that wrap around you like a blanket.

You open your mouth to speak, but the words come out as butterflies. They flutter up toward the violet sky, one by one, until you have nothing left to say. The world expands and contracts with your breath.

And then, the storm pauses. A single drop of the floating rain lands on your hand. It is heavy, warm, and real. It triggers a sudden jolt in your mind—a connection. The violet sky shatters like a mirror, and you are suddenly falling backward, away from the trees, away from the colors, down through a tunnel of rushing noise and rushing light.

Your eyes snap open. The ceiling of your bedroom is stark white, the morning light is just a pale gray, and the only sound is the rhythmic *tap-tap-tap* of actual rain against the windowpane. You sit up, breathless, and look at your hand, wondering if you can still feel the weight of the rain that never actually hit you.
