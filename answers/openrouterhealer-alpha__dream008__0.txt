The air in the office smells like ozone and stale coffee. Your monitor flickers to life not with a familiar desktop, but with a single, pulsing line of text on a black screen:

**PROJECT: CHRONOSYNCLINE**
**DEADLINE: YESTERDAY**
**STATUS: OVERDUE**

Your boss, whose face you can never quite remember, stands by your cubicle. Their silhouette is sharp, but their features swim like oil on water. “We need the initial framework by the time the coffee finishes brewing,” they say, gesturing to a pot on the counter that is simultaneously full and empty, steam rising in slow-motion tendrils.

You turn back to your screen. The keyboard keys begin to levitate, hovering an inch above the desk. When you reach for them, your fingers phase through the letters. You try to type, but the keystrokes register in the air as glowing, golden symbols that drift upward and dissolve. The project brief on the screen starts to rewrite itself, sentences turning into flocking birds of text that flutter around the monitor.

A low hum fills the room. The office walls seem to breathe, expanding and contracting gently. You look down at your hands; they are both solid and translucent. You can see the glowing circuitry of the desk through your palms.

You take a deep breath, and instead of air, you inhale the scent of rain on hot asphalt. You focus. You remember a tool—a function, a piece of code, a concept—and you reach out, not to the keyboard, but to one of the floating, glowing letters. You catch it. It feels warm, like a small, smooth stone.

You begin to assemble the letters in the air, not typing, but *weaving*. You pull a ‘C’, then an ‘h’, from the shimmering cloud. They snap into place with a soft chime, forming the start of a header. The project isn’t built on a machine. It’s built in the space between thought and light. The deadline isn’t time; it’s the moment the coffee pot decides to boil over.

As you weave the first line of code into a shimmering lattice in the air, the oppressive weight of the deadline begins to change. It’s no longer a pressure, but a gravity—a gentle, constant pull that guides the glowing symbols into elegant, orbiting structures around you.

The boss’s silhouette nods once, then slowly dissolves into a flock of dark, silent moths that drift up toward the fluorescent lights, which now look like distant, cold stars.

You are left alone, suspended in the hum, weaving light into logic. The dream shifts. The project is no longer a burden, but a landscape you are mapping with your fingertips, and you realize you have all the time in the world. The coffee is still brewing. The deadline is a story you tell yourself, and you’ve just reached the interesting part.
