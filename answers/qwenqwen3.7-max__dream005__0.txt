You ask a man in a sharp tuxedo if he’s seen your friend. He pauses, tilts his head, and hands you a heavy, brass key. "They went to check on the tide," he says, though his mouth doesn't move. The words simply arrive in your mind.

You take the key and step away. The living room, which was just a normal size, suddenly stretches into a vast, dimly lit gallery with impossibly high ceilings. The wallpaper is breathing, expanding and contracting in a slow, rhythmic pulse. 

You encounter a second group gathered around a long mahogany banquet table. They aren't eating; instead, they are carefully folding shadows. They peel the shadows of vases and chairs right off the wall, crease them with their bare hands, and stack them neatly on silver platters. A woman holding a folded shadow of a bird points toward a door at the far end of the hall that you are certain wasn't there a moment ago.

You walk toward it. The sounds of the party fade into a low, muffled humming, like a cello being played underwater. You insert the brass key into the doorknob. It turns with a satisfying, heavy *click*.

You push the door open and step into a small, quiet room that looks exactly like your childhood bedroom, bathed in the golden light of late afternoon. Your friend is sitting on the edge of the bed. Relief washes over you. You say their name, and they turn around. 

But it isn't their face. Where their face should be, there is a small, perfectly round mirror. 

You step closer and look into the mirror. You don't see your own reflection. Instead, you see a bird's-eye view of the party you just left. You watch a tiny version of yourself wandering through the shifting, surreal crowds. The tiny you looks up, makes direct eye contact with the real you through the mirror, and smiles.

Suddenly, the floor beneath you turns to warm, shallow water. You don't fall; you just begin to float upward, drifting slowly toward the ceiling. The golden light fades into a deep, comforting indigo. The rustling leaves and the underwater cello blend into the sound of your own steady breathing, and as your head passes through the plaster and wood of the ceiling, you finally wake up.
