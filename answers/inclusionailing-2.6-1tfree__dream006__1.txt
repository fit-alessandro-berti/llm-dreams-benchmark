…and you begin to breathe, but the air tastes like honey and static.  

Your shoes don’t click on the wooden floor; they leave soft footprints of warm amber light that evaporate after a few seconds, as if the stage is digesting your steps.  

The crowd is silent, yet you hear them—not as voices, but as a slow, synchronized tide of color behind their eyes: indigo for curiosity, gold for patience, violet for questions they haven’t formed yet.  

You open your mouth, and instead of words, a flock of origami birds made of sheet music pours out, circling once, twice, then settling gently on the heads of the people in the front rows. They don’t move. They let the birds perch there, their hands opening like petals to catch stray notes that fall like confetti.  

The microphone in front of you isn’t metal; it’s a pale, warm seed, and when you lean in, it cracks open like an egg and hums a single note that feels like remembering a dream you forgot you had.  

Suddenly the ceiling dissolves. You see a river flowing upward, carrying lanterns with faces you almost recognize. The audience doesn’t look up in alarm—they smile, as if they expected the sky to behave this way all along.  

You realize you haven’t started your speech yet, but somehow, you’ve already told them everything.  

And then you wake up—not in your bed, but in the applause, your hands still warm from the shape of words you never had to say.
