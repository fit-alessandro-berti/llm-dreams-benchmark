...the questions are in a language I almost know.

Not a foreign language — something worse. English, but arranged wrong, the way a face is wrong in a photograph taken mid-blink. *Question 1: Describe the smell of the number seven. Show your work.*

I look up. The exam hall is the gymnasium of a school I attended when I was nine, except the ceiling has been replaced with the inside of a cathedral, and the ceiling is also, somehow, raining very slowly. Not water. Chalk dust. It settles on everyone's shoulders like the beginning of an argument.

The proctor is my old piano teacher. She has been dead for years, which in the dream is not remarkable but is *slightly rude of her*. She walks the aisles with her hands behind her back, and every time she passes my desk she says, without looking at me, "You had all summer."

I did have all summer. I remember the summer. I remember specifically deciding not to study, and I remember the reason being *excellent*, airtight, a reason that would exonerate me completely if I could just retrieve it. I reach for it and get instead the taste of a swimming pool.

I look down again. The page has more questions now. It keeps having more questions, the way a hallway keeps having more hallway.

*Question 2: You were told. Explain when.*
*Question 3: Diagram the part of your life you have been avoiding. Label the exits.*
*Question 4: (worth 90% of your final grade) Please continue.*

Around me, everyone is writing furiously. The sound of their pens is enormous, an insect sound, a locust field. I glance at the boy beside me and see he is filling page after page with a single word repeated in beautiful cursive, and the word is his own name, and he is weeping with confidence.

I try to write. My pen produces no ink. I press harder and it produces a thin thread of music instead — three notes, descending, my piano teacher's least favorite phrasing. She stops walking. The gymnasium goes quiet. Everyone turns.

"That," she says, "is not what the question asked."

And here is the thing about the dream: I know she's right. I know it completely. I feel the knowing settle in my chest with the weight of a verdict, and I feel the specific grief of understanding that the exam was never about the answers, that the answers were a courtesy, a formality, that the real question was asked years ago in a room I can't remember and I said *sure, later.*

I stand up. I am going to explain myself. I am going to say: I have been working the whole time, in ways that don't photograph well; I have been sitting this exam every day without recognizing it as an exam; the paper is blank because the answers went somewhere else, into the living, and surely — surely — there's a place on the form for that.

But when I open my mouth, chalk dust comes out.

And the desk is a bed. And the questions are the shadows of the blinds on the wall. And the terrible important thing I was supposed to know is dissolving even as I grab for it, going the way sugar goes in water, leaving only a sweetness and the certainty that it mattered.

The clock says 4:12.

The exam, somewhere, is still being administered.
