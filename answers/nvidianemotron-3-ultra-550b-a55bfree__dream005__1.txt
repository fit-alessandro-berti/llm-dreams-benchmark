...you encounter various groups of people and **the air between them hums with a frequency you can almost taste—metallic, like a battery on the tongue.**

A circle of women in dresses made of folded maps stands near a fountain that flows upwards. They are arguing about the capital of a country that hasn't been founded yet. When you ask for directions, the tallest one peels a layer of latitude from her sleeve and hands it to you. It dissolves into confetti the moment it touches your palm.

You drift past a buffet table where the food is arranged by memory, not flavor. There is a gelatin mold of your seventh birthday party (tasting of chlorine and cake), a terrine of the first time you drove a car alone at night (salty, vibrating), and a soup made entirely of the silence between you and the person you came here to find. You don’t eat. You never eat in these places.

A man in a suit woven from static electricity bumps your shoulder. He doesn't apologize; he just flickers. His face is a television screen tuned to a channel that only plays the backs of people’s heads as they walk away. He tries to hand you a business card, but the text rewrites itself every time you blink: *Architect of Almost*, *Curator of Near Misses*, *Minister of the Threshold*.

You push through a hedge maze that has grown indoors, sprouting from the Persian rugs. The hedges are trimmed into the shapes of unresolved arguments. You hear your own voice echoing from the center, saying things you *will* say tomorrow, things you *should* have said in 2012.

Finally, you see them.

They are standing on a balcony that overlooks a ballroom with no floor—just a drop into a starfield. They are wearing your favorite shirt, the one you lost in a laundromat three moves ago. They are laughing at a joke told by a figure made of smoke and porch-light glow.

You scream their name, but the sound comes out as a flock of starlings, bursting from your mouth, wheeling once around the chandeliers, and vanishing into the vents.

They turn. Their face is smooth, featureless as an egg.

Then, the dream does the only cruel thing it knows how to do: **it lets you recognize them anyway.**

"You're late," they say, their voice sounding like the house settling at 3:00 AM. "The gravity switched off ten minutes ago. We’re all just waiting to see who floats up first."

You look down at your feet. Your shoes are untied. The laces are snakes, sleeping.

You wake up with your hand outstretched, grasping the sheet, the taste of map-confetti and chlorine fading behind your eyes, the joke the smoke-figure told lingering like a half-remembered promise: *“...and so the duck says to the bartender, 'Put it on my bill,' but the bill was already paid by the version of you that got away.”*
