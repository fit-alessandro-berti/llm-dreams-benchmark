…not questions at all.

Each page has a photograph.

The first photo is of your childhood bedroom. The walls are the wrong color, but the desk is exactly where you remember it. On the desk sits a small object you’re supposed to recognize. Beneath the photo the “question” simply says: *Explain why you left it there.*

You turn the page.

The second photo is of a conversation you once had—impossible, but unmistakable. You see yourself mid‑sentence, the other person frozen just before replying. Underneath: *What should you have said instead?*

Your chest tightens. Other students are scribbling furiously, but no one looks confused.

Page three shows a path you never took: a job offer you declined, a message you never sent, a train you almost boarded. The prompt reads: *Calculate the distance between who you are and who you might have been.*

Your pencil hovers.

You look up to ask the proctor what kind of exam this is, but the proctor is you—older, patient, already knowing the grade.

When you look back down, the final page has only one line:

*For full credit, answer honestly.*

But the space for the answer is a mirror.
