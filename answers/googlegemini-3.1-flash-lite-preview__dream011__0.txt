*Question 1: Describe the exact shade of the sky on the day you felt most untethered.*

I reach for my pen, but my hand is translucent, fading into the mahogany of the desk. I look around the lecture hall, but the rows of desks have dissolved into a vast, silent forest. My classmates are gone, replaced by tall, spindly trees whose leaves whisper the answers I should have studied. 

The weight of the career I thought I wanted—the suits, the buildings, the certainty—suddenly feels like a heavy coat I’m no longer required to wear. I glance down at the paper again. The glowing ink is now forming a map of a city I’ve never visited, yet one I seem to know by heart. 

*Question 2: What would you do if you were never afraid of being wrong?*

The proctor—a towering figure draped in shadows at the front of the room—steps closer. I can’t see their face, only the faint, rhythmic ticking of a clock that seems to be coming from inside my own chest. 

I realize then that this isn't a test I can pass by memorization. There is no passing or failing here, only the necessity of being honest. I pick up the pen, which now feels like a lightning rod, and instead of writing on the paper, I press it against the air. A glowing line appears, hovering in the space before me. 

I start to write, not answers, but the truth. With every word, the walls of the lecture hall peel away like old wallpaper, revealing a horizon that is endless and terrifyingly bright. The dream isn't about the exam anymore; it’s about the fact that I’ve finally stopped asking for permission to choose my own path. 

I sign my name, the ink burning gold, and the floor beneath me simply stops existing. I begin to fall upward, into the light, and for the first time, I am not afraid of the landing.
