The community garden we spent three years building together stretches out around us: tomato vines straggling half-picked, a rusted rain barrel leaking onto the gravel path, and the last of our donated lumber stack half-buried in pine mulch. You’d been pushing to use that lumber for a raised wheelchair-accessible planting bed for the neighborhood’s two senior gardeners who can no longer bend over to tend their rows, but your friend swears you’re ignoring the regulars who’ve brought us homemade cookies, donated power tools, and showed up every single Saturday to help dig beds—they need to fix the splintered picnic tables first, so the folks who come to sit and swap stories have a safe, stable place to rest.

Your throat aches raw from yelling, and you’re seconds away from spitting a hurtful line about how they always prioritize “fun” over real, urgent good, when you spot the frayed blue band-aid caked with sawdust wrapped around their thumb. You remember last Tuesday, when they’d texted you “just had a rough night” but refused to say why—they’d been cutting those table legs in their garage after work each evening, trying to surprise you with a finished set before the grant deadline hit.

The sharp edge of the argument deflates like a popped party balloon. You reach into the cooler under the tool shed for two cold lemonades, sweat beading down the sides of the paper cups, and hand one to your friend before sitting down on the only partially-sanded bench of the half-fixed picnic table between you. You don’t apologize right away, but you tell them you had no idea they’d been working on the tables alone, and they admit they were scared you’d dismiss their idea as “boring” next to your passionate push for the accessible beds.

As you talk, a group of your regulars rounds the garden’s iron gate: Mrs. Hernandez, who brings flaky conchas every Sunday, and Mr. Lee, who’s been using a walker since his knee surgery last winter. They’re hauling a stack of pre-cut cedar lumber they pooled together at the local hardware store, and they’d been planning a surprise all along to split the grant funds between both projects. Mr. Lee grins and nods toward the raised bed blueprint you’d been clutching, saying that’s exactly what he’s been asking the neighborhood association for, but he refuses to sit on a splintered bench when he comes to visit to share his kimchi with the group.

In the dream, the sun shifts low and golden, washing over the garden like the warm glow of the night we first planted our very first tomato seeds together on this exact plot of land. Your friend claps you on the back, and you both start hauling the lumber together, the lemonades sweating sticky in your hands, and for a second you swear you can smell the fresh turned dirt and the vanilla sugar from Mrs. Hernandez’s pastries. By the time the moon climbs high over the neighborhood, you’ve got the frame for the raised bed half-assembled and the first picnic table sanded smooth, and you realize that the best part of the garden—of your whole friendship—was never the plants or the projects. It was remembering that you didn’t have to pick just one thing to care about, that you could fight for everyone, even when it felt like you had to choose.
