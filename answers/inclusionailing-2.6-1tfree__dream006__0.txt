…and you begin to speak, but no sound comes from your mouth. Instead, your words spill out in color—ribbons of indigo and gold ribboning through the air like smoke, weaving themselves into shapes above the crowd: a flock of origami birds, a map of constellations, a slowly turning hourglass made of light.

The audience doesn’t clap; they inhale. Every breath they take pulls the colors closer, turning the auditorium into a lung filling with dusk. You realize you aren’t standing on wood or carpet, but on the surface of a tide that rises and falls in time with your pulse.

Somewhere in the back, a door opens that wasn’t there before. It doesn’t lead outside—it leads *up*, into the ceiling, which has become a sky of bruised plum and pear. A child steps through it, holding a mirror that shows not your face, but the back of your own head, smiling. You turn, and your shoulders brush the horizon.

The microphone in your hand softens, becoming a stem of wheat. The stage swells into a hill. The expectant faces blur into fireflies, each one carrying a fragment of the speech you meant to give. They rise in a helix, spelling out a single word you’ve known all your life but never said aloud.

You try to remember why you were afraid.

But before the memory can take shape, the ground remembers it for you—rippling like water struck by a stone, sending you gently upward. The ceiling is gone. The sky is made of paper. And you are no longer giving a speech; you are the echo inside the word, learning how to listen.
