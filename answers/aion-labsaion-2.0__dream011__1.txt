You are about to take the final exam for a course that determines your future career. As you sit down to start the exam, you notice the questions are not written in any language you recognize. The symbols on the page swirl and pulse, like living ink. You glance around, but the other students are already writing furiously, their pens scratching in a frantic, synchronized rhythm. Panic rises in your throat. You look back at your paper, and now the questions have shifted. They are in your own handwriting, but they ask things like: "What is the name of the childhood friend you betrayed?" and "When did you first realize you were capable of true cowardice?" and "List every time you chose the safe path." This isn't a test of knowledge; it's a moral audit. The clock on the wall isn't ticking; it's spinning backwards, years flying off with each revolution. The professor at the front of the room is now your own future self, aged and disappointed, shaking their head slowly. You pick up your pen. It feels heavy as a hammer. You know that whatever you write will become true.
