…walk away before you say something you can’t take back.

You turn down a hallway that wasn’t there a second ago. The walls are covered in old photos of you and your friend—laughing, traveling, surviving things you thought would break you. Your anger starts to crack.

At the end of the hall is a door with both your names carved into it. When you open it, you’re suddenly standing in your childhood kitchen, except outside the window is an ocean at night. Your friend is already there, sitting at the table, not angry now—just tired, like you.

Neither of you speaks for a while. A kettle whistles, though no one turned it on.

Finally, your friend says, “I wasn’t trying to win. I was trying not to lose you.”

You sit down. The argument feels smaller, but what it was protecting feels huge. You both start explaining the fear underneath the anger, and with every sentence the ocean outside gets calmer.

By the time you finish talking, dawn light spills into the room. The carved names on the door fade into a single word:

**Listen.**

And just before you wake up, your friend reaches across the table, and you take their hand.
