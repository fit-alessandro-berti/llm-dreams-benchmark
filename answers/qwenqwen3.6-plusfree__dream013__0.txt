At first, you rely on sharp focus: cross-referencing symbols, testing sequences, holding entire branches of possibility in your mind at once. But the deeper you go, the more the logic begins to bend. A puzzle that demands prime numbers suddenly requires you to remember the exact shade of your first pet's collar. Another won't yield until you stop calculating and simply hum a melody you haven't thought of in years. The maze isn't just testing your reasoning anymore; it's asking what lies beneath it. Memory. Emotion. Instinct. The parts of you that don't need proof to know something is true.

Time stretches and folds. You can't tell if you've been walking for minutes or seasons. The puzzles start solving themselves as you learn to stop pushing and start listening. Walls become translucent, revealing layers upon layers of interlocking paths, each one a reflection of a thought you've had, a choice you've made, a question you've carried.

Finally, the passages converge into a circular chamber. In the center rests a single, unmarked pedestal. On it sits a cube made of glass, inside of which floats a miniature version of the maze you've just traveled. There's no instruction. No lock. No obvious next step.

You realize the final puzzle isn't something to crack. It's something to release.

You place your palm against the glass. It doesn't shatter—it dissolves. The miniature maze rises like smoke, spirals upward, and slips into your chest. For a moment, you are infinite. You understand every turn, every dead end, every moment of frustration and clarity. You didn't conquer the maze. You completed yourself.

Then the floor softens. The light dims. And you wake with your hand resting gently over your heartbeat, the quiet hum of solved patterns still echoing behind your eyes.
