You are about to give a speech to a large audience. As you step onto the stage, you see the crowd looking expectantly at you, and you begin to feel the sharp, fizzy flutter of nerves in your stomach, like you’ve swallowed a jar of popped popcorn. Your leather shoes scuff the warm, honey-toned stage planks, and for a split second, your mind goes completely blank—every single line you’d rehearsed in the shower, every note you’d scribbled in the middle of the night, has dissolved into static. The crowd’s faces blur and shift for a heartbeat: half strangers with crinkled, warm smiles, half the faces of childhood friends you haven’t spoken to in years, all gazing at you like they’re glad you’re here.

You fumble for the microphone at the podium, your palms slick with sweat, and when you finally speak, your voice comes out higher and waverier than you intended, cracking on the first planned opening line. A soft, gentle ripple of laughter moves through the audience, and a woman in the front row waves a hand, mouthing “take your time” before lifting a paper cup of lemonade in a toast. Something tight in your chest unwinds, and you abandon the script entirely. You tell them about tripping over your cat while grabbing your speech notes that morning, about the way the stage lights smelled like the summer bonfires you went to as a kid even though this was a cold downtown auditorium, about how when you first looked out at the crowd you’d realized nearly everyone in the room was someone you’d crossed paths with once, all gathered for the same quiet, silly purpose.

As you talk, the dream twists softly around you: the rigid rows of theater chairs melt into patchwork picnic blankets, the audience sitting cross-legged, passing around jars of lemonade and sugar cookies. A group of tiny, grinning kids bursts through the side stage doors, weaving between your ankles to hand you crumpled crayon drawings of shooting stars. You hold one up to the microphone, and the crowd cheers, the sound blending with the chirp of a stray sparrow that’s somehow fluttered through the open high window. You don’t remember exactly how your speech wraps up—one minute you’re talking about the joy of showing up even when you’re scared, the next the crowd is standing, waving glowing paper lanterns that drift toward the ceiling like dandelion seeds. When you step down from the stage, a stranger presses a crumpled daisy behind your ear, and by the time your alarm blares the next morning, you can still taste the sweet lemonade and hear the hum of the crowd’s cheers.
