The audience doesn't look confused. Instead, they nod in deep appreciation, scribbling notes onto clipboards that you suddenly realize are slices of buttered toast. 

You try to gesture toward the podium to anchor yourself, but your arm feels impossibly heavy, as if your bones have turned to wet clay. When you look down, the wooden podium has morphed into a grand piano. The black and white keys are slowly shifting, looking uncomfortably like rows of human teeth. You press one gently, and instead of a musical note, it plays the distinct, echoing sound of a screen door slamming in your childhood home.

A murmur ripples through the crowd. You look up and realize the audience is no longer a sea of strangers. Every single seat in the sprawling auditorium is occupied by a different version of yourself—seven-year-old you in a yellow raincoat, teenage you with a terrible haircut, you from five years ago looking exhausted. In perfect, eerie unison, hundreds of your own faces lean forward and whisper, *"But what about the ending?"*

Panic flutters in your chest. You look down at your cue cards for salvation, but the ink is moving. The words are swimming across the thick cardstock like tiny black tadpoles, rearranging themselves into a topographical map of a city you’ve never visited. 

Suddenly, the stage begins to tilt at a steep, impossible angle. The floating blue letters shatter into heavy, wet snow. You realize with absolute, inexplicable dream-certainty that you aren't a speaker at all; you are a lighthouse keeper, and the audience is the sea. 

You step off the slanted edge of the stage, but instead of falling, you drift upward into the velvet darkness of the rafters. The sound of the audience's applause morphs into the rhythmic, deafening crash of ocean waves against rocks. The cold water rises to meet you, splashing against your face, and with a sharp gasp, you open your eyes to the morning light.
