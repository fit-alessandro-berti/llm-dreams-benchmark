The carvings are not static; they ripple like water disturbed by a stone, and as you lean closer, the box emits a low, resonant hum that vibrates in your chest. The patterns resolve into a language you somehow understand—not with your mind, but with your bones. They tell you the box is not locked to keep you out, but to keep something in. And that something is a memory you forgot you lost.

You trace a spiral with your fingertip, and the box sighs open. Inside, there is no key, no treasure—only a small, perfect mirror. But the reflection is not yours. It shows a version of you standing in a field of silver grass under two moons, smiling a smile you haven’t worn in years. The reflection reaches out, and your hand moves to meet it. The moment your fingers touch the glass, the dream dissolves into a cascade of images: a childhood home with doors that lead to oceans, a clock that ticks backward, a voice calling your name from the bottom of a well filled with stars.

You wake with the taste of moonlight on your tongue and the certainty that the box is still there, waiting in the space between sleeping and waking, for you to remember the rest.
