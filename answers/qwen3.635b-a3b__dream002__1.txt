The thunder doesn't booms; it hums, a deep vibration that resonates in your bones, turning the air into liquid gold. You reach out to touch the nearest branch, but your fingers pass through it like smoke. The trees are shifting. Their bark softens to velvet, and their leaves turn into whispering wings, fluttering in rhythm with the storm's heartbeat.

The rain begins to fall upward. Droplets of iridescent water drift from the ground toward the sky, each one holding a tiny, floating memory of a place you've never visited—a city of glass, an ocean of clockwork, a forest where time smells like cinnamon.

You realize you aren't afraid. You feel lighter, your feet lifting inches off the moss. The storm isn't a threat; it's an invitation. The wind wraps around you, lifting you gently into the air. As you rise, the forest shrinks beneath you, transforming into a sprawling map of stars that pulse in time with your breathing.

A path of stepping stones made of moonlight extends from your chest toward a gap in the clouds, widening to reveal a door standing alone, suspended in the void. It has no handle, only a keyhole shaped like your own eye. You hear a voice, faint and familiar, calling you from the other side.

You look down at your hands, which are now transparent, filled with swirling galaxies. You know, with absolute dream-certainty, that you must jump.

You close your eyes and let go.

You feel the lurch of falling, the rush of wind that tastes like cold mint and static, and then...

Your alarm clock is beeping. The light of morning is creeping through the blinds. For a moment, just a heartbeat, you can still taste the cinnamon and see the stars in the shape of your own breathing.

Then the dream dissolves, leaving you sitting up in bed, wondering where you left your wings.
