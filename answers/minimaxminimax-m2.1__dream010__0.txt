# The Dream Job

You are dreaming. Can you complete the following dream?

---

The lights dim. The audience disappears. Suddenly, you find yourself standing in an endless white room, your heart pounding with anticipation. A voice booms from everywhere and nowhere: "Welcome to the final challenge."

Before you materializes a desk—your desk, the one you've imagined in countless daydreams. Sunlight streams through floor-to-ceiling windows overlooking a cityscape that seems to stretch into eternity. On the desk sits a single envelope, sealed with gold wax.

"You have three dreams within dreams to complete," the voice continues. "Each will test a skill you didn't know you possessed. Open the first envelope."

Your fingers tremble as you break the seal. Inside, a photograph slips out—it's you, but younger, standing at a crossroads you remember vividly from years ago. The fork in the road that led you away from your true path.

"First challenge: Convince your past self to choose differently."

The room dissolves. You stand on a rainy street corner, watching yourself—twenty-one years old, umbrella-less, tears mixing with rain—about to walk away from the music store where you first felt alive. Your younger self holds a business degree acceptance letter, a "sensible" future wrapped in crisp paper.

You approach yourself. The younger you looks up, eyes red-rimmed and desperate.

"I know you don't believe me," you say, your voice catching. "I know you're scared that passion doesn't pay rent. But I'm you—I'm proof. Twenty years from now, you still hear that piano in your dreams. You still smell the wood polish and feel the keys beneath your fingers. The sensible path leads to a life of quiet mourning. This choice, this terrifying beautiful choice, leads to something else entirely."

Your younger self stares at you, and in that moment, recognition flickers. Not understanding—recognition. The way you recognize yourself in a dream.

The photograph glows in your hand, and the younger you slowly, deliberately, lets the acceptance letter fall into a puddle. They turn toward the music store.

The white room returns. The desk remains, but now a second envelope appears.

"Second challenge: Create something from nothing."

This time, you're not in the white room. You're floating in a vast darkness, surrounded by other competitors—faces blurred, figures indistinct. Each of you has been given an impossible task: build a bridge across the void using only your memories.

You close your eyes and reach back. The memory of your grandmother's garden rises first—the wooden bridge over her koi pond, painted red, peeling in places but sturdy enough to hold a child's entire world. You pull it from your mind, and it materializes, glowing faintly, solid enough to stand upon.

Others are doing the same: bridges of summer camps and first apartments, of treehouses and library staircases, all the small architectures of a life. But the void is infinite, and one by one, the bridges crumble under the weight of the darkness.

You remember something else then: not a place, but a feeling. The moment you helped a stranger find their way and felt, for just a breath, like you belonged in the universe. You pull that feeling forward, and your bridge transforms—not wood anymore, but something luminous, something made of pure connection. It stretches endlessly in both directions, and when you step onto it, it holds.

The final envelope appears, lighter than the others, almost weightless.

"Third challenge: Accept what you cannot change."

You're sitting in a hospital room. The machines beep their steady rhythm. In the bed lies someone you love—the face shifts and changes, becoming everyone you've ever lost. They take your hand, and you know this moment. This is the moment you couldn't save them. This is the moment you learned that love sometimes means letting go.

But this time, the competition doesn't ask you to change it. It asks you to sit with it. To acknowledge that some doors close forever, and that the grief is not a failure but a proof—of how deeply you were able to love.

You stay with them. You don't fight the ending. You simply hold their hand and let yourself feel every ounce of sorrow, and when the final breath comes, you whisper: "Thank you for letting me love you."

The white room returns for the last time. The desk is gone. In its place stands a door—ordinary in every way, with a brass handle that gleams like morning light.

The voice softens: "You have completed all three challenges. But here's the secret: the job was never the prize. The job was always you—the person you had to become to claim it. The one who can face their past, build from memory, and hold love even in its ending."

You reach for the handle.

"WAKE UP," the voice commands—but gently, the way a mother wakes a child from a beautiful dream.

---

You open your eyes.

Morning light filters through curtains you don't remember choosing. Your phone buzzes with a calendar reminder: *Interview - Dream Company - 10:00 AM*.

But something has changed. You don't feel like a candidate anymore.

You feel like someone who already knows they belong.
