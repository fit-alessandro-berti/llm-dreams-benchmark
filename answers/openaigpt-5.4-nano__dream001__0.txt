You start to run—at first just on instinct—then slower, because the louder your footsteps get, the more the buildings seem to listen.

The city is too big to be real. Streets fold into themselves like paper creased the wrong way. Every intersection looks slightly angled, as if your eyes are trying to remember how to stand still. You pass shops with names you can’t read twice—each time you glance back, the letters rearrange into something almost familiar. Overhead, a sky the color of old tin reflects your movement back at you a moment late, so for half a second you’re always trailed by a version of yourself that hasn’t caught up.

An hour. An important meeting. A clock you can’t find.

You try to call someone, but your phone unlocks to a map that won’t stabilize. It zooms in and out with each blink, refusing to settle on any street longer than a breath. You tap “Search,” and instead of results you get a single sentence, typed as if by a tired hand:

**FOLLOW THE PLACE THAT FEELS LIKE YOU’VE BEEN LATE BEFORE.**

That’s when you notice it—not a sign, not a landmark, but a sensation. Near your left ear there’s a pressure, like standing beside a door that’s been opened too many times. It pulls you forward with a kind of personal gravity.

You follow it down a boulevard lined with lamps that flicker in patterns like Morse code. You don’t know the code, but your body reads it anyway. Every few steps, the air changes texture: from warm to cool, from dry to damp, from ordinary to something that tastes faintly of metal and rain.

A crowd gathers at an alleyway that wasn’t there a moment ago. People stream in two directions at once—except you realize they’re not going different places. They’re going the same place from different times. In the same doorway you see a man arriving with coffee already spilled on his shirt, and then—without any break in the image—himself arriving again, brisk and untouched, as if the universe is trying to replay a missed moment.

You step closer and the crowd parts for you, not because they recognize you, but because your panic fits the shape of an opening.

Inside the alley, the walls are lined with bulletin boards made of stacked mirrors. Headlines repeat across them like echoes: *MEETING MOVED.* *MEETING CANCELLED.* *MEETING RECONSIDERED.* Each message is stamped with a date that slides forward and backward when you try to focus.

At the far end, a clerk sits behind a counter that looks like it was built from old receipts. The clerk doesn’t look up immediately. When they do, their eyes have the calm expression of someone who has been waiting long before you understood you were late.

“Your hour,” the clerk says. “Is it the one you were promised, or the one you inherited?”

“I—” you begin, but the words snag. You can feel your important meeting hovering like a forgotten sentence at the back of your throat.

The clerk points to a number painted on the counter: **12:00**.

But the wristwatch on your arm reads **11:07**.

Then, without warning, your watch flickers and becomes someone else’s wrist—same skin, same bone structure, but a different time printed in the tiny, stubborn numerals. **11:59.** Then **11:08.** Then **11:00** again, like the dream is practicing what “urgent” feels like.

“What do I do?” you ask.

The clerk smiles in a way that doesn’t make you feel comforted. It makes you feel *observed.*

“You’re looking for a location,” they say. “But the city is looking for the version of you that can arrive.”

You swallow. “I just need directions.”

The clerk slides a thin card across the counter. It’s blank at first. Then, as you pick it up, letters begin to form from the edges toward the center:

**ROOM 0 / FLOOR — / MEETING WITH: YOU (LATER)**

You blink. The card warms in your palm, like it contains blood.

“Room zero?” you repeat, laughing once, sharp and involuntary. “There’s no such thing.”

“There is,” the clerk says. “But you have to choose the hallway that leads to it. The city won’t help you find the hallway that saves you. It will only help you find the one that makes you deserve arriving.”

You look up, and the alley has changed. Where there was one doorway, there are now three. Each door has a different kind of handle:

- One is brass and cold, shaped like a question mark.
- One is made of glass, with your reflection trapped inside.
- One is simple wood, with a handle that feels like warm skin.

Behind each door, you hear a different sound: the brass door hums with distant conversation; the glass door crackles like static; the wooden one is quiet except for the soft clicking of a metronome.

You realize something: the meeting isn’t just important. It’s *yours*. It belongs to a you that has already decided what they’re going to say.

You don’t know which door is right, so you do the only thing you can do in a dream—something you’ve never done awake.

You ask yourself, very carefully, a question you don’t usually ask:

*What am I afraid to walk into?*

The answer comes immediately, not as a thought but as a sensation: the fear of being seen in the moment you finally stop pretending you’re fine.

That makes your hand close around the wooden handle.

It’s warm, like someone has been holding it just before you did.

The door opens without resistance.

Inside is a conference room that looks both ordinary and impossibly exact. The chairs are too comfortable. The table is the right length. The fluorescent lights don’t buzz—they breathe. A small sign on the wall reads:

**WELCOME BACK.**

There are people in the room, but you can’t make out their faces. Their features slide away from focus as if your eyes don’t have permission to remember them.

At the far end of the table sits a single person with their back toward you—upright, waiting, perfectly still. The posture looks familiar in the way a childhood scar looks familiar.

When you step fully into the room, the air tightens, and time seems to pause like a page held open mid-sentence.

The person turns.

It’s you, but older—same eyes, same shape of worry around the mouth, only softened by having survived whatever moment you’re about to survive. Their expression is not triumphant. It’s relieved.

“You made it,” they say.

“I don’t know how,” you whisper, because you do not trust the ease of it.

Older-you gestures to the chair across from them. “Sit. Tell me what you were trying not to say.”

You sit.

Your hands are resting on the table, and for a second you feel the table’s surface hum like a living thing. In the silence, you suddenly remember why you came into this city in the first place: the meeting wasn’t about work or plans. It was about a choice you kept postponing.

You open your mouth.

But instead of a sentence, the dream gives you a soundless image—your own calendar, your own unsent email drafts, your own swallowed apologies. A series of almosts. A stack of moments filed under *later.*

Older-you watches you with a kind of patience that only exists when you’re finally no longer running.

“You have an hour,” they repeat. “But you never meant an hour. You meant *permission.*”

The fluorescent lights brighten until they’re almost white. For the first time, you feel your fear turn into something like resolve.

You look at the older version of yourself and speak, finally, clearly:

“I’m done delaying.”

The moment the words leave you, the room loosens. The faces in the periphery sharpen, not into strangers, but into other versions of you—some younger, some braver, some broken, some laughing. They nod as if you’re stepping into a chorus you didn’t know you’d been part of your whole life.

Older-you exhales, and the sound is like a lock turning.

A clock appears on the wall—one you hadn’t noticed. The hands begin to move forward for real, not the skipping, flickering movement your watch had been doing.

**11:59.**

**12:00.**

The meeting begins—not with agendas, but with an acceptance. With the simple, miraculous fact that you can show up even when you don’t know the address yet.

Older-you reaches out across the table.

When your fingers meet, it feels like remembering something you always knew: where you were going all along.

And then—because dreams love to pay you in symbolism—you wake up standing in the middle of your own room, breathless, phone in hand, one notification glowing on the screen:

**“Meeting Location: [saved in your contacts]. Today. 12:00 PM.”**

Only now, when you reread it, you understand the final twist—

The city you searched for was never outside you. It was the part of you that was finally willing to arrive.
