…plant a garden.

At first, it seems ridiculous. The city is a skeleton of glass and rust, the rivers run black after rain, and every night something howls beyond the collapsed freeway. But beneath an overturned bus, you find a packet of seeds sealed in wax paper.

**SUNFLOWERS**, it says.

You carry them for days.

You trade half a can of peaches for a shovel with a cracked handle. You choose a patch of earth beside an abandoned library, where the roof has fallen in and sunlight reaches the floor. Between shelves of swollen books, you dig.

Each seed feels impossibly small in your palm.

You plant them anyway.

Weeks pass. Raiders come and go. Storms strip paint from the buildings. You learn which streets are safe before dawn and which shadows move when nobody is looking. Every morning, before searching for water, you visit the library.

Nothing.

Then one day, a green hook pushes through the dirt.

You laugh so suddenly and loudly that birds rise from the ruined rooftops.

By the end of summer, the library is full of sunflowers. Their yellow faces turn toward the broken sky, bright enough to be seen from blocks away. Strangers begin to arrive—not to steal, at first, but to stare.

An old woman brings a jar of clean water.

A child brings three more seeds.

Someone repairs the library door.

And, in the dream, you understand something that feels more important than survival:

The world did not end.

It was only waiting for someone to begin again.
