…walk away—not out of anger, but because you suddenly realize the ground beneath you is dissolving.

The floor becomes liquid silver, shimmering like mercury under a full moon, and your feet sink slightly as if wading through memory. Your friend’s voice echoes behind you, fractured and distant:  
*“You never listen—you never really see me!”*

But instead of turning back, you feel a strange calm. The air smells like rain on pine needles, and the walls around you—once lined with framed photographs of laughter, vacations, late-night talks—begin to unravel into origami cranes, each one folded from pages of letters you never sent.

A voice, neither yours nor theirs, whispers from the ceiling:  
*“The argument wasn’t about who was right. It was about who was afraid.”*

You look down. In your palm, a small, glowing key has formed—warm, alive. Beneath it, etched in delicate script:  
*“The door you’re running from is the one you locked when you stopped telling the truth.”*

You turn slowly.

Your friend is no longer angry. They stand barefoot on the silver floor, eyes wet but soft. In their hand: another key. They smile—not triumphantly, but tenderly—and hold it out.

“You always did,” they say, “know how to run… but never how to stay.”

The space between you fills with petals—each one a moment you ignored:  
*The time you missed their birthday because you were “too busy.”  
The silence after their confession of anxiety.  
The hug you didn’t give when they needed one most.*

You reach for their hand.  
The keys touch.

The world doesn’t explode in light.  
It inhales.

The silver floor solidifies into mossy earth. The cranes melt into fireflies. The air hums—not with tension, but with the hum of a lullaby your grandmother used to sing.

You’re sitting on an old wooden porch swing now, wrapped in the same blanket you both had as kids. The stars above are stitched with constellations shaped like your inside jokes.

Your friend leans into you, quiet.

“I’m sorry,” you whisper.

“Me too,” they reply.

And for the first time in years—you both breathe at the same time.

You wake up.

Your phone buzzes.

It’s a message from them:  
*“Hey. Still mad? Or… can we talk?”*

You stare at the screen.  
Your cheeks are wet.

You type back:  
*“I’m home. I’m listening.”*

And just before you hit send,  
you swear you hear a whisper—  
*“Welcome back.”*
