**Round One: The Archive**

The first challenge is deceptively quiet. You're led into a vast room — think cathedral-sized — lined with floor-to-ceiling bookshelves. But the books aren't paper. They're made of compressed light, warm to the touch, and their contents shift when you hold them.

A voice, neither male nor female, echoes from the ceiling:

*"Find the one story that was written about you before you were born."*

There are millions of spines to choose from. You walk for what feels like hours. Your fingers trail across glowing edges. Some books are cold. Some vibrate. One burns your palm and you pull back, breathless.

Then you find it. A slim volume, no thicker than your thumb, bound in a material that looks like fog. You open it and the words aren't words — they're *weather*. A gust of wind that smells like rain on warm pavement. A patch of sunlight on the back of your neck. The feeling of finishing a sentence you didn't know you'd started.

You close it. The room dissolves.

---

**Round Two: The Kitchen of Impossible Meals**

Now you're standing in a kitchen that stretches in all directions. The counters are made of river stone. The stoves burn with different colored flames — one blue, one amber, one the deep violet of a bruise.

A judge sits at a table far away. You can't see their face, only their silhouette.

*"Cook the meal you needed most but never had."*

The pantry contains ingredients that don't have names. There's something that tastes like the feeling of being forgiven. There's a root vegetable that hums when you slice it. There's salt, but it's black, and when it hits your tongue, you remember a dream you had when you were seven.

You cook. Not with recipes. With instinct. You move the way water moves around a stone — not forcing, just *finding the path*.

You plate something. It's not beautiful. But the judge tastes it, and for a moment, they stop moving entirely, like a film caught between frames.

They nod once. The kitchen folds itself into a hallway.

---

**Round Three: The Hallway That Remembers**

The hallway is long and lined with doors, but each door is a memory — not yours, but *theirs*. Everyone who has ever wanted this job.

Behind one door, someone is crying in a bathroom at 2 AM.
Behind another, someone is learning a language they'll never be fluent in.
Behind a third, someone is laughing so hard they've forgotten what they were laughing about.

Your task, the voice says, is simply this:

*"Walk to the end without opening a single door."*

This is the hardest challenge. Because every door calls to you with a version of empathy that feels like drowning. You want to help. You want to witness. You want to *know*.

But you walk. One foot in front of the other. Eyes forward. Breathing slow.

You don't open a single door.

---

**The Final Room**

At the end of the hallway is a room with no walls. Just sky. A twilight sky, the kind that exists for three minutes at sunset when the world can't decide what color it wants to be.

There's a chair. Just one. Wooden. Unfinished.

The voice says:

*"The job isn't behind that chair. The job is that you chose to sit in it — knowing it wasn't finished. Knowing you'd have to build the rest yourself."*

You sit.

And the sky cracks open like an egg, and light pours in, and somewhere — from the direction of waking — you feel the faintest pull, like a thread behind your sternum, saying:

*Time to remember.*

---

The dream ends here, at the threshold between the unreal and the real, where the prize isn't something you *won* but something you *recognized*.
