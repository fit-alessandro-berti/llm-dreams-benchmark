You are in a maze filled with complex puzzles. Each puzzle requires intense concentration and logical thinking to solve, and as you move deeper into the maze, you begin to notice that the walls are made of equations—living equations that shift and rearrange themselves whenever you look away.

The first puzzle is a door with no handle, only a sequence of glowing symbols: a triangle, a spiral, a triangle, a spiral, and then... emptiness. You reach out and trace a triangle in the air, and the door dissolves like sugar in water.

Deeper in, the puzzles grow stranger. One room contains a chessboard where the pieces move on their own, but they're playing by rules you almost remember—rules from a game you played in childhood, or perhaps a game you haven't invented yet. You realize the solution isn't to win, but to convince the white king to surrender peacefully. You whisper something to him. You won't remember what you said when you wake, but it works.

The maze begins to respond to your thoughts now. When you imagine a staircase, one unfolds before you. But this becomes its own puzzle: the maze gives you exactly what you expect, so progress requires expecting nothing. You learn to walk with an empty mind, and the corridors open like flowers.

Then you reach the center—or what should be the center. Instead of a treasure or an exit, there is a mirror. In the reflection, you see yourself sitting at a desk, drawing this very maze on paper, designing each puzzle you just solved.

The reflection looks up at you and smiles.

"Did you enjoy it?" your reflection asks. "I made it just hard enough."

And you understand, with the strange certainty that only exists in dreams, that you have been both the architect and the wanderer all along. The maze was never a prison. It was a gift you gave yourself—a place where your mind could play.

The walls begin to soften into light. Somewhere far away, an alarm is ringing, gentle as rain.

You wake with the faint feeling that you've forgotten the answer to a question you can no longer remember asking—and the equally faint suspicion that tonight, the maze will be waiting for you again, with new puzzles already drawn.
