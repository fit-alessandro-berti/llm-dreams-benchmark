...despite the hardships, you decide to build.

Not with steel or concrete—those are ghosts of a forgotten age—but with what the new world offers: resilience, adaptability, and the stubborn spark of creation.

You start with your hands, digging into the cracked earth to find a vein of clean water, brackish but life-sustaining. You collect fallen branches and weave them into a lattice, a frame for something that doesn't yet exist. You scavenge clear plastic from the ruins of a greenhouse, stretch it taut over your structure, and anchor it with stones. It’s a makeshift greenhouse, a fragile bubble of intention in a world that has forgotten what flowers look like.

Inside, you plant seeds you’ve hoarded—carrots, beans, something you salvaged from a pantry buried under rubble. You use your urine and ash to make a crude fertilizer, learning the alchemy of turning waste into nourishment. Each day, you tend to the seedlings like a priest tending to sacred embers.

As weeks turn into months, the greenhouse becomes your sanctuary and your signal. Birds begin to circle. A fox sniffs at the edges, wary but curious. One day, you find a child—skinny, eyes wide with a hunger that isn’t just for food—peeking through the holes in your plastic walls.

You don’t turn them away.

Together, you expand. You teach them how to purify water, how to coax life from poison. They teach you how to listen to the wind the way the old world taught you to read stock reports—both are languages of survival.

Years later, your little bubble of green is a village. Solar panels scavenged from abandoned cars power a water purifier. Rainwater is harvested and shared. Stories are traded like seeds—tales of cities that reached for the stars, and warnings about the poison they left behind.

You are no longer just surviving. You are growing a new world.

And in this dream, as the sun sets over a horizon painted with the smoke of distant, necessary fires, you realize: the apocalypse wasn’t the end. It was the compost.
