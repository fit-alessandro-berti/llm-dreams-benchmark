...not written in ink, but in light. 

They are shimmering, translucent lines of gold that pulse against the paper like a slow, rhythmic heartbeat. As I lean closer, the words begin to drift upward, breaking free from the page and swirling around the room like glowing embers. I realize then that there is no pen in my hand—instead, I am holding a single, dried wildflower.

The proctor, a figure draped in heavy, velvet robes whose face is obscured by a mask made of shifting constellations, paces the aisles. Every time their footsteps hit the floor, the sound isn't a thud, but a low piano note that vibrates in my chest. 

I look back at the paper, but the questions have changed. They are no longer sentences; they are shapes, colors, and half-remembered melodies. One question is the smell of rain on hot asphalt; another is the feeling of falling in slow motion. To answer them, I realize I must not write, but breathe. I have to exhale the correct emotion to make the light settle into the right pattern.

Panic rises, but it doesn't feel like fear—it feels like rising water. The room begins to tilt, the desks turning into long, wooden boats floating on a sea of ink. The golden questions hover above me like tiny, glowing jellyfish. I reach out to catch one, my fingers brushing against a question that feels like a secret I forgot a long time ago, and just as I prepare to answer, the floor dissolves into a cloud of white feathers, and I am falling upward into a sky made of silk.
