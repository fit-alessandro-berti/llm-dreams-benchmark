…speak, but no words come out—only a ribbon of golden fish that swim through the air like sentences you forgot in childhood.  

The audience doesn’t gasp. They nod, as if this is exactly what they paid to see.  
So you raise your hands, and the stage stretches into a pier over a moonlit ocean. Every seat becomes a small boat. Every face becomes someone you’ve loved, lost, or almost recognized in a train station years ago.

Then your voice returns—not from your throat, but from the sky.  
It says: *“You were never here to impress them. You were here to remember them.”*

The boats drift closer. The fish-words circle back and settle on your shoulders, warm as scarves.  
You laugh, finally, and the crowd laughs with you, and the sound turns into rain—soft, silver rain that falls upward, lifting everyone gently into the stars as your speech ends exactly where it began:

“Thank you for listening to the parts of me I was afraid to say.”
