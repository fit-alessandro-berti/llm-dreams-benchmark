:::writing
…as the competition progresses, you face various challenges and strange, shifting rules. One moment you’re solving complex problems on a whiteboard that stretches into the clouds, the next you’re navigating a maze made of office cubicles where the walls quietly slide when you’re not looking.

Other competitors appear and disappear without explanation. Some are incredibly skilled, typing at impossible speeds or delivering flawless presentations to judges whose faces you can’t quite see. Sometimes they feel like rivals. Other times they help you, handing you clues scribbled on sticky notes that weren’t there a second before.

In one round you’re asked to build something meaningful with limited time. The materials keep changing—code turns into paper, paper turns into tools, tools turn into ideas. You realize the judges aren’t scoring perfection. They’re watching how you adapt.

As the final round approaches, the setting transforms into a huge auditorium filled with quiet anticipation. The last challenge is surprisingly simple: you must explain why you want the dream job.

When you start speaking, the room fades. Instead of judges, you see moments from your life—things you learned, mistakes you made, people who encouraged you, late nights, small victories. Your words feel less like a speech and more like remembering who you are.

When you finish, there’s silence.

Then the judges stand. One of them smiles and says, “The competition was never about beating the others. It was about discovering whether you would still want the job after seeing what it asks of you.”

A door appears behind them.

You open it, expecting an office or a stage or something impressive.

Instead, it leads to a quiet workspace filled with tools, possibilities, and a blank project waiting for you to begin.

And just as you step inside, the dream fades—leaving you with the strange certainty that the real competition hasn’t started yet.
