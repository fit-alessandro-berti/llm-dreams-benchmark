…survive long enough to become useful.

At first, panic comes like weather. The air smells of smoke, wet soil, animals. No engines, no wires, no distant hum of electricity—only wind through rough wooden beams and the murmur of voices speaking a language you almost understand. You sit up on a straw pallet in a dim room and stare at your own hands as if they might explain everything.

Outside, the world is all mud lanes, hand-hewn carts, low stone walls, and people whose lives depend on things you have only ever taken for granted. Fire is not a switch. Water is not a tap. Night is truly dark.

You realize very quickly that knowing the future is less useful than knowing how to live without convenience.

So you begin with the smallest things.

You learn how to ask for water without sounding like a threat. You learn to keep your eyes lowered when soldiers pass. You trade a button from your shirt for a heel of bread. You discover that a smile can cross centuries even when words cannot. Hunger sharpens you. Cold disciplines you. Shame leaves you when necessity arrives.

Soon you understand that survival here means becoming valuable before you become suspicious.

You watch the baker wake before dawn and offer to carry wood. You help an old woman sort herbs, pretending your clumsy guesses are confidence. In the fields, you remember fragments from school—crop rotation, boiling water, washing wounds—and present them not as miracles, but as suggestions. Never too much. Never enough to sound unnatural. Just enough to be invited back tomorrow.

Days turn strange and vivid. Your soft hands blister and harden. You stop expecting glass windows, hot showers, instant answers. Time lengthens. A loaf of bread becomes an achievement. Dry socks feel like wealth. A roof that does not leak feels like a blessing spoken directly by heaven.

And yet the deeper you settle into this lost age, the more unsettling your role becomes.

Because you know things.

Not everything. Not dates and kings and exact battles, not the neat timeline history books pretend existed. But you know enough to notice the omens hidden in ordinary life: the crowded rats near grain stores, the tension in the market, the gossip about troops moving on the far road, the priest coughing into a cloth. You know how fragile these people are because you know, dimly, what is coming for eras like this—war, plague, famine, winter, taxes, fire.

You try to warn them gently.

Store more grain, you say.
Boil the water, you say.
Keep the sick apart, you say.
Wash the hands, the sheets, the knives.

Some laugh. Some listen. A few begin to trust you.

One evening, in the orange light of sunset, the village healer asks who taught you so much. You look at the mortar and pestle in her hands, the bundles of drying rosemary overhead, the centuries pressing in around you, and you almost tell her the truth: that you come from a world beyond imagining, where voices travel invisibly through the air and stars are photographed up close and death is delayed by chemistry and light.

But in the dream, you know better.

So you say, “I have always been afraid of losing people.”

The healer nods as if this is the oldest education there is.

Then the dream shifts, as dreams do.

Winter arrives all at once. Snow buries the road. A fever moves through the houses. You run from bedside to bedside with steaming water, clean cloth, willow bark, broth, whatever comfort your patchwork knowledge can invent. Some recover. Some do not. Every life saved feels impossibly heavy; every life lost feels like an accusation from time itself.

You begin to understand that being sent here was never about changing history in grand gestures. It was about smaller salvations. One child who lives. One infection prevented. One family taught to store food before the frost. One generation nudged, barely, toward mercy.

In the final part of the dream, spring returns.

You stand on a hill above the village. The trees are green again. Smoke rises in thin blue threads from the chimneys below. Somewhere, someone is hammering metal. Somewhere else, someone is laughing. The world is still harsh, still old, still missing everything you once called essential. Yet beneath your bare feet the earth feels steady in a way your other life never did.

And you realize what you need to do.

Not escape.
Not conquer.
Not reveal the future.

You need to belong long enough to leave something behind.

A cleaner well.
A better habit.
A book of remedies.
A child taught to read.
A field planted differently.
A handful of people who will remember that once, years ago, a strange person arrived with odd clothes and stranger ideas, and because of them, things got a little less dark.

At that moment, the church bell rings, deep and bronze and lonely, and everyone below turns toward the sound.

And then you wake again—

this time in your own bed, your phone glowing on the nightstand, rain ticking softly at the window, electricity humming through the walls like a tame miracle—

with dirty fingernails, straw caught in your sleeve,

and the certain, impossible feeling that somewhere in another century, someone still remembers your name.
