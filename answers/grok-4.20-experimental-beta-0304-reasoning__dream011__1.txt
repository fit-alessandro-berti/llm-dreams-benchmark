**...all in your own handwriting.**

But not the handwriting you have now. It's the clumsy, looping script from when you were maybe nine years old — the same handwriting that used to sign your name on drawings of rockets and dinosaurs.

The first question reads:  
**1. Why did you stop believing you could fly?**  
(There are no multiple-choice options. Just a blank line that somehow feels judgmental.)

Question 2:  
**2. Remember when you wanted to be an astronaut-cartoonist-oceanographer? What happened to that guy?**

Question 3 is just a drawing of you — the current you — standing in this exact exam room, looking terrified. The stick-figure version of you has a speech bubble that says:  
*"You already know the answers. You've just been pretending the test is in the wrong subject."*

You look up. The professor is your future self, twenty years older, wearing the exact clothes you have on right now. He gives you a small, tired smile and taps his watch.

The clock on the wall isn't ticking forward. It's counting down the years you've spent being afraid to be ridiculous.

You pick up your pencil (which is now a lightsaber) and start writing. The words come easily, like they've been waiting for this exact moment your entire life.

And somewhere in the distance, you hear yourself laughing — the real laugh you haven't used since you were nine.

Then the dream starts to dissolve, the way dreams do, but not before older-you leans down and whispers:

"Final answer was always 'yes.'  
The rest was just extra credit."
