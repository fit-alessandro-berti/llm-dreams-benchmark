You are feeling lost and alone in a bustling city. Suddenly, a group of kind strangers offers to help you find your way, and you...

...hesitate at first. Their faces are unfamiliar, and the city has taught you to keep your guard up. But there's something in their eyes — a patience that doesn't rush you, a warmth that doesn't demand anything in return.

One of them, a woman with silver hair and paint-stained hands, smiles and says, "We know what it's like. Everyone gets turned around here."

They don't just give you directions. They walk with you.

The group moves like a small current through the crowd — not fighting the river of people, but flowing with it, creating space for you in their middle. You notice things you'd missed before: a bakery with its door propped open, releasing the smell of fresh bread; a mural of a whale swimming through clouds on the side of a parking garage; the particular way the late afternoon light turns the glass towers amber.

The man on your left points to a narrow alley you would have never noticed. "Shortcut," he says. "The city hides its best paths from people who look like they know where they're going."

You laugh — the first time in what feels like days.

As you walk, the strangers begin to drift away one by one, each peeling off at a different corner with a wave or a nod, as if they were always going separate directions and simply chose to share this stretch of road with you. The silver-haired woman is the last to leave.

"This is your stop," she says.

You look up. You recognize the street. You're not lost anymore. You turn to thank her, but she's already halfway down the block, her silhouette dissolving into the crowd.

And you realize you never asked their names, and they never asked yours — and somehow, that makes it feel more like something that actually happened rather than something that was just dreamed.

You stand there for a moment in the amber light, feeling a little less alone, and then you keep walking.
