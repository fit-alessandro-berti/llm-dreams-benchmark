Despite the hardships, you decide to **plant a garden.**

It doesn't make sense. That's how you know it's a dream.

The sky is the color of an old television left on static - pale grey and buzzing. The highway behind you is cracked open like dry lips, and the wind smells like hot metal and dust. Everything is rust and ash and quiet.

Except for the library.

You've taken shelter in what used to be a library. The roof is half gone, the books are swollen with rain, but in the middle of the reading circle, where the children's carpet used to be, there is a perfect circle of dark, damp soil. Living soil. It shouldn't be there. It smells like after it rains.

In your pocket - you don't remember putting it there - is a little paper packet. The ink has bled, but you can still read: MORNING GLORIES - MIXED COLORS. And at the bottom, in a child's handwriting: *for later.*

So you do it. You kneel down on that dead carpet and you start digging with your hands. The dirt gets under your fingernails. In the dream, your hands leave little glowing prints where you touch the ground.

You have no water. But then you notice the drinking fountain in the corner is dripping. One drop. Then another. Then a slow, steady trickle, despite there being no pipes anymore. Dream logic. You cup water in your hands and carry it, over and over.

While you work, you start humming. You don't know the song, but it feels right. And because it's a dream, the song comes out as actual light - small, warm notes that hang in the air for a second before floating up to the broken roof.

That's when they come.

Not raiders. Not monsters. Just other people. A woman with a shopping cart full of tangled extension cords. A kid wearing two different shoes. An old man carrying a broken radio that only plays static.

They stop at the doorway and watch you, confused.

"What are you doing?" the kid asks.

You realize you don't have an answer that is practical. You just say, "Making something for later."

The woman is the first to kneel. She empties her cart, and it turns out it's not full of cords, but of glass jars. She starts making little greenhouses. The old man sets his radio down, and suddenly the static clears and it's playing your humming back to you. The kid finds more packets - carrots, beans, impossible strawberries.

You all work until the sky gets that deep-blue that only happens in dreams right before you wake up.

By then, the library is not a library anymore. It's a greenhouse. Vines are already climbing up the empty bookshelves, impossibly fast, pushing out big, trumpet-shaped flowers that glow faintly pink and blue. Morning glories, opening all at once.

And you think: Oh. This is why I stayed.

Then you wake up, with the feeling of dirt still under your fingernails, and for some reason, you're smiling.
