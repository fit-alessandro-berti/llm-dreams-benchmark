You run your fingers along the edges, and the patterns respond—glowing a soft cyan where your skin makes contact. A memory surfaces, though you can't place where it came from: *the key was never meant to be found. It was meant to be remembered.*

You close your eyes.

Images flood your mind—a garden you've never visited, a song your grandmother hummed but never taught you, the exact shade of blue from a sky in a country you've never been to. The box grows warmer. The humming beneath your palms becomes a chord, then a voice, then words in a language you somehow understand:

*\ do you want to open?\ isn't locked at all. It never was. The lid has been slightly ajar this whole time, and a thin sliver of golden light spills out from within. But opening it fully feels monumental—like a decision that cannot be undone.

You hesitate. Inside, you sense, is either everything you've forgotten or everything you've been running from. Possibly both.

Your hand trembles as you lift the lid, and—

—you wake up, heart pounding, with the distinct feeling that your palms are still warm, and somewhere, just beyond the edge of hearing, that chord is still resolving.

On your nightstand, there is a small brass key you've never seen before.
