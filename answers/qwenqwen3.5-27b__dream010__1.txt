...the rules of reality begin to dissolve.

The interview room stretches out into an infinite corridor where the floor is made of rippling water and the walls are lined with floating clocks that tick backward. The first challenge isn't a question, but a climb. A staircase spirals upward into a sky that smells like old books and ozone. As I ascend, my feet leave no footprints, but every step I take turns into a glowing rune that vanishes behind me.

The second challenge is silence. I am placed in a room filled with the loudest noise imaginable—a roar of a thousand voices, static, and crashing waves. To pass, I must find the single note of quiet hidden within the chaos. I close my eyes, and suddenly, the noise isn't outside me; it's inside my chest. I breathe it out, and the room goes still. A door made of stained glass opens before me.

I step through into the final arena. There is no interviewer, only a large, blank mirror in the center of the room. A voice resonates from everywhere and nowhere at once. *"What is your dream job?"* it asks.

I open my mouth to answer, to recite the title I've rehearsed for years, but I catch my reflection. In the mirror, I am not wearing the suit I put on this morning. I am wearing clothes made of starlight, and I am holding a pen that writes in the air.

That’s when I realize the glass isn't reflecting the room; it's reflecting the truth. The competition wasn't to get a job. The competition was to realize I don't need one.

*"The prize is not a position,"* the voice says, softer now. *"The prize is the authority to create."*

I look at my hands. They are glowing. I understand then that I am not a candidate; I am the architect. I am dreaming.

The realization hits me like a warm wave. The corridor, the ocean, the clocks—they are all mine. I don't need to climb the stairs anymore. I simply will the room to change. The glass door shatters into butterflies, and the floor turns into grass. I am no longer running toward a goal; I am already standing in the place I wanted to be.

The edges of the dream begin to blur, the colors softening into the grey of dawn. The voice whispers one last time: *"Wake up."*

I open my eyes. The sunlight is hitting my alarm clock, and the sheets are warm. There is no job offer on my phone, but there is a notebook on my nightstand, open to a fresh page, waiting for the first line of a story that only I can write. I sit up, the feeling of the dream still humming in my fingertips, and I know that I have already won.
