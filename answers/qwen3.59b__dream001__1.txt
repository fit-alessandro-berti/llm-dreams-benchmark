You check your wristwatch, expecting to see the hands spinning backward or the face dissolving into liquid, but instead, the numbers remain stubbornly fixed at a time that feels like both tomorrow and yesterday. You pull out your phone, scanning contacts for a single name that might anchor you to reality, but the screen flickers, cycling through faces of strangers who all wear identical, familiar smiles. You realize there are no addresses on this city; instead, buildings have names like "Regret," "Echo," or "The Moment We Almost Spoke."

You begin to ask every passerby, but they don't speak with words; they repeat what you last thought of before waking up, their voices coming from your own throat. Panic begins to sharpen into clarity as you look down at the streets and notice that your footprints are disappearing behind you, leaving no trace. A realization strikes: you aren't trying to find a location; the meeting isn't an external event. You start to walk backward, retracing steps that haven't been taken, following the path of your own mind where the destination is the moment you decide to understand why you need to meet yourself in this place at all. The hourglass in the square above your head shatters, and a new door opens behind you, revealing not an exit, but an invitation to arrive exactly when you stop looking for how.
