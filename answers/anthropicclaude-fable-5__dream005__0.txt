You are at a large social gathering where you only know one person. As you try to find them, you encounter various groups of people and each group seems to already know you. They greet you by a name that isn't quite yours—close, but wrong in a way you can't pin down—and you nod along because correcting them feels impossible, like trying to speak underwater.

The rooms keep multiplying. You pass through a kitchen that opens into a ballroom that opens into what is unmistakably your childhood living room, except the furniture is wrong and the windows look out onto a sea that was never there. The guests don't seem to notice. They're holding drinks that never empty, laughing at jokes that end before the punchline.

You catch a glimpse of the person you know—just the back of their head, their unmistakable way of standing—across the room. But the room stretches as you walk. The faster you move, the more people fill the space between you, all of them turning to talk to you, asking questions about a job you don't remember having, congratulating you on something you can't recall doing.

"There you are," someone says, taking your elbow. You turn, relieved—but it's a stranger wearing your friend's smile. "We've been waiting for you to give the speech."

And suddenly there's a microphone in your hand, and the entire party has gone quiet, hundreds of faces turned toward you with patient expectation. You open your mouth, not knowing what you're meant to say—and somewhere in the crowd, finally, you spot your friend, the real one, watching you from near the door.

They raise their glass slightly, as if to say: *you've always known what to say. You just have to remember it.*

The microphone hums. The sea outside the windows rises. Everyone waits.

What do you say?
