The leaves around you flip from a soft whisper to a clattering drumroll. The air thickens, tasting of iron and crushed mint, and the shadows under the trees deepen as if night is falling upward. The birds don’t fly away—they simply stop, mid-breath, and the forest holds its lungs.  

A wind arrives that doesn’t feel like wind; it feels like the sky leaning down to speak. It carries the scent of ozone and old lightning, and the path ahead ripples as though it were water. Your footsteps grow heavier, not because of mud, but because gravity itself is flexing, preparing.  

Then the first drop hits—not on your skin, but inside your chest, a cold tap at the heart.  

The storm doesn’t break; it unfurls. Clouds boil through the canopy in bruised purples and greens, and for a heartbeat you see shapes in them—not faces, exactly, but intentions: curiosity, hunger, mercy. Thunder doesn’t rumble from above; it blooms from below, traveling up through the soles of your feet, humming in your teeth.  

And yet the forest does not flee.  

The trees lean in, bark darkening to mirror-black, and you realize they have been waiting. The storm is not attacking; it is remembering. Each fork of lightning stitches a memory into the air—old floods, newborn seeds, centuries of falling and rising. Rain crashes down, not in drops but in sheets of liquid time.  

You stand, neither resisting nor running, and as the wind lifts your hair, you feel your own edges blur. The storm reaches for you not to harm, but to recognize: here, in the heart of the wild weather, you are no longer walking through the dream.  

You are the path. You are the lightning. You are the forest holding its breath, then letting it go in a roar of green fire—awake, alive, and unbroken.
