...written in a language of shifting, liquid silver. At first, the symbols look like standard calculus or multiple-choice letters, but as you focus, they untangle and reassemble into concepts that have no business being on a test. 

**Question 1:** *Calculate the exact weight of a conversation you left unfinished three years ago. Show your work using only the colors of a fading bruise.*

You reach for your pencil, but the moment your fingers brush it, it dissolves into a handful of dry, fragile autumn leaves. You look around the room. The other students are no longer people, but silhouettes made of gently swaying reeds, their heads bowed over their desks, whispering in a language that sounds like wind through tall grass. 

The clock on the wall has no numbers. Instead, it features a single, slowly rotating eye that blinks with a soft, wet *snap* every time a minute passes. There is no panic, though. In dreams, the anxiety of the test is replaced by a strange, heavy calm. You realize this isn't an exam for a lawyer, or a doctor, or an engineer. The faded letterhead at the top of the page reads: *Department of Becoming.*

**Question 2:** *If you fold the horizon in half, where does the ocean keep its secrets? Map the coordinates.*

You don't know the answer, but in the logic of the dream, knowing isn't required; *feeling* is. You press your palm flat against the center of the exam paper. The silver ink responds instantly, rising from the page like warm, metallic vines. It wraps around your fingers, tracing the lines of your palm, reading your pulse, your memories, the quiet hopes you’ve never spoken aloud. The paper absorbs the rhythm of your heartbeat, translating it into swirling, intricate constellations of answers. 

**Question 3:** *Who are you when no one is watching?*

You open your mouth to speak the answer, but instead of words, a flock of bioluminescent moths flies out, filling the classroom with a soft, blue glow. The walls of the exam hall begin to dissolve, peeling away like wet paper to reveal a vast, starry sky. The desks float upward, turning into clouds. 

A gentle, resonant voice echoes not from the room, but from inside your own chest: *"You have shown your work. You have passed. Now, it is time to wake up."*

The blue light brightens, swallowing the paper, the leaves, and the sky, until all that is left is the slow, steady rhythm of your own breathing in the dark.
