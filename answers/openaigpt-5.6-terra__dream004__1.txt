When you touch the handle, the landscape pauses.

The shifting hills hold their breath. The sky folds itself into a quiet square. Even the distant, wandering shapes—triangles with bird wings, spirals that hum like violins—turn toward you.

You make one stroke across the canvas.

A door appears.

You paint another, and the door opens onto a memory: a room filled with rain, where every drop contains a tiny version of you, asleep and dreaming. You paint a gold circle above it, and it becomes a sun. The little versions of you wake up, look upward, and begin to climb out of the rain.

But as they reach the canvas, the colors around you begin to fade.

The world is waiting for you to decide what it will become.

So you paint a horizon—not straight, but gently breathing. You paint trees with windows in their trunks, rivers that flow upward into the clouds, and a moon shaped like an open eye. Finally, in the center of the canvas, you paint yourself holding the brush.

The painted you smiles.

Then steps out of the canvas.

It takes the brush from your hand and whispers, “You can wake up now. I’ll keep dreaming for you.”

And somewhere beyond the changing colors, you hear morning knocking softly at the door.
