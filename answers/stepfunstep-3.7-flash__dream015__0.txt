You decide to follow the faint, unplaceable tinkle of a music box no other scavenger you’ve passed in the last week seems to hear, even though every one of them has yelled after you to stick to the mapped routes, to avoid the stretch of cracked highway where the billboard shamblers herd at dusk.

The air tastes like burnt cinnamon and wet ash as you walk, your boots crunching over drifts of dried locust shells and crushed plastic packaging. You only have three things in your pockets: a half-squashed peppermint lip balm that still smells like the looted drugstore you raided three weeks ago, a single Flintstones vitamin stuck to the lining of your jeans, and a brass key you found wedged in the ribs of a rusted old bicycle that doesn’t open any of the locked doors you’ve tested so far. You don’t know why the music matters—you just know the grey, ash-dusted weight of this broken world lifts a little every time the tinkling swells.

The music gets louder when you step over patches of bioluminescent clover that sprouted out of flattened soda cans, and you swat at swarms of fireflies that used to be the lighters people left in their abandoned cars. The loping, half-melted shamblers stitched together from old road signs, fast food wrappers, and smashed cell phone shells skitter away from the sound, hissing like punctured radiators, so you don’t have to fumble for your chipped knife for once. You pass the ruins of your old elementary school, its playground slide half-buried in ash, and you swear you hear kids laughing for a split second, even though you haven’t seen another living person in two days.

The music leads you to the half-buried ferris wheel at the edge of town, the one that used to spin at the county fair every July. The top car is propped open, and inside sits a kid no older than eight, wearing a crown crinkled from a Hershey’s bar wrapper, winding a tiny porcelain music box painted with dancing bears. He looks up like he’s been waiting for you, and says, “Took you long enough. I’ve been playing it for three years, ever since the sky turned orange.”

You realize suddenly that you know the song: it’s the jingle from the ice cream truck that used to turn the corner of your block when you were seven. You’d forgotten the way the bell jingled, the way the inside of the truck smelled like vanilla and rainbow sprinkles, until this second. The brass key in your pocket is warm now, and when you pull it out, it’s not a key anymore—it’s a swirled red-and-white lollipop stick, just like the ones the ice cream lady used to give you for free when you had a good day at school.

The kid hands you the music box. “If you wind it all the way,” he says, “it doesn’t just play the song. It fixes things, a little.” You turn the knob, and the tinkling swells into that familiar, bouncy ice cream jingle. Suddenly the ash falling from the sky turns to cotton candy that melts on your tongue before it hits the ground. The shamblers down on the highway melt into puddles of cherry soda, the bioluminescent clover bursts into sunflowers that glow like soft paper lanterns, and the rusted ferris wheel creaks and starts to turn, slow and smooth, like it never broke at all.

The kid grins and holds out a paper cone he pulled out of nowhere, and you realize you can hear the sound of other people laughing in the distance, other survivors who must have heard the music too. For a split second, you see the drugstore you looted three weeks ago, intact, its neon “OPEN” sign buzzing, through the spokes of the ferris wheel. You know if you climb down, you can go find it, find the other people, build something that isn’t just surviving.

But then the music fades a little, and you feel the edge of your blanket under your fingers, the warm weight of your pillow against your cheek. The kid waves, holding up a peanut butter cup, and you know if you close your eyes tight enough, you can stay here a little longer, where the worst thing you have to worry about is whether the ice cream truck will have your favorite flavor when it comes around the corner.
