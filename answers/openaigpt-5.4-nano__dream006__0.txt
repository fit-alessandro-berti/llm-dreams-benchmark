…begin to forget the words.

At first it’s small—just a hiccup in your mind, like a single page stuck in a book you’ve opened too quickly. You lean into the microphone anyway, smiling at the familiar weight of attention. The spotlight warms your face. Somewhere in the back rows the lights flicker, and the crowd’s faces shift, not into monsters or strangers, but into something like mirrors—each person’s expression reflecting a version of you at a slightly different age, with a slightly different set of worries.

You try again. Your mouth moves, but what comes out isn’t your opening line. It’s a sentence you don’t recognize, spoken in your own voice with your own breath. The words are perfectly clear, yet they don’t fit the moment, like wearing a coat that belongs to someone else.

You glance at your notes.

There are no notes.

Instead, your hands hold a blank sheet of paper that slowly curls at the edges as if it’s made of warm air. The paper keeps “refilling” itself—dark lines appear, then blur, then rewrite themselves into other words. The ink won’t settle. Every time you focus, the sentence changes.

You decide to improvise.

That’s when you notice the microphone isn’t connected to anything. It hovers a few inches away from your mouth, on a thin thread of sound. When you speak, the thread tightens, vibrating like a string on an instrument. When you pause, it slackens and the audience leans forward as one body, hungry for the next vibration.

A thought arrives—simple, sharp, and somehow older than you: *Tell them what you’re trying not to say.*

You take a breath and feel the breath travel through you in a new way. The fear in your chest doesn’t vanish, but it reorganizes itself, becoming something usable. You look up and instead of reading faces, you listen. You can’t hear the room the way you normally would. You can feel it—like the crowd is a single organism holding its breath along with you.

Your speech begins to form out of that feeling.

You say:

“I thought you came here for answers. But what I have is a question I’ve been carrying around for years, and it finally grew heavy enough that I had to bring it to light.”

The words land without effort. They don’t echo strangely anymore. They settle. The spotlight seems steadier now, as if the stage has decided to cooperate.

As you continue, the crowd’s faces stop changing. The mirrors become faces again—real, individual, each person holding the kind of attention that says *I’m listening for you to understand yourself.*

You reach the part where you planned to speak about something—your topic, your theme, your prepared lesson.

Instead, you speak about what you’ve been avoiding.

You talk about the moment you made a choice you couldn’t undo, the apology you never delivered, the fear you called “being busy” so you wouldn’t have to look at it directly. You confess with the careful courage of someone who knows that honesty isn’t dramatic—it’s simply accurate.

And as you speak, you notice something extraordinary: the audience isn’t judging you.

They’re recognizing you.

Not in a flattering way. In a “yes, that’s true” way. Like each person has their own hidden sentence burning behind their teeth, and your courage gives it permission to surface.

Then, halfway through your confession, the microphone moves.

It pulls itself closer, like a shy animal stepping out of the dark. You lean forward without thinking. The thread of sound tightens again, but this time it doesn’t feel like pressure—it feels like connection.

A voice comes from the crowd—not one voice, but many, harmonizing in the rhythm of your own breath.

You realize they aren’t asking you to finish your speech.

They’re waiting for you to stop trying to impress them and start telling the truth.

So you do.

You stop pacing and you let your hands rest at your sides. You stop reaching for the “right” words and you let the words you mean come out the way they come.

When you finally look down again, your blank paper is gone. In its place is a single line, steady and dark, as if it has decided to be permanent:

*Speak until the fear has nowhere left to hide.*

The crowd applauds, not loudly, but in a slow, collective release—like a door opening after being stuck for years.

And then you wake up, still standing in the middle of the stage, still holding the microphone close enough to feel its cool metal against your lip, your heart pounding with the strange certainty that you were never really trying to give a speech at all.

You were practicing.
