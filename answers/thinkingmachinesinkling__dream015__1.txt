...plant a garden in the ruins of an old library.

The shelves are collapsed and the books are water-damaged, but the stone foundation holds. You clear the rubble not for shelter, but for soil—scavenging rainwater in cracked ceramic tiles, weaving vines through broken window frames where sunlight still cuts through the dust. You don’t hoard the first sprouts of tomatoes or bitter greens; instead, you leave them at the edge of the overgrown parking lot with a note made of torn paper and charcoal: *Take what you need. Add to it if you can.*

The dangers don’t vanish—there are still shadows moving between the burned-out cars, still nights when the wind sounds like something hungry—but you decide that scarcity is not a reason to become small. You begin to map the safe paths for others. You trade seeds for stories. Slowly, the library garden becomes a whisper of green in the gray, and strangers start leaving things back: a jar of preserved berries, a shoe repaired with wire, a song hummed into the silence.

In the dream, the apocalypse doesn’t end. But you decide that survival isn’t just about lasting longer—it’s about refusing to let the world forget what it felt like to grow something together. You wake with the taste of rain and earth still on your tongue, and the strange, stubborn feeling that hope is just another kind of resource—one that multiplies only when you give it away.
