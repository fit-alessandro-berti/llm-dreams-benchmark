You realize you need to **find water before the sun climbs any higher**. Your tongue feels thick, and the air smells of dust and pine resin.  

A narrow path of crushed quartz leads downhill between oaks whose bark is scarred with old axe marks. You follow it to a stream that is more memory than current—just a ribbon of wet stones—until you kneel and dig with your hands in a patch of ferns. The earth gives way to a seep. You cup it, drink, and let the cold shock your wrists.  

When you stand, your shadow is short and sharp. You notice a wisp of smoke rising beyond the ridge, and the wind carries the rhythm of something struck—wood on wood, deliberate, like a signal. You decide to follow it, not because you know what it is, but because you know what you are: a body that thirsts, that listens, that can carry a spark if it has to.  

By the time the ridge flattens into a clearing, the sound resolves into the ring of a heavy blade splitting billets. A woman with a burn across her forearm pauses, leans on the axe, and nods as if she expected you. She gestures to a stack of wood taller than you, then to the sky, where storm clouds are bruising into purple.  

She does not ask your name. She hands you a wedge and a maul.  

You do not ask where you are.  

You swing. The wood opens like a door.
