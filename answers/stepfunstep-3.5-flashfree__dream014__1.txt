The city’s roar fades to a hum as they gather around you, not pointing at streets, but asking about where you *feel* you’re supposed to be.

“It’s not about a place on a map,” says a quiet man in a tweed cap, handing you a steaming paper cup of chai. “It’s about the feeling you’re searching for. Does it feel like a memory? Or a hope?”

As you sip the sweet, spicy warmth, the neon signs above seem to soften, their colors blending into a dawn-like hues. The strangers don’t lead you by the arm; they walk beside you, their conversation a gentle counterpoint to the city’s chaos. You notice details you’d missed before—a hidden courtyard with a fountain, a mural that looks exactly like a forgotten childhood drawing, the way the morning light catches the dust in a bookstore window.

You realize you haven’t spoken your destination aloud. You don’t need to. With each step, the weight of being lost lifts, replaced by the quiet understanding that you were never truly lost—just waiting to be found by the unexpected kindness of a city that, for this one glowing walk, feels like home. The group eventually stops at a crossroads, smiling. “This is where our paths diverge,” the woman says. “But you’ll know your way now.”

And you do. The streets ahead are clear, bathed in golden light. You turn to thank them, but they’ve already melted into the morning crowd, leaving only the lingering warmth of the cup in your hand and the profound, unshakable sense that you are, and have always been, exactly where you need to be.
