You start to run.

At first the streets seem ordinary—wide avenues slick with rain, buses sighing at the curb, people moving with the certainty of those who know exactly where they’re going. But the farther you hurry, the less the city agrees with itself. Street signs change when you look away. Buildings lean together like old friends sharing secrets. A clock above a bakery says you have fifty-seven minutes, then twelve, then simply: **SOON**.

You stop a woman carrying a blue umbrella.

“Excuse me,” you say, “do you know where my meeting is?”

She studies your face as if checking whether you’re joking. “Of course,” she says. “You’re already late.”

Then she points upward.

Above the rooftops, impossibly high, a train glides between towers on rails made of light. Its windows glow gold. Somehow you know you need to be on it.

You follow a staircase that wasn’t there a moment ago, climbing between buildings, past apartment windows where strangers sit at dining tables set for breakfast, past a laundromat full of spinning galaxies, past a tiny park where children are flying kites shaped like keys.

At the top, breathless, you reach a platform suspended in the air. A train arrives without sound. The doors open.

Inside, every passenger looks familiar, though you can’t place a single name. They all glance up as you enter, then return to reading newspapers filled entirely with photographs of your own childhood.

You take the only empty seat.

An announcement crackles overhead: “Next stop: The Place You Forgot.”

You search your pockets for an invitation, an address, anything. Instead you find a small envelope with your name written in handwriting you haven’t seen in years. Inside is a blank card except for one sentence:

**The meeting is with yourself.**

The train passes through a tunnel, and for a moment everything goes dark.

When the light returns, you are standing before a door in the side of a building that reaches into the clouds. There is no handle, only a keyhole shaped like an eye. You lean closer.

From the other side, someone knocks.

Once.

Twice.

Then a voice, unmistakably your own, says, “Are you ready?”
