You find yourself in a post-apocalyptic world, with resources scarce and dangers everywhere. Despite the hardships, you decide to plant a garden.

Not where anyone would expect — not in the cracked concrete of a ruined city park, nor in the shadow of a hollowed-out skyscraper. No, you choose the heart of the old botanical conservatory, its glass dome shattered by time and storms, now patched with salvaged solar panels and rain-collecting tarps. The air smells of damp earth, mildew, and something else — something sweet, stubborn, alive.

You dig with your bare hands, fists raw and bleeding, though you don’t notice. You’ve stolen seed packets from abandoned labs — heirloom tomatoes, moonflowers, chard that used to be called “Swiss,” but now is just… *green*. You whisper names to them as you plant: *Papa’s Red Heirloom. Grandma’s Sunbeam Spinach. The one that survived the Dust Bowl.*

The night you plant the last seed, a wolf-dog with one ear torn and eyes like molten copper creeps from the rubble. She doesn’t growl. Doesn’t snarl. Just watches. You offer her a scrap of dried apricot — the last you have. She licks your palm. Lingers.

Days pass. Rain comes. Then sun. Then a miracle — a single green shoot pushes through the soil near the trellis.

You sit with your back against the broken glass, watching the dawn paint the leaves gold.

On the fifth day, the wolf-dog brings a child — no older than six, ragged and silent, her hair full of ash. She doesn’t speak. Doesn’t cry. Just stares at the plant.

You kneel. Hold out a tiny tomato, still green, no bigger than a thumbnail.

The child reaches. Touches it.

And for the first time in years — perhaps ever — she smiles.

Somewhere beyond the ruined city, sirens wail. Raiders scout the ruins. The sky is still gray.

But here, now — beneath the broken dome — something is growing.

And that, you realize, is how the world heals.

You take the child’s hand.

“Come on,” you whisper. “Let’s plant another one.”
