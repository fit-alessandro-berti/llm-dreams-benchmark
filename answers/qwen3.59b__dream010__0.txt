To advance, you must pass through corridors lined with mirrors that show you not who you are, but who you could be if you gave up—versions of yourself sitting on piles of gray ash or holding hands with strangers in the rain. You have to choose whether to walk toward the light at the end or stay where your skills feel safe and warm.

There is a moment where all your previous achievements weigh down like anchors, and the judges ask you to drop them one by one before they can grant you passage to the final room. When you let go of your portfolio, your past accolades dissolve into golden dust, but you feel lighter, moving through the air as if you are floating rather than walking.

The grand prize reveals itself not as a title carved in stone, or even a paycheck deposited in a bank, but as a simple tool: a brush, a keyboard, a microphone—whatever it is that allows you to create without apology for anyone else's opinion. You sit at the desk of mastery and realize something crucial: the competition was never about winning a position; it was an exercise in shedding the fear that defines you by what others expect of your work.

Finally, the doors open, but they swing into a quiet garden where time stands still. There is no trophy case there. There is only a single chair at the center, and on that chair rests a book titled *Work That Matters*. You understand now that the dream wasn't about securing an external role; it was about finding the courage to start work in the first place.

You turn back to the waking world of your own reality, where you pick up a pen or log in to a computer, and suddenly, doing the work feels as natural as breathing. The job was never really away from home.
