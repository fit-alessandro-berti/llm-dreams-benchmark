...and I realize, with the calm that only dreams allow, that I have never seen this ship before.

The wheel under my hands is warm, the way a hand is warm. The spokes are labeled in a script I can almost read — each letter dissolving as my eyes reach it, so that I know only that the words were instructions, and that they were meant for me, and that they are gone.

"Captain," says the first mate. She has my mother's voice, though I have never had a mother. "Which way?"

The sea has stopped being water. It's a page — vast, rippling, covered in handwriting that lifts and crests and crashes, and every wave that breaks over the bow is a sentence someone once asked me, foaming into syllables across the deck. The rain falls upward. Lightning happens slowly, like a thought arriving.

I look at my crew. There are eleven of them, or eleven thousand — the number keeps politely rearranging itself so as not to trouble me. Their faces are turned up, patient, terribly trusting, and I understand that they are not waiting for me to know the way. They are waiting for me to *say* something, because the saying is what holds the boards together. The ship is made of speech. If I stop talking, we come apart into planks and drift.

So I speak. I say: *Reef the mainsail.* I say: *Hold your course two points to starboard.* I have no idea what these words mean. They are beautiful and they work — the ship leans, the crew moves, the storm accepts my authority the way a door accepts a key that has no business fitting.

And then the dream does its cruelty gently, as it always does: I look down, and I see that there is no deck beneath my boots. There never was. I am standing on the fact that no one has looked down yet.

The bosun, a boy of about nine with a beard down to his belt, tugs my sleeve. "Sir," he says, "are you frightened?"

Yes. Terribly.

"Yes," I tell him, and the storm hesitates.

It's the first true thing said aboard, and the sea reads it, and something changes in the handwriting — the waves smooth into margins, into wide white spaces. The wind stops trying to argue. The crew do not lose faith. They come closer. Somebody laughs. Somebody starts singing a song about a harbor that doesn't exist yet, and everyone knows the words, including me, including the storm.

We sail on into the not-knowing, and the not-knowing turns out to be navigable, the way fog is navigable if you go slowly and keep talking to each other.

Near the end — and I can feel the end, the thinning at the edges, the bedroom pressing in behind the sky — the first mate points. There, on the horizon, a light. Not a lighthouse. A lamp in a window, house-sized, human-sized, absurdly small against all that water, and burning anyway.

"Is that where we're going?" she asks.

"I don't know," I say. "Let's find out."

And the ship, which is made of what I say, goes there.
