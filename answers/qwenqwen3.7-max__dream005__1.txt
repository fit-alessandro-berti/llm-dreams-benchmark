…and you realize that the geometry of the house is slowly shifting. 

The first group you pass is gathered around a grand piano, but no one is playing. Instead, they are intensely debating the "color of Tuesday," and as you walk by, a woman in a velvet dress hands you a perfectly ripe peach that feels as heavy as a bowling ball. You nod politely and keep moving.

You head toward the kitchen, assuming your friend would be near the food, but the hallway stretches impossibly long. The framed photographs on the walls begin to change as you pass them. At first, they are standard family portraits, but soon the people in the frames turn their heads to watch you walk by. One man in a sepia-toned photo taps on the glass from the inside, pointing urgently toward a door you hadn't noticed before.

The ambient noise of the party—the clinking glasses, the dull roar of chatter, the muffled bass of the music—begins to sound like the rushing of water. 

You push through a set of swinging doors and suddenly, you aren't in a kitchen. You are standing on a vast, twilight-lit beach. The sand beneath your shoes is cool and dark, and the sky above is a deep, bruised violet, scattered with stars that pulse in time with your heartbeat. The party is still happening, but now the guests are scattered across the dunes, holding glowing paper lanterns, speaking in hushed, reverent whispers.

And there, standing at the very edge of the black, glass-like ocean, is your friend. 

Relief washes over you. You jog across the sand, calling out their name. They turn around, smiling, but as you get closer, you notice they are holding an antique pocket watch. The ticking is deafeningly loud, echoing across the beach. 

"I've been looking everywhere for you," you say, breathless.

Your friend looks down at the watch, then back up at you. Their eyes are kind, but entirely silver, reflecting the violet sky. "I know," they say, their voice sounding like it's coming from underwater. "But you’re looking for me in the wrong house. You left the front door open in the real world, and the cold is getting in."

You look down at your hands. The heavy peach you were given earlier is now a snow globe, and inside it, a tiny version of your bedroom is shaking violently. The ticking of the watch speeds up, merging with a rhythmic, electronic beeping. 

Your friend reaches out and gently touches your forehead. "Wake up," they whisper. "Close the window."

The violet sky shatters like thin ice. The sound of the ocean dissolves into the harsh, familiar blare of your alarm clock. You gasp, your eyes snapping open to the pale gray light of your bedroom ceiling. The room is freezing, and as you sit up, shivering, you realize the bedroom window is, in fact, wide open to the crisp morning air. 

You get out of bed to close it, the feeling of the heavy peach and the sound of the ticking watch lingering in your mind just long enough to fade into the waking world.
