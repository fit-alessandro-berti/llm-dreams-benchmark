You start to run, but the pavement softens into sand, and the skyscrapers lean away from you like trees in a wind you can’t feel. You flag down strangers for directions, yet every face is either blank, familiar from a life you haven’t lived, or a mirror reflecting your own widening eyes. They hand you maps drawn in water—streets that blur and vanish the moment you try to follow them. Your watch spins backward; the hour collapses into seconds, then stretches into an eternity of golden, unmoving light.

Just as panic crests in your throat, you notice a door hanging in the air three feet above the sidewalk—no handle, no frame, just an outline of blue. You reach up and push through. On the other side, the city is folded like a paper crane, silent and small in your palm. You sit down at a table that wasn’t there a moment before, and the meeting begins—not with strangers, but with every version of yourself you’ve ever been, nodding in unison. You realize, with a strange, floating calm, that you were never late. In this city, being lost was the only map that could have led you here, and the important thing wasn’t the destination—it was remembering, finally, how to arrive without needing to know the way.
