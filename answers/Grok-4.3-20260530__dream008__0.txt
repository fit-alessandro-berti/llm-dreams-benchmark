You are at your workplace, and suddenly, you are given a project with an impossible deadline. Your boss is waiting for you to start, and you feel the familiar knot in your stomach tighten—until something shifts.  

The fluorescent lights overhead flicker and bloom into soft, glowing orbs that drift like fireflies. Your desk stretches and warps, becoming a vast wooden raft floating on a sea of swirling equations that ripple like water. Your boss opens his mouth to speak, but instead of words, colorful butterflies pour out, each one carrying a tiny fragment of the project specs.  

You stand up. Your feet leave the ground. You’re weightless now, gliding through the air as the office dissolves into an endless library of floating books whose pages turn themselves. The deadline clock on the wall melts into a puddle of liquid gold that reforms into a compass pointing straight at “Done.”  

You reach out and pluck one of the butterflies from the air. It unfolds into a holographic blueprint that rearranges itself into the perfect solution—elegant, impossible, beautiful. Your fingers move faster than thought, weaving code and starlight together. The entire team (now a chorus of friendly nebulae) cheers in harmonic frequencies that feel like laughter.  

Just as the last piece clicks into place, your boss—now wearing a crown made of clock gears—gives you a slow, approving nod. The golden compass spins wildly, then explodes into a shower of sparkling confetti that tastes like fresh coffee and victory.  

You open your eyes.  

The real world is quiet. Your actual desk is still there. The real deadline is still looming. But something lingers from the dream: a strange, electric certainty that the impossible is only impossible until you start flying anyway.  

You smile, open your laptop, and begin.
