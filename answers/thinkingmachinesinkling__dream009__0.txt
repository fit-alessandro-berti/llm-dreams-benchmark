...find the one thing that still connects you to the world you left behind.

You wander through narrow, mud-brick streets that smell of woodsmoke, fresh bread, and salt—perhaps an ancient river valley, or a port city of wooden ships and linen sails. The people wear woven robes and speak in rhythms your ear almost recognizes, yet the words slide past like water. You reach for your phone, your keys, your identity—nothing is there. Only a heavy iron key in your pocket, cold against your palm, that fits no door you can see.

As you explore a bustling marketplace of spices and clay tablets, you realize survival isn't the problem; the era has its own logic, its own food and shelter. The problem is that you are fading at the edges, like ink left in rain. The buildings, the faces, even the massive red sun hanging low in the sky seem arranged like a sentence written in a language you once knew but have forgotten.

You don't need to escape. You need to **remember why you were brought here**—to read the city itself, to find the one anachronism hidden in plain sight (a glass bottle, a melody, a name carved in stone) before the dream swallows your modern self completely, and you wake up not in your bed, but as someone who never left this time at all.
