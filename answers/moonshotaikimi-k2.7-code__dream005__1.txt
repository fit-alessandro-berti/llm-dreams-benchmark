…you encounter various groups of people and each cluster seems suspended in its own pocket of warmth, laughter rising and falling like distant tides. You weave through them, apologizing for shoulders you never quite brush, and ask if anyone has seen the one you know. No one answers directly. Instead, a woman in a silver dress hands you a glass of something that tastes like childhood summers and says, “They were here when the clocks were still blue.”

You move on. The rooms keep shifting. One doorway leads to a library where the books whisper titles you almost recognize; another opens onto a balcony under a sky that is somehow both night and dawn. On the balcony, a circle of guests is playing a card game with photographs instead of playing cards. They look up as one and gesture toward a spiral staircase that wasn’t there a moment ago.

You climb. The steps are soft, like piano keys, and each one hums a note that matches your heartbeat. Halfway up, you pass a mirror and see yourself wearing a coat you’ve never owned, carrying a key that glows faintly at its teeth. You don’t stop to wonder why; in dreams, you rarely do.

At the top, the music changes. It is the song your friend used to hum while making coffee. You follow it down a hallway lined with doors, each one slightly ajar, each room lit by a different decade of light. You catch glimpses of familiar strangers—faces that look like people you once knew, rearranged just enough to feel like memories rather than people.

Then, finally, you turn a corner and there they are, standing by a window that overlooks an ocean made of streetlamps. They smile as if no time has passed, as if you didn’t just search through a maze of rooms and years to reach them.

“I knew you’d find me,” they say, and hand you a cup of coffee.

You take it. The steam smells like waking up. Outside, the streetlamp waves begin to shimmer, and you realize the gathering has gone quiet, the whole house holding its breath. Your friend tilts their head.

“Are you ready?” they ask.

You open your mouth to answer—and the coffee cup slips through your fingers, dissolving into light before it hits the floor. The room tilts. The music swells. For a moment, you are falling upward, still looking into their eyes, still trying to say yes.

Then the alarm begins to ring, soft at first, like someone knocking from very far away.
