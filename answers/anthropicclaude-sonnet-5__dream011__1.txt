The clock on the wall has too many hands, all moving at different speeds, and you can't tell if you're early or catastrophically late. Somewhere behind you, a door you don't remember walking through clicks shut.

You look down at your own hands to ground yourself—a trick you half-remember from somewhere—and count your fingers. The number keeps changing. Six, then eleven, then four again.

A proctor drifts between the rows of desks, and when you try to catch their face, there isn't one, just a suggestion of features that rearranges when you focus on it. Somewhere in the room, someone is quietly humming a song you almost recognize, and it makes your name sound less certain in your own head.

You pick up your pen. It's heavier than it should be, and when you touch it to the paper, instead of writing symbols, it starts writing *actual words*—not answers, just a single sentence, over and over:

*"You already know how this ends."*

You look up. Everyone else in the room has turned to look at you now, all at once, all with that same faceless patience.

The exam, you realize, was never the point.
