You start to walk, and the pavement beneath your feet feels like it’s breathing—slightly soft, warm, humming with the rhythm of a thousand footsteps that aren’t yours. The buildings around you are impossibly tall, their windows flickering with scenes from lives you almost recognize: a woman stirring tea, a child pressing a palm against the glass, a room full of clocks all showing different times.

You check your pockets for the address, but instead of a phone or a scrap of paper, your fingers close around a brass key, a dried flower, and a ticket printed in a language that keeps rearranging itself when you look away. The clock on a nearby tower strikes a note that tastes like copper, and you remember—with the sudden certainty of dreams—that the meeting isn’t in a building at all.

It’s underground. Or overhead. Or both.

A train rumbles past on tracks that loop through the air like ribbon, and you leap onto the back of a passing trolley without thinking, your body lighter than it should be. The city unspools below you: canals of silver light, streets that fold back on themselves, a cathedral made entirely of doors. Somewhere a phone rings, and you know it’s for you, but the sound is coming from inside a stranger’s briefcase, from a bird’s beak, from the marrow of your own bones.

“You’re almost there,” says the person beside you on the trolley, though when you turn, they have your face, or your mother’s, or no face at all. They hand you a map that’s blank except for one word, written in your handwriting: *Trust.*

The trolley slows. You step off onto a platform that seems to be made of the sound of rain. Ahead of you, a single door stands in the middle of an empty square. You don’t know what’s on the other side—whether it’s the meeting, or the reason you were rushing, or something older and stranger than both. But your hand is already reaching for the knob.

And just as it turns, the city holds its breath.
