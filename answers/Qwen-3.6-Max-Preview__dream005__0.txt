The room itself feels alive. The chandeliers above aren’t hanging from the ceiling; they are growing downward like inverted roots, dripping light instead of sap. Every time someone speaks, their words manifest as small, glowing moths that flutter up into the darkness, leaving trails of silver dust in the air.

You push through a crowd of people who are all wearing masks made of old book pages. They part for you silently, their paper faces rustling like dry leaves. You feel a sudden, sharp pull in your chest—a compass needle spinning wildly—and you know, with dream-logic certainty, that the one person you know is near.

You spot them standing by a window that doesn’t look out onto a street, but onto a vast, starless ocean. They are holding a glass of liquid that swirls with colors not found in nature. When they see you, they don’t smile. Instead, they raise the glass in a toast, and as the liquid touches their lips, the entire room dissolves into sand, slipping through your fingers until you are standing alone on a beach, the sound of the party replaced by the rhythmic crashing of waves that whisper your name.
