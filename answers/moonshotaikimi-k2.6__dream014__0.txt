Together, you begin to walk, but the city begins to breathe. The skyscrapers inhale and exhale, their glass facades rippling like water. The crosswalk lines peel up from the asphalt and twist into ribbons of light that float ahead of you, not showing a route, but humming a melody you suddenly remember from childhood. The strangers link arms with you, and where their skin touches yours, you sprout feathers, or perhaps roots—you can’t quite tell, and in the dream, it doesn’t matter.

The crowd doesn’t part so much as dissolve into fireflies. The honking taxis become a chorus of deep, soothing bells. The strangers lead you to a fountain that wasn’t there a moment ago, and as you lean to drink, you see your reflection not as one face, but as a constellation, a network of every person who has ever helped you find your way. You realize, with the soft certainty of dreaming, that the bustling city was never a maze—it was a garden, and every stranger was a bloom waiting to recognize you as one of its own.

You look up to thank them, but they have already become the architecture: one is the blue neon of a diner sign, another the steady rhythm of a revolving door, a third the quiet shelter of an awning during rain. You are alone again, but the loneliness has been replaced by a vast, breathing companionship. You step forward, and the pavement rises to meet your foot like a familiar hand. You know exactly where you are going, even though the name of the place hasn’t reached you yet. You wake with the echo of bells in your chest, certain, for just a moment, that the city is still holding you.
