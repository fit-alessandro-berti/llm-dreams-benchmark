You dip your fingers into the air, and the canvas shivers like the surface of a still pond. Where you touch, color blooms—not one color, but all of them at once, shifting from deep violet to molten gold to a green that has no name in the waking world. The shapes around you slow their dance, leaning in to watch.

You realize the canvas is not blank; it is waiting. It is asking.

So you begin to paint without intention. A sweep of your hand sends spirals of cobalt unfurling into feathers. Dots of crimson scatter and become eyes, then windows, then distant suns. The lines you draw lift off the surface, bending into staircases that climb into the sky and then loop back into themselves like thoughts half-remembered.

As you paint, the landscape around you begins to mirror the canvas. Mountains melt into the soft curves you sketched. Rivers flow backward into the shapes you once imagined were mistakes. Even your own shadow separates from your feet, steps onto the canvas, and starts painting *you*—a softer version, made of silver thread and starlight.

You understand, in the way one understands dreams, that this canvas is the border between waking and sleeping. Every stroke is a choice to stay a little longer, to let the world keep changing.

And so you paint a door. It opens not outward, but inward—into a room filled with quiet light, where someone who feels like an old friend is waiting with tea and a story about the time the moon learned to hum.

You step through.

The canvas, now full, sighs behind you, and the colors settle into something like a smile.
