Instead, you follow the distant, honeyed trill of a wren, a bird you haven’t spotted since the ash choked the skies five years ago, when the final crop failures sparked the last widespread riots.

The dust sticks thick to your sweat-slick neck as you wind through skeletons of strip malls, past a billboard advertising a pre-collapse fast-food chain that’s now little more than rusted metal peeling paint. The trill grows louder, and you round a corner to find a small clearing carpeted in soft green moss, where a single wren hops atop a cracked clay birdbath crusted with mineral deposits. Tucked behind the birdbath is a beat-up metal lunchbox, its latch rusted shut but peeking open to reveal handfuls of plump heirloom carrot seeds and a crumpled note scrawled in looping handwriting: “For anyone who still cares to grow.”

Deeper into the clearing, you spot a makeshift greenhouse strung up between two fallen oak trees, its plastic skin patched with scraps of old bed sheets. When you push aside the tattered canvas flap, the air inside wraps around you like a warm blanket, smelling like damp soil and sun-ripened strawberries. A woman with silver hair tied back in a braid stitched with glowing fireflies—something that shouldn’t be possible in the ash-choked wasteland, but feels completely natural in your dream—turns from a row of potted basil, holding a cloth-wrapped trowel.

“I’ve been hiding this spot for two years,” she says, without even asking who you are. “Most folks just dig for ammo or canned soup. But… what’s the use of surviving the end if you don’t get to taste something that tastes like hope, right?”

She hands you a plump, crimson strawberry, and when you bite into it, the juice bursts across your tongue so vividly you almost forget the acrid tang of burnt asphalt clinging to your clothes. Over the next hour, she teaches you how to plant the carrot seeds in small paper cups filled with compost salvaged from an abandoned community garden, and when the sun starts to dip below the ruined skyline, a flock of wrens descends on the clearing, perching on the greenhouse frames and chirping like they’re celebrating the small harvest.

Just as you open your mouth to ask how she’s stayed hidden so long without being found, you hear the sharp, familiar beep of your alarm clock. You blink awake, your hand curled around a single dried carrot seed you’d tucked into your jeans pocket that morning while planting your windowsill herb garden, and the soft trill of a real wren filters through your open bedroom window.
