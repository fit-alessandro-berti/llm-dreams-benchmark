Your heart does that weird dream-logic stutter where it’s racing but also oddly calm. The microphone feels too warm in your hand, like it’s been waiting for you. 

Then someone in the front row nods — not impatient, just curious — and it clicks: this isn’t a test. It’s a conversation you just haven’t had yet.

You take a breath. The words aren’t planned, but they show up anyway:

“I had this whole thing prepared, I swear. Bullet points, a clever opening, maybe a joke about how nervous I am. But standing here, looking at all of you... none of that feels like what matters.”

The room shifts. People lean in a fraction. The dream-physics of stages means your voice carries without you having to raise it.

“So let me try this instead. I think we all know what it’s like to show up somewhere and feel like we’re supposed to have it figured out. To have the right answers, the right version of ourselves. But the weird thing is, the moments that actually stick with us — the ones we remember years later — they’re almost never the rehearsed ones.”

A few people smile. Someone in the back mouths “true.” The lights don’t feel so harsh anymore.

“You didn’t come here for my bullet points. I didn’t come here to recite them. Maybe we both just came here to remember that it’s okay to start with ‘I don’t know’ and see where it goes.”

The silence after that isn’t empty. It’s the good kind — the kind where everyone’s thinking the same thing at the same time. And in dream logic, that’s when you know the speech is working.

Then the dream does what dreams do: the stage stretches a little, the audience blurs into something warmer, and you get the sense that whatever you say next, it’ll land exactly how it needs to.
