You frantically pat your pockets for your phone to check the location. Your fingers close around it, but when you pull it out, it isn’t a phone at all—it’s a smooth, heavy stone covered in moss. You throw it away in frustration, and it shatters into a flock of starlings that scatter into the smoggy sky.

Panic rises in your throat, tasting like copper. You spot a clock tower in the distance, but its face has no hands; instead, the numbers are falling off one by one like dominoes, crashing to the street below. You realize you don't even know *who* you are meeting, only that if you miss this, something terrible will happen—or perhaps something wonderful will never begin.

You flag down a taxi. It pulls up silently—a sleek, black vehicle with a driver whose face is obscured by shadow. You yank the door open and gasp the address, though you realize you’re speaking in a language you’ve never learned. The driver nods solemnly and hits the meter. The fare display doesn't show money; it displays your remaining memories, rapidly counting down.

The car lurches forward, but the streets have rearranged themselves. The grid of the city has become a maze of Escher-like staircases looping into the clouds. You pass landmarks that shouldn't exist together: the Eiffel Tower melting next to your childhood home, a subway entrance that opens directly onto the surface of the moon.

"We're here," the driver intones, his voice sounding exactly like your own.

The car stops. You stumble out into a vast, empty plaza. In the center stands a single, ordinary door frame, standing unsupported in the open air. Through the frame, you can see the conference room: a long mahogany table, stern faces, and an empty chair waiting for you. It is ten feet away.

You try to walk toward it, but you suddenly realize you aren't wearing shoes, and the ground is made of broken glass. No—wait—the ground is made of water, and you are sinking. You thrash, screaming that you’re here, you made it!

But as you sink, the door frame begins to shrink, receding into the distance until it is the size of a matchbox, then a speck of dust, swallowed by the gray fog of the waking world.
