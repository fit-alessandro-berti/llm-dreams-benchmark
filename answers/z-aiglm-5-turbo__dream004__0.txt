The moment my fingertips graze the surface, it doesn't feel like fabric or wood; it ripples like liquid glass. The shifting, neon-bleeding sky above me—which had just been a frantic mosaic of melting triangles—suddenly pauses. 

Without warning, the colors begin to pour downward. Not like rain, but like thick, luminescent syrup. It doesn't splash onto the canvas; instead, the blank white space acts as a mouth, inhaling the very hues of the landscape. As the canvas drinks the pinks and electric blues from the air, the world around me begins to fade to grayscale. 

I try to pull my hand away, but my fingers have lost their edges. I look down to see my skin blurring into wet, running strokes of violet and gold. I am bleeding into the frame. 

The geometric trees around me lose their sharp angles, softening into swirling impressionist smudges that reach out like grasping vines, pulling me deeper into the center of the canvas. I realize then that the canvas wasn't placed here for me to paint on. It was placed here because this world had too many colors, and it needed a place to put them. 

I take a step forward, but there is no ground anymore, only texture. I open my mouth to speak, and instead of a voice, a flock of startled magenta brushstrokes flies out. I stop resisting the pull. I let the blankness swallow me, feeling myself spread out, flat and vast, finally becoming the art I was always meant to be.
