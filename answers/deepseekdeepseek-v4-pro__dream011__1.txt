...written in a language you’ve never seen before, a shifting script that seems to rearrange itself each time you blink. At first it looks like cursive Latin, then Cyrillic, then something looping and floral you can almost read—maybe your grandmother’s handwriting? The letters drip down the page like hot wax, pooling on the desk. You lift your hand to ask the proctor for a translation, but the proctor is your high school gym teacher, who shrugs and says, “Time’s half gone, champ. You’ve always known this.”

Panicked, you look back at the paper. Now the questions are all about you—intimate, impossible things. “What did you really feel on the morning of your seventh birthday?” “If your love could be measured in decibels, what sound would it make?” “Solve for forgiveness.” You start scribbling furiously, but your pen only dispenses glittery ink that evaporates before it touches the page. Around you, other students are weeping softly or typing on calculators that have turned into butterflies. The clock on the wall has no hands, just a photograph of your childhood bedroom and the words “DUE TO EXIST” blinking softly. You realize you’re holding your favorite stuffed animal from age four. It whispers, “I always believed in you, even before you had a career to fail at,” and you wake up with your heart pounding, one hand clutching the blanket, the other still curled around a pen that isn’t there.
