The first puzzle is a broken music box. Inside, a tiny ball bearing rolls along a path etched with your childhood laughter. To solve it, you must replay the memory aloud—your voice, shaky, admitting you were jealous when your sister won the science fair. As you speak, the music box chimes clear and sweet. The wall melts into mist.

The second puzzle is a door with nine locks, each requiring a question you’ve never dared to ask yourself:  
*“Why do you punish yourself for being human?”*  
*“What are you running from when you pretend to be busy?”*  
Each answer you whisper opens a lock with a sigh—the sound of your breath, finally released.

By the time you reach the center, the air hums with silence. The final puzzle is a mirror—not reflecting your face, but your shadow… alive, speaking in your voice, but gentler.

“*You’ve solved every puzzle,*” it says. “*But you still think you need to escape.*”

You realize—you aren’t trying to get out.

You’re trying to remember why you came in.

The maze wasn’t a prison.

It was an invitation.

You step forward—and the mirror dissolves. The walls open into a field beneath a sky stitched with constellations shaped like your favorite words: *kindness, courage, enough.*

And there, sitting cross-legged on the grass, is the version of you you’ve been trying to become.

She doesn’t say anything.

Just smiles.

And offers you tea.

You sit down.

And for the first time in years—

you don’t solve anything.

You just breathe.

—

And then you wake up.

...but your pillow smells like chamomile.

And your hand is clenched around a tiny, folded paper crane.  

You don’t remember making it.
