
You wake up on a hard, straw-filled pallet in a dimly lit room. The air smells of woodsmoke, animals, and something earthy—damp stone and old rushes. There are no electric lights, no hum of appliances, no glow of screens. Through a small, glassless window, you hear the distant clang of a blacksmith's hammer and the lowing of cattle.

As you push yourself up, your modern clothes—a t-shirt and jeans—feel absurdly out of place. Your heart hammers against your ribs. *Where am I? When am I?*

You stumble outside into a narrow, muddy street lined with half-timbered houses. People in rough-spun woolen tunics and leather boots hurry past, glancing at you with open curiosity or suspicion. A cart rattles by, pulled by a single, shaggy horse. No cars. No paved roads. The sky is vast and unfiltered by city lights.

Your stomach growls. Your throat is parched. You realize, with a jolt of primal urgency, that you need **to communicate, to find food and water, and to understand the rules of this world before someone decides you are a witch, a spy, or simply mad.**

You fumble in your pocket and find a single, familiar object: your old keycard from work. It’s useless here. But then your fingers close around something else—a smooth, cold coin from your pocket change. A quarter. Its stamped eagle and state motto might as well be hieroglyphs.

A woman with a basket of eggs eyes you. You take a breath, point to her basket, then to your mouth, and mime eating. She frowns, then slowly offers you an egg. It’s a start. But you know this is just the first, smallest step.

You need to learn the language. You need to find shelter before nightfall. And you need to figure out if you’re in the past... or if this is some other kind of impossible place entirely.

What do you do first?
