You are at your workplace, and suddenly, you are given a project with an impossible deadline. Your boss is waiting for you to start, and you fumble for the edge of your desk, legs tangling in the desk chair’s rolling wheels like they’ve been bound with twine. When you finally yank open your company laptop, the screen blinks to life not with the requested fresh brand brief for your firm’s biggest client, but a loop of your 7th birthday cake—chocolate, frosting smudged at the corners, shaped like a retro gaming controller.

Your boss, whose charcoal-gray suit has melted into a patchwork of office supply stickers, taps a crumpled Post-it note on your keyboard. The words shift before your eyes: first “DELIVER BY 3PM” then “DELIVER BY 5 MINUTES AGO” before fading entirely. You open your mouth to beg for an extension, but all that spills out is the high-pitched trill of the breakroom’s microwave beep, which somehow doesn’t trigger the building’s fire alarms.

When you glance down, your arms have turned into stacks of printed quarterly reports, each page covered in numbers that swim like tiny minnows. A coworker glides past your cubicle—their head a ceramic coffee mug half-full of lukewarm chamomile tea, lanyard strung with paper clips—and tosses you a sticky note that sticks fast to your report-arm. Peeling it off, you find your own handwriting scrawled: “Hide in the supply closet.” You don’t remember writing it, but your feet carry you toward the hallway before you can overthink it.

Yanking open the supply closet door, you don’t find mops and toilet paper—instead, it’s a treehouse strung with fairy lights identical to your office’s overhead LEDs, built in the gnarled oak tree outside your childhood home. Your 7-year-old self is cross-legged on the floor, holding the exact Megacorp brand kit you were supposed to deliver, grinning with frosting stuck to their cheek. “I finished it while you were busy eating Lucky Charms this morning,” they say, handing over the folder.

You take it, and the impossible deadline suddenly feels trivial—until your younger self gasps, “Wait! You forgot their cat!” You’d blanked on the client’s mandatory 30-second animated tabby clip, the one they’d emphasized in their initial inquiry. Just as you reach for a pen to sketch the cat, the dream warps again: you’re back at your desk, the sun streaming through the office windows, and it’s 9 AM the following day.

Your boss is leaning over your cubicle wall, holding a latte exactly the way you like it—oat milk, one pump vanilla. “Well?” they say. “Ready to present?” You blink at your laptop screen, and the full brand kit is open, complete with the fluffy animated tabby curling atop a company logo. You can’t remember a single second of working on it.

Before you can answer, the microwave beep trills again, this time from your phone in your bag. The dream frays at the edges, and you jolt awake in your actual bed, phone buzzing on the nightstand. It’s a voicemail from your actual boss: “Hey, just a reminder about that Megacorp project—we pushed the deadline to next Friday, no rush!” On your nightstand, nestled next to your phone, is a single dried blue cornflower, the kind that grew in your grandma’s backyard you haven’t thought about in a decade.
