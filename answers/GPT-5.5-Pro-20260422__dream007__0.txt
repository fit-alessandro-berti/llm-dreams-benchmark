…step outside before either of you says something that cannot be unsaid.

The door closes behind you with a sound like a book snapping shut. For a moment, the hallway is ordinary: dim light, scuffed floorboards, the low hum of a refrigerator somewhere nearby. Then you notice the walls are breathing.

Not in a frightening way. More like they are trying to stay calm too.

Your friend follows you out, but when you turn around, they are much farther away than they should be, standing at the other end of a corridor that now stretches like a train tunnel. Their mouth is moving, but their words arrive as folded paper birds, fluttering weakly through the air before dropping at your feet.

You pick one up.

“I thought you knew me better than that,” it says.

Another lands beside it.

“I was scared you’d leave.”

The anger in your chest shifts. It does not disappear, but it changes shape, becoming something heavier and more familiar.

You try to answer, but your own words come out as stones. They fall from your mouth and crack the floor. Your friend looks at them, then at you, and you suddenly understand that this has been happening for years: paper birds from them, stones from you, both of you mistaking the other’s language for an attack.

So you kneel down and begin gathering the stones. One by one, you stack them into a small bridge across the widening hallway. Your friend watches, uncertain. Then they pick up the fallen paper birds and unfold them into planks, railings, little flags.

Together, without speaking, you build a way back to each other.

When the bridge is finished, it is impossibly delicate and impossibly strong. You both step onto it at the same time. Halfway across, the hallway falls away beneath you, revealing a dark ocean where the floor used to be. Far below, all the things you never said swim like silver fish.

Your friend reaches you first.

“I don’t want to win,” they say.

You realize you don’t either.

The moment you admit this, the ocean rises, warm and quiet, lifting the bridge until it becomes a table. You are both seated across from each other again, but now the room is filled with morning light. Between you sits a bowl of fruit you have never seen before, glowing softly from within.

Your friend takes one piece and breaks it in half.

You eat your half.

It tastes like apology, and thunder, and coming home.
