**...written entirely in lowercase cursive, with the answers being 'The Way You Felt' or 'What You Forgot to Say.'**

The realization hits me with the sickening weight of a dropped stone. *Lowercase cursive*. The elegant, looping script seems plucked from a 19th-century novel, utterly discordant against the stark, modern typeface of the exam booklet. But that’s only the first mistake. The second is the subject matter.

The course is Advanced Macroeconomics. It requires models, theorems, graphs of supply and demand, complex mathematical proof. The first question, supposedly on the Austrian School's view of monetary policy, reads instead: "How did the color cyan make you feel at age seven?"

My heart performs a frantic, useless drum solo against my ribs. This is impossible.

I glance over my right shoulder. The professor, Dr. Albright—a woman famous for her rigid adherence to quantitative rigor—is grading papers at her desk. She hasn't looked up, but the slight, almost imperceptible twitch at the corner of her mouth suggests she knows something is wrong.

Sweat beads on my forehead, cold and clammy. I try to rationalize it. A joke? A variant form? Maybe the course shifted? I lean closer, my knuckles white gripping the laminated edge of the desk.

The second question is more devastating: "What is the name of the person who was supposed to remind you of your passion before you entered this room?"

My vision begins to blur at the edges. The fluorescent lights hum too loudly, escalating from a normal buzz to a high-pitched, whining whine that seems to be vibrating inside my skull. I feel a profound, unsettling emptiness that has nothing to do with the subject matter, and everything to do with *me*.

Panic, thick and acidic, starts rising in my chest. I look around wildly. My classmates, previously absorbed in their own visible despair, are now looking at me. Their eyes are wide, not with fear of the exam, but with a shared, terrible recognition. They aren't confused; they are resigned.

I try to write an answer on the blank space below the question—anything that relates to GDP or inflation indices. But the graphite feels foreign in my hand, resisting the structured thought. My pen starts writing on its own, moving sluggishly across the paper, forming a single, looping, indecipherable word: *Almost.*

The paper seems to expand, the desk surface growing dimmer, the smell of chalk dust and coffee suddenly overpowered by the scent of cut grass and ozone—the smell of coming storm.

I lift my eyes to Dr. Albright. She finally stops grading. Her tweed blazer seems to ripple, and she slowly pushes her glasses up her nose, fixing me with a gaze that isn't judging, but utterly knowing.

She doesn't speak. She just tilts her head, and the ambient hum of the lights drops in frequency, flattening into a deep, sustained drone that seems to vibrate in my bones. The drone is not sound; it is pressure.

In that pressurized moment, I understand. This isn't an exam about economics or linguistics. It's an exam about *me*. It’s about the assumptions I've made about myself, about my trajectory, my perceived destiny. It's designed not to test my intelligence, but to expose the lie I've been living.

And then, the paper shimmers.

The words, the cursive, the questions—they all begin to swim, to wobble like submerged film. The paper softens, liquefies, and the solid wooden desk beneath my forearms melts away, replaced by a curtain of dense, emerald green foliage. I am no longer in a university lecture hall. I am in the middle of a quiet forest, and the droning vibration peaks, until suddenly—

I wake up.

I am bathed in the dull orange glow of morning sunlight. My breath hitches, and my heart slams itself rhythmically against my ribs, demanding acknowledgement. I am tangled in my sheets, sweat plastering my hair to my temples.

It was so real. The smell of the coffee, the weight of the cheap plastic comb on the desk... the dread.

I stare at the empty spot on my nightstand where the exam booklet would have been. Nothing.

I sit up, adrenaline-fueled, and scramble out of bed, needing cold reality. I walk to the window, my hand braced against the cool glass. Outside, the neighborhood is just a messy, ordinary collection of trees, roofs, and distant car horns.

But my hand continues to twitch, and I feel a faint, unmistakable residue on my skin—a residue that smells strangely, hauntingly, of ozone and cut grass.
