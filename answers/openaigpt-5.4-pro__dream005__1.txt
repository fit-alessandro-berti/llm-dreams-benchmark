...each group seems to know something about you that you don’t remember telling anyone.

One circle of guests laughs softly as you pass, and when you glance over, they are all wearing name tags with your handwriting on them. Another group is gathered around a long table covered in keys—house keys, car keys, tiny golden keys, old iron keys—and each person lifts one, studies your face, then shakes their head as if it isn’t the right one.

You keep moving through the crowd, calling your friend’s name, but the music swells at exactly the wrong moments, swallowing the sound. The rooms seem to rearrange themselves when you’re not looking. A hallway becomes a staircase. A staircase opens into a garden lit by chandeliers hanging from tree branches. Everywhere you go, people smile like they recognize you from somewhere important.

At last, someone points toward a door at the far end of the house. “They’ve been waiting for you,” she says, as if this explains everything.

You cross the room, but the floor turns soft beneath your feet, like thick carpet laid over water. The door is taller than it should be, painted the deep blue of evening. When you open it, you don’t find another room. You find a quiet train platform at dusk.

Your friend is there, standing beside a bench with a suitcase at their feet.

“You took a long time,” they say, though they don’t sound annoyed. More like relieved.

“I couldn’t find you,” you answer.

They tilt their head. “You were never supposed to find me in there. You were supposed to find your way out.”

Behind you, the party is gone. No music. No voices. Just the hum of distant tracks and a sky full of slow-moving stars. You want to ask where this is, where the train goes, why they seem so calm, but a warm wind moves through the station and carries away every question before you can say it.

A train approaches without lights, silent except for the faint clink of glass, like someone setting down a champagne flute in another room. The doors open.

Your friend picks up the suitcase and smiles. “Come on,” they say. “You know everyone on this part.”

And with the strange certainty that only exists in dreams, you do.

You step onto the train, and every passenger looks up at once—not like strangers, but like people from every version of your life: childhood neighbors, classmates you barely remember, faces from photographs, people you haven’t met yet but somehow miss already. They make room for you without speaking.

As the train begins to move, you look out the window for the party, the house, the garden of chandeliers—but all you see is your own reflection, smiling back as if it has been waiting for you all night.

Then you wake up with the feeling that you almost remembered something important.
