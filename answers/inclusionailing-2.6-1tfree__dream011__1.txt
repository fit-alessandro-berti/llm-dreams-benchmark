You notice the questions are written in a language that shifts every time you blink—like liquid clockwork, glyphs sliding off the page and reassembling into new words that hum just below hearing. 

The room is silent except for the soft, wet sound of your own heartbeat. The clock on the wall doesn’t have numbers; it has windows. Through each window, you see a version of yourself: one dressed in surgical scrubs, bloodless and precise; another standing at a podium, voice cracking under stadium lights; another kneeling in soil, planting seeds that glow like tiny lanterns. 

You realize the exam isn’t testing what you know. It’s testing which future you can forgive. 

Your pen, when you lift it, feels heavier than it should, as if it remembers every choice you didn’t make. The first question unscrolls itself across the desk, and instead of asking for facts, it asks for the exact weight of the moment you decided to want something impossible. 

You look up, and the proctor is no one you know. Their face is made of the quiet between thunderclaps. They smile, and the smile says: *You’re allowed to change the answer as long as you’re willing to pay the price in breath.*

You uncap your pen. The room exhales. The questions begin to bleed color.
