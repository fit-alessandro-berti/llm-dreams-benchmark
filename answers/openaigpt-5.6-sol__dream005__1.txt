You are at a large social gathering where you only know one person. As you try to find them, you encounter various groups of people and each group seems to recognize you.

They call you by different names.

At the edge of a glittering ballroom, a circle of elderly women welcomes you as a childhood friend. In the kitchen, strangers in silver masks congratulate you on a promotion you don’t remember earning. Outside, beneath trees strung with blue lanterns, a group of children insists you promised to show them where the moon goes during the day.

You keep asking if anyone has seen your friend, but whenever you say their name, the music stops for half a second.

Finally, across the crowd, you spot them slipping through a narrow red door.

You follow.

Beyond it is not another room, but the hallway of your childhood home. The party’s music pulses faintly behind the walls. Family photographs line the corridor, except every face has been replaced by yours—smiling, sleeping, growing older.

Your friend waits at the far end.

“You took a long time,” they say.

“Who are all those people?”

“They’re everyone you might have been.”

Behind you, the red door disappears. The hallway begins filling with warm, golden water. Photographs loosen from the walls and float past like leaves.

Your friend holds out a hand.

“Come on,” they say. “You’re about to wake up.”

You reach for them, but before your fingers touch, you realize they have your face.

Then all the lanterns go out.

You wake with your hand extended into the dark, while somewhere beyond your window, a crowd erupts in applause.
