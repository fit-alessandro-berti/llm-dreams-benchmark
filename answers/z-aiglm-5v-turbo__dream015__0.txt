...plant a garden.

Not in soil—there is none left, only gray ash that chokes and clings—but in the hollow ribcage of something enormous that fell from the sky years before anyone remembers. The bones have weathered to something like ivory, like stone, like the architecture of a cathedral built for a god who already left.

You carry seeds in a jar you've guarded for longer than you can count. They are small and dark and ordinary-looking. You know—*somehow*, the way you know things in dreams—that they are not seeds at all, but compressed moments: a mother's laughter, the smell of rain on hot pavement, a dog's tail thumping against a wooden floor.

You place each one in the curve of bone where marrow once lived. You weep over them, and your tears are not water but light—golden and thick as honey. Where it touches the bone, color returns. Red. Green. *Blue.*

Something stirs in the ashes around you.

You look up. Others are watching from the skeletal remains of buildings, eyes wide and hungry not for food but for this—for the impossible thing you are doing.

The garden blooms in seconds. Hours. Decades. Time moves wrong here (it's a dream, after all). Vines curl up the ribs like they were always meant to be trellises. Flowers open their mouths and sing in frequencies you feel rather than hear.

And you understand, suddenly, that this is how it begins. Not with walls or weapons or plans.

Just one person, stubborn enough to be tender.
