…and you can already tell it isn’t really “waiting” like a person waits. It’s waiting the way a clock does—quietly, inevitably, with hands that don’t care if you blink.

You sit down at your desk and the computer boots too fast. The screen fills instantly, not with an inbox but with a timeline that has been inked directly onto the air above the keyboard. The hours are marked with thick black lines, and the final one is already smudged, as if it has been erased and rewritten a hundred times.

Your boss leans over your shoulder. Up close, their face looks ordinary—same tired eyes, same practiced smile—but their words arrive before their mouth moves.

“Start,” they say.

The cursor appears without you touching the mouse. It starts typing on its own, not letters but instructions—clauses, requirements, constraints—spilling across the screen like a dam breaking. The project file grows heavier with each word. Every time you try to delete something, the deleted text reappears somewhere else, rearranged into a more complicated version of the same problem.

You stand, as if standing will make the impossible feel more negotiable. The chair shudders behind you and settles like it has been waiting for your decision. When you look at your hands, your fingers are smudged with graphite, like you’ve been writing for hours. You don’t remember starting, but you know exactly where you’d been when you did—it’s a memory you can’t prove.

The office around you begins to change.

At first it’s subtle: the fluorescent lights hum in a different key, the carpet pattern repeats too regularly, and the air smells like warm paper and old coffee. Then the walls slowly lose their corners. Desks drift toward each other like magnets aligning. Your coworkers pass by with expressions frozen in the middle of sentences, moving their mouths without sound.

A thought strikes you—sharp, bright, almost helpful:

*This is a dream. So there’s a rule. Find the rule.*

You walk to the conference room because you’ve always hated the conference room, and in dreams hatred turns into a door.

Inside, the table is too long and stretches beyond its frame. The chairs are occupied by versions of you—some younger, some older, some with different haircuts, different skin tones, different ages written into their posture. They all look up at once, as if the room has been waiting for your arrival too.

In the center of the table is a single clock face. No hands. No numbers. Just a circle that seems to be measuring you.

One of the “you” stands and speaks, and their voice is your own voice on a day you don’t remember:

“You can’t finish it by working harder.”

You ask, “Then how do I finish it?”

They point to the clock. “Finish it by changing what it *is*.”

The other versions of you begin to murmur, overlapping like a chorus of different decisions. Their words aren’t in any language you know, but you understand them anyway, the way you understand a song you’ve never heard.

You turn back toward the clock and realize the face isn’t blank—it’s full of something you can’t see unless you stop trying to look directly at it. When you relax your eyes, the clock face becomes a sheet of paper.

Contracts. Milestones. Deliverables.

But each line is written in a different ink, and the ink is different *from you*. Some of it is your boss’s neat signature. Some is your coworker’s sloppy notes. Some is older than your entire career. It’s as if the project isn’t a task—it’s a negotiation between versions of reality.

Your throat tightens. “So what—someone else already did it?”

Another you shakes their head. “No. Someone else *agreed* to a different deadline.”

The clock shivers, and suddenly you remember the moment you accepted the project: the boss asking the question, you nodding automatically, the signature you added without reading the fine print.

In the dream, you had never questioned it.

You look at your own hands again. The graphite smudges have become faint fingerprints, as if you’ve been trying to locate yourself through residue. You press your palms flat on the table. The wood feels warm, like it’s alive.

“What do I have to do?” you whisper.

The room grows very still.

From somewhere outside your awareness—beneath the carpet, behind the walls—a soft ticking begins. Not the clock’s ticking. Something deeper. Like an engine starting.

Your boss’s voice drifts into the room. “Start,” they say again, but this time it doesn’t sound like an order. It sounds like a permission you didn’t know you could refuse.

You stand at the head of the table and finally do the one thing that has always felt dangerous at work:

You stop pretending you can meet the deadline.

You say, clearly, “This schedule is impossible.”

The words land with weight. They don’t just echo—they take root. The clock face flickers, and the lines of deliverables begin to unravel, letter by letter, into blank spaces. The conference room walls regain their corners. The versions of you sit down, relieved, like you’ve returned something to its rightful place.

But the dream doesn’t let you keep the relief without a cost.

As the impossible deadlines fade, a new deadline appears—closer, harsher, and more personal.

*Not the due date of the project.*

A due date for you.

Your stomach drops. The clock circle fills with a countdown made of breath. Every exhale shortens it. Every inhale lengthens it again. You realize the deadline isn’t external anymore. It’s internal—time you spend dodging decisions, time you spend agreeing when you mean no.

You feel the office tug at you, like a tide. The conference room begins to dissolve into the familiar shape of your workplace: the same desk, the same fluorescent buzz, the same hostile schedule pinned over your monitor like a verdict.

You are back at your computer.

The boss stands beside you, arms folded, waiting for your keyboard to betray you.

The cursor blinks.

For the first time, the screen is blank.

A single line appears, not typed by you, but written as if carved directly into the light:

**START BY SAYING WHAT YOU CAN’T DO.**

You swallow. Your mouth feels heavy with truth.

You look up at your boss and, in the dream, you hear your real voice—plain, unpolished, but unmistakably yours. You say, “I can’t deliver this by then. But I can deliver this if we change the scope, or we change the deadline, or we change the people.”

The boss’s expression shifts—just slightly—like a lock clicking open.

Behind them, the office shudders, and all those muted coworkers start making noise again. Chairs scrape. Phones ring. Emails whoosh across screens. The building becomes more solid, less like a set and more like a place where consequences actually exist.

The impossible timeline above your keyboard rearranges itself into something believable, not effortless—just possible. The final erased hour returns, crisp and clean, now a date you recognize. A date you haven’t wasted yet.

Then you wake—except you don’t.

You open your eyes and you’re still at your desk.

Your cursor is still blinking.

Only now you notice the strangest detail:

You can feel your real-life body—your weight in the chair, your breath against your ribs, the mild ache in your shoulders. The dream has turned translucent, like it’s overlaying the present instead of replacing it.

Your boss is still there, but softer now, less like a judge and more like a person with a problem they don’t know how to ask.

The project isn’t magically finished.

Instead, it’s offered to you with an honest shape.

You’re dreaming, yes.

But the dream ends in a decision.

So you pick up your phone—hands steady, heart still pounding—and you begin to send the first message you’ve been avoiding for months:

Not an apology.

Not a promise.

A boundary.

And as the text appears, you feel the ticking of the clock in your chest slow down, like the deadline has finally learned how to breathe.
