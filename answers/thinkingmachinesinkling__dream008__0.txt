The floor is now made of unfinished calendars, and each step you take tears a page—yesterday, tomorrow, next year—until you’re walking on thin air.

Suddenly, you’re not at a desk anymore. You’re *inside* the project: a vast, impossible architecture of glass deadlines and staircases that loop back into themselves. Your boss hands you a pen that writes in disappearing ink, and you understand, with the strange clarity of dreams, that the task was never meant to be completed. It was only meant to be endured.

So you begin. You build the impossible report out of clock ticks and shadow. The harder you work, the more the room expands—until the office becomes an endless sky, and you are floating in it, holding a finished document that reads, in your own handwriting: *You were never late. You were only dreaming of being small.*

Then you wake up—not in your bed, but still at your desk, with one more minute on the clock, and the strange feeling that you’ve already done it all before.
