Our disagreement had never been small: we were fighting over the childhood treehouse we’d nailed together from scrap lumber and stolen nails at 11, the rickety oak-perch the city was set to bulldoze in two weeks for a downtown parking garage. I’d wanted to chain myself to its rotting railings, livestream the protest, drag reporters out to shame the council; she’d wanted to take the city’s last-minute compromise—dismantle the treehouse piece by piece, rebuild it in the town’s new community park, keep every splintered board and our carved initials, just move it somewhere permanent. I’d called her a coward for giving up the spot that had been only ours; she’d called me reckless for risking a misdemeanor that would tank the work visa I’d spent three years chasing to move abroad.

My boots hit the sidewalk outside the coffee shop, and that’s when the dream bends, the way dreams do: brick storefronts warp into oak trunks, the hum of traffic fades into crickets, and suddenly I’m 10 years old again, scuffed sneakers crunching through fallen leaves, climbing the same overgrown hill that led to our treehouse. When I haul myself over its rail, she’s already there—Lila, both 32, in the same leather jacket she’d worn to the coffee shop, and 11, in the sparkly rain boots she refused to take off that summer, perched on the moldy bean bag we’d dragged up there in 2008. She’s holding the chipped mason jar we’d stuffed with notes to our future selves, the one we’d sealed behind a loose board before we went off to college.

I cross the splintered floor to sit next to her, and she passes me my note, the crayon still bright even after all those years: “If me and Lila ever fight about something that matters, I hope I remember the treehouse was never just the tree. It was us building it, even when our hands got splinters.” Her note, scrawled in the same messy 11-year-old handwriting, says the same thing, almost word for word.

We don’t yell this time. She admits she was scared my protest would get me arrested, that I’d lose the future I’d worked my whole life to build; I admit I was scared moving the treehouse would mean all the parts of us that were still that scared, lonely pair of preteens that only felt safe up there would vanish too. Before I wake up, I glance down the hill, and both versions of the treehouse are glowing: the old one, still rooted in the gnarled oak on the hill, and a new one, exactly the same, sitting in the sunlit community park at the bottom. Lila’s hand is in mine, and for the first time since the fight started, I don’t feel like I have to choose one or the other. The dream lets me have both.
