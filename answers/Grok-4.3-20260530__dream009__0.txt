You wake up in a completely different era, with no modern technology around. As you explore your new surroundings, you realize you need to **find a way to belong here before the locals decide you're a witch or a spy**.

The ground beneath you is cool, damp earth. Your fingers brush against rough wool instead of the soft cotton of your old clothes. No phone. No watch. No hum of distant traffic—just the creak of wooden cart wheels, the lowing of oxen, and the sharp scent of woodsmoke and wet stone. You’re standing at the edge of a thatched-roof village nestled between rolling green hills and a dark forest. Smoke curls from chimneys. A woman in a linen kirtle and wimple stares at you from a doorway, one hand frozen on her spindle.

Your heart hammers. This isn’t a costume party. The air tastes older. The sky is a deeper blue, untainted by city light. Somewhere in the distance, a church bell tolls the hour in a language you somehow understand even though you’ve never studied it.

You need to **blend in. Fast.**

You duck behind a hay cart and quickly strip off anything too obviously modern—your sneakers go into a muddy ditch, your jacket buried under leaves. The simple tunic and breeches you woke up wearing will have to do. When the spindle-woman approaches, you force your voice steady and claim to be a traveler from the northern fens whose horse threw a shoe. She eyes your bare feet suspiciously but nods and points you toward the blacksmith.

The blacksmith is a giant of a man with soot on his beard and arms like tree trunks. He grunts, hands you a pair of wooden clogs, and sets you to work pumping the bellows. Your modern lungs burn. Your modern hands blister within an hour. But you learn. You listen. You smile when the apprentices tease you for your “strange accent” and offer to buy the first round of ale at the tavern when the day ends.

Days blur into weeks. You barter your strange knowledge carefully—showing the herbalist how to make a stronger poultice for fevers, teaching the miller’s son a quicker way to calculate grain shares using patterns in the dirt that look like childish games but are actually basic arithmetic. You never say the words “electricity” or “internet.” You call them “old family secrets” and let people laugh.

But the dreams within the dream grow stronger. Every night you see flashes of your old life—neon signs, glowing screens, the weight of a laptop on your knees—then they dissolve into the smell of rain on thatch and the distant howl of wolves. You start to wonder if you were ever meant to leave.

Then the rumors reach the village: a hooded stranger in the deep woods who speaks with the voice of the wind and offers “paths between worlds” to those brave enough to pay the price. The price is always something precious—a memory, a name, a promise never to return.

You realize you need to **choose**.

One misty dawn you slip away from the village while the blacksmith sleeps. You carry only a small pack of dried meat, a borrowed cloak, and the single modern thing you kept hidden: a tiny silver USB drive you found in your pocket the first morning, now strung on a leather thong around your neck like a talisman. You don’t even know what’s on it anymore. Maybe nothing. Maybe everything.

The forest swallows you. Branches whisper. A white stag watches from the shadows, then leads you to a clearing where the air shimmers like heat haze even though the morning is cool. In the center stands a circle of standing stones older than the village, older than any king.

The hooded figure waits inside the circle. You can’t see their face, only the glint of eyes that have watched centuries pass.

“You have learned the rhythm of this world,” the voice says, neither male nor female. “Will you stay and become part of its song? Or will you trade what you carry for the door home?”

Your fingers close around the USB drive. It feels absurdly light. You think of the blacksmith’s laugh, the herbalist’s grateful tears when her child’s fever broke, the way the stars here actually form pictures instead of pollution.

You smile.

“I think,” you say slowly, “I need to finish what I started.”

You step into the circle.

The world tilts.

You wake up in your own bed, heart still racing, the faint smell of woodsmoke clinging to your hair. Your phone is on the nightstand, screen dark. For a long moment you just lie there, listening to the distant hum of traffic, and wonder which life was the dream.

Then you reach up and feel the leather thong still around your neck.

The tiny silver USB drive is warm against your skin.

And outside your window, for just a second, you swear you hear a church bell tolling the hour in a language you now understand.
