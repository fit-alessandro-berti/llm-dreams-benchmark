You wake up in a completely different era, with no modern technology around. As you explore your new surroundings, you realize you need to **relearn how to measure time without a screen**.

The air smells of iron and woodsmoke. Above, the sky is a scroll of bruised purples and slow-moving clouds. You find a river that doesn’t appear on any map you remember, its water so clear it seems to be made of glass that hasn’t yet decided to harden. On the opposite bank, a village is assembling itself out of gestures rather than plans—roofs going up like hands folding into prayer, children tracing maps in the dirt that vanish when it rains.

You realize you are hungry, but hunger here is a different shape. It pulls at your ribs like a question. You follow the smell of baking to a stone oven built into a hillside. A woman with forearms like knotted rope offers you a loaf that is still warm enough to remember the wheat. She doesn’t ask your name, and you don’t offer it; instead, she nods at the sun, which is already leaning toward evening, and points to a stack of clay tablets by the door.

On one, a stylus has been left behind. You pick it up. The clay is cool and willing. You press a wedge into it, not knowing the language but discovering that the shape of your urgency fits the mark you make. When you finish, the tablet hums, very faintly, like a struck bell.

Night comes quickly. The stars are close enough to stitch you into the sky if you stand still long enough. You lie on a reed mat and realize that in this era, sleep is not a shutting off but a traveling. You close your eyes and feel the ground tilt gently, as if the earth itself is rolling you toward a version of tomorrow that has never heard of haste. 

Your last thought before the dark takes you is simple, and absolute: *You do not need to know the year, so long as you know how to mend a tear in your coat with sinew and patience.*

Then you dream inside the dream, and the river finds you, and you let it carry you downstream, learning the names of things by the way they feel in your mouth when you speak them aloud for the first time.
