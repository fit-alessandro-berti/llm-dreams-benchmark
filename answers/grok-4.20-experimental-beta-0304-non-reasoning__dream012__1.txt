...and you feel the dream's logic begin to bend, the way dreams do.

The wheel is alive in your hands—warm, pulsing like a heartbeat that isn't quite yours. The crew isn't just looking at you anymore. Their eyes are too wide, reflecting lightning that hasn't struck yet. One of them, old Tomas, opens his mouth to speak, but seawater pours out instead of words.

You realize with the calm certainty that only exists in dreams that this isn't your ship.

It never was.

The vessel groans beneath you, not from the waves, but from something shifting deep in its hull—like bones rearranging. The storm isn't above you. It's *inside* the ship, trying to get out.

A woman stands at the bow who wasn't there a moment ago. She wears your captain's coat, the one you lost in the wreck of '98. Her face keeps flickering between your mother's and someone you haven't met yet.

She turns to you and smiles with too many teeth.

"Captain," she says, and her voice is every order you've ever given, played backward. "The sea wants its due. But it doesn't want the ship."

The deck tilts violently. Not because of the waves.

Because the ship is turning to look at you.

You suddenly understand: you aren't the captain.

You're the figurehead.

And something beneath the waves has been pulling your strings for years.

The woman extends her hand—your hand, but older, scarred in ways you've never been scarred.

"The crew already knows," she whispers as the first mast begins to bleed. "They're just waiting for you to remember how to drown."

What do you do, Captain?
