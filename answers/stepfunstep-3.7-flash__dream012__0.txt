You slam your palm flat against the wheel first, stopping it from spinning out of your grip, cold, splintered wood searing through the oilskin of your glove. Your voice is the first thing that cuts through the howling wind, rough and unshaking even as the ship pitches so hard you almost lose your footing: “Mr. Hale, check the starboard rigging! Jem, lash those flour barrels before they crush the galley!”

The crew moves, shaky but steady, their faces streaked with rain and sea foam, every eye fixed on you like you’re the only solid thing left in a world that’s coming apart at the seams. A wave crashes over the bow, icy water swallowing the deck up to your knees, and you taste copper and old rum on your tongue. The mast groans, a splintering crack that makes half the crew flinch, and you see Elara, your first mate, motion for you to meet her in the crow’s nest, the faint silver scar on her forearm glowing where lightning struck it an hour prior.

By the time you haul yourself up the rigging, your palms raw and bleeding, the wind is so strong it feels like it’s trying to wring the breath out of your lungs. And then you see the sail.

It’s not canvas, not like it was when you left port that morning. It’s stitched together from scrawled, crumpled pieces of paper: the letter you wrote to your mother when you ran away from home at 16, the apology you scrawled on a napkin for your old first mate after the fight that got him marooned, the note you left on your ex-lover’s doorstep and ran from before they could read it. The wind is tearing at the edges, ripping the words apart, and for the first time you realize the storm isn’t weather. It’s every “I’m sorry” you never said, every “stay” you never yelled, every fear you stuffed down so deep you forgot it was there.

You fumble for the frayed rope to lash the sail back, your fingers too numb to tie a proper knot, when you hear Jem yell from the deck below: “Captain! The starboard anchor’s jammed!” You look down, and the anchor he’s wrestling with isn’t cast iron—it’s carved out of polished sea glass, the edges worn smooth by a hundred years of tide.

“Cut it loose!” you yell back, and the second it hits the water, the sea glows soft, bioluminescent blue. The waves stop slamming the hull so hard, the wind dying down just enough that you can hear Elara’s voice, sharp and clear, cutting through the remaining roar: “The mast’s holding, but the sail’s still tearing!”

You look back at the words flapping in the wind, and you spot the line you wrote to your little sister when she turned 21, the one you stuffed in a drawer and never mailed: *I’m proud of you, even when I can’t say it.*

You let go of the rigging, let the wind whip your hair back from your face, and you yell it as loud as you can, over the fading rumble of the storm: “I AM PROUD OF YOU! I’M SORRY I NEVER SAID IT!”

The storm breaks then, not with a crash, but with a soft sigh. The rain turns to warm mist, the wind softens to a gentle breeze, and the sail stops tearing—instead, the words stitch themselves back together, glowing faint gold now, like they were never broken at all. The crew cheers, rough and loud, and when you climb back down, Elara claps you on the shoulder, her silver scar fading to a faint, silvery line.

You lean against the wheel, watching the horizon. The churning gray waves have smoothed to glass, reflecting a sky streaked with pink and gold, and the shore line you thought was miles away is right there, glowing with warm, soft light, the kind you only see in dreams right before you wake up. Jem hands you a mug of spiced rum, and when you take a sip, it tastes like honey and the chocolate chip cookies your grandma used to make, and you realize the crew isn’t just your sailors. They’re the parts of you you thought you’d left behind: the scared 16-year-old who ran away, the kid who used to draw constellations on her bedroom ceiling, the part of you that still believes it’s okay to be soft.

The wheel feels warm under your palm now, and the constellations carved into its surface are the exact ones you used to draw, the ones you thought you’d forgotten. Somewhere, a gull cries overhead, and when you look up, its feathers are made of torn notebook paper, fluttering soft in the breeze.

You’re still dreaming, but for the first time in as long as you can remember, you’re not scared of the storm. You know you can steer through anything, as long as you’re the one holding the wheel.
