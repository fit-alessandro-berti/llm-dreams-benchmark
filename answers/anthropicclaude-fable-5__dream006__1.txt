You realize, with the strange calm that only exists in dreams, that this is going better than expected.

You look down at your notes, but the podium has become a kitchen sink from your childhood home, and your speech is written on the surface of the water filling it. The words ripple when you breathe. You cup your hands and lift the water to show the audience, and somehow they understand—they nod, some of them crying, though you can't remember what the speech was about. Something important. Something about doors, or birds, or the way your grandmother used to hum.

The back wall of the auditorium is gone now. Beyond the last row of seats, there's an open field at dusk, and the audience members in the back rows have grass growing up around their ankles. No one seems to mind.

A person in the front row raises their hand. When you call on them, you realize it's you—a younger version, maybe twelve years old, wearing the shoes you lost at summer camp.

"How does it end?" your younger self asks.

You open your mouth to answer, and the lights dim, and you understand that you're about to wake up—you can feel the morning pulling at the edges of the room like a tide. The audience begins to applaud, and the sound of their clapping slowly becomes the sound of rain against a window, your window, and the stage dissolves beneath your feet...

...but gently. The dream lets you go gently.
