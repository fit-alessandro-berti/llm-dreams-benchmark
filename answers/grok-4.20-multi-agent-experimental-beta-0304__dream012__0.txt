You close your eyes for a moment (a foolish thing for a captain to do in a hurricane), and when you open them again, the crew has changed. Their oilskins shimmer like scales. Their eyes are too wide, too knowing. The first mate, old Jenkins, grins with teeth that definitely weren't that sharp an hour ago.

"Captain," he says, voice layered with the sound of surf on rocks, "you've been steering us in circles for years. This storm isn't coming. It's *remembering* you."

The wheel spins under your hands without being touched. The compass melts into liquid silver and drips onto the deck, spelling out words you recognize from childhood nightmares. The ship tilts violently, but instead of falling into the sea, the sea folds upward, becoming a vast dark throat that swallows the sky.

You laugh. Because of course this isn't a ship. It never was.

You leap from the helm, arms spread, and the turbulent water catches you—not like waves, but like hands that have been waiting. The crew dissolves into bioluminescent foam, cheering in frequencies only dreams can hear. The mast blooms into an enormous ancient tree. Sails become wings made of starlight and regret.

As you plunge deeper, the pressure doesn't crush you. It *remembers* you too. You are no longer the captain.

You are the storm.

And the sea has been dreaming of *you* this entire time.

(You wake up with salt on your lips and the sound of waves still roaring in your chest.)
