**you decide to plant a garden.**

Not out of naive optimism—resources are still razor-thin and the sky still carries that bruised, perpetual twilight—but because something in the dream-logic insists that *this* is the one thing the wasteland hasn't taken from you yet: the stubborn impulse to make something live.

You scavenge cracked plastic bottles for makeshift greenhouses, trade a rusted knife for three withered potato eyes, and dig in soil that tastes of ash and old regret. The first shoots that push through the gray dirt look impossibly green, almost defiant. Other survivors begin appearing at the edges of your little plot. A woman with a scarred face brings a handful of beans she kept sewn inside her coat lining for three years. A half-feral teenager offers silent company and a surprisingly gentle hand with the watering can made from an old fuel container.

Nights are still dangerous. Raiders prowl. Mutated coyotes sing their broken songs. But every morning the garden is still there, a small green rebellion against the end of the world. You start teaching the others what you remember: how to read the clouds, how to save seeds, how to tell the difference between a weed and a future meal. Laughter—actual, rusty laughter—starts happening around the fire at dusk.

One night you dream *inside* the dream: the garden has grown into a forest that stretches past the ruined horizon. Trees heavy with impossible fruit. Rivers running clear. And in the middle of it all stands a version of yourself that never gave up, smiling like they knew this was always possible.

You wake up still in the wasteland… but the garden is real. The people are real. And for the first time since the sky cracked open, the future doesn’t feel like a threat.

It feels like the next row you still have to hoe. 

And somehow, in the dream, that’s enough.
