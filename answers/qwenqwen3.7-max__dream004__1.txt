Instead of feeling woven fabric, your fingertips sink into the canvas as if it were a pool of cool, viscous mercury. The moment you make contact, the "blankness" ripples, and you realize it isn't actually empty—it is painted with the exact color of a forgotten childhood memory, a hue that doesn't exist in the waking world.

You don't have a brush, so you peel your own shadow off the ground. It comes away like wet silk, heavy and shimmering with liquid obsidian. Using your shadow as a brush, you sweep it across the canvas. 

Where you paint, the shifting, chaotic colors of the world around you suddenly freeze into solid, geometric sounds. A triangle you paint tastes sharply of copper; a circle hums a low, resonant cello note that vibrates in your teeth. You are painting with synesthesia.

As you work, the wooden frame of the canvas begins to melt, twisting into dark, thorny vines that root themselves into the floating, shifting ground. The canvas itself stretches backward, no longer a flat surface but a deep, vaulted corridor. Driven by a dream-logic compulsion, you step inside the painting.

Inside, the rules of the world invert. The air is thick and breathable like water, and gravity pulls you gently toward the ceiling. You walk on the sky, looking down at a floor made of ticking clock faces, their hands spinning wildly backward. 

At the end of the corridor stands a figure. As you approach, you see it is painting on a massive, invisible wall. With every stroke it makes, your physical form in the dream changes. It paints a sweeping arc of cerulean, and your left arm dissolves into a flock of glass butterflies that scatter into the air. It dabs a spot of golden yellow, and your heartbeat begins to chime like a distant bell. 

You try to speak, to ask the figure who it is, but when you open your mouth, only a stream of blooming jasmine flowers spills out. 

The figure finally turns to face you. It has no face—only a swirling, fractal vortex of the very first color you saw when you opened your eyes as a newborn. It holds out the brush toward you. 

You take it. The moment your fingers close around the handle, the corridor shatters like a dropped mirror. The shards hang in the air for a breathless second, reflecting a million different versions of you, before they all dissolve into a blinding, warm white light. 

The shifting colors fade. The geometric sounds quiet into a soft, rhythmic rushing noise. You feel the heavy, comforting weight of a blanket, the cool air of your bedroom on your face, and the lingering, fading taste of copper and jasmine on your tongue as you finally open your eyes.
