You start to run, but your legs feel like they’re moving through lukewarm honey. The street signs flicker, swapping languages you’ve never seen, then dissolve into watercolor smudges. The skyline leans in, glass towers breathing in and out, their windows blinking like slow eyelids.

A tram made of stitched-together maps groans past, doors sliding open to reveal a handful of passengers who are all— impossibly— versions of you. One is wearing your childhood haircut, another has your face but hands that are clearly someone else’s, a third is old enough to be your descendant, smiling as if they know a secret you’ve forgotten since breakfast.

You check your pockets. No phone, no watch, no wallet. Just a smooth river stone that hums when you squeeze it, and a folded ticket with a single word: *listen*.

The city begins to fold. Cobblestones curl upward like paper scrolls, alleyways stretch into spirals, and the horizon peels back to reveal that the sky is actually the ceiling of an enormous, dimly lit archive. Shelves tower into the clouds, each one packed with books whose spines pulse faintly. Between them, ladders glide on invisible rails, ferrying silhouettes that shush you with gloved fingers when you try to call out.

Suddenly, rain starts falling upward, collecting in silver puddles that hover just above the ground. In one of those puddles, you see the meeting reflected— a table, two chairs, a window overlooking a sea that shouldn’t exist this far inland. The reflection waves. You wave back, and the puddle shivers, spilling time in careful, ticking droplets.

A streetlamp flickers and speaks in a voice that sounds like pages turning. “You’re late,” it says, “but you’re early. Which do you trust more— the map or the territory?”

The stone in your pocket grows warm, then hot. It unfurls into a compass that points not north, but *purpose*. The needle spins wildly before settling on a doorway that wasn’t there a moment ago— set into the side of a building that looks like it was built from half-remembered lullabies.

You push through.

Inside, the meeting is already happening. Around the table sit people you’ve loved, lost, and haven’t met yet. They don’t look up. They’re busy building a bridge out of questions and answers, weaving it across a canyon that is quietly, patiently growing wider.

Someone slides a chair out for you. It fits perfectly. On the table in front of you is a cup of tea that smells like forgiveness, and a notebook open to a page that says, *Begin here*.

Outside, the city exhales, folding itself into a pocket in your coat. The hour is gone, but time, you realize, is only a rumor the present tells to keep itself company.

You take a sip of tea, and the meeting begins— not to decide where to go, but to remember that you’ve been arriving the entire time.
