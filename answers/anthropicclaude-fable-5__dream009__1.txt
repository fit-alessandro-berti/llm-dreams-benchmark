The cobblestone street beneath your feet is slick with morning dew, and somewhere a rooster crows. People in rough woolen clothes are beginning to emerge from timber-framed houses, and they're starting to notice you—your strange clothing, your bewildered expression.

An old woman with flour-dusted hands grabs your sleeve. "You're late," she says, as if she's known you all your life. "The baker's been asking for you since dawn."

In the strange logic of dreams, you don't question this. You follow her down a narrow alley that smells of woodsmoke and fresh bread. Your hands, you realize, already know things—how to knead dough, how to judge the heat of a brick oven by holding your palm near its mouth. It's as if this life has been waiting for you all along.

Days pass in minutes, the way they do in dreams. You learn the rhythms of this place: the church bells dividing the hours, the market on Thursdays, the way everyone falls silent when riders in dark cloaks pass through town.

But something nags at you. A word you can't quite remember. A glowing rectangle you used to hold in your hands. Sometimes at night, you look up at the stars—so many more than you remember—and feel homesick for a place you can't quite picture anymore.

Then one evening, the old woman finds you staring at the sky. She smiles, and her eyes flash with something knowing.

"You're starting to remember," she says. "That's usually when you wake u—"

The rooster crows.

You open your eyes. Your alarm is going off. But on your nightstand, where your phone should be, there's a small loaf of bread, still warm.
