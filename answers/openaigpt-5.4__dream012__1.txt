…raise your voice above the wind and tell them to cut the mainsail before it tears itself to ribbons. The words come out as gulls instead of sound, white birds bursting from your mouth and circling the mast. No one seems surprised. The crew nods as if this is the most ordinary thing in the world.

The sea is black, but not empty. Beneath each wave something enormous rolls its silver eye toward the hull, watching. Lightning forks across the sky and for an instant the ocean is a field of shattered mirrors, each one reflecting a different version of your ship: one burning, one frozen in glass, one sailing upside down among the stars.

“Captain,” says your first mate, though when you turn, it is your childhood teacher wearing a coat made of soaked maps. “We are approaching the place that was erased.”

Ahead, through the rain, a lighthouse appears. It stands in the middle of the water with no island beneath it, its beam turning slowly, not across the sea but across the sky, searching the clouds as if expecting land to fall from them. With every sweep of its light, the storm hesitates. The thunder draws in a breath. Even the waves seem to listen.

You know, with the absolute certainty dreams allow, that you have been here before, though not yet.

The wheel trembles under your hands like a living thing. The compass spins, then settles, pointing not north but inward—its needle aimed at your chest. The crew waits. Rain runs down their faces like tears they borrowed from someone else.

So you steer toward the lighthouse.

As you near it, the ocean rises in great dark walls on either side of the ship, making a corridor through the storm. In the deep water below, bells begin to ring. One by one, shapes emerge: drowned ships, drifting upright as if still sailing, their lanterns glowing green. Figures stand on their decks, silent and patient, hats in hand, as though they have gathered to witness your arrival.

At the base of the lighthouse is a door, red and dry despite the sea. You order the anchor dropped, but instead of chain, the ship spills a long strand of golden hair into the water, and that holds you fast.

You descend alone.

The steps inside the lighthouse spiral both upward and downward at once. On the walls hang portraits of captains with your face at every age—young, old, scarred, sleeping, skeletal, smiling. At the top, or perhaps the bottom, there is a room with a single round window. In the center stands a small wooden table set for one. Upon it rests a brass key, a compass made of bone, and a cup of steaming tea.

Seated beside the table is the storm.

It wears your reflection like a cloak. Its voice is the creak of the hull at midnight. “You have kept them afloat this long,” it says. “Will you keep them, or will you wake?”

You try to answer, but seawater pours from your sleeves and fills the room ankle-deep, then knee-deep, and in the rising water you see tiny versions of your crew sailing paper boats in circles around your boots. They are laughing. Outside the window, dawn is gathering itself, pale and enormous.

The storm pushes the brass key toward you.

When you take it, every bell in the sea rings at once.

The lighthouse opens like an eye.

And your ship, your crew, the black water, the watching things below—all of it lifts gently, as if the whole ocean were only a blanket someone is drawing back—while far away, from another world or the next room, someone is calling your name.
