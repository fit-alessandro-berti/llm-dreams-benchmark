At first they appear to be flowers, then constellations, then tiny streets in a city you almost remember. You run your fingers over the carvings, and the box grows warm beneath your touch. Somewhere inside it, something sighs.

There is no lockhole.

Only a small circular mark on the lid, smooth as a moon reflected in water. When you press your thumb to it, the room around you dims. The walls dissolve into mist, and the floor becomes a black lake scattered with stars. The box is suddenly heavier, as if it contains not an object but a season, or a memory, or a promise you made before you were born.

A whisper rises from the patterns.

“Not every key is metal.”

You realize then that the carvings are moving in rhythm with your breath. When you inhale, the vines curl outward. When you exhale, the stars draw closer together. So you close your eyes and breathe slowly, letting the box listen.

With a soft click, the lid opens.

Inside is darkness at first. Then a tiny light flickers awake: a firefly, trapped in a glass bead. It floats upward, passes through the lid as though wood were smoke, and hovers before your face. In its glow you see images unfolding—your childhood bedroom, a road after rain, a door you never opened, someone’s hand slipping out of yours.

The firefly drifts toward the lake.

You follow.

Each step across the water sends ripples through the stars. The locked box remains in your hands, now light and empty, yet somehow more important than before. Ahead, the firefly stops above a doorway standing alone in the mist. It has no walls around it, only a brass handle shaped like the same pattern carved into the box.

You understand, suddenly, that the box was never meant to keep something in.

It was meant to teach you how to open.

You place your hand on the door.

From the other side comes the sound of your own voice, younger and older at once, saying, “I’ve been waiting.”

You turn the handle.

And wake with your fingers curled around an invisible key.
