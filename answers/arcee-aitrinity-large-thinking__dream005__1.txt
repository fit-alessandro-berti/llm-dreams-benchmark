
You push through the crowd, the murmur of a hundred conversations washing over you like static. The room seems to breathe, expanding and contracting with each pulse of music you can’t quite locate. You catch fragments:

A group in crisp, white linen is debating the philosophical implications of cloud shapes. One points at a cumulus formation only they can see and declares, “That one is clearly *ennui*.”

Near a towering, melting ice sculpture of a question mark, a circle of people in vintage clothing pass around a single, glowing peach. Each takes a bite and sighs with profound understanding before passing it on.

You glimpse your friend’s laugh—a bright, familiar sound—across a sea of strangers. But when you weave through the crowd, it’s not them. It’s someone with their same smile, but their eyes are the color of a sunset you’ve never seen.

A hush falls over one corner. Everyone is staring at a large, ornate door that wasn’t there before. A woman in a shimmering dress steps through from the other side, shaking rain from an umbrella. “It’s lovely on the other side,” she says, “but the party is so much louder here.”

You finally spot them by the window, backlit by a moon that seems too large for the sky. You call their name, but your voice comes out as the chime of a distant bell. They turn, and for a moment, you see a flicker of recognition, then a gentle, sad confusion. They mouth something you can’t hear and point to their chest, then to the crowd.

The room tilts. The groups begin to blur together, their conversations merging into a single, harmonious chord. You realize you are no longer looking *for* your friend, but *through* them, to the space they occupy in the dream. The gathering isn’t about finding one person; it’s about the beautiful, bewildering architecture of connection itself—a vast, temporary city built of glances, half-heard words, and the silent understanding that everyone here is both lost and exactly where they need to be.

You wake up with the taste of that glowing peach on your tongue and the certain, fading feeling that you were just at the most important party of your life, and you danced with everyone.
