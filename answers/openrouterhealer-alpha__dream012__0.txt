I grip the wheel with both hands, knuckles white against the salt-crusted wood, and taste iron in my mouth — fear, or maybe just the storm.

"Steady!" I shout, but the wind swallows the word before it reaches anyone's ears.

The crew's faces blur like watercolors. Some are young, some are old, some I think I've known my whole life, and some I've never seen before. That's the thing about this dream — the sea doesn't care about the details. It only cares about the wave.

And here it comes.

A wall of black water rises higher than the mast. For a moment, everything goes quiet — the kind of quiet that precedes something enormous. I can hear my own breathing. I can hear the creak of the hull deciding whether it wants to hold together or not.

I have to choose. And I know — the way you know things in dreams, without being told — that the choice matters, but the reason behind it matters more.

"Below deck!" I signal. Not with words. With something older. A gesture, a feeling I push outward like heat from my chest.

The crew moves. Not because they trust my plan — I don't *have* a plan. They move because I'm still standing. Because I haven't turned away.

The wave hits.

The ship doesn't fight it. It *goes with it* — sliding down the face of the water like a hand down a banister. For a breathless, impossible moment, we're surfing the storm itself, and I'm laughing because what else can you do when the world tilts that far sideways?

Then we're through. The sea exhales. The sky lightens just enough to show me the horizon — and on it, something I didn't expect.

A harbor. Warm lights. Still water.

I look down at my hands on the wheel. They're shaking.

I wake up — but the salt stays on my skin, and the feeling stays in my chest:

*You don't have to calm the storm. You just have to stay at the wheel.*
