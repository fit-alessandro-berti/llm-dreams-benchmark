You are walking onto a stage that’s too bright to look at directly. The air smells like warm paper and rain—like a library that’s been caught outside in a storm. A panel of judges sits behind a glass wall, their faces slightly blurred, as if the world can’t decide what they’re supposed to look like. At the center of the room, a podium waits for you, and a screen above it reads, in letters that rearrange themselves like thinking:

**GRAND PRIZE: YOUR DREAM JOB.**

You’re told the rules in a voice that sounds like it’s coming from inside your own chest.

“Every round, you’ll face a challenge,” it says. “You’ll have to solve it using what you *really* want. Not what you can explain. Not what you can perform. What you want when no one is watching.”

The first challenge appears immediately.

A door made of frosted glass slides open, and beyond it is a hallway full of doors—hundreds of them—each with a different label. You recognize some from your waking life: **WELCOME CALLS**, **SPREADSHEETS**, **MEETINGS**, **DEADLINES**, **INTERVIEWS**. But others are strange, tender, and completely unfamiliar: **THE MOMENT BEFORE YOU SPEAK**, **THE QUESTION YOU’RE AFRAID TO ASK**, **THE THING YOU WON’T ADMIT YOU MISS**.

A timer begins to tick backwards.

You try to choose the correct door based on logic—based on what a “successful person” would pick—but your hand stops halfway. The glass door is cold in a way that feels personal. You remember, suddenly, not a fact but a sensation: the moment you first felt alive while doing something—how time bent, how your thoughts stopped filing themselves into useful shapes.

In the dream, you don’t open the door with your fingers. You open it by stepping into the exact feeling you’d been avoiding.

The glass door melts like sugar.

Inside is a small room with a desk, a single chair, and a whiteboard. On the board, in your own handwriting, is written:

**ROUND ONE: WRITE THE TITLE OF YOUR LIFE AS IF IT’S HAPPENING.**

You pick up a marker. It doesn’t write ink—it writes daylight. Letters appear as if the room is remembering something.

*You are building things that matter.*

You can’t tell if the judges are impressed or confused, because the glass wall flickers and for a second the panel is not people at all, but mirrors reflecting your own face at different ages. Each version of you looks at you with the same quiet question:

*Is this what you mean?*

You feel your throat tighten, then loosen. You erase one line and rewrite it, more honestly:

*You are doing work that makes you forget to check the time.*

The timer stops. Somewhere behind the frosted glass wall, applause starts but sounds like pages turning.

A new door opens.

---

### The second challenge: The Interview With No Words

You’re led to a smaller stage. The podium is gone now. In its place sits a circular platform with a microphone embedded in the center. The judges stand around it like planets in a small private solar system. Each of them holds a clipboard made of translucent ice.

Your job title—the dream job—appears on the screen again, but this time it’s not readable. The letters keep shifting until they become either too clear to bear or too blurry to grasp.

The voice says, “Answer the question. But you may only use what you can’t lie about.”

A glowing prompt floats in front of you:

**WHY THIS JOB?**

You open your mouth. No sound comes out—just a thin thread of light that slips into the microphone and vanishes. Panic blooms, sharp and immediate.

You try again. Still nothing.

Then you realize the dream isn’t forbidding words; it’s forbidding *performance*. You are allowed to speak, but only if you’re speaking from the place that doesn’t need to convince.

So you stop trying to “answer” and instead you let your hands move.

You lift them, palms up, and the air around you begins to assemble itself into scenes: a desk with the perfect mess, a doorway you’ve walked through in your imagination a hundred times, a team laughing at something you didn’t expect to be funny, the kind of silence that happens after a good idea lands.

You don’t narrate it. You *show* it—like dreams do.

The ice clipboards melt into clear water and run down their forms, leaving behind the only words they can’t hide:

**She/He/They knows what it feels like.**

You hear a soft chime.

Round two complete.

A trapdoor opens under your feet—but it doesn’t drop you. It lowers the stage into a deeper room, one that seems older than the competition itself.

---

### The third challenge: The Problem You’ve Been Avoiding

The next space is a maze made from whiteboards. Every wall is covered in diagrams, arrows, and tasks—so many tasks that your eyes want to close just to escape.

The voice returns: “One path is what you want. One path is what you fear you can’t do. Choose, but don’t confuse them.”

A grid appears on the floor, with your name in the center like a stamp. Around it are six doors. Each door has a small paper sign taped to it:

1. **IMPOSSIBLE STANDARDS**
2. **THE WAITING**
3. **BEING SEEN**
4. **STARTING OVER**
5. **ASKING FOR HELP**
6. **DOING IT BADLY FIRST**

Your dream job isn’t behind all of them. It’s behind the door that holds the truth you’ve been postponing.

You notice something: none of the doors are locked. They all open easily.

That’s the trick. The dream wants you to choose with your body, not your arguments.

You walk toward **DOING IT BADLY FIRST**. Your stomach knots with dread, like your ribs are remembering rejection letters you never received. The thought of doing something imperfectly feels like standing naked in front of a crowd of ghosts.

But then you remember how you felt the first time you tried something that mattered—how you didn’t worry about being right, only about continuing.

You reach for the handle.

The sign falls off and dissolves into fireflies.

The door opens into a room full of sand. In the sand are tools—small, ordinary tools that look like they belong to your actual hands: a compass, a spool of thread, a screwdriver, a notebook. But the catch is that every tool is labeled with a warning:

- **THIS WILL BREAK**
- **THIS WILL NOT MATCH**
- **THIS WILL FEEL WRONG AT FIRST**
- **THIS WILL TAKE LONGER THAN YOU WANT**

A new prompt floats above the room:

**BUILD A BRIDGE TO YOUR DREAM JOB IN ONE MINUTE.**

There’s no time to plan. You crouch and start assembling the tools without trying to make them pretty. You connect what’s meant to connect, even if it’s crooked.

When the minute ends, you’re left with a bridge that wobbles.

It doesn’t matter. It stands.

You step onto it.

The bridge carries you forward like a decision you’ve finally made.

---

### The fourth challenge: The Uncomfortable Offer

You emerge into a bright hall where the air smells like citrus and metal. A receptionist desk appears out of nowhere, and behind it sits a person who looks like your future self but with a different hairstyle—like reality is trying on versions to see which one fits.

They slide a contract across the desk. It’s covered in fine print.

Your dream job is written at the top in crisp, confident font. Under it, a new clause appears:

**CONDITION OF EMPLOYMENT: YOU MUST TRADE SOMETHING.**

Your heart jumps. The dream seems to be asking for a sacrifice.

The receptionist says gently, “You don’t have to trade it to win. But you’ll only win if you choose to.”

You look closer and see the trade isn’t dramatic—no grand villain moment. The options are small and specific, like they were hiding in your daily life:

- **TRADE YOUR CERTAINTY**
- **TRADE YOUR NEED TO BE UNDERSTOOD IMMEDIATELY**
- **TRADE YOUR HABIT OF HIDING YOUR WORK**
- **TRADE YOUR FEAR OF FAILING IN PUBLIC**
- **TRADE THE VERSION OF YOU THAT WAITS TO BE READY**

You feel tears sting, not because it’s sad, but because it’s familiar. The sacrifices aren’t external. They’re internal habits.

You pick up the pen.

Your hand hovers. For a moment you try to bargain with yourself.

*What if I win and still keep the habit?*

The dream answers by showing you a quick montage: you in the dream job, but always behind a locked door, always shrinking your voice to avoid being blamed for existing.

Then it shows you something else—something terrifyingly hopeful: you in that same job, doing the thing you came for, and even when you mess up, you don’t disappear. You don’t punish yourself into silence. You keep building anyway.

You sign.

Not because you’re giving something away.

Because you’re finally choosing what to keep.

---

### The final challenge: The Door That Only Opens for You

The competition room dissolves into a long corridor. At the end is a single door, plain wood with no handle. Above it is the grand prize title, now readable and steady:

**YOUR DREAM JOB.**

But there’s no handle. No lock. Just the door and the pressure of your own breath.

The voice says, “This is not a test of skill. This is a test of whether you can walk through while still being afraid.”

You step closer. The door doesn’t move.

You realize the dream wants you to do what you often don’t do when you wake up: commit without proof.

You look down at your hands. They’re not trembling now. They’re dusty from building. They’re warm with effort. Real effort.

You press your palm against the door.

The wood becomes soft, like it’s made of memory. It doesn’t open outward—it opens inward, turning into a doorway made of light that looks like your own morning.

You feel the weight of every earlier round settle into one clear understanding:

Your dream job isn’t something you earn.

It’s something you become.

So you step through.

---

You expect to land in an office, a company lobby, a desk, a schedule. Something measurable.

Instead you land in a room that feels like a studio—but the walls are not blank. They’re covered in unfinished drafts of your ideas, half-written, half-lived. Tools sit where they always belonged to you. A whiteboard waits for your thoughts. A window shows a sky you’ve dreamed about before, the color of beginnings.

There’s no applause. No judges.

Just a quiet moment where you understand:

You’re not being interviewed anymore.

You’re working.

And the first thing you do—before anyone can evaluate you, before anyone can judge you—is you start.

The pen moves. The ideas line up. Time bends in your favor.

The dream fades around the edges, but you can still hear something like a stamp closing on paper.

**CONGRATULATIONS.**

And then, in the soft final seconds before waking, the voice returns one last time—not as a judge, but as a reminder:

“Now keep going, even after you forget you’re dreaming.”
