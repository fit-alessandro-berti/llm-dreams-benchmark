"Captain!" your first mate shouts, though when you turn to look at her, she has your mother's face. "The compass is spinning!"

Of course it is. You glance down and see the needle whirling madly, but instead of pointing north, it points to *memories*—there's your childhood home at 40 degrees, your first heartbreak at 175, something you've forgotten but desperately need to remember at 310.

The waves rise around the ship like dark cathedral walls. They should terrify you, but in the way of dreams, you feel a strange calm settle over your shoulders like a wool coat. You know, with that bone-deep dream-certainty, that the storm isn't trying to sink you. It's trying to *tell* you something.

"Lash yourselves to the mast!" you call out, and your crew obeys—except now there are fewer of them, and weren't there more a moment ago? The ship's deck stretches longer than it should, the bow disappearing into fog.

A wave crests above you, impossibly tall, and suspended within it you see lights. Windows. An entire city glowing inside the water, upside down, and the people there are looking back at you, waving—not in distress, but in greeting.

The wheel turns under your hands of its own accord, steering you *toward* the wave instead of away. Every instinct says fight it, but the dream says *trust*.

The water swallows the ship whole—

—and instead of drowning, you find yourself sailing through the drowned city's sky, your sails full of currents instead of wind, fish drifting past like birds. The storm is gone. It was never above you at all. It was a door.

Your first mate—your mother—someone—puts a hand on your shoulder and says, "You always knew the way. You just had to stop steering."

Somewhere far away, you can feel morning coming, the dream beginning to thin at the edges like fog. The city's lights flicker. You try to hold on, to memorize the route, to remember the door—

But you're already waking up.
