**Yes, I am dreaming.**

You are participating in a competition where the grand prize is your dream job. As the competition progresses, you face various challenges and...

First, the stage lights feel hotter than they should. A panel of judges sits in shadow, their faces flickering between everyone who ever inspired you and everyone who ever doubted you. The first challenge is simple: “Demonstrate that you already do the work better than the title requires.” You open a laptop that wasn’t there a second ago and build something alive—code that breathes, designs that move, words that rearrange themselves into the exact presentation the room needs. A timer shaped like a stopwatch made of burning string counts down. You finish just as the last ember falls.

Second challenge: the Maze of Prerequisites. Corridors labeled “Years of Experience,” “Specific Degree,” “Coffee Chat with Someone Who Knows Someone.” You keep taking wrong turns into fog that smells like rejection emails. Then you notice the walls are paper. You tear through them. On the other side is an empty room with a single desk and a note that reads: “The maze was optional. Most people never stop walking into walls.” You sit. The desk becomes a view looking out over the exact kind of work you want to be doing.

Third challenge: Impostor confetti. Sparkling notes fall from the ceiling—“Who do you think you are?” “They’ll find out.” You catch one. Instead of cringing you turn it over. The reverse side says only your name, handwritten, confident. You pocket them like currency. By the end of the round the confetti has become tickets: to the next round, to the interview that matters, to the first day.

Final round. You stand on a circular platform that slowly rises. Below you are all the earlier versions of yourself—the ones who almost quit, the ones who applied late, the ones who rewrote the cover letter at 2 a.m. They look up and… they are cheering. The host (who looks a lot like the future version of you) hands you a key that is also a contract that is also a set of keys to an office that smells like possibility and good coffee.

You wake up with the key still warm in your palm.

The dream isn’t finished. When the real competition starts tomorrow—or next week, or the day you finally hit send—you will remember: the walls were paper, the confetti was currency, and the people who once scared you are already rooting from the stands.

Now go win the waking version.
