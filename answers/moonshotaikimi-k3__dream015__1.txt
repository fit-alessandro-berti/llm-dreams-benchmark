...plant a garden.

You find the seeds in a rusted coffee tin inside a collapsed farmhouse — tomatoes, beans, and a packet labeled only "flowers." Your hands shake holding them. You haven't eaten in two days, and the smart thing would be to trade the seeds for food. Instead, you clear rubble from a sunlit patch of earth behind what used to be a library.

People notice. A woman with a rifle watches you from across the street for three days before finally crossing over. "That soil's no good," she says. But the next morning she brings a bucket of compost she's been saving, though she won't say for what.

Word travels strangely in the wasteland. A man arrives carrying a dented watering can. A child shows up with nothing, but sits beside you and names each sprout as it emerges — this one's Gerald, that one's Captain. Someone builds a fence from salvaged doors, each one a different color, and it shouldn't be beautiful, but it is.

The first tomato ripens in late summer, and it's dream-wrong in the way of dreams: too red, glowing faintly from within, warm as if it's been sitting under a sun that no longer exists. You cut it into twelve slices and everyone eats, and it tastes like *before*. Like summers none of you can quite remember.

The flowers bloom last. They're a color you've never seen while awake, and the bees — where did the bees come from? — hum a song you almost recognize. The child tugs your sleeve and says, "See? I told you it would work," though they never told you any such thing.

And the dangers are still out there — the raiders, the rust, the long cold nights. But the garden grows faster than any garden should, and nobody questions it, because this is the part of the dream where hope is allowed to be unreasonable.

You wake with dirt under your fingernails.
