The argument has become a vessel for everything unsaid: fears, insecurities, past hurts that have been accumulating like sediment.

You take a slow, deliberate step forward. Not to attack, but to bridge the gap. You reach out, not with a finger pointed in accusation, but with an open hand. Your friend flinches slightly, then freezes, watching your movement with wary curiosity.

"I’m tired," you say, your voice surprisingly soft, cutting through the tension like warm water. "I’m tired of fighting *you* when I should be fighting *for* us."

Your friend’s shoulders drop. The defensive posture crumbles. They let out a breath they seemed to have been holding for hours. The anger doesn’t vanish instantly, but it loses its fuel. In the quiet space between you, the dream shifts. The walls of the room seem to dissolve, replaced by a vast, starry sky. You are no longer in a cramped apartment or office; you are standing on a hill under a canopy of galaxies.

Your friend looks up, awe replacing frustration. "What is this?" they whisper.

"This," you say, taking their hand, "is what we’re losing sight of."

And in that moment, the argument dissolves not because one side won, but because both sides remembered why they cared enough to argue in the first place. The dream fades into a feeling of profound relief, leaving you with the lingering sensation that understanding is more powerful than being right.
