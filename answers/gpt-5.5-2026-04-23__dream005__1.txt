You are at a large social gathering where you only know one person. As you try to find them, you encounter various groups of people and overhear fragments of conversations that seem important, though you can’t quite understand why.

A woman in a green velvet coat is laughing beside a fountain filled with paper boats. A group of children in formal clothes are teaching an old dog how to dance. Somewhere, music is playing from a room you can’t find, and every time you turn toward it, the hallway stretches longer.

You ask someone if they’ve seen your friend, but when you say the name, it comes out as a color instead of a word.

“Oh,” the person says knowingly. “They were just here.”

They point toward a staircase that spirals upward and downward at the same time. You follow it, passing doors with tiny brass plaques: **Things You Forgot**, **Places You Almost Went**, **Conversations You Should Have Had**.

At the top—or maybe the bottom—you find a balcony overlooking the entire party. From there, you finally see your friend standing alone in the crowd, looking directly up at you, as if they have been waiting.

You wave.

They smile, but instead of coming closer, they place a finger to their lips and point behind you.

You turn around.

There is another you standing there, dressed for the party, holding a paper boat from the fountain.

“I’ve been looking for you,” they say.

Before you can answer, the music stops. Everyone in the building turns toward you at once, their faces calm and expectant.

Then the paper boat in your other self’s hand begins to unfold, larger and larger, until it becomes a map of the house. At the center of the map is a room you don’t remember entering.

Written across it in your own handwriting are the words:

**Wake up only if you’re sure.**
