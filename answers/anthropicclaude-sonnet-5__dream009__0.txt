You wake up in a completely different era, with no modern technology around. As you explore your new surroundings, you realize you need to figure out *when* you are before anything else makes sense.

The air smells like woodsmoke and wet straw. Your bed—if you can call it that—is a pile of furs on a dirt floor, and the walls around you are rough-hewn logs, chinked with moss. Sunlight cuts through a gap in the timber in a single gold blade, and dust motes swim through it like slow-moving fish.

You stand, and your clothes are wrong—coarse wool, too heavy, smelling of tallow and smoke. Your hands look like your hands, but the nails are rimmed with dirt that has clearly been there a while, like this body has been living a life you don't remember.

Outside, the village is small: a cluster of thatched roofs, a muddy path worn smooth by cart wheels, chickens picking at the ground. Someone is hammering metal nearby—rhythmic, unhurried. A woman passes with a yoke across her shoulders, two buckets swinging, and she nods at you like you're expected to nod back, like this is Tuesday.

You reach instinctively for a phone that isn't there. Then for a watch. Then you just stand in the mud, feeling the specific, embarrassing panic of a person who has taken modern conveniences so thoroughly for granted that their absence feels like missing a limb.

A boy runs past holding a wooden hoop, laughing. Somewhere a bell rings—not a phone, an actual bell, deep and resonant, calling people to something. Prayer, maybe. Or dinner.

You realize the only way forward is to *ask*. Not to demand answers, not to search a screen, but to open your mouth and speak to another human being and hope the words that come out make sense in whatever century this is.

You turn to the nearest person—an old man sitting on a stump, whittling something with unhurried hands—and you ask the only question that matters:

"Excuse me... what year is it?"

He looks up, squinting, like it's a strange thing to ask. Like the year is not a number he thinks about often, because why would he need to.

And just as he opens his mouth to answer—

*you wake up. For real this time. Alarm blaring, phone buzzing, the 21st century crashing back in around you like a wave.*

You never do find out what year it was.
