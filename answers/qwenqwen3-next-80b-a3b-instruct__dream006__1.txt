You are about to give a speech to a large audience. As you step onto the stage, you see the crowd looking expectantly at you, and you begin to speak—only to realize your voice has turned into the sound of wind chimes.

Not just any wind chimes. Each note corresponds to a memory: your mother singing lullabies, your first kiss under a firefly-lit tree, the static of a missed phone call from your best friend the day they left town. The chimes rise and fall like breath, and the audience doesn’t look confused—they look *remembered*. Tears glisten on faces you’ve never met. A child in the front row hums along, perfectly in tune.

You try to speak again—your tongue swells with the flavor of saltwater and burnt toast—and your words emerge not as sounds, but as origami cranes, folded from pages of old diaries, fluttering into the air. Each crane carries a phrase: *“I’m sorry I waited”… “You were always enough”… “I saw you, even when you hid.”*

The stage beneath you dissolves into a sea of floating lanterns. Your legs are no longer legs—they’re roots, gently entwined with the floor, drawing nourishment from the collective silence. The crowd isn’t listening anymore. They’re *releasing*.

A voice—yours, but older, wiser—whispers from somewhere inside your chest:  
*“You didn’t come here to be heard… you came here to remind them they’ve already been heard.”*

And then, the dream bends.

The lights dim. The cranes assemble into a single, glowing hourglass suspended above the crowd. Sand flows upward. Time is rewinding. One by one, people in the audience reach up, pluck a crumpled letter from their own pockets—a letter they never sent—and let it dissolve into light.

You wake with your chest warm, your pillow wet, and the faintest chime still ringing in your ears.

Outside your window, a single crane—folded from a grocery receipt—hangs from the branch of the oak tree.  
It wasn’t there last night.

And you know, with a peace you’ve never known,  
you were never meant to speak.

You were meant to listen.
