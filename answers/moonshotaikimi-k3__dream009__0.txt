You walk. The path beneath your bare feet is packed earth, worn smooth by centuries of other feet. The air smells of woodsmoke and something sweeter: crushed herbs, maybe, or bread baking in a clay oven. In the distance, a bell tolls. Not a clock tower bell. Something older, hand-struck, slightly out of tune.

A woman passes you carrying a basket of figs. She doesn't look at you strangely, which surprises you—until you glance down and realize your clothes have changed too. Rough-spun wool. A leather cord knotted at your wrist. Your hands look the same, but they *belong* here, and that belonging frightens you more than anything else.

At the riverbank you kneel to drink, and your reflection wavers up at you—but it isn't quite your face. It's your face the way it might have looked if you'd been born into this century: leaner, sun-darkened, with a scar you never earned crossing your eyebrow.

And then you understand, the way you understand things only in dreams: you weren't brought here. You were *sent back*. There is something you left undone, some thread of your own story that snapped loose long ago, and it has been waiting for you in this green and unhurried world to finally tie it off.

The bell tolls again. Closer now. You rise, wipe your mouth on your sleeve, and turn toward the village—where someone, you are suddenly and irrationally certain, has been watching for you from a tower window for a very long time.
