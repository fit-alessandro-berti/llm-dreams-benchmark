• You are in the middle of a heated argument with a close friend. The disagreement is about something important to both
  of you, and you decide to

  take the long route home instead of saying the last sharp sentence.

  You pull the hood of your jacket up, even though it’s warm, and keep walking. Your friend’s face stays with you—eyes
  narrowed, jaw tight, lips pressed as if holding back both apology and hurt. You both said too much, both believed too
  much. By the time you reach the old bridge over the dry canal, your breathing finally matches the rhythm of the
  river’s low, stone-dry sound.

  You stop and look back once. The streetlight catches on something moving in the puddles: a yellow umbrella, bright as
  a warning. They’re standing there, still. Just as you think to keep going, you turn around.

  “Hey,” you call. Your voice comes out gentler than you expect.

  They glance over, and for a moment neither of you says anything. Then your friend says, “You looked like you were
  leaving me.”

  You don’t answer right away. Instead, you open your bag and pull out the old photo you’d both carried around in your
  wallet for years—the one with both of you, ten years younger, grinning like the future was guaranteed. You hold it
  out.

  “It’s not that,” you say. “It’s that I’m scared.”

  The bridge light flickers, then steadies. Your friend laughs once—short, bitter, relieved. “Of what?”

  “Losing you over this.”

  Silence stretches, then breaks with rain. Not hard, just a fine mist that starts at the edge of your eyebrows and
  slides down your hands. Your friend steps closer, shoulder brushing yours.

  “Then tell me what matters,” they say.

  You don’t have to shout anymore.

  For the first time since the argument began, you both understand that the point wasn’t the issue, it was the fear
  underneath it. You keep walking, now side by side, umbrella forgotten, arguing still—but now softly, as if the night
  itself might overhear and call it something fragile, something true.
