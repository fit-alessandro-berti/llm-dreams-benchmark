**Despite the hardships, you decide to plant a single seed.**

Not metaphorically. Literally.

In the cracked asphalt of what used to be Main Street, between the rusting husks of cars and the skeletal remains of streetlights, you kneel with trembling hands and press a single dried corn kernel into the poisoned soil. It’s the last one from the scavenged packet you’ve been hoarding for weeks. Everyone told you it was madness—radiation, acid rain, the packs of feral dogs that hunt at dusk. But something in you refused to accept that the world had already ended.

You water it with the last of your filtered rainwater. You build a pathetic little barricade of broken bricks and wire around it. Then you sit and wait.

Days blur. You fight off a pack of mutated rats with a sharpened pipe. You trade a half-empty can of beans for a rusted watering can from a grizzled old woman who laughs at your “garden.” You almost die when a drone—still somehow operational after all these years—mistakes you for a threat and strafes the block. But every morning you check the tiny patch of dirt.

And one dawn, there it is.

A single, defiant green shoot pushing through the ash-gray soil like a middle finger to the apocalypse.

Word spreads in whispers through the ruins. People start showing up—not many, but enough. A former teacher with a book on hydroponics. A kid who somehow still knows how to fix solar panels. The old woman returns, this time carrying three more seeds and a look that says *maybe*. You don’t become a leader so much as a stubborn anchor. You teach them to filter water through charcoal and cloth. You rig up wind turbines from car alternators. You turn the old high school gym into a greenhouse, its shattered roof patched with scavenged plastic sheeting.

Months pass. The dangers never fully disappear—raiders still come, storms still rage, the sky still burns orange at noon—but something shifts. The garden grows. People laugh again, even if the sound is rusty from disuse. Children who have never known a world without ruins play tag between the cornstalks.

One evening, as the sun sets in a blaze of toxic beauty, you stand at the edge of your little field and watch the first real harvest being gathered. Someone hands you an ear of corn still warm from the sun. You take a bite.

It tastes like hope.

And then the dream begins to fade—not with fear, but with quiet satisfaction. You feel yourself drifting upward, the wasteland dissolving into softness. Just before you wake, you hear the old woman’s voice, cracked but steady:

“See? Even the end of the world can grow something new… if someone’s stubborn enough to plant the first seed.”

You open your eyes in your real bed, heart still pounding, the taste of impossible corn lingering on your tongue like a promise.

The dream is over.

But the decision you made inside it?

That part stays with you.
