...you come across a blank canvas propped against a drift of floating, opalescent geometric shards that hum like a plucked harp string.
The rest of the landscape thrums with constant, quiet chaos: the ground beneath your bare feet shifts from frosted moss to cracked obsidian every time you blink, trees made of liquid neon stretch and coil into impossible spirals before dissolving into flocks of tiny, bioluminescent moths, and the sky bleeds through every shade of the rainbow in slow, syrupy waves. The canvas, though, is a pocket of perfect stillness. Its edges are stitched with thread that glows soft silver, and it doesn’t reflect the shifting world around it—instead, it seems to drink the light, holding a quiet, matte void that makes your eyes ache a little if you stare too long, like looking into a space where no color has ever existed.
You reach out automatically, your fingertips brushing the fabric. It’s cool, like linen soaked in mountain stream water, and the second you make contact, the chaotic thrum humming around you drops to a hush. The neon tree you’d been watching melt into moths pauses mid-dissolve, the sky’s color shift stutters, and for the first time since you arrived in this world, something stays still long enough for you to look at it.
Without thinking, you drag your index finger across the canvas’s surface. It leaves no smear, no mark—until you think of the small bluebird that used to nest in the windowsill of your childhood home, the one with the patch of white feathers on its left wing. The second the memory solidifies, the mark blooms: first a smudge of soft, cornflower blue, then the curve of a wing, the sharp little beak, the patch of white so bright it seems to glow from inside the canvas. Before you can pull your hand back, the bird flutters off the fabric, its wings leaving trails of shifting, opalescent dust that hangs in the air for ten full seconds before dissolving.
You stare, half-laughing, half-terrified, and drag your finger across the canvas again, this time thinking of the old wooden door at the back of your grandma’s garden, the one painted chipped robin’s egg blue, with a brass knob that always stuck if you didn’t jiggle it just right. The mark blooms slower this time, the wood grain so real you can almost smell the damp pine and faded lavender sachets tucked behind it, and when it’s finished, the door swings open. There’s no wall behind it, only a soft, pillowy cloud of lavender light, and curled up on the threshold is a cat made of fused stained glass, its fur shifting from amber to emerald to deep indigo as it blinks up at you. Its purr sounds like wind chimes caught in a gentle breeze.
You lean in, resting your hand on the edge of the canvas, and realize two things at once: first, the things you draw on the canvas don’t stay trapped in its borders—they drift out into the shifting world, and they change slower than everything else, holding their shape for hours, days, maybe even longer, little anchors of permanence in the constant chaos. Second, when you glance down at the blank space left in the bottom corner of the canvas, you see a faint, smiling reflection of yourself, holding a paintbrush made of twisted starlight, like it’s been waiting for you to show up this whole time.
The stained glass cat jumps down from the door, pads across the canvas, and leans its head against your wrist, warm even through the dream. Somewhere in the distance, a chorus of soft, chiming laughs rings out, and the sky shifts to a soft, peachy pink—the first color you’ve ever seen stay still for longer than a few seconds.
You pick up the starlight paintbrush from the reflection’s hand, and you realize you don’t have to draw things you’ve lost, or things you know. You can draw whatever you want, and it’ll stay, for as long as you need it to.
