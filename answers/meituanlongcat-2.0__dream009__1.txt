You realize you need to find the river. Not because you're thirsty—though you are—but because the last thing you remember before this place was the sound of water. Your hands were in it, cold and moving, and then nothing. Now the air smells like woodsmoke and wild thyme, and the ground beneath your bare feet is a patchwork of moss and sun-warmed stone.

You walk. The landscape unfolds in layers: first a hillside stitched with sheep paths, then a stand of oaks so old their branches twist into familiar shapes—a cradle, a reaching hand, a question mark. You almost expect to see electricity lines smudged across the sky, but there is only the pale seam of a dirt road winding toward a cluster of low, thatched roofs.

A woman kneels at the edge of a garden, pulling turnips with a rhythm that feels like a language. When she sees you, she doesn't startle. She just looks up, her face brown and lined as walnut bark, and says, "You're the one who'll mend the bridge?" Her voice carries an accent that isn't quite foreign, just… older. Like the words are being translated from something you once knew.

You don't know what bridge. But in a dream, you don't question—you agree. She nods toward the tree line, where a gap in the woods pulses faintly, like a wound. "It's where the two times meet," she says. "Folks keep falling through."

So you go. And as you walk, the air begins to hum, a low frequency that vibrates in your teeth. You realize the need isn't just to mend some arch of wood or stone—it's to mend the break you feel in your own chest, the one that split open when you lost something precious in the modern world, something no technology could fix. The river sound grows louder, and you understand: the bridge isn't over water. It's over the memory of the last person you said goodbye to without meaning it.

When you reach the gap, you see them standing on the other side, slightly out of focus, exactly as they were that final morning. They're waiting. All you have to do is step forward and mean it this time.

But then the dream shifts—the bridge becomes a tangled root system, the light changes, and a child laughs somewhere behind you. You turn, and the question of what you needed to do lingers, unanswered, dissolving like mist on the river you never quite reached.
