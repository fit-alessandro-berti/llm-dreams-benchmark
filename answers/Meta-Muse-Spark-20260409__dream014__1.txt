The strangers don’t rush you or ask why you’re lost. One of them, a woman with silver rings on every finger, just nods toward a narrow side street where the noise of the city softens to a hum. 

“Come on,” she says, not unkindly. “The city’s loud, but it’s not heartless.”

A teenager with a battered skateboard hands you half a croissant, still warm. An older man in a newsboy cap points to the skyline and starts naming buildings like he’s introducing old friends: “That one’s the Ellis Building. She’s seen a hundred years of lost people find their way.”

As you walk with them, the neon signs stop blurring together. You notice the saxophone player on the corner, the bookstore cat sleeping in the window, the way the tram cables crisscross like someone tried to stitch the skyline together. 

One of them asks where you were trying to go, and you realize you don’t actually know. But that’s okay. The panic has ebbed. The woman with the rings laughs and says, “Sometimes the place you’re meant to find isn’t on a map anyway.”

They don’t walk you to a door or a destination. At the next intersection, they pause. The teenager bumps your shoulder and grins. “You’ve got it from here.” And somehow, you do. 

The city is still bustling, still huge, but it feels less like a maze and more like a story you’ve just been invited into. Your feet know which way to turn. 

You wake up before you take the step, but the warmth stays.
