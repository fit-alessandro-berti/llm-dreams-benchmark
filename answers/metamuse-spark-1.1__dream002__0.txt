You feel a storm approaching and the forest holds its breath with you.

One moment, the light is golden and filtered through the leaves. Dust motes hanging in the air. The path is soft under your feet, moss and pine needles so quiet you can hear your own breathing.

Then - 

The wind shifts.

It doesn't just get colder. It changes direction all at once, like the whole forest inhaled. All the leaves on all the trees flip over at the same time, showing their pale silver undersides. The green world turns silver.

The birds stop. That absolute, heavy silence that only happens right before.

You look up and the canopy that was sunny a second ago is now moving fast, too fast, branches swaying against a sky that bruised from blue to deep purple in a blink. The air smells like electricity and wet earth and something old, like stone.

You feel the pressure drop in your ears. Your hair lifts. 

Then you hear it - not thunder yet, but a low rumbling that seems to come from under the ground, under your feet. The trees lean in toward the path, creaking. You realize you've been walking barefoot, and the moss is now vibrating.

You should run. You should find shelter. But you don't feel afraid. You feel... recognized.

A single, fat raindrop hits your forehead, warm. Then another on your shoulder. Then the wind hits you all at once, and it isn't just wind - it's full of whispers, and they're not scary. They're saying your name, but in a language you forgot when you woke up as a child.

You start to run, not away from the storm, but with it, deeper into the trees. The path disappears. The rain comes down in a curtain now, but where it hits you, it doesn't make you wet. Each drop lights up for a second on your skin before sinking in, like little stars.

Then you see it. In the middle of a clearing that shouldn't be there is a huge, ancient oak, hollowed out. Its inside glows with a soft, amber light, like a lantern is burning inside. It is completely dry under there.

You duck inside just as the sky finally breaks open. The thunder cracks right overhead and shakes the whole forest, shakes the hollow, shakes your ribs.

And inside the tree, carved into the wood in your own handwriting, are the words:

*You made it back. Rest now.*

Outside, the storm rages and rages and washes the whole world clean. But inside, you are warm, and dry, and finally completely alone in the best way.

You sit down, lean your back against the wood, and listen to the rain drumming on the leaves above you like a lullaby, pulling you even deeper.
