The sky bleeds from sun-dappled green to bruised inky purple in the span of three heartbeats, and the first gust of wind doesn’t carry pine scent—it carries the sweet, sticky smell of cherry lollipops, a childhood treat you haven’t thought of in a decade.

The first raindrops land on your cheeks, but they don’t run cold and wet. They fizz on your skin, blooming into tiny golden fireflies that circle your head, casting warm streaks of light through the darkening trees. You stumble forward, chased by a low rumble of thunder that sounds like your grandma’s old record player skipping, and burst into a clearing you’ve never seen before, even in all the times you’ve wandered this woods in your waking life.

In the middle of the clearing sits a cottage stacked from sun-bleached wild mushrooms, its chimney puffing clouds of dandelion fluff instead of smoke. Leaning in its doorway is a girl with your own 10-year-old face, but her braids are woven through with the same fireflies that followed you through the trees, and she’s holding two chipped mugs of hot cocoa piled high with marshmallows. “Took you long enough to notice the storm,” she says, and her laugh sounds like the wind chimes that used to hang on your parents’ first porch. She explains the storm only comes for people who’re carrying too many borrowed worries—all the fears you stole from your friends’ stress, your parents’ grief, the strangers’ sad posts you couldn’t shake loose.

As another roll of thunder rumbles, you feel a lightness spread through your chest: the weight of a friend’s breakup you’d internalized, the guilt you’d carried for an offhand comment you made three years prior, all the what-ifs that never even belonged to you, dissolving into the rain like sugar in tea. The storm never feels scary, not once—it feels like a hug. You sit down next to your 10-year-old self on the mushroom step, and the fireflies from the raindrops weave a fuzzy blanket over your laps. By the time the sky starts to pinken at the edges, your cup is empty, and the trees behind you melt back into the familiar peaceful forest, like the storm and the cottage were just a quiet secret the woods had kept just for you.
