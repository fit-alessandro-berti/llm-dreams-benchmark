...and you begin to speak.

But the words don't come out as words.

They tumble from your mouth as golden confetti that sparkles in the stage lights, swirling into the shape of ancient constellations above the crowd. The audience doesn't clap—they *hum* in perfect unison, a single low note that vibrates through your bones like a tuning fork made of starlight. Your feet leave the ground. You're floating now, weightless, the microphone cord stretching behind you like a silver thread connecting you to the center of the universe.

You try to remember your speech. Something about courage. Something about the future. But every time you open your mouth, new wonders spill out: a flock of origami cranes that fold themselves into flying bicycles, a river of liquid laughter that pools at the front row and turns everyone's shoes into dancing fish. The theater ceiling dissolves into a night sky full of your childhood memories—there’s your third-grade hamster riding a comet, your grandmother’s laugh printed on the moon.

The crowd is no longer people. They’re all versions of you at different ages, every version you’ve ever been or could have been, all smiling with the same eyes. They reach up, not to catch the confetti, but to hand you something: a single glowing key.

You catch it.

The moment your fingers close around it, the entire auditorium folds in on itself like a paper fortune teller. You’re suddenly backstage again, but the “backstage” is the inside of your own mind, warm and echoing with the sound of a standing ovation that never ends.

You wake up.

Your heart is racing, your cheeks are wet with happy tears you don’t remember crying, and for the first time in years, you feel completely, ridiculously ready to say whatever it is you were always meant to say.

The dream doesn’t end when you open your eyes.

It just hands you the microphone.
