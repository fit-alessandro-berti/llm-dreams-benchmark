...build a garden.

Not a grand, sprawling farm—just a single, sun-dappled clearing in the shadow of a crumbling overpass. You scavenge a rusted wheelbarrow, a cracked watering can, and a tattered tarpaulin to catch rain. You spend days digging through debris, not for canned food or ammunition, but for *seeds*: a crumpled packet of sunflower seeds from a long-dead garden center, a few beans from a forgotten pantry, a sprig of wild mint rooted in a crack in the asphalt.

Dangers still lurk—you barricade the clearing at night, set tin-can alarms, and move with quiet caution—but this space is different. It’s a quiet rebellion. Each sprouting shoot is a statement against the silence, the decay, the forgetting.

One evening, as you carefully water a fledgling sunflower, you notice something odd: a small, smooth seed pod, perfectly preserved, nestled in the dirt. It’s not from anything you planted. It’s from a tree—an oak, maybe—that stood here long before the world broke. You hold it in your palm, its weight small and solid.

For the first time since the dreaming began, you feel a pure, uncomplicated hope. You don’t know if you’ll live to see the tree grow, if the garden will survive the next raider party or the coming winter. But in this moment, with the green shoots rising and the seed in your hand, you’re not just surviving. You’re *remembering*. You’re *creating*.

The dream holds on this image: your calloused hand cradling that seed, the first green leaves unfurling against gray ruin, and a single, bright sunflower turning its face toward a hazy, golden sunset—a quiet promise that something, however small, can still grow.

[The dream fades not with a bang, but with the soft rustle of leaves.]
