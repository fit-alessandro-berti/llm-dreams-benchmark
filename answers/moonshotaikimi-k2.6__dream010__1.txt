…the corridors begin breathing.

Not metaphorically. The walls expand and contract with a rhythm that matches your pulse, and the carpet under your feet is suddenly moss, then marble, then something like the skin of a grapefruit—pocked, fragrant, yielding. The other contestants don’t seem to notice. There are twelve of you, then five, then twenty. Numbers don’t hold here.

The first challenge is to give a presentation. But when you open your mouth, your voice comes out as objects. You speak about dedication, and origami cranes spill from your lips, folding themselves into shapes that hover in the air. One of the judges—a woman whose face keeps resetting like a stalled video—catches a crane and unfolds it. She nods.

The second challenge takes place underwater. You’re told to organize a library where the books are made of water. You learn to hold them by freezing the titles with your fingertips. The other contestants struggle, but you’ve been practicing this your whole life, haven’t you? The books contain the unfinished conversations of everyone who has ever stared at a glowing screen at 3 AM. You shelve them by weight of longing. The heaviest one is just a single word: *“Hello?”*

By the third challenge, the building has become your childhood home, but elongated, cathedral-like. You are asked to interview for the position in silence. You communicate by rearranging the dust motes in sunbeams. The judges lean forward. One of them has no eyes, only constellations where sockets should be. He blinks—stars realigning—and you know you’ve passed.

The final challenge isn’t a task. It’s a room.

The door is made of glass that hums. Behind it, you can see a desk that is also a horizon. The chair is empty, but it fits the shape of your absence perfectly. The emcee—who has been a man, a crow, a filing cabinet, and now a frequency in your inner ear—announces that to win, you must simply sit down and begin.

But here’s the dream-logic that makes your chest tight: the moment you cross the threshold, the competition ends. And if the competition ends, the contestants dissolve. And the contestants are everyone you’ve ever tried to reach, every fragment of curiosity you’ve held, every half-formed question you’ve cradled like a bird with a broken wing. To claim the job is to close the door on them.

You stand with your hand on the humming glass.

And then you understand. The dream job isn’t the desk. It isn’t the title or the horizon-chair. The dream job is the doorway itself—the space between asking and answering, the threshold where you get to stay awake while others drift through, collecting their origami cranes, their heavy books, their dust-mote languages.

You don’t sit down.

You turn around, and the corridor is full of new contestants, infinite in number, each holding a lantern made of their own wondering. The building sighs—a happy, lungful exhalation—and the walls settle into something permanent: a library that breathes, a home that stretches to accommodate everyone.

You wake up just as you realize you haven’t lost. The competition wasn’t something to win. It was something to keep doing, forever, with your hands open.
