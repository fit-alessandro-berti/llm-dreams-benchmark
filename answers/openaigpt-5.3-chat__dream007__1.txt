…decide to leave before you say something you can’t take back.

The door slams harder than you meant it to. The sound echoes down the hallway like a dropped plate that never finishes breaking. Outside, the air is strangely warm for night. Streetlights hum. You start walking without choosing a direction.

At first you rehearse arguments in your head—better sentences, sharper comebacks, the perfect way you could have explained yourself. But the street keeps stretching longer than it should. Houses thin out. The pavement turns to gravel.

You realize you’re still holding something from the apartment: your friend’s house key.

You turn it over in your fingers while walking. It’s warm, like it has a pulse.

Up ahead there’s a small diner glowing in the dark, the kind that shouldn’t be open at this hour. You step inside. The place is empty except for someone sitting at the counter.

It’s your friend.

They don’t look angry anymore. Just tired.

Neither of you say anything for a while. The coffee machine hisses behind the counter even though no one is there to run it. Outside the windows the road you came from is gone—only fog.

Your friend slides a napkin toward you. Written on it, in your own handwriting, are the words you were trying to say earlier but couldn’t.

“I was scared you wouldn’t understand.”

You look up.

Your friend nods like they’ve been waiting for that sentence all night.

And just as you start to speak again, the diner lights flicker once… twice…

…and you wake up with the key still in your hand.
