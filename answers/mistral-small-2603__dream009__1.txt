**…find a way to survive.**

The air smells of woodsmoke and damp earth. Around you, the world hums with unfamiliar sounds—hooves on dirt, the creak of a wooden cart, distant laughter in a language you don’t recognize. The sky is a vast, unbroken blue, and the sun hangs too high, too bright, as if time itself has stretched.

You’re dressed wrong. Your clothes—soft cotton, sturdy stitches—are too clean, too *modern* for the rough-spun tunic and leather belt you now wear. A woman with sun-browned arms and a basket of apples pauses as she passes, her eyes flicking over you with quiet suspicion. You try to speak, but the words that come out are clumsy, accented, wrong.

*Where am I?*

The answer comes not in words, but in the weight of the knife at your belt—a tool, not a weapon. In the way the villagers eye you, not with fear, but with calculation. You’re not from here. That much is clear.

And then you see it: a river, wide and slow, cutting through the village. A man in a tattered cloak stands at the bank, staring into the water as if it holds answers. His hands are scarred, his face lined with years you can’t begin to guess. He turns, and his gaze locks onto you.

*"You’re not from the valley,"* he says. It’s not a question.

You shake your head, then force yourself to speak, to explain—*somehow*—that you don’t belong. That you don’t even know *when* you belong.

His lips curl, not in kindness, but in something sharper. *"Then you’ll have to prove it."*

Prove what? That you’re not a spy? A thief? A ghost?

Before you can ask, he tosses you a rough-hewn paddle. *"The current’s strong today. If you can cross to the other side before the sun sets, maybe we’ll talk."*

The river roars ahead of you, dark and hungry. The villagers watch. The man waits.

And you realize, with a sinking dread, that this isn’t just a dream.

It’s a test.

*(What do you do next?)*
