Despite the hardships, you decide to **plant a flower in the center of the ruins.**

It is a foolish gesture. There is no soil left to hold a root, only pulverized concrete and grey dust. There is no water in the canisters, only a thick, rusty sludge. Yet, you dig your hands into the ash until your fingers bleed, and you place a single seed from your pocket into the hollow of your palm.

You close your fist. You do not look for the sun, for the sky is choked with clouds that smell of copper and old ash. Instead, you listen to the sound of your own breath, slow and rhythmic in the quiet wasteland. You focus on the memory of rain. You focus on the warmth of a summer day that no longer exists.

As you concentrate, the grey world begins to pulse. The dust shifts, not with the wind, but with the rhythm of your heartbeat. From the hollow in your hand, a shoot of impossible blue-green breaks through the stone. It unfurls rapidly, twisting upward, not with the frantic growth of nature, but with the deliberate grace of thought.

Where the stem touches the ground, the concrete cracks and softens. The grey ash turns to rich, dark loam. The clouds above swirl and part, revealing a sun that looks too bright to be real, like a bulb switched on in a closed room.

You realize, then, that you are not planting a garden to survive the winter. You are planting it to remember that there was ever a spring.

And as the first petal blooms, releasing a scent of ozone and lilies, you feel a sudden, violent jerk. The ash falls away like water. The sky dissolves into the white light of a ceiling. You wake up gasping, your heart still racing, your hand clenching around the empty air, holding onto the memory of a blue flower that no longer exists outside of your mind.

It is morning. The world is real again, and for the first time today, it feels safe enough to try again.
