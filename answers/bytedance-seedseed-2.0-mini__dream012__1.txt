You are the captain of a ship in the middle of a turbulent sea. Your crew looks to you for guidance as the storm worsens, and you slam your palm against the wheel’s polished teak rim, barking orders in a voice roughened by salt spray and years of salt-stung laughter and late-night watches. “Reef the foresail and lash the life buoys tight—now! The bilge pumps are straining, so pass the word to bail any standing water from the lower decks!” Your first mate, a grizzled man with a scar slicing through one eyebrow, nods once and bounds for the rigging, his boots slipping twice on the sloshing, seaweed-slick deck before he catches himself, knuckles white around the rope.

A rogue wave slams into the port side next, tilting the ship so far I can see the swirling, inky black water churning below the rail. A young apprentice stumbles, flying toward the edge, and I dive to grab the collar of his oilskin coat, yanking him back just as another wall of water crashes over the bow, drenching us both to the bone. When I stand, I glance at the compass box mounted to the wheel’s pedestal—and my throat goes dry: the glass has shattered, the magnetized needle spinning wildly in a blur of brass and broken glass, rendered useless by the storm’s ferocious electromagnetic pull.

Panic flutters through the crew for a heartbeat, but I lock eyes with the first mate, and my voice steadies, sharp as a knife edge: “We don’t need the compass. My old man taught me to read the swell—when the waves start to flatten out and run shallower, we’re near shelter.” I don’t know why the words come out, not with any formal training, just a hazy memory of sitting on my dad’s lap as we sailed home from a fishing trip, watching seabirds ride the swells.

Then something surreal cuts through the chaos: a flock of common terns, normally the first to flee a storm, burst through the squall, circling low over the ship’s bow, their wings beating furiously against the wind. They don’t fly away—they fly toward a narrow, silver gap in the churning black clouds, a break in the storm that wasn’t there a minute before. I wheel the ship hard to starboard, following their path, the crew silent now, trust replacing the fear as we push through the gap.

The rain slows to a mist, the wind dies down to a gentle gale, and suddenly we’re gliding into a tiny, hidden rocky cove, its cliffs sheltered from the worst of the storm. We drop anchor in the shallow, glittering water, and within minutes the last of the storm clouds drift away, leaving a sky streaked with pink and orange sunset. A single bright star pricks through the twilight, and for a second, the water around the ship glows with faint, silver stardust, like someone spilled a jar of moonlit dust.

One of the terns lands light on my shoulder, chirping once before flapping off to join the rest of the flock perched on the ship’s rigging. The crew cheers, rough and hoarse, some slapping each other on the back, others collapsing onto the deck, laughing and crying all at once as they unload the last of the gear. I lean against the wheel, my bones aching, and stare out at the calm, glowing water, and for a second I swear I can hear my dad’s voice laughing in the wind, telling me I did good.
