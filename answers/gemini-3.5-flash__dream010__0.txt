First, I am asked to catalog the shadows of a city that has not yet been built. I run through streets made of tinted glass, catching dark silhouettes in a butterfly net woven from cello strings. My rivals—shadowy figures with faces like grandfather clocks, their hands ticking furiously—whisper doubts into the wind, trying to scatter my collection. But I manage to capture the shadow of a sigh, and the judges, who are three giant, hovering owls with warm human eyes, nod in silent approval.

Suddenly, the glass city melts. The floor beneath me becomes a deep, still ocean of ink, and the arena transforms into a vast library where the bookshelves stretch infinitely upward into a violet sky. This is the second challenge: I must retrieve the *Book of Unsaid Things* from the very bottom. 

I dive in. The ink is warm and smells of peppermint and old paper. As I swim deeper, the pressure isn't physical, but emotional—it is the heavy, quiet weight of all the apologies never spoken, the love letters never sent, and the ideas forgotten upon waking. I find the book resting on a pedestal of coral. When I open it, the pages are entirely blank. I realize the challenge is not to find a written story, but to write one using only the movement of my hands through the water. I trace a wave, a spiral, a heartbeat. The ink glows, absorbing my touch, and the book floats to the surface on its own, leaving a trail of starlight behind it.

Finally, only I and one other competitor remain. 

The setting shifts to a tightrope stretched between two crescent moons. Below us is a swirling void of colors I have no names for. My opponent stands at the opposite end. It is a mirror reflection of myself, but made of pure, crackling electricity and cold logic. 

Between us, hanging in the air, is the grand prize: a glowing key made of stained glass. It is the key to the ultimate job—the **Architect of the Horizon**, the one who designs the quiet spaces where human imagination meets reality.

My reflection lunges forward, reaching greedily for the key. But I remember the logic of this place. In a dream, to grasp too tightly is to lose your grip entirely. 

I do not reach for the key. Instead, I close my eyes, step off the tightrope, and let myself fall into the void of nameless colors.

The wind rushes past my ears, sounding like a distant piano. But I am not falling; the void is rising to meet me. My reflection shatters with the sound of a chiming bell. The stained-glass key dissolves into a warm, luminous rain, sinking into my skin. 

I don't need to hold the key, because I have become the door. I am no longer just a competitor; I am the canvas, the sky, and the horizon itself. 

As a soft, waking light begins to bleed through the edges of my eyes, I realize I didn't need to win the job. I was already doing it.
