I open my mouth to deliver the opening line I’d practiced a hundred times in the shower and on the drive over—“Thank you all for being here today to talk about community-led green spaces”—but what comes out is, “The first rule of talking to goldfish is never mention the shag carpet they were found under.”

The entire auditorium leans in, rapt. I glance down at my notecards, which are now covered in doodles of sunflowers wearing tiny top hats, and realize somewhere between pulling into the parking garage and stepping onto the stage, I’d forgotten every single word of the carefully researched, 20-minute speech I’d spent three weeks writing. Half the crowd are people I haven’t spoken to since high school, the other half are side characters from the after-school cartoon I used to watch before bed when I was 10. My old third grade teacher sits front row next to the barista who makes my oat milk latte every morning, both of them holding giant goldfish bowls, scribbling notes like my random non-sequitur is exactly the keynote address they flew across the country to hear.

Dream logic holds, so I don’t stop talking. The more I ramble, the more engaged they get. I tell them about the time I tried to bake a cake for my neighbor’s cat’s 12th birthday, used salt instead of sugar by accident, and the cat loved it so much it left a dead field mouse on my porch as a thank you. I tell them about the time I got lost in IKEA when I was 7 and found a secret greenhouse behind the kitchen display, full of plants that whispered knock-knock jokes when you watered them. The crowd laughs, cheers, a few people in the back are crying a little like I’ve just told them the most meaningful story of their lives. The guy in the 10th row who looks exactly like my dad is standing up and clapping so hard his flannel shirt comes untucked, and I only now notice the stage lights are actually fireflies, drifting down to land on the shoulders of the people in the front row, no one reacting at all to the soft glow buzzing around their heads.

My hands, when I hold them up to gesture, are made of warm honey, dripping slow onto the stage floor, which I just realize isn’t the polished wood I thought it was, but soft clover dotted with tiny white wildflowers. The mic cord tangled at my feet has turned into a piece of red licorice, and when I glance off to the side of the stage, there’s a penguin in a tiny tuxedo standing there holding a neon sign that flashes YOU’RE DOING GREAT, and I nod at it like that’s the most normal thing in the world.

I get so caught up in rambling about the secret IKEA greenhouse that I forget I was supposed to be announcing the new community garden initiative, until a tiny paper crane flutters down to land on my honey hand. It’s made from a scrap of my original speech notes, and when I unfold its wing, I read: “free seed swaps every Sunday, compost bins for every block, a bronze statue of the neighborhood’s first golden retriever mayor in the center plot.”

I read the line out loud, and the whole crowd roars. The penguin waddles over, tucks the neon sign under its flipper, and hands me a handful of sunflower seeds that glow faintly gold. The guy who looks like my dad is now wearing a hat made of lettuce, and he’s waving it in the air. The crowd starts singing the lullaby my mom used to sing to me when I was small, and I’m singing too, my voice a little wobbly, the honey on my hands drying into a soft gold dust that floats up to swirl with the fireflies.

I don’t want the speech to end. I know if I stop talking, I’ll wake up, so I keep rambling, making up stories about talking squirrels and cloud-shaped park benches and libraries where the books read themselves to you. But the dream has its own rules: just as I get to the part where I’m describing the library’s cat librarian who only grants books to people who bring her tuna, the dandelion puff I’d grabbed to test the mic earlier drifts past my face, and all its seeds turn to glitter, and the whole auditorium fades to soft white.

The last thing I hear, before I open my eyes in my own bed, is my old art teacher yelling from the front row, “WE BREAK GROUND ON THE GARDEN NEXT TUESDAY! DON’T FORGET THE SUNFLOWER TOP HATS!”
