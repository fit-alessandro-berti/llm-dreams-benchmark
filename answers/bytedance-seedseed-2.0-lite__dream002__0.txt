the air thickens with the sharp, nostalgic smell of the rubber rain boots I outgrew when I was seven—no acrid storm wind, just that specific, familiar scent that yanks me straight to childhood afternoons hiding from thunder under my grandma’s porch. The sky doesn’t just gray; it bleeds deep indigo, like someone upturned a jar of ink across a watercolor canvas, and the first rumbles that shake the oak boughs aren’t thunder at all: they’re the giddy, crinkly laugh grandma used to make when she’d sneak extra chocolate chips into my pancakes, faint but warm, weaving through the wind.

I fumble to run for shelter, only to notice the dirt path under my feet has softened into dandelion fluff that never sinks under my weight, and the first raindrops that hit my cheeks are sticky, sweet, like lemonade. Half a mile ahead, a cottage glows through the trees—but its roof is stacked with tattered copies of the picture books I read until their spines split, its chimney puffing wispy marshmallow smoke that sticks to the air like cotton candy. When I throw open its splintered wooden door, it’s not a cottage living room I step into: it’s the backseat of my parents’ old minivan, the one I took to every summer camp, and every lost thing I’d grieved as a kid is piled soft on the seats: my floppy-eared rabbit I left at an Ohio rest stop, the silver dollar the tooth fairy left that I dropped at a playground, the half-finished friendship bracelet I’d been making for my first best friend before we moved across the country.

The storm raps gentle against the minivan windows, and when I turn, grandma is in the passenger seat, stirring a mug of hot cocoa that steams up the glass. “These woods’ storms don’t chase you,” she says, like she’s been waiting for me. “They lead you back to the pieces you left along the path, too busy growing up to stop and carry them with you.” I sit down next to her, and the lemonade raindrops keep tapping, and for the first time in years, I don’t feel like I’m running to catch up with anything. I just am.
