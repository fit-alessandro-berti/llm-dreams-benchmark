...realize that I haven't prepared anything at all. My notes—did I even bring notes? I pat my pockets. Empty. The podium in front of me has a glass of water and nothing else.

The crowd keeps growing. Faces stretch back farther than the room should allow, and somehow I recognize all of them: my third-grade teacher, a barista from a coffee shop I visited once, my grandmother who looks exactly as she did in old photographs.

I open my mouth to speak and discover I don't know what the speech is even about. The title was on a banner somewhere, but when I look up, the letters rearrange themselves into shapes I can't read.

Then I glance down and notice I'm not wearing shoes. Or—wait, the floor has become sand. Warm sand. The audience doesn't seem to mind. They're still waiting, patient and kind, leaning forward.

So I just start talking. Words come out, and they feel important, profound even, though I can't quite hear them myself. The crowd nods. Someone in the back begins to applaud. The applause sounds like rain.

And the strange thing is—it's going wonderfully. The terror melts into something warm. I was never unprepared. I always knew exactly what to say.

The sand is rising now, gently, up to my knees, and I keep speaking, and everyone is smiling, and—
