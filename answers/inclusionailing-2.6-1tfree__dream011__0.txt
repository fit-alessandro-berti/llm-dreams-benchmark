The questions are not printed on paper at all.  
They are written in a slow, glowing script that unspools across the inside of your eyelids, so you have to blink to read each one and blink again to hold it in place. The first question is in a language you’ve never studied, yet you can feel its grammar like the shape of a key you once swallowed years ago.  

You glance at the clock and see that the hands are sweeping counterclockwise, but the proctor’s voice is a tide that fills the room, saying you have exactly until the bell of your own pulse stops ringing. The room smells of ozone and old library paste. The rows of desks stretch backward until they become ladders, and above you the ceiling is a sheet of dark water where pale shapes drift, taking the form of jobs you have never heard of, careers that haven’t been invented, lives that end in quiet, useful flames.  

You pick up your pencil and realize it is a thin bone that fits exactly like a childhood memory you didn’t know you’d forgotten. The glowing script rearranges itself into the name of your mother, then into a map of a city you’ve never visited but know you must save. Your fingers are ink. Your pulse is underlining every answer you haven’t given yet.  

When you finally write something, the paper sighs and the margins bloom with small, necessary disasters—missed trains, apologies that arrive late, kisses that work anyway. You understand, suddenly, that the exam is not testing what you know, but what you are willing to become in order to pass.  

The glowing script peels away from your eyelids and settles on your skin like a second shadow. The bell rings—not in the room, but inside the bone of your chest—and you realize the exam has already been graded, and the grade is a doorway. You stand, the door is already open, and your future career is waiting in the hallway, holding a clipboard made of your childhood laughter, ready to sign you into the only life that fits the shape of your leaving.
