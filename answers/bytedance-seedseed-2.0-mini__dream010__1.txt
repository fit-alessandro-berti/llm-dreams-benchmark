You are participating in a competition where the grand prize is your dream job. As the competition progresses, you face various challenges and now stand alone in the final round, the sterile fluorescent competition hall melting into something softer—walls lined with floating shelves holding half-finished picture books that flutter like pigeons when warm, sweet wind seeps through the windows. Your dream job, you’d learned weeks prior, is senior illustrator for *Bright Skies Medical Kits*, a nonprofit that distributes art supplies and custom storybooks to kids undergoing long hospital stays, and you’ve spent months poring over their past work, memorizing the way they draw scraped knees as shooting stars and IV lines as constellations.

The first challenge had been a speed round: draft a 4-page spread in 20 minutes about a kid turning a hospital stay into an adventure, which you’d aced by writing about a boy who turned his hospital gown into a superhero cape stitched with band-aid patches. The second had been a team challenge, where you’d rallied your panicking group to turn a pile of scrap fabric and glue sticks into an interactive story prop for a fictional 7-year-old patient, winning extra points for leaning into a child’s love of chaotic sticker-covered details. But this final challenge feels like it’s reaching inside your chest: the judges set a crumpled hospital wristband, a half-used sticker sheet of space rockets, and a stack of blank index cards on a table, and one of them, a woman with a stethoscope tattoo curled around her wrist, says, “Tell us the story you’d write for the kid who wore this.”

You pick up the wristband. It’s smudged with blue acrylic paint, and scrawled on the back in wobbly child’s handwriting is “LUNA’S SPACE ADVENTURE”. You’d met Luna briefly that morning: she’d been sitting in the waiting area, picking at the hem of her hospital gown, and you’d shared a strawberry lollipop with her right before the final round started. You don’t remember telling her anything about your own story ideas, but she’d leaned up and whispered that her favorite thing to imagine was floating off her hospital bed to visit the moon, where she’d plant a garden of band-aid flowers.

You grab a broken-crayon tip of sky-blue, your hands fumbling a little in the dream’s hazy warmth, and start sketching: Luna floating just above her hospital bed, her IV line trailing a ribbon of shimmery stardust, her teddy bear dressed in a tiny astronaut helmet she’d made out of a plastic cup and a pipe cleaner. You scrawl the story below the drawing in messy, looping handwriting: “Even when your body is stuck, your heart can fly to the moon.” When you hold up the index card, the judges lean in, and the stethoscope-tattooed woman pulls out a sealed envelope from her bag—addressed to the new senior illustrator, sealed with a space rocket sticker exactly like the ones on Luna’s sticker sheet.

Just as she’s about to hand it to you, the floating shelves of picture books start to sway, and Luna pops out from behind one, clutching your half-finished speed-round drawing that you’d accidentally left on the competition hall counter that morning. “You forgot this!” she says, grinning, and the dream shifts again: suddenly you’re standing in a sunlit hospital playroom, with stacks of *Bright Skies* kits spread out across the floor, and a group of kids are grabbing your drawings, laughing as they point to the shooting-star scraped knees and constellation IV lines. The strawberry lollipop stick you shared with Luna is still tucked in your jeans pocket, and when you blink the dreamy fuzz away, your phone buzzes with a new email: it’s from *Bright Skies Medical Kits*, telling you your application has been shortlisted for an in-person interview, and they’d love to hear the story you wrote for Luna.
