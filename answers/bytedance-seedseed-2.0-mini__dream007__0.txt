I flop down on the cracked picnic table bench beside you, pushing the notebook into your hands. “I messed up. I should’ve talked to you first. You don’t push me away when I reach over to offer you the half-empty lemonade we’d packed for the drive. Instead, you sniffle, flipping to the page where we’d scribbled a to-do list that included “buy extra blankets for grandma’s couch” and “steal her famous peach pie recipe.” “I was so mad because I thought you forgot that this trip was the only thing that made sense after… after she asked me to take care of her photo albums when she’s gone,” you mumble, and my own tears finally spill over.

We sit in the quiet for a minute, flipping through the notebook together, and you finally sigh, crossing out the exact departure date we’d circled and writing “after first quarter of job” in the margin. “We can still split the drive. I’ll drive the first half, you the second. And we’ll bring her albums along, so we can sit under those stars and show her all the pictures we found.”

I lean my head on your shoulder as the golden hour sun filters through the oak trees above us, and the crumpled ticket stub to the desert campground we’d pre-booked sticks out of the notebook’s pages. The tension in my chest unravels, slow and soft, like the way the wind rustles the grass around the table. For a second, I’m not mad anymore—we’re just two friends fixing the thing that mattered most, together.
