I find myself in a post-apocalyptic world, with resources scarce and dangers everywhere. Despite the hardships, I decide to open a library.

Not a fortress. Not a weapons cache. A library.

The building is half-collapsed — an old municipal center with a roof that mostly holds. I spend weeks hauling books from houses, offices, schools. Novels waterlogged and swollen. Textbooks with covers warped into strange shapes. A children's picture book about a rabbit, its pages fused together, still I keep it.

People come at first for practical reasons. Someone finds a manual on water purification. A teenager discovers an engineering textbook and starts repairing the solar panels on the east wall. An older woman tears up holding a poetry collection — she says she'd read that same copy to her daughter years ago.

But something else happens slowly. People linger. They sit on overturned crates and read passages aloud. Arguments start — real ones, passionate ones — about what a story means or whether a historical account can be trusted. Children gather to hear tales, and the storytellers begin adding their own endings, their own characters, their own world into the old ones.

I realize the books were never the point. The point was giving people a reason to sit together and remember that humans built all this once — the language, the ideas, the wild audacity of imagining things that don't exist yet.

Danger still lurks outside. Resources are still thin. But inside this crumbling building with its crooked shelves and flickering candles, something is being reconstructed that no disaster can fully destroy.

The last book I place on the shelf is the waterlogged rabbit picture book. A child opens it that same afternoon, carefully peeling the pages apart, and begins to read.
