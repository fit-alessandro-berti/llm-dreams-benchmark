Your friend stops mid-sentence. They take the ribbon carefully, and as they read, the room begins to change — the walls soften into the kitchen of your childhood, or maybe theirs, you can't tell anymore, and somehow that doesn't matter.

When they finish, they look at you, and without a word they reach into their own chest and pull out their side of the argument. You unroll it slowly. The handwriting is different, but the words are the same. The same fear. The same hope. You were both fighting for the same thing all along — you'd just been saying it in different languages.

You laugh, and they laugh, and the sound of it makes the argument itself — which has been standing in the corner this whole time, you realize now, shaped like a small gray animal — finally lie down and close its eyes.

You sit together on the kitchen floor. Outside the window it's snowing, or the snow is falling upward, or the stars have drifted very close to the glass. Someone is making tea. You don't remember what the argument was about anymore, only that it mattered, and that you were heard.

And just before you wake, your friend says the thing that in waking life they've never quite been able to say — and you understand it the way you only understand things in dreams: completely, and without words.
