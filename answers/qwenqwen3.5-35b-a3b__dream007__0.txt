You decide to stop arguing and simply close your eyes.

As soon as your lids lower, the heat in the room vanishes, replaced by the cool, static hum of a library at midnight. The walls dissolve into shelves that grow endlessly into a dark ceiling, filled not with books, but with thousands of ticking clocks. Your friend is still standing across from you, but they look slightly out of focus, like a photograph left too close to a flame. They are holding the clock you dropped earlier during the argument, and the second hand is moving backward.

"You keep looking at the door," your friend says. Their voice sounds like it's coming from inside the room, not their throat.

"It's not about winning," you say, opening your eyes. The library shelves shimmer and rearrange into a vast, open ocean. You are standing on the surface of the water, but the floor beneath your shoes is still the floorboards of your bedroom.

"We are arguing over which way to row," your friend replies. They point at the horizon. There is a sunrise there, a violent, purple explosion that feels too bright for this setting.

You look down at your hands. They are transparent, rippling like the water. You realize the disagreement wasn't about the future or the plan; it was about the vessel you were using to get there. You decide to reach out and take their hand. The moment your fingers touch, the ocean stops moving. The sun freezes in place, caught in a bubble of amber light.

"Okay," you whisper. "I'm ready to stop rowing."

You wake up. Your heart is beating fast, but your room is quiet. On the nightstand, your phone lights up with a notification from your friend: *Hey, did I say too much last night? I don't remember what I said.*

You sit up and realize you don't feel angry anymore. You just smile, turn off the light, and for the first time in months, you don't know what the next day looks like—and that feels like enough.
