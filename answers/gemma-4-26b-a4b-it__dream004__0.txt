I feel a strange tugging at my own edges. 

The more the canvas consumes, the more the world around me begins to fray. The sky, once a swirling marble of tangerine and teal, starts to unravel into thin, translucent threads. The ground beneath my feet—a shifting mosaic of velvet shadows—begins to liquefy, flowing upward toward the frame.

I realize then that I am not the artist. I am the medium.

My thoughts, once heavy and linear, begin to fracture into prismatic shards. I try to pull my hand away, but my arm has already become a brushstroke of iridescent gold, trailing behind me in a long, glowing arc. My memories—the smell of rain on hot asphalt, the sound of a distant cello, the feeling of sunlight on skin—are being pulled from my chest, manifesting as splashes of textured crimson and deep, melancholic indigo across the growing masterpiece.

The world is disappearing, collapsing into the center of the frame. The roar of the colors fades into a single, perfect note. As the last of the landscape is swallowed, leaving only the vast, shifting emptiness, I find myself standing within the frame itself. 

I am no longer looking at the art. I *am* the art. And as the final stroke of pure, blinding white sweeps across my vision, I realize the dream isn't ending—it's just being framed.
